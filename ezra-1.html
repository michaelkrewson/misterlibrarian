Babylon has fallen and the first year of Persian rule has begun — the decree of Cyrus, the first party of returners, and the temple vessels counted out of a foreign treasury.
538 BC — 'the first year of Cyrus king of Persia,' counted from his capture of Babylon in 539. The chapter dates itself by Jeremiah's seventy years (Jer 25:11-12; 29:10) reaching their term, and the policy behind it is externally fixed by the Cyrus Cylinder.

Translator’s Notes

Verses 1–3 · the sentence the Hebrew Bible ends on, finished

⚠ These three verses are how the Jewish Scriptures END. In the Hebrew canon Chronicles is the last book, and its last two verses are this decree — and they stop mid-sentence. 2 Chronicles 36:23 gets as far as “whoever is among you of all his people, may Jehovah his God be with him, and let him go up” — and that is the final word of the Hebrew Bible. Ve-ya’al. Let him go up.

Ezra begins by quoting the same decree and then finishing the sentence: go up to Jerusalem, which is in Judah, and build the house of Jehovah. Whatever the literary relationship between the two books — and it is much argued — the effect for a reader who has just closed one and opened the other is that a command left hanging is completed. It is worth reading them in that order once.

«So that the word of Jehovah from the mouth of Jeremiah might be completed.» The seventy years (Jeremiah 25:11–12; 29:10). The book opens by treating a prophecy as an accounting entry that has come due, and the whole point of dating it “the first year of Cyrus” is that the sum works out.

«Jehovah STIRRED UP the spirit of Cyrus.» He’ir — roused, woke. Note that the same verb returns four verses later of the people who volunteer (“everyone whose spirit God had stirred up,” v5). One verb, two ends of the empire: it wakes a Persian emperor and it wakes a handful of Judean householders, and the book treats those as the same act.

⚠ «THUS SAYS Cyrus king of Persia.» Koh amar is the prophetic messenger formula — the words that introduce an oracle all through Isaiah, Jeremiah and Ezekiel: thus says Jehovah. Here a pagan emperor’s administrative rescript is introduced in exactly the same words, and Isaiah 45:1 has already called Cyrus God’s anointed, his mashiach — the only foreigner in the Bible to get that title. This chapter is not embarrassed about any of it.

⚠ And here is the historical question, which is a good one. The policy in this decree is externally attested. The Cyrus Cylinder in the British Museum — a clay barrel inscribed after Cyrus took Babylon in 539 BC — records exactly this programme: returning deported peoples to their homes and restoring their sanctuaries and their gods. But on the Cylinder Cyrus credits MARDUK, Babylon’s god, with giving him the kingdoms and commissioning the work, in wording strikingly close to verse 2. So:

• A genuine rescript, in the local idiom. Persian administration routinely issued decrees to each people in its own language and its own theology — the Cylinder is the Babylonian version, this is the Judean one. On this reading Cyrus’s scribes wrote “Jehovah the God of the heavens” for the same reason they wrote “Marduk” in Babylon, and no one is being misquoted.
• The gist, in the writer’s own words. The decree is real and the return happened, but the theology is the Judean historian’s framing of it, in the vocabulary of his own tradition.

The library prints the decree and does not choose. It is worth adding that “the God of the heavens” (elohei ha-shamayim) is precisely the title one expects here: it is the standard Persian-period way of naming the supreme god in official documents, usable by a Judean, an Aramean or a Persian without either side having to specify whose god is meant. Diplomacy has its own vocabulary in every century.

Verses 4–6 · the neighbours hand over silver and gold — where have we seen this?

⚠ The shape of verse 4 is not an accident. Those who stay are to assist the ones who go — with silver, with gold, with goods, with cattle. Set that beside Exodus 12:35–36, where Israel leaving Egypt asks the neighbours for objects of silver and gold and is given them. A departure from a foreign land, funded by the people they lived among: the book is describing a second exodus, and it signals it by the shape of the sentence rather than by saying so. The prophets of the exile had been promising exactly this comparison for decades (Isaiah 43:16–19 tells the returning exiles not to remember the former things, because a new one is coming). Ezra does not make the point. He arranges the furniture and lets the reader make it.

«Assist him» is yenasse’uhu, from nasa, to lift or carry — the men of his place are to hold him up. KJV “let the men of his place help him”; ASV the same; NIV “provide him with.” The Hebrew is more physical than any of them.

«The heads of the fathers’ houses of JUDAH AND BENJAMIN.» Two tribes. This is what is left of the twelve, and the book states it without a word of lament — along with the priests and the Levites, who are the other half of everything Ezra cares about. A reader coming from the genealogies of Chronicles, which insisted on tracing all twelve, feels the size of the gap.

«Everyone whose spirit God had stirred up.» The verb from verse 1, now applied to ordinary people — and it is worth noticing what it implies about the ones who stayed. Most of the Judean community in Babylonia did not go. They had houses, businesses and (as the Murashu archive from Nippur shows a century later) a settled, prosperous life. Going back meant leaving that for a ruined town and a building site. The book does not criticize those who remained; it simply says that some were stirred.

«Strengthened their hands.» An idiom for backing someone materially, and the same phrase that will be used against the enemies of the work later in the book. Everyone in this chapter is either lifting or strengthening somebody.

Verses 7–8 · the vessels come home — and a man nobody can identify

⚠ The vessels have a history, and this site has already published its beginning. Daniel 1:2 opens with Nebuchadnezzar carrying part of the vessels of the house of God to the land of Shinar and putting them in the treasure house of his god — which is where verse 7 finds them. In between, Daniel 5 has Belshazzar drink wine from them at a feast and see a hand writing on the wall. Now a Persian king takes them out of that same temple and counts them back. The vessels are the one physical thing that survives the whole catastrophe intact, and the writer clearly enjoys returning them by inventory.

«By the hand of Mithredath the treasurer.» A Persian name (Mithra-given) and a Persian office. The paperwork is the point: this is a transfer of state property, recorded as such, with an officer named on one side and a receiving official on the other.

⚠ And the receiving official is a puzzle. Who is SHESHBAZZAR? He is called “the prince of Judah,” he receives the vessels here, and Ezra 5:14–16 says the same man was made governor and laid the foundation of the house of God. Then he disappears, and every other account of the foundation credits ZERUBBABEL. The readings:

• Two names for one man — a Babylonian court name and a Hebrew one, exactly as Daniel and his three friends were renamed (Daniel 1:7). Neat, but nowhere stated, and elsewhere the two are not equated.
• Two different men — Sheshbazzar an older Davidide who led the first party under Cyrus, Zerubbabel his successor a generation later under Darius. This accounts for the awkwardness of the foundation being laid twice.
• Sheshbazzar is Shenazzar, listed among the sons of Jehoiachin at 1 Chronicles 3:18 — which would make him an uncle of Zerubbabel and genuinely “prince of Judah.” The names are close, and the identification is old and attractive, and unproven.

No vote. It is a real gap in one of the best-documented periods of Israel’s history, and the honest thing is to say so.

Verses 9–11 · an inventory that does not add up

⚠ The numbers in this list do not total the number given. Add them: 30 gold basins, 1,000 silver basins, 29 knives, 30 gold bowls, 410 silver bowls, 1,000 other vessels — 2,499. Verse 11 says the total was 5,400. This is a well-known crux and the readings are these:

• The itemized list is partial — a selection of the notable categories, with the grand total covering everything, including quantities of small items never broken out. The difficulty is that a line already reads “other vessels, a thousand,” which looks like it was meant to sweep up the remainder.
• Numerals have been corrupted in copying — the likeliest single explanation, and numbers are the most fragile thing in any manuscript tradition, as the parallel lists in Chronicles and Kings show repeatedly.
• Another version of the list existed. The Greek 1 Esdras gives a different set of figures for the same inventory, which suggests the difficulty was felt early and someone tried to fix it.

The library prints the Masoretic figures and states the arithmetic plainly rather than quietly correcting one of the numbers, which is what a translation is tempted to do.

Two of the vessel-words are uncertain. Agartelei (vv9) occurs nowhere else in the Bible — KJV “chargers,” ASV “platters,” NIV “dishes”; we print “basins” and flag it. Machalafim is stranger still: from a root meaning to change or replace, so either knives (things that pass through) or duplicates, replacement pieces. KJV and ASV read “knives”; we follow them and note the alternative.

⚠ A rarity in the Hebrew text, and the fourth on this site in four chapters: the Masoretes place a paragraph mark inside verse 10, between the gold bowls and the silver ones. The same feature appeared at 1 Kings 1:19, 2 Kings 1:17 and 1 Chronicles 1:51. Here it may simply be an accountant’s ruling between two columns.

And the last words of the chapter are the verb the whole book turns on. “All this Sheshbazzar brought up when the exiles were brought up from Babylon to Jerusalem” — he’elah… he’alot, the going-up word twice in one clause. It was the last word of the Hebrew Bible (“let him go up”), it is the command of verse 3, and it is what actually happens here: gold and silver and people, going up the same road, counted.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the exact / distinct words: “go up” for alah in all four places, because it is the decree’s verb, the Hebrew Bible’s last word, and the chapter’s last clause; “stirred up” for he’ir, kept identical of the emperor (v1) and of the householders (v5); “Thus says Cyrus” for koh amar, the prophetic formula, unaltered; “assist him” for yenasse’uhu, the lifting-verb; “freewill offering” for nedavah, what is given because the giver chose to; “the God of the heavens” for the Persian-period diplomatic title; and the inventory figures as the Masoretic text gives them, with the arithmetic stated rather than repaired.

Echoes paid. 2 Chronicles 36:22–23, the last two verses of the Hebrew Bible, whose broken-off sentence this chapter completes; Jeremiah’s seventy years, treated as an account come due; Exodus 12:35–36, the neighbours’ silver and gold, in a second exodus the book never names; and the vessels of Daniel 1:2, carried into the treasure house of a foreign god and now counted back out of it.

Echoes planted. Sheshbazzar, who receives the vessels, lays a foundation in chapter 5, and is never explained. Zerubbabel and Jeshua, who lead the list in chapter 2 and build the altar in chapter 3. The opposition, which arrives in chapter 4 and stops the work for a reign and a half. And the house itself — commanded by a Persian in verse 2, finished in chapter 6, and wept over by the old men who remembered the first one.

Ezra is a new book on the site. Next in sequence: Ezra 2 — the register of those who came up: family by family, town by town, with the singers and the gatekeepers and the temple servants counted, and at the end a handful of priests who could not prove their descent and were put out of the priesthood until a priest should arise with Urim and Thummim.
