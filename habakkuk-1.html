A prophet argues with God: how long will you tolerate the violence rotting Judah? — and God's answer, that he is raising up the Chaldeans, only sharpens the question, since a holy God should not use a nation more wicked than the one it punishes.
c. 605 BC — the book turns on the rise of Babylon: the Chaldeans, who under Nebuchadnezzar crushed Egypt at Carchemish in 605 and were sweeping west toward a Jerusalem they would burn in 587. Habakkuk is a contemporary of Jeremiah, standing at the very end of Judah's independence, wrestling with the God who is about to hand his people to the empire. The chapter's question is answered in chapter 2 ('the righteous will live by his faithfulness') and resolved in chapter 3's psalm of trust-in-the-dark.

La carga que vio Habacuc el profeta.

עַד-אָנָה יְהוָה שִׁוַּעְתִּי, וְלֹא תִשְׁמָע: אֶזְעַק אֵלֶיךָ חָמָס, וְלֹא תוֹשִׁיעַ.
How long, Jehovah, must I cry for help and you do not hear? I cry out to you, “Violence!” — and you do not save.note
¿Hasta cuándo, Jehová, clamaré, y no oirás? Grito a ti «¡Violencia!» —y no salvas—.

3
לָמָּה תַרְאֵנִי אָוֶן וְעָמָל תַּבִּיט, וְשֹׁד וְחָמָס לְנֶגְדִּי; וַיְהִי רִיב וּמָדוֹן, יִשָּׂא.
Why do you make me see iniquity, and look on trouble? Destruction and violence are before me; strife breaks out, and contention rises up.note
¿Por qué me haces ver iniquidad, y contemplas la aflicción? Destrucción y violencia hay delante de mí; surge el pleito, y se alza la contienda.
4
עַל-כֵּן תָּפוּג תּוֹרָה, וְלֹא-יֵצֵא לָנֶצַח מִשְׁפָּט: כִּי רָשָׁע מַכְתִּיר אֶת-הַצַּדִּיק, עַל-כֵּן יֵצֵא מִשְׁפָּט מְעֻקָּל.
So the law grows numb, and justice never goes out; for the wicked hem in the righteous, and so justice comes out twisted.note
Por eso la ley se entumece, y la justicia nunca sale; porque el impío rodea al justo, y así la justicia sale torcida.
5
רְאוּ בַגּוֹיִם וְהַבִּיטוּ, וְהִתַּמְּהוּ תְּמָהוּ: כִּי-פֹעַל פֹּעֵל בִּימֵיכֶם, לֹא תַאֲמִינוּ כִּי יְסֻפָּר.
Look among the nations and watch; be utterly astonished! For a work is being done in your days that you would not believe if it were told.note
Mirad entre las naciones y contemplad; asombraos, quedad atónitos. Porque en vuestros días se hace una obra que no creeríais aunque os la contaran.
6
כִּי-הִנְנִי מֵקִים אֶת-הַכַּשְׂדִּים, הַגּוֹי הַמַּר וְהַנִּמְהָר; הַהוֹלֵךְ, לְמֶרְחֲבֵי-אֶרֶץ, לָרֶשֶׁת, מִשְׁכָּנוֹת לֹּא-לוֹ.
For look — I am raising up the Chaldeans, that bitter and headlong nation, who march across the breadth of the earth to seize dwellings not their own.note
Porque mira: yo levanto a los caldeos, esa nación amarga y precipitada, que marcha por la anchura de la tierra para tomar posesión de moradas ajenas.
7
אָיֹם וְנוֹרָא, הוּא; מִמֶּנּוּ, מִשְׁפָּטוֹ וּשְׂאֵתוֹ יֵצֵא.
Dreadful and terrible he is; his justice and his rank come only from himself.note
Espantosa y terrible es; de sí misma salen su justicia y su rango.
8
וְקַלּוּ מִנְּמֵרִים סוּסָיו, וְחַדּוּ מִזְּאֵבֵי עֶרֶב, וּפָשׁוּ, פָּרָשָׁיו; וּפָרָשָׁיו, מֵרָחוֹק יָבֹאוּ--יָעֻפוּ, כְּנֶשֶׁר חָשׁ לֶאֱכוֹל.
His horses are swifter than leopards, keener than the wolves of evening; his horsemen come charging — his horsemen come from afar, they fly like a vulture swooping to devour.note
Sus caballos son más veloces que leopardos, más feroces que lobos al anochecer; sus jinetes cargan —sus jinetes vienen de lejos, vuelan como buitre que se lanza a devorar—.
9
כֻּלֹּה לְחָמָס יָבוֹא, מְגַמַּת פְּנֵיהֶם קָדִימָה; וַיֶּאֱסֹף כַּחוֹל, שֶׁבִי.
Every one of them comes for violence; their faces are set to press forward, and they gather captives like sand.note
Todos ellos vienen para la violencia; sus rostros están puestos hacia adelante, y reúnen cautivos como arena.
10
וְהוּא בַּמְּלָכִים יִתְקַלָּס, וְרֹזְנִים מִשְׂחָק לוֹ; הוּא לְכָל-מִבְצָר יִשְׂחָק, וַיִּצְבֹּר עָפָר וַיִּלְכְּדָהּ.
He scoffs at kings, and rulers are a joke to him; he laughs at every fortress, heaps up dust against it, and takes it.note
Se burla de los reyes, y los príncipes le son un chiste; se ríe de toda fortaleza, amontona polvo contra ella, y la toma.
11
אָז חָלַף רוּחַ וַיַּעֲבֹר, וְאָשֵׁם: זוּ כֹחוֹ, לֵאלֹהוֹ.
Then he sweeps on like the wind and passes by — and he is guilty, this one whose own strength is his god.note
Entonces pasa como el viento y sigue de largo —y es culpable, este cuya propia fuerza es su dios—.
12
הֲלוֹא אַתָּה מִקֶּדֶם, יְהוָה אֱלֹהַי קְדֹשִׁי--לֹא נָמוּת; יְהוָה לְמִשְׁפָּט שַׂמְתּוֹ, וְצוּר לְהוֹכִיחַ יְסַדְתּוֹ.
Are you not from of old, Jehovah my God, my Holy One? We will not die. Jehovah, you have set him for judgment; Rock, you established him to rebuke.note
¿No eres tú desde la antigüedad, Jehová, Dios mío, Santo mío? No moriremos. Jehová, tú lo has puesto para juicio; Roca, tú lo estableciste para reprender.
13
טְהוֹר עֵינַיִם מֵרְאוֹת רָע, וְהַבִּיט אֶל-עָמָל לֹא תוּכָל: לָמָּה תַבִּיט, בּוֹגְדִים--תַּחֲרִישׁ, בְּבַלַּע רָשָׁע צַדִּיק מִמֶּנּוּ.
You are of purer eyes than to look on evil, and you cannot gaze on trouble — why then do you look on the treacherous, and stay silent while the wicked swallows one more righteous than himself?note
Eres de ojos demasiado puros para mirar el mal, y no puedes contemplar la aflicción; ¿por qué, pues, miras a los traidores, y callas mientras el impío devora al que es más justo que él?
14
וַתַּעֲשֶׂה אָדָם, כִּדְגֵי הַיָּם--כְּרֶמֶשׂ, לֹא-מֹשֵׁל בּוֹ.
You make humankind like the fish of the sea, like crawling things that have no ruler.note
Y haces a los hombres como los peces del mar, como reptiles que no tienen quien los gobierne.
15
כֻּלֹּה, בְּחַכָּה הֵעֲלָה--יְגֹרֵהוּ בְחֶרְמוֹ, וְיַאַסְפֵהוּ בְּמִכְמַרְתּוֹ; עַל-כֵּן, יִשְׂמַח וְיָגִיל.
He hauls all of them up with a hook, drags them off in his net, and gathers them in his dragnet; and so he rejoices and exults.note
A todos los saca con anzuelo, los arrastra en su red, y los junta en su barredera; por eso se alegra y salta de gozo.
16
עַל-כֵּן יְזַבֵּחַ לְחֶרְמוֹ, וִיקַטֵּר לְמִכְמַרְתּוֹ: כִּי בָהֵמָּה שָׁמֵן חֶלְקוֹ, וּמַאֲכָלוֹ בְּרִאָה.
So he sacrifices to his net and burns incense to his dragnet; for by them his portion is fat and his food rich.note
Por eso ofrece sacrificio a su red y quema incienso a su barredera; porque por ellas es opulenta su porción y abundante su comida.
17
הַעַל כֵּן, יָרִיק חֶרְמוֹ; וְתָמִיד לַהֲרֹג גּוֹיִם, לֹא יַחְמוֹל. {ס}
Will he then keep emptying his net, slaying nations without ceasing, and never spare them?note
¿Vaciará por eso su red, y seguirá matando naciones sin cesar, sin perdonar jamás?

Translator’s Notes

Verse 1 · the prophet who argues back — and the scroll a hidden community copied out

⚠ Habakkuk is the odd book of the Twelve, and the first oddity is its DIRECTION. Every other prophet speaks FROM God TO the people; Habakkuk speaks FROM the prophet TO God, and waits for a reply. The book is a DIALOGUE — a two-round argument in which the prophet lodges a complaint, God answers, the prophet objects to the answer, and God answers again. It is the Bible’s most sustained wrestling-match with the oldest hard question there is: if God is just, why does injustice win? Job asks it as a sufferer; Habakkuk asks it as a prosecutor, on behalf of a whole society.

⚠ The name fits the book exactly. Chavaqquq most likely comes from chabaq, “to embrace” — or, in its more forceful sense, “to grapple, to wrestle.” Luther called him “the embracer,” one who takes hold of God and will not let go; the name could equally be read as “the wrestler,” which is what he does for three chapters. (An older guess links it to an Akkadian garden-plant name, hambaququ; the “embrace” reading is the one that means something.) Nothing else is told about him — no father, no home town — but he is called “the prophet” outright, a title given to very few in their own headings.

The heading calls the book a burden (massa, the heavy doom-oracle, the same word that opened Nahum) “that Habakkuk saw” — a vision, though it arrives as an argument. KJV and ASV “the burden which Habakkuk the prophet did see”; DRB, from the Vulgate, spells him “Habacuc.”

⚠ And Habakkuk has a distinction almost no biblical book can match: we have an ancient commentary on it, older than any complete Bible manuscript. Among the very first Dead Sea Scrolls pulled out of a cave at Qumran in 1947 was the Habakkuk Pesher (1QpHab) — a running verse-by-verse commentary on chapters 1 and 2, copied about a century before Christ, applying Habakkuk’s “wicked” and “righteous” to the community’s own struggle between a “Wicked Priest” and a “Teacher of Righteousness.” It is one of the best-preserved of all the scrolls, and it shows this small, strange book being read with intense seriousness two thousand years ago.

Verses 2–4 · the first complaint: how long, and why won’t you look?

⚠ The book opens with a word no other prophet dares aim at God so bluntly: «HOW LONG?» Ad-anah is the cry of the psalms of lament (Psalm 13, “how long, Jehovah? will you forget me forever?”), and Habakkuk hurls it as an accusation: “How long must I cry for help and you do not HEAR? I cry to you ‘Violence!’ and you do not SAVE.” The complaint is not that God is far away but that he is present and apparently doing nothing.

⚠ The charge is chamas — the flood-word. Chamas, “violence,” is the same lawless, society-wrecking cruelty that had “filled the earth” before the flood (Genesis 6:11–13) and that Obadiah laid on Edom. Habakkuk is describing his own Judah — the last decades before Babylon, a country rotting from inside with the strong preying on the weak. And he stacks the words for it: iniquity, trouble, destruction, violence, strife, contention — six terms in two verses, a society named by its symptoms.

⚠ And the diagnosis is a courtroom collapse. “So the law grows NUMB” — tapug, goes cold, loses its grip, like a limb gone to sleep — “and justice never goes out; for the wicked hem in the righteous, so justice comes out TWISTED” (me’uqqal, bent, crooked). When the guilty surround the innocent in court, a verdict still emerges — but it comes out warped. It is the exact grievance of Amos and Micah, put now not to the judges but to the Judge.

Verses 5–11 · God’s answer — and the cure looks worse than the disease

⚠ God answers — and the answer is so alarming he tells them in advance they won’t believe it. “Look among the NATIONS … be utterly astonished! For a work is being done in your days that you would not believe if it were told.” The remedy for Judah’s internal rot is not a reformer or a revival; it is an invasion. KJV “wonder marvellously … I will work a work in your days, which ye will not believe”; DRB “wonder, and be astonished.” ⚠ Paul quotes this very verse at Antioch (Acts 13:41) as a warning to scoffers — the “unbelievable work” becoming, for him, the gospel itself.

⚠ «I am raising up the CHALDEANS» is the shock the whole book turns on. The Chaldeans are the neo-Babylonians, who under Nebuchadnezzar had just crushed Egypt at Carchemish (605 BC) and were sweeping west — “that bitter and headlong nation” (mar, bitter; nimhar, reckless, hasty). God is not merely permitting Babylon; he says “I am raising them up.” The empire that will burn Jerusalem is called God’s own doing. That is the answer — and it is what triggers the prophet’s second, harder complaint.

The description of the Babylonian war-machine is a masterpiece of dread. Horses swifter than leopards and keener than wolves of evening (hungriest at dusk); cavalry that “fly like a vulture swooping to devour”; a people who take captives “like sand,” who “scoff at kings,” for whom “rulers are a joke,” who “laugh at every fortress” and simply heap up siege-ramps of dust and walk in. They are a force of nature with no reverence for anything. ASV “their horses also are swifter than leopards, and are more fierce than the evening wolves”; NWT “their horses are swifter than leopards, more ferocious than the wolves of the evening,” both keeping the terrible dusk-image the older “evening wolves” carries.

⚠ And verse 11 names their real sin in four Hebrew words. “Then he sweeps on like the wind and passes by — and he is GUILTY (ashem), this one whose own strength is his god” (zu kocho le’eloho). That is the theological heart of the answer: Babylon is God’s instrument AND a guilty idolater, because it worships its own power. The verse is famously hard — KJV read it “then shall his mind change … imputing this his power unto his god” — but the Masoretic sense is sharper: a conqueror who has made a deity of his own might, and stands condemned by the very success God is using. It plants the answer chapter 2 will give: the proud one is already doomed, and “the righteous will live by his faithfulness” (2:4).

Verses 12–17 · the second complaint: how can holy eyes watch this?

The prophet takes God’s answer and turns it into a harder question. He does not deny that Babylon is God’s instrument; he grants it — “Jehovah, you have SET him for judgment; Rock, you established him to rebuke” — and then presses the contradiction that opens under it. He addresses God with a piled-up confession of trust first: “Are you not from of old, Jehovah my God, my Holy One? WE WILL NOT DIE” — a defiant clause, holding onto the covenant even while accusing. “Rock” (tzur) is one of the oldest titles for God, the immovable thing you shelter behind.

⚠ Then the sharpest theological sentence in the Minor Prophets. “You are of PURER EYES than to look on evil, and you cannot gaze on trouble — WHY THEN do you look on the treacherous, and stay SILENT while the wicked swallows one more righteous than himself?” This is the theodicy problem stated as cleanly as it has ever been: God’s holiness (he cannot even LOOK at evil) set flat against God’s apparent inaction (yet he WATCHES it and says nothing). And it sharpens what Habakkuk complained of in verse 4: there the wicked hemmed in the righteous; now a MORE wicked nation is being used to devour a LESS wicked one. The cure is more guilty than the disease. DRB “Thy eyes are too pure to behold evil … why lookest thou upon them that do unjust things?”; ASV “Thou that art of purer eyes than to behold evil.”

⚠ And the closing image is Babylon as a FISHERMAN, and the whole human race its catch. “You make humankind like the FISH of the sea, like crawling things that have no ruler” — leaderless, defenseless, there for the taking. Babylon “hauls them all up with a hook, drags them off in his net, gathers them in his dragnet, and rejoices.” Then the barb: “so he sacrifices to his net and burns incense to his dragnet” — the conqueror worshipping his own weapons, offering incense to the instruments of his conquest, because “by them his portion is fat.” It is the same idolatry as verse 11, drawn as a picture: a man on his knees before his own tackle. ASV “therefore he sacrificeth unto his net, and burneth incense unto his drag”; NWT “that is why he sacrifices to his dragnet and makes sacrificial smoke to his fishing net” — both keeping the literal, shocking picture of a man offering worship to his own fishing gear. The chapter ends on a question left hanging in the air — “will he then keep emptying his net, slaying nations without ceasing, and never spare them?” — and the prophet, having fired it, goes up to his watchtower to wait for the reply (2:1).

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the exact / distinct words: “How long” for ad-anah (v2), the lament-cry aimed straight at God; “violence” for chamas (vv2, 3, 9), the flood-word for society-wrecking cruelty, running from Habakkuk’s Judah to the Babylonian army; “the law grows numb” for tapug torah (v4), a limb gone cold; “twisted” for me’uqqal, justice that comes out bent; “the Chaldeans” kept, the neo-Babylonians God says HE is raising up; “whose own strength is his god” for zu kocho le’eloho (v11), the empire that deifies its own power; “of purer eyes than to look on evil” (v13), the theodicy problem in a phrase; and “Rock” for tzur (v12), the ancient shelter-title.

Echoes paid. Chamas, the “violence” that filled the earth before the flood (Genesis 6) and marked Edom in Obadiah, now the charge against Judah itself. The “how long?” of the lament psalms, turned from petition into cross-examination. The massa, “burden,” that also headed Nahum — two neighbouring prophets, one certain of Babylon’s victim and one troubled by it. And the storm-God who raises up empires as his rod — the same conviction Isaiah put on Assyria (“the rod of my anger,” Isaiah 10), here put on Babylon and then questioned.

Echoes planted. “The righteous will live by his faithfulness” (2:4) — the answer to this chapter’s question, and the single verse the New Testament quotes THREE times (Romans 1:17; Galatians 3:11; Hebrews 10:38), the seed of Paul’s whole gospel and, through Luther, of the Reformation. The five woes on the Chaldean tyrant that fill the rest of chapter 2 (“woe to him who heaps up what is not his own…”). “The earth will be filled with the knowledge of the glory of Jehovah as the waters cover the sea” (2:14). And chapter 3’s great psalm — “Jehovah, I have heard your report and I fear” — ending on the highest note of trust-in-the-dark in the whole Bible: “though the fig tree does not blossom … yet I will exult in Jehovah.”
