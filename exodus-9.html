Plagues five, six, and seven: the pestilence on Egypt's herds, the boils that drive the magicians from the court, and the hail with fire in it — the first plague announced a day ahead with instructions for surviving it, and the first that some Egyptians believe.
c. 1446 BC on the early-date chronology this project foregrounds. The ripening barley of 9:31 places the hail in roughly late January or early February — about two months before the Passover in the month of the ear.

Exodus 9 — The Mister Translation

1
וַיֹּאמֶר יְהוָה אֶל-מֹשֶׁה, בֹּא אֶל-פַּרְעֹה; וְדִבַּרְתָּ אֵלָיו, כֹּה-אָמַר יְהוָה אֱלֹהֵי הָעִבְרִים, שַׁלַּח אֶת-עַמִּי, וְיַעַבְדֻנִי.
And Jehovah said to Moses, “Go in to Pharaoh, and you shall say to him, ‘Thus says Jehovah, the God of the Hebrews: Send my people away, that they may serve me.note
Y Jehová dijo a Moisés: «Entra a la presencia del faraón y dile: Así dice Jehová, el Dios de los hebreos: Deja ir a mi pueblo, para que me sirva.
2
כִּי אִם-מָאֵן אַתָּה, לְשַׁלֵּחַ, וְעוֹדְךָ, מַחֲזִיק בָּם.
For if you refuse to send them away, and you still hold on to them,note
Porque si te niegas a dejarlos ir, y todavía los retienes,
3
הִנֵּה יַד-יְהוָה הוֹיָה, בְּמִקְנְךָ אֲשֶׁר בַּשָּׂדֶה, בַּסּוּסִים בַּחֲמֹרִים בַּגְּמַלִּים, בַּבָּקָר וּבַצֹּאן--דֶּבֶר, כָּבֵד מְאֹד.
behold, the hand of Jehovah is coming upon your livestock that are in the field — upon the horses, upon the donkeys, upon the camels, upon the herds and upon the flocks — a very heavy pestilence.note
he aquí que la mano de Jehová viene sobre tu ganado que está en el campo — sobre los caballos, sobre los asnos, sobre los camellos, sobre las vacadas y sobre los rebaños —: una peste muy pesada.
4
וְהִפְלָה יְהוָה--בֵּין מִקְנֵה יִשְׂרָאֵל, וּבֵין מִקְנֵה מִצְרָיִם; וְלֹא יָמוּת מִכָּל-לִבְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל, דָּבָר.
And Jehovah will make a division between the livestock of Israel and the livestock of Egypt, and nothing shall die of all that belongs to the sons of Israel.’ ”note
Y Jehová hará distinción entre el ganado de Israel y el ganado de Egipto, y no morirá nada de todo lo que pertenece a los hijos de Israel».

5
וַיָּשֶׂם יְהוָה, מוֹעֵד לֵאמֹר: מָחָר, יַעֲשֶׂה יְהוָה הַדָּבָר הַזֶּה--בָּאָרֶץ.
And Jehovah set an appointed time, saying, “Tomorrow Jehovah will do this thing in the land.”note
Y Jehová fijó un tiempo señalado, diciendo: «Mañana hará Jehová esta cosa en la tierra».
6
וַיַּעַשׂ יְהוָה אֶת-הַדָּבָר הַזֶּה, מִמָּחֳרָת, וַיָּמָת, כֹּל מִקְנֵה מִצְרָיִם; וּמִמִּקְנֵה בְנֵי-יִשְׂרָאֵל, לֹא-מֵת אֶחָד.
And Jehovah did this thing on the next day, and all the livestock of Egypt died; but of the livestock of the sons of Israel not one died.note
Y Jehová hizo esta cosa al día siguiente, y murió todo el ganado de Egipto; pero del ganado de los hijos de Israel no murió ni uno.
7
וַיִּשְׁלַח פַּרְעֹה--וְהִנֵּה לֹא-מֵת מִמִּקְנֵה יִשְׂרָאֵל, עַד-אֶחָד; וַיִּכְבַּד לֵב פַּרְעֹה, וְלֹא שִׁלַּח אֶת-הָעָם. {פ}
And Pharaoh sent, and behold, not so much as one of the livestock of Israel had died. But the heart of Pharaoh was heavy, and he did not send the people away.note
Y el faraón mandó a ver, y he aquí que del ganado de Israel no había muerto ni siquiera uno. Pero el corazón del faraón se hizo pesado, y no dejó ir al pueblo.
8
וַיֹּאמֶר יְהוָה, אֶל-מֹשֶׁה וְאֶל-אַהֲרֹן, קְחוּ לָכֶם מְלֹא חָפְנֵיכֶם, פִּיחַ כִּבְשָׁן; וּזְרָקוֹ מֹשֶׁה הַשָּׁמַיְמָה, לְעֵינֵי פַרְעֹה.
And Jehovah said to Moses and to Aaron, “Take for yourselves handfuls of soot from a furnace, and let Moses throw it toward the heavens before the eyes of Pharaoh.note
Y Jehová dijo a Moisés y a Aarón: «Tomad puñados llenos de hollín de un horno, y que Moisés lo esparza hacia los cielos ante los ojos del faraón.
9
וְהָיָה לְאָבָק, עַל כָּל-אֶרֶץ מִצְרָיִם; וְהָיָה עַל-הָאָדָם וְעַל-הַבְּהֵמָה, לִשְׁחִין פֹּרֵחַ אֲבַעְבֻּעֹת--בְּכָל-אֶרֶץ מִצְרָיִם.
And it shall become fine dust over all the land of Egypt, and it shall become upon man and upon beast a boil breaking out in blisters, throughout all the land of Egypt.”note
Y se convertirá en polvo fino sobre toda la tierra de Egipto, y vendrá a ser sobre el hombre y sobre la bestia una úlcera que brota en ampollas, por toda la tierra de Egipto».
10
וַיִּקְחוּ אֶת-פִּיחַ הַכִּבְשָׁן, וַיַּעַמְדוּ לִפְנֵי פַרְעֹה, וַיִּזְרֹק אֹתוֹ מֹשֶׁה, הַשָּׁמָיְמָה; וַיְהִי, שְׁחִין אֲבַעְבֻּעֹת, פֹּרֵחַ, בָּאָדָם וּבַבְּהֵמָה.
And they took the soot of the furnace and stood before Pharaoh, and Moses threw it toward the heavens; and it became a boil of blisters, breaking out on man and on beast.note
Y tomaron el hollín del horno y se pusieron delante del faraón, y Moisés lo esparció hacia los cielos; y hubo úlceras de ampollas que brotaban en el hombre y en la bestia.
11
וְלֹא-יָכְלוּ הַחַרְטֻמִּים, לַעֲמֹד לִפְנֵי מֹשֶׁה--מִפְּנֵי הַשְּׁחִין: כִּי-הָיָה הַשְּׁחִין, בַּחַרְטֻמִּם וּבְכָל-מִצְרָיִם.
And the magicians could not stand before Moses because of the boils, for the boils were on the magicians and on all Egypt.note
Y los magos no pudieron estar delante de Moisés a causa de las úlceras, porque las úlceras estaban en los magos y en todo Egipto.
12
וַיְחַזֵּק יְהוָה אֶת-לֵב פַּרְעֹה, וְלֹא שָׁמַע אֲלֵהֶם: כַּאֲשֶׁר דִּבֶּר יְהוָה, אֶל-מֹשֶׁה. {ס}
And Jehovah made strong the heart of Pharaoh, and he did not listen to them, just as Jehovah had spoken to Moses.note
Y Jehová endureció el corazón del faraón, y no los escuchó, tal como Jehová había hablado a Moisés.

13
וַיֹּאמֶר יְהוָה, אֶל-מֹשֶׁה, הַשְׁכֵּם בַּבֹּקֶר, וְהִתְיַצֵּב לִפְנֵי פַרְעֹה; וְאָמַרְתָּ אֵלָיו, כֹּה-אָמַר יְהוָה אֱלֹהֵי הָעִבְרִים, שַׁלַּח אֶת-עַמִּי, וְיַעַבְדֻנִי.

14
כִּי בַּפַּעַם הַזֹּאת, אֲנִי שֹׁלֵחַ אֶת-כָּל-מַגֵּפֹתַי אֶל-לִבְּךָ, וּבַעֲבָדֶיךָ, וּבְעַמֶּךָ--בַּעֲבוּר תֵּדַע, כִּי אֵין כָּמֹנִי בְּכָל-הָאָרֶץ.
For this time I am sending all my plagues against your heart, and upon your servants and upon your people, so that you may know that there is none like me in all the earth.note
Porque esta vez envío todas mis plagas contra tu corazón, y sobre tus siervos y sobre tu pueblo, para que sepas que no hay como yo en toda la tierra.
15
כִּי עַתָּה שָׁלַחְתִּי אֶת-יָדִי, וָאַךְ אוֹתְךָ וְאֶת-עַמְּךָ בַּדָּבֶר; וַתִּכָּחֵד, מִן-הָאָרֶץ.
For by now I could have stretched out my hand and struck you and your people with pestilence, and you would have been wiped from the earth.note
Porque a estas alturas yo podría haber extendido mi mano y haberte herido a ti y a tu pueblo con peste, y habrías sido borrado de la tierra.
16
וְאוּלָם, בַּעֲבוּר זֹאת הֶעֱמַדְתִּיךָ, בַּעֲבוּר, הַרְאֹתְךָ אֶת-כֹּחִי; וּלְמַעַן סַפֵּר שְׁמִי, בְּכָל-הָאָרֶץ.
But for this very reason I have kept you standing: to show you my power, and so that my name may be declared in all the earth.note
Pero precisamente por esto te he mantenido en pie: para mostrarte mi poder, y para que se declare mi nombre en toda la tierra.

17

You are still exalting yourself against my people, refusing to send them away.note
Todavía te ensalzas contra mi pueblo, negándote a dejarlos ir.
18
הִנְנִי מַמְטִיר כָּעֵת מָחָר, בָּרָד כָּבֵד מְאֹד, אֲשֶׁר לֹא-הָיָה כָמֹהוּ בְּמִצְרַיִם, לְמִן-הַיּוֹם הִוָּסְדָה וְעַד-עָתָּה.
Behold, about this time tomorrow I will rain down a very heavy hail, such as has not been in Egypt from the day it was founded until now.note
He aquí que mañana a esta hora haré llover granizo muy pesado, como no lo hubo en Egipto desde el día en que fue fundada hasta ahora.
19
וְעַתָּה, שְׁלַח הָעֵז אֶת-מִקְנְךָ, וְאֵת כָּל-אֲשֶׁר לְךָ, בַּשָּׂדֶה: כָּל-הָאָדָם וְהַבְּהֵמָה אֲשֶׁר-יִמָּצֵא בַשָּׂדֶה, וְלֹא יֵאָסֵף הַבַּיְתָה--וְיָרַד עֲלֵהֶם הַבָּרָד, וָמֵתוּ.
And now, send, bring your livestock to shelter, and everything you have in the field. Every man and beast that is found in the field and is not gathered into the house — the hail shall come down upon them, and they shall die.’ ”note
Y ahora, manda poner a salvo tu ganado y todo lo que tienes en el campo. Todo hombre y bestia que se halle en el campo y no sea recogido bajo techo — el granizo caerá sobre ellos, y morirán».
20
הַיָּרֵא אֶת-דְּבַר יְהוָה, מֵעַבְדֵי פַּרְעֹה--הֵנִיס אֶת-עֲבָדָיו וְאֶת-מִקְנֵהוּ, אֶל-הַבָּתִּים.
The one who feared the word of Jehovah among the servants of Pharaoh made his servants and his livestock flee into the houses;note
El que temió la palabra de Jehová entre los siervos del faraón hizo huir a sus siervos y su ganado a las casas;
21
וַאֲשֶׁר לֹא-שָׂם לִבּוֹ, אֶל-דְּבַר יְהוָה--וַיַּעֲזֹב אֶת-עֲבָדָיו וְאֶת-מִקְנֵהוּ, בַּשָּׂדֶה. {פ}
but whoever did not set his heart to the word of Jehovah left his servants and his livestock in the field.note
pero el que no puso su corazón en la palabra de Jehová dejó a sus siervos y su ganado en el campo.
22
וַיֹּאמֶר יְהוָה אֶל-מֹשֶׁה, נְטֵה אֶת-יָדְךָ עַל-הַשָּׁמַיִם, וִיהִי בָרָד, בְּכָל-אֶרֶץ מִצְרָיִם: עַל-הָאָדָם וְעַל-הַבְּהֵמָה, וְעַל כָּל-עֵשֶׂב הַשָּׂדֶה--בְּאֶרֶץ מִצְרָיִם.
And Jehovah said to Moses, “Stretch out your hand toward the heavens, and let there be hail in all the land of Egypt — upon man and upon beast and upon every plant of the field, in the land of Egypt.”note
Y Jehová dijo a Moisés: «Extiende tu mano hacia los cielos, y haya granizo en toda la tierra de Egipto: sobre el hombre y sobre la bestia y sobre toda planta del campo, en la tierra de Egipto».
23
וַיֵּט מֹשֶׁה אֶת-מַטֵּהוּ, עַל-הַשָּׁמַיִם, וַיהוָה נָתַן קֹלֹת וּבָרָד, וַתִּהֲלַךְ אֵשׁ אָרְצָה; וַיַּמְטֵר יְהוָה בָּרָד, עַל-אֶרֶץ מִצְרָיִם.
And Moses stretched out his staff toward the heavens, and Jehovah gave thunders and hail, and fire went down to the earth; and Jehovah rained hail upon the land of Egypt.note
Y Moisés extendió su vara hacia los cielos, y Jehová dio truenos y granizo, y descendió fuego a la tierra; y Jehová hizo llover granizo sobre la tierra de Egipto.
24
וַיְהִי בָרָד--וְאֵשׁ, מִתְלַקַּחַת בְּתוֹךְ הַבָּרָד: כָּבֵד מְאֹד--אֲשֶׁר לֹא-הָיָה כָמֹהוּ בְּכָל-אֶרֶץ מִצְרַיִם, מֵאָז הָיְתָה לְגוֹי.
And there was hail, and fire flashing in the midst of the hail — very heavy, such as had not been in all the land of Egypt since it became a nation.note
Y hubo granizo, y fuego que llameaba en medio del granizo — muy pesado, como no lo hubo en toda la tierra de Egipto desde que llegó a ser nación.
25
וַיַּךְ הַבָּרָד בְּכָל-אֶרֶץ מִצְרַיִם, אֵת כָּל-אֲשֶׁר בַּשָּׂדֶה, מֵאָדָם, וְעַד-בְּהֵמָה; וְאֵת כָּל-עֵשֶׂב הַשָּׂדֶה הִכָּה הַבָּרָד, וְאֶת-כָּל-עֵץ הַשָּׂדֶה שִׁבֵּר.
And the hail struck, in all the land of Egypt, everything that was in the field, from man to beast; and the hail struck every plant of the field, and it shattered every tree of the field.note
Y el granizo hirió, en toda la tierra de Egipto, todo lo que estaba en el campo, desde el hombre hasta la bestia; y el granizo hirió toda planta del campo, y quebró todo árbol del campo.
26
רַק בְּאֶרֶץ גֹּשֶׁן, אֲשֶׁר-שָׁם בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל--לֹא הָיָה, בָּרָד.
Only in the land of Goshen, where the sons of Israel were, there was no hail.note
Solamente en la tierra de Gosén, donde estaban los hijos de Israel, no hubo granizo.

27
וַיִּשְׁלַח פַּרְעֹה, וַיִּקְרָא לְמֹשֶׁה וּלְאַהֲרֹן, וַיֹּאמֶר אֲלֵהֶם, חָטָאתִי הַפָּעַם: יְהוָה, הַצַּדִּיק, וַאֲנִי וְעַמִּי, הָרְשָׁעִים.
And Pharaoh sent and called for Moses and Aaron, and said to them, “I have sinned this time. Jehovah is the righteous one, and I and my people are the wicked.note
Y el faraón mandó llamar a Moisés y a Aarón, y les dijo: «He pecado esta vez. Jehová es el justo, y yo y mi pueblo somos los inicuos.

28

Rogad a Jehová — y basta ya de truenos de Dios y de granizo —, y os dejaré ir, y no os quedaréis más».
29

And Moses said to him, “As I go out of the city I will spread out my palms to Jehovah. The thunders will cease and the hail will be no more, so that you may know that the earth belongs to Jehovah.note
Y Moisés le dijo: «Al salir yo de la ciudad extenderé mis palmas a Jehová. Los truenos cesarán y el granizo no será más, para que sepas que de Jehová es la tierra.
30
וְאַתָּה, וַעֲבָדֶיךָ: יָדַעְתִּי--כִּי טֶרֶם תִּירְאוּן, מִפְּנֵי יְהוָה אֱלֹהִים.
But as for you and your servants, I know that you do not yet fear Jehovah God.”note

Translator’s Notes

Verses 1–7 · the fifth blow, and a line drawn again

The chapter numbering realigns here. The Masoretic offset that ran through chapters 7 and 8 closes at the end of the last chapter; Hebrew and English Exodus 9 are the same 35 verses. From here the two number alike.

The fifth plague falls on the herds — and with them on the wealth, the transport, the plowing and the altars of Egypt at one stroke. Egypt’s cattle were also its gods: Hathor wore the cow’s horns, the Apis bull was worshiped as a living god at Memphis, and Khnum was ram-headed. The pestilence is called “very HEAVY” (kaved) — the same word this book keeps using for Pharaoh’s heart.

⚠ «All the livestock of Egypt died» — and yet livestock are struck again nine verses later, and the firstborn of the cattle die at the last plague. The difficulty is old and the answer is in verse 3: the threat is aimed at “your livestock THAT ARE IN THE FIELD.” Hebrew kol, “all,” regularly means all of a stated kind rather than every last one in existence — here, all that were out in the open. The animals penned or stalled survived, which is exactly why verse 19 can warn Pharaoh to bring the rest indoors.

The DIVISION sharpens. The verb is hiflah, to set apart — the same distinction drawn at the swarms (8:18), now stated in advance and verified by inspection: Pharaoh SENDS to check, and finds not one animal of Israel’s dead. He has proof in hand, from his own investigators, and it changes nothing. KJV “the LORD shall sever”; the ESV “will make a distinction.”

Verses 8–12 · the magicians cannot stand — and the hand changes

The sixth plague is thrown, not spoken. Moses takes soot from a kiln and flings it skyward in front of the court, and it comes down as fine dust on the whole land: boils breaking out in blisters on man and beast. KJV “a boil breaking forth with blains”; the ESV “boils breaking out in sores.” Egypt had gods for this too — Sekhmet, the lion-headed mistress of plague, and Imhotep, the healer — and neither answers.

⚠ The professionals are finished. At the gnats the magicians could not duplicate; here they cannot even stand. The men whose whole office was to stand before Pharaoh are driven from the room by sores they cannot cure on their own bodies. They are never heard from again.

⚠ And now the sentence that changes hands. Read the hardening notices in order and something happens at this exact verse. Through the first five plagues the narrator says either that Pharaoh’s heart “was heavy” or “was strong” — a description with no subject — or that Pharaoh himself made it heavy (8:11, 8:28). Here, for the first time in the narration of events, Jehovah is the grammatical subject: “And JEHOVAH made strong the heart of Pharaoh.” It had been announced in advance (4:21; 7:3), but it is only now enacted, and only after the man has hardened himself five times over.

That order is the textual basis of the reading long called judicial hardening — that God confirms a man in the direction he has already chosen, so that the sentence is the sin repeated. It is a reading of the sequence, not a solution to it: the book still says both things and never reconciles them, and Paul will take the same material somewhere else entirely four verses from now. The library reports what the text does with the verbs and leaves the argument standing.

Verses 13–21 · “for this reason I have kept you standing”

The seventh plague opens with the longest speech of the cycle, and its subject is not hail but Pharaoh himself — why he is still alive.

⚠ Verse 15 is a counterfactual, and versions split on it. The Hebrew can be read as a plain past (“I stretched out my hand…”) or, as most now take it, as what could have happened: by now I could have struck you with pestilence and you would have been wiped off the earth. KJV reads it as future — “For now I will stretch out my hand” — while the ESV and NIV read the potential past, “By now I could have…” The point either way is that the plagues have been restrained: five blows in, Egypt is still standing on purpose.

⚠ Verse 16, and the word Paul used. “For this very reason I have he’emadticha” — literally “made you stand,” which can mean raised you up (to a throne, for a purpose) or kept you standing (left you alive when you might have died). The Greek Old Testament chose “raised up,” and Paul quotes that Greek at Romans 9:17 as the hinge of his argument about God’s freedom. So the verse arrives in the New Testament through a translation that had already picked one of its two senses. KJV “have I raised thee up”; the ESV “I have let you live”; the NWT “I have kept you in existence.” Both senses point the same direction — “so that my NAME may be declared in all the earth” — but they are not the same claim, and it is worth knowing which one you are reading.

⚠ Then a warning, which is new. No previous plague came with instructions for surviving it. Here Pharaoh is told to bring men and animals in from the field, and the announcement runs a full day ahead: “about this time tomorrow.” The plague is announced in order to be escaped.

⚠ And some Egyptians BELIEVE. “The one who FEARED the word of Jehovah among the servants of Pharaoh” ran his people and his herds indoors; the one who “did not set his heart to the word” left them out. It is the first crack in Egypt: the line no longer runs simply between nations but through Pharaoh’s own household, drawn by whether a man takes the word seriously. These are plausibly the beginning of the “mixed multitude” that walks out with Israel at 12:38 — Egyptians who had learned to believe an announcement.

Verses 22–35 · fire in the hail, and the first confession

The storm itself is described as something unnatural: thunders, hail, and “fire flashing in the midst of the hail” — ice and fire in the same fall. KJV “fire mingled with the hail”; the ESV “fire flashing continually.” Egypt gets little rain; a hailstorm that shatters trees is not a bad season, it is an intrusion — and the sky was Nut’s, the air Shu’s, the storm Seth’s. Only Goshen is untouched.

⚠ «I have sinned THIS TIME.» Pharaoh’s first confession, and it is exact courtroom language: Jehovah is the RIGHTEOUS one (ha-tzaddiq), and I and my people are the WICKED (ha-resha’im) — the verdict a judge pronounces, said by the defendant about himself. But note the qualifier he attaches: this time. And note what he asks for — not forgiveness, only that the noise stop. His own word for God shifts too: he swears by “Jehovah” in verse 27 and asks relief from “the thunders of GOD” in verse 28, a phrase old translators read as a superlative for sheer violence (KJV “mighty thunderings”) and modern ones as plain “God’s thunder.”

Moses is not fooled, and says so: he will pray, and the hail will stop, “so that you may know that THE EARTH BELONGS TO JEHOVAH” — the claim widened from Egypt to the whole world — “but as for you and your servants, I know that you do not YET fear Jehovah God.” The prophet grants the confession and denies the repentance in the same breath, and verse 34 proves him right within the hour: the rain stops, and Pharaoh sins again and makes his heart heavy — he does, the verb back in his own hands.

⚠ Two verses of botany that quietly date the plague (vv31-32). The flax and barley were destroyed because the barley was “in the aviv” — in the ear, ripening — and the flax in bud; the wheat and spelt survived “for they are late.” In Egypt’s calendar that is roughly late January or early February: barley heads and flax flowers weeks before wheat. The detail is useless to a storyteller and decisive to a farmer, and it sets the clock running toward Passover in the month of Abib — a month named for that very word, the ripening ear. KJV “the barley was in the ear, and the flax was bolled,” and calls the spelt “rie”; the ESV and NIV read “spelt.”

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the exact / distinct words: “a boil breaking out in blisters” for shechin…ava’bu’ot; “I have kept you standing” for he’emadticha, with the Greek “raised up” that Paul quotes noted rather than substituted; “the righteous one…the wicked” kept as the courtroom pair tzaddiq / resha’im; “in the ear” for aviv, the word behind the month-name Abib; and the three heart-verbs held apart — his heart was heavy (v7), Jehovah made it strong (v12), he made it heavy again (v34) — where most versions print one word, “hardened,” for all three.

Echoes paid. The division between Israel and Egypt, drawn at the swarms and now verified by Pharaoh’s own inspectors; the magicians, beaten at the gnats and here driven from the room; Goshen, spared a third time; and the announced hour — “about this time tomorrow” — that has proved the Name since the frogs.

Echoes planted. The Egyptians who feared the word and brought their households in, plausibly the beginning of the “mixed multitude” that walks out at 12:38; “that my name may be declared in all the earth” (v16), which Paul will carry into Romans 9:17; “the earth belongs to Jehovah” (v29), the claim widened past Egypt; and the ripening barley, which sets the clock running toward a Passover in the month of the ear.

Next in this book: Exodus 10 — the locusts and the darkness that can be felt, and the chapter in which Pharaoh’s own officials break before he does: “How long shall this man be a snare to us? Do you not yet know that Egypt is destroyed?”
