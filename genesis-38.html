Translator's Notes

Verses 1–11 · the brother who walked away — and two deaths

"At that time" — and the story stops dead. Joseph has just been sold and is on the road to Egypt, and Genesis abruptly drops him to follow Judah for thirty verses. Readers have called this an interruption since antiquity; it is the opposite. Watch what the chapter does to the man who said "what gain is it?" — and note that the very words he used to break his father will be used, here, to break him. Chapter 38 is the book's own comment on chapter 37, and it is placed exactly where the comment belongs.

"Judah went DOWN from his brothers." Vayyered — the same verb, in the same breath of narrative, that is carrying Joseph down to Egypt. Two brothers go down in these two chapters: one is dragged, one walks. The Hebrew lets you hear that Judah's departure is a descent in more than direction — he separates from the family, marries outside it, and buries two sons before the chapter is half over.

A Canaanite marriage, reported without comment. Abraham had made a servant swear an oath to prevent exactly this (24:3), and Isaac and Rebekah were made bitter by Esau's Canaanite wives (26:34-35). Judah simply "saw… and took." The narrator declines to editorialize — this book prefers to let a reader who has been paying attention supply the judgment.

The levirate duty, two centuries before the law. "Do the duty of a brother-in-law" is a single Hebrew verb, yabbem, built from the noun for a husband's brother — the custom later written into law at Deuteronomy 25:5-10: when a man dies childless, his brother marries the widow, and the first son counts as the dead man's, so the name is not blotted out. It is a provision for the widow and for the dead, and everything in this chapter turns on Judah's household refusing it. The KJV's "perform the duty of an husband's brother unto her" spells the idea out; the NWT reads "perform brother-in-law marriage with her," which is exactly the institution.

What Onan actually did, and what the text actually says about it. The verb is worth keeping: Onan ruined it on the ground — shichet, the same root this translation renders "ruin" all through the flood, where the earth was ruined and God resolved to ruin it in turn (6:11-13). And the narrator gives the motive without being asked: "Onan knew that the offspring would not be his… so as not to give offspring to his brother." That is a property calculation — as the firstborn's death had made him heir, a son credited to Er would displace him. The chapter's stated charge is the withholding: he took the marriage and refused the duty that was its whole point, using his brother's widow while cheating his brother's name. The word "onanism" is a much later coinage that reads a different sin back into the verse; the older Jewish and Christian commentaries argued the point at length, and a reader is entitled to know that the argument is theirs and not the text's. Genesis says what Onan withheld, and from whom.

Judah's own withholding. Then the pattern repeats one rung up. Judah tells Tamar to sit as a widow in her father's house until Shelah grows up — and the narrator lets us hear the private reason he does not say aloud: "Otherwise he too will die, like his brothers." He suspects the woman. So he sends her home indefinitely, neither married nor free to remarry, in exactly the limbo Onan's refusal created. Onan withheld a son; Judah withholds a son too. She waits. He forgets.

Verses 12–19 · the veil, the Opening of the Eyes, and a pledge

"And Judah was comforted." Vayyinnachem — and this is the cruelest quiet sentence in the Joseph story. One chapter earlier Jacob, told his son was dead, refused to be comforted — vayema'en lehitnachem, from this same root nacham. Judah, who engineered the sale that caused it, buries a wife and is comforted, and goes off to a sheepshearing — which in this culture is a festival. The father cannot be consoled; the son gets over it and goes to the party. The narrator sets the two verbs eleven verses apart and says nothing.

The veil, in a family that knows what veils are for. Tamar's disguise is one garment, and this house has form: Rebekah covered herself with a veil when she first saw Isaac (24:65), and Laban's wedding-night substitution of Leah for Rachel (29:23-25) only works because a bride's face is hidden. Jacob deceived a father who could not see; Leah deceived a husband who could not see; now a daughter-in-law deceives a father-in-law who does not look. Note carefully what verse 15 says: he thought her a prostitute because she had covered her face — the covering is what marks her as available, not the reverse of what a modern reader might assume.

The place is a joke at his expense. She sits at petach einayim — "the entrance of Enaim," a real town on the Timnah road. But einayim is also, transparently, the ordinary Hebrew word for EYES, so the phrase reads at the same time as "the Opening of the Eyes." She takes her seat at the place called Eyes-Open, and the next verse is "he did not know that she was his daughter-in-law." The KJV misses both the town and the pun, reading "in an open place"; the ASV has "in the gate of Enaim" and the NIV "at the entrance to Enaim," which recover the place-name. This translation keeps the name and lets the note carry the wordplay — it is untranslatable, but it is not decoration: the whole chapter is about a man who cannot recognize what is in front of him.

He offers a goat. Of course he does. Judah's price is a kid of the goats — gedi izzim. Two chapters ago his brothers slaughtered a male goat to soak Joseph's tunic in its blood (37:31), and before that Jacob wore goatskins to rob his blind father (27:16). The goat is this family's instrument of deception, passed down three generations, and here Judah cheerfully offers one as the fee — walking into a deception on the very animal he has used to run one.

The pledge is his identity. She does not ask for money. She asks for his seal, his cord, and his staff — a man's cylinder seal on its neck-cord (the thing he signs with, the ancient equivalent of a signature) and the carved staff that marks his standing. It is the most extraordinary demand in the chapter, and he hands it over without a blink: everything that proves who he is, surrendered to a stranger he does not intend to see again. The KJV's "thy signet, and thy bracelets, and thy staff" reads petil as "bracelets"; it is a cord or thread — the ASV, the NIV and the NWT all read "cord," and the cord is what the seal hangs on.

Verses 20–23 · a friend's careful vocabulary

The word changes on the way to town. This is a small thing the versions mostly bury, and it is very funny. In verse 15 the narrator says Judah took her for a zonah — the plain word for a common prostitute. But when Hirah has to go and ask directions from the locals, he does not use it. He asks for the qedeshah — literally a consecrated woman, from the root qadosh, "holy," the same root as the sanctuary. Whether the two words describe genuinely different roles (a shrine functionary versus a roadside prostitute) is still argued, and the evidence outside the Bible is thinner than confident commentaries suggest. What is not in doubt is the social register: Hirah reaches for the dignified term when he has to say it out loud to strangers, on his friend's behalf. The KJV flattens both words to "harlot" and the distinction vanishes entirely; the NIV reads "shrine prostitute" and the NWT "temple prostitute," both committing to the cultic reading; the ASV keeps a difference in the line and flags the Hebrew in its margin. This translation renders "consecrated woman" — close to what the word literally says, without deciding an institution the evidence will not settle.

"Or we will become a laughingstock." Judah gives up. He has lost his seal, his cord and his staff to a woman nobody can find, and the only thing that worries him is being laughed at — buz, contempt. He would rather write off the instruments of his own identity than be seen looking for them. Three months later that decision arrives back with a witness.

Verses 24–26 · "recognize, please" — the sentence comes home

The double standard, stated without a flicker. Judah has just bought a roadside prostitute and is untroubled. Told that his daughter-in-law is pregnant, his entire deliberation is two words in Hebrew: hotsi'uha ve-tissaref — "bring her out and let her be burned." Not a hearing, not a question. Burning is a punishment the later law reserves for a very few cases; whatever legal standing Judah imagines he has here as head of the family, the narrator reports the speed of it, and the speed is the point. The KJV's "Bring her forth, and let her be burnt" has never needed improving.

And then the two words. "Haker-na — recognize, please — whose are these." It is the same verb and the same particle of polite entreaty that Judah and his brothers used when they sent their father a bloodied coat and asked him to identify it: "haker-na, is it your son's tunic, or not?" (37:32). He is answered in his own sentence. And the props rhyme as exactly as the words: he sent a garment with a goat's blood on it to make a father believe a lie; he is sent his own seal and staff to make him admit a truth. Note too that the verb has now failed and landed three times in three generations of this family — Isaac "did not recognize" the son in front of him (27:23), Jacob did recognize a coat that was lying to him, and Judah recognizes the one thing he cannot argue with, because it is his signature.

"She is more righteous than I." Tsadqah mimmenni — the first honest sentence Judah speaks in the Bible, and the hinge of his life. The root is tsadaq, the righteousness that was "counted" to Abram when he believed (15:6); here it is counted to a Canaanite widow who tricked a patriarch, by the patriarch, out loud, in public. The Hebrew is comparative and slightly awkward — "she is in the right, more than I" — and every version reaches for it a little differently: the KJV "She hath been more righteous than I," the NWT "She is more righteous than I am," the NIV "She is more righteous than I," the Geneva "She is more righteous then I." He does not excuse himself and he does not blame her; he names the withheld son as the cause, which is the one fact he had kept quiet for years. Everything Judah becomes later — the brother who offers his own body as surety for Benjamin, the one who gets the kingship in Jacob's blessing — starts in this verse. The chapter that looked like a detour was the road.

Verses 27–30 · twins again, and the line to David

The family's oldest struggle, one more time. Twins wrestling at birth over who comes out first is not new here: Jacob himself was born gripping Esau's heel, having lost that race (25:26), and the whole book has been prying the birthright away from firstborns ever since. The midwife ties a scarlet thread — shani — on the hand that appears, an early and entirely practical piece of record-keeping; and then the hand goes back in and the other child is out first anyway. Even the marker cannot make the order hold.

Perez — "breach." "How you have broken out — a breach upon you!" The midwife's cry is a pun on the name: parats, to burst through, and perets, a gap torn in a wall. It sounds like an exclamation of dismay and is preserved as a name. The KJV spells him Pharez, the Douay Phares (through the Greek), the ASV and moderns Perez — the same man under three spellings, which is worth knowing when you meet him in a genealogy.

Which is where this chapter has been going all along. Ruth ends by tracing its own hero's line — "these are the generations of Perez" — ten names down to David (Ruth 4:18-22), and the Gospel of Matthew opens the New Testament by running the same line to Jesus, naming in its first paragraph "Perez and Zerah by Tamar" (Matthew 1:3). She is one of only four women in that genealogy, and Matthew keeps her. So the messianic line does not descend through the son Judah protected, or through the marriage he arranged, or through any part of his own plan. It descends through the widow he wronged and the twins conceived at a roadside — and Genesis, which never once calls Tamar righteous in its own voice, lets Judah say it for the record and leaves the sentence standing.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the words exact / distinct: "ruined it on the ground" for shichet — the flood's own "ruin" verb, kept audible; "do the duty of a brother-in-law" for the single verb yabbem; "prostitute" for zonah but "consecrated woman" for qedeshah, keeping Hirah's careful upgrade visible where the KJV flattens both to "harlot"; "seal and cord and staff" (not the KJV's "bracelets"); "Enaim" kept as the town it is, with the Eyes-Open pun left to the note; and "She is more righteous than I" plain, in Judah's own mouth.

Echoes paid. Haker-na, "recognize, please" — sent to a father in 37:32, returned to its sender here, with a garment-and-goat answered by a seal-and-staff; nakar completing its third turn in three generations after Isaac's bedside; nacham — Jacob refusing comfort while Judah "was comforted" eleven verses later; the goat of 27:16 and 37:31 offered as a fee; the veil of 24:65 and the wedding-night substitution of 29:25; struggling twins after 25:26; and tsedaqah, "righteousness," counted at 15:6 and now spoken over Tamar.

Echoes planted. Judah, who has just learned to say "I was wrong," becoming the brother who offers himself as surety for Benjamin (43:9; 44:33) — the change starts here; the scepter that "shall not depart from Judah" (49:10); Perez's line running through Ruth to David (Ruth 4:18-22) and into Matthew's first paragraph, where Tamar is named (Matthew 1:3); and the levirate duty, unwritten here, becoming law at Deuteronomy 25:5-10 — and the engine of the book of Ruth.
