The tariff of the last four kings — Shallum carried to Egypt, Jehoiakim's donkey-burial, Coniah the signet torn off; 'is that not to know me?' and 'write this man childless.'
Delivered across Jehoiakim's reign (c. 609–598 BC), with its Coniah oracle reaching the deportation of 597 — the chapter surveys Josiah's dead and his three heirs in turn: Jehoahaz already in Egypt, Jehoiakim reigning, and Jehoiachin's fall previewed. The kings run in the reverse of Abraham: called out of their homeland by force, not promise.

Translator's Notes — verse by verse

Each note explains this translation's choice and compares the seven versions on the shelf. Quotations from copyrighted versions (NIV, TLB, NWT) are kept to short phrases for comparison; KJV, Geneva, Douay-Rheims and ASV are public domain.

Verses 1–9 · בִּי נִשְׁבַּעְתִּי bi nishbati — "by myself I have sworn"

A charge to the whole royal house. Jeremiah is sent "down" from the temple to the palace to lay one condition before the throne of David: do justice and righteousness (mishpat u-tsedaqah — the same paired demand of 21:12, and the very thing the coming righteous Branch will do, 23:5), rescue the robbed, and protect the three who have no protector — the resident alien, the fatherless, and the widow (ger, yatom, almanah, the Torah's standing triad; ger is kept "resident alien" as at Genesis 23:4 and Exodus 2:22). Obey, and kings keep riding through these gates; refuse, and the house becomes "a ruin."

The oath that cuts both ways. God seals the threat with bi nishbati, "by myself I have sworn" — and that self-oath has one unforgettable precedent: it is word-for-word the oath that sealed the blessing to Abraham on Moriah, "by myself I have sworn, declares Jehovah" (Genesis 22:16). When God swears "by himself," there is no higher court to appeal to — and here the same unbreakable oath that once guaranteed the promise now guarantees the palace's fall if it will not do justice. The chapter's Gilead-and-Lebanon imagery follows: the royal house is God's own "head of Lebanon," its cedar halls (see v14) his cedar forest — which is exactly why "they will cut down your choicest cedars and cast them on the fire," the fire promised for the palace's "forest" back at 21:14. And the passers-by who ask "why has Jehovah done this?" are answered in the old covenant-lawsuit formula (Deuteronomy 29): "because they forsook the covenant."

Verses 10–12 · שַׁלֻּם Shallum — the first son to fall

Do not weep for the dead — weep for the exile. The "dead" is good king Josiah, killed at Megiddo (609 BC) and mourned across Judah; but Jeremiah says the tears belong elsewhere — to "the one who goes away," who "will return no more, nor see the land of his birth" (erets moladto — the very word, moledet, of Abram's call, "go from your land and your birthplace," Genesis 12:1: Abraham left his homeland by promise, this king loses his by force). The exile named is Shallum — the personal name of the king better known by his throne-name Jehoahaz, Josiah's son, who reigned three months before Pharaoh Necho hauled him to Egypt in chains (2 Kings 23:31-34). "In the place where they exiled him, there he will die" — and he did, in Egypt, the first of Josiah's sons to be carried off, never to see Judah again. Josiah's death was the mourned one; his son's living exile is the one to weep over.

Verses 13–19 · הֲלוֹא-הִיא הַדַּעַת אֹתִי ha-lo hi ha-da'at oti — "is that not to know me?"

Woe to the palace-builder. Now the woe (hoy) falls on Jehoiakim, Josiah's other son, whom Necho set on the throne after taking Jehoahaz. His crime is written in his architecture: a "spacious house" with cedar panelling and vermilion paint (shashar, a costly red ochre) — built with unpaid labor, "who makes his neighbor work for nothing and does not give him his wages." The taunt is withering: "Do you reign because you vie in cedar?" (the KJV's famously opaque "because thou closest thyself in cedar" is trying to render the same rare verb metachareh, "compete, contend to excel"). Kingship is not measured in lumber.

The verse at the chapter's heart. Against the son, Jeremiah holds up the father: Josiah "ate and drank" — lived, and lived well — and "did justice and righteousness; then it went well with him. He judged the cause of the poor and needy; then it was well. Is that not what it means to know me? declares Jehovah" (v16). This is one of the most important sentences in the prophets: to know God (ha-da'at oti — the noun da'at, "knowledge," that Proverbs 1:7 calls the thing the fear of Jehovah begins) is not a doctrine held in the head but justice done for the poor. Jehoiakim's eyes and heart, by contrast, are on nothing but dishonest gain (betsa), innocent blood, and extortion. So his end is the anti-funeral: no one will cry the customary laments over him — "Alas, my brother! … Alas, lord!" — the very cry hoy that opened the woe returns as the mourning-cry he will not receive. Instead, "the burial of a donkey" (KJV "ass") — that is, no burial at all: "dragged off and flung out beyond the gates." (Kings and Chronicles are quieter about Jehoiakim's actual death; Jeremiah's oracle stands as the sentence pronounced, and the Hebrew leaves the grim picture undimmed.)

Verses 20–30 · חוֹתָם … עֲרִירִי chotam … ariri — the signet, torn off; "childless"

Wail from the heights. The address turns to Jerusalem as a woman: climb Lebanon, Bashan, and Abarim — the northern and eastern heights — and cry, "for all your lovers are broken" (me'ahavayikh, the allied nations, Egypt chief among them, she trusted instead of God). "From your youth you have not obeyed my voice" — lo shama'at be-qoli, the exact inverse of the reason Abraham was blessed, "because you obeyed my voice" (Genesis 22:18): the royal house is the anti-Abraham. There is a bitter pun in v22 — "the wind (ruach) will shepherd (tir'eh) all your shepherds (ro'ayikh)": the leaders who were to pasture the flock are themselves pastured — swept off — by the wind. Verse 23 carries two ketiv/qere pairs (the archaic feminine ending -ti written, the standard -t read) — Mechon-Mamre prints both forms, and this translation keeps the received reading, "you who dwell … nested in the cedars."

The signet torn off. The last king in the roll is Coniah — a clipped, almost contemptuous form of Jehoiachin (also Jeconiah), Jehoiakim's eighteen-year-old son, three months on the throne before Nebuchadnezzar carried him to Babylon in 597. God says: even if Coniah "were a signet (chotam) on my right hand" — the king's own seal-ring, the emblem of delegated authority pressed into every royal decree — "yet from there I would tear you off." Then the threefold cry, unforgettable in the Hebrew: erets, erets, arets — "Land! Land! Land! Hear the word of Jehovah!" And the sentence: "Write this man down as childless" (ariri).

"Childless" — and the line that would not die. The word is a puzzle the verse itself unlocks, and one of the Bible's quietest hinges. Jehoiachin was not literally childless — he fathered seven sons in Babylon (1 Chronicles 3:17-18). The curse is defined in the next breath: "childless" as to the throne — "for none of his seed will prosper, sitting on the throne of David and ruling again in Judah." And so it held: no descendant of Jehoiachin ever again reigned over the old kingdom. Yet two things bend back against the curse. First, the signet is expressly restored to his own grandson: "I will make you like a signet, for I have chosen you," God tells Zerubbabel (Haggai 2:23) — the very image torn off Coniah pressed back onto his line. Second, when the Gospels trace the Messiah's descent, Matthew's royal, legal line runs straight through Jeconiah (Matthew 1:11-12), while Luke's runs by another of David's sons — the two genealogies long read together as the throne-right passing through the cursed king by adoption while the bloodline slips the curse. The king "written childless" as to David's old throne becomes an ancestor of the one who inherits a throne that outlasts it. The Hebrew does not say any of that; it says ariri, and lets the centuries answer.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the words exact / distinct: "the resident alien, the fatherless, and the widow" for ger-yatom-almanah (with the NWT's "alien resident," kept consistent with Genesis 23 and Exodus 2, vs the KJV's vaguer "stranger"); "vie in cedar" for the rare metachareh (the KJV's "closest thyself in cedar" is guessing at a verb that means to compete); "the burial of a donkey" (KJV "ass"); and "Coniah" kept as the text's own clipped form (as KJV/ASV), with Jehoiachin/Jeconiah explained rather than silently swapped in.

Echoes paid. "By myself I have sworn" (v5) — the Aqedah's oath (22:16), the blessing's seal turned into judgment's; the land of your birth (moledet, v10) — Abram's forsaken homeland (12:1) lost the other way, by exile; "you have not obeyed my voice" (v21) — the exact reverse of Abraham's obedience (22:18); "is that not to know me?" (v16) — justice for the poor as the true da'at, the knowledge Proverbs 1:7 prizes; and the cedars felled and burned (v7) — the fire set to the palace "forest" of 21:14.
