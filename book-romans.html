📖 Romans

Πρὸς Ῥωμαίους (Pros Rhōmaious) — 'To the Romans.' Not a title the author gave it — the manuscripts simply label the letters by their recipients — but an unusually informative one, because the address is the whole problem the letter is solving: Paul is writing to a congregation in the capital of the empire that he did not found, has never visited, and needs.

The most consequential letter ever written, by almost any measure of consequence — the document behind Augustine's conversion, Luther's Reformation, Wesley's warmed heart and Barth's commentary; and, read plainly, a travelling missionary's careful letter of introduction to strangers whose backing he wanted for a journey to Spain.

Chapter by chapter

Romans 1The opening of the most consequential letter ever written. Paul is in Corinth, about fifty-six years old, with the eastern Mediterranean behind him and Spain in front of him — and he needs the backing of congregations in the capital that he has never met and did not found. So he writes them a letter of introduction, and it turns into the most sustained argument in the New Testament. The first seven verses are one enormous Greek sentence in which he calls himself a SLAVE before he calls himself an apostle; then a careful, rather charming approach to strangers, in which he offers to give them a spiritual gift and then catches himself and says he wants one back. And then, in two sentences, the thesis that reorganised Europe: “I am not ashamed of the gospel; for it is God’s power for salvation to everyone who believes, to the Jew first and also to the Greek. For in it God’s righteousness is revealed, from faith to faith” — quoting Habakkuk, the answer the prophet climbed his watchtower to wait for, and the line Augustine, Luther and Wesley each dated their turning to. The rest of the chapter is the indictment: humanity knew God, would not honour him, and traded his glory down through the creature-list of Genesis 1 in reverse — man, birds, beasts, things that crawl — after which God, three times, simply “gave them over.” ⚠ It contains the most argued-over paragraph in the Bible, printed here with the vocabulary laid out and the readings given their pedigrees, and no vote cast. ⚠ And it is a trap: the vice list is bait, and the next sentence after the chapter break springs it — “therefore you are without a defense, whoever you are who judges.”

About the book

Where it sits
The FIRST of Paul's letters in every Bible — placed first not because it was written first (it was not) but because the collection is ordered by LENGTH, longest to shortest, which is how ancient letter collections were arranged. It stands at the head of the New Testament's second half, immediately after Acts, which is the book that tells you who Paul is.
Genre
A LETTER, and the most sustained piece of argument in the New Testament — but a real letter, dictated aloud to a secretary who signs his own name at the end ('I, Tertius, who wrote this letter, greet you' — 16:22), carried by a named woman, and ending with twenty-six personal greetings. It uses the DIATRIBE, the classroom style of Greco-Roman moral teaching in which the speaker argues with an imaginary objector: 'What shall we say then?' 'By no means!' Reading Romans as a treatise loses the voices.
Author
PAUL, uncontested — one of the seven letters accepted as his by essentially all scholarship. He dictated it: Tertius, the scribe, breaks in at 16:22 to greet the readers in his own voice, one of the most human moments in the New Testament and a reminder that these documents were spoken before they were read.
Date written
Around AD 56–57, and datable with unusual confidence for an ancient document. Paul is about to carry the collection to Jerusalem (15:25), has finished his work in the eastern Mediterranean, and is planning Spain by way of Rome (15:24). The place fits Corinth in the winter of the third missionary journey — Gaius his host (16:23) and Erastus the city treasurer (16:23) both belong there, and a first-century Corinthian pavement naming an aedile Erastus has been found.
Place
Almost certainly CORINTH, or its port Cenchreae — Phoebe, who carries the letter, is a deacon of the congregation at Cenchreae (16:1).
Audience
The congregations in ROME — several house-groups rather than one assembly, and mixed. The mix is the letter's live problem: the emperor Claudius had expelled Jews from Rome around AD 49 (Suetonius says it was over disturbances 'at the instigation of Chrestus'), leaving the Roman congregations Gentile; after Claudius died in 54 the exiles came back, to churches that had learned to run without them. Chapters 14–15, on the strong and the weak, are addressed to exactly that room.

How it's laid out

1:1–17Salutation, thanksgiving, and the thesis: the gospel is God's power for salvation, and in it God's righteousness is revealed 'from faith to faith.'
1:18–3:20The indictment — the nations, then the religious, then everyone: 'there is no one righteous, not even one.'
3:21–5:21The answer — righteousness through faith, Abraham as the proof, peace with God, and the two Adams.
6:1–8:39The new life — dead to sin, freed from law, led by the Spirit; the groaning creation, and 'nothing can separate us.'
9:1–11:36Israel — the letter's most anguished section, and the reason 'to the Jew first' had to be said in chapter 1.
12:1–15:13The life that follows — bodies as a living sacrifice, the body and its gifts, governing authorities, and the strong bearing with the weak.
15:14–16:27Travel plans (Jerusalem, Rome, Spain), and a closing chapter of greetings that is a census of the early church.

What it's about

  • The RIGHTEOUSNESS OF GOD — the letter's key phrase, and the one whose grammar has been argued over for five centuries: God's own covenant faithfulness, or a righteousness he gives, or both.
  • FAITH, and what it is that faith does — announced from Habakkuk 2:4 in chapter 1 and never left alone thereafter.
  • JEW AND GENTILE in one people — not an appendix to the argument but its engine; 'to the Jew first and also to the Greek' is stated in chapter 1 and defended for three chapters in the middle.
  • SIN as a power, not merely acts — something people are 'under', which is why the letter's answer is a rescue rather than an improvement.
  • The SPIRIT and the new humanity — chapter 8, the summit of the letter and arguably of Paul.
  • GOD'S FAITHFULNESS to his promises, which is what makes Israel's unbelief an emergency rather than a footnote.

The source text

Translated from the Greek of the SBL GREEK NEW TESTAMENT (SBLGNT), edited by Michael W. Holmes. Romans is textually well attested — P46, from about AD 200, carries most of it — but it has one famous oddity: the ENDING. The closing doxology (16:25-27) floats, appearing after chapter 14 in some manuscripts, after 15 in others, after 16 in others, in two places in one, and not at all in a few; and a handful of witnesses lack 'in Rome' at 1:7 and 1:15. The usual explanation is that a shortened, de-localised edition circulated early for general use. Chapter 1 itself is textually calm apart from two Byzantine expansions the KJV prints: 'the gospel OF CHRIST' at 1:16 and 'fornication' in the vice list at 1:29.

Looking forward

Romans says less about Jesus' life than any comparable New Testament book and more about what his death and resurrection accomplished. He is introduced in the first sentence by a formula that may be older than the letter — descended from David according to the flesh, appointed Son of God in power by resurrection — and thereafter he is the place where God's righteousness is demonstrated (3:21-26), the second Adam undoing the first (5:12-21), the one whose death and rising the believer is baptised into (6:1-11), and the one at God's right hand from whose love nothing can separate (8:34-39). Chapter 1 supplies only the frame: a gospel God had already promised through his prophets, about a son who is both David's descendant and the Lord.

Where the debates are

The MEANING of dikaiosynē theou — God's own righteousness, or a status he confers — which is the hinge of the Reformation and of the 'New Perspective' arguments that reopened it. Whether pistis Christou at 3:22 is faith IN Christ or the faithfulness OF Christ. What chapters 9-11 conclude about Israel, and whether 'all Israel will be saved' means what it appears to. Whether chapter 7's 'I' is Paul, Adam, Israel, or a rhetorical figure. Whether chapter 16 was originally part of this letter or a note to Ephesus. And how much of the letter is timeless theology and how much is aimed at one specific quarrel between Jewish and Gentile believers in one city in one decade. The library reports the positions; it does not vote.
