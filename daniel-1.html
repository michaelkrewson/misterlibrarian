Translator's Notes — verse by verse

Each note explains this translation's choice and compares the seven versions on the shelf. Quotations from copyrighted versions (NIV, TLB, NWT) are kept to short phrases for comparison; KJV, Geneva, Douay-Rheims and ASV are public domain.

Verses 1–2 · וַיְבִיאֵם אֶרֶץ-שִׁנְעָר vayvi'em erets-Shinar

A fourth book opens on this shelf — and it is the strangest one yet: half court-tales (chapters 1–6), half apocalyptic visions (7–12), and written in two languages — Hebrew here, but at 2:4 the book will switch to Aramaic in mid-verse and stay there through chapter 7: the Bible's longest stretch of its second tongue, the diplomatic language of the empires Daniel served. The Hebrew Bible shelves Daniel among the Writings, between Esther and Ezra; the Greek tradition behind the English Bibles moved him to the Prophets, after Ezekiel. And the book carries the sharpest dating debate on this site so far, reported per the house rule: its setting is the sixth-century exile; much modern scholarship dates the finished book to the second century BC (Greek loanwords in chapter 3's orchestra; visions whose detail tracks history precisely down to Antiochus IV and blurs after 164 BC), while the traditional reading holds the sixth-century claim the book itself makes. Both cases have real arguments; this translation renders the text and files the dispute where it belongs — here.

"The third year of Jehoiakim" — and a date you can check. 605 BC: the year Nebuchadnezzar crushed Egypt at Carchemish and swept south — a campaign fixed by the Babylonian Chronicle tablets, which makes this the first verse on this site pinned by a document outside the Bible (the Chronology page's "third clock," engaged at last). Jeremiah 25:1 calls the same year Jehoiakim's fourth — Babylonian counting starts a reign's "year one" at the first New Year, Judean counting at the accession itself; both point at 605. The theology is in the verb: "the Lord GAVE Jehoiakim into his hand" — natan: Babylon did not win, God handed over; and the same verb will structure the chapter (God gave the king, v2; gave kindness, v9; gave knowledge, v17 — every power in this story is on loan). The vessels go "to the land of Shinar" — not the ordinary word for Babylonia but the archaic name of the tower (Genesis 10:10; 11:2; NIV flattens it to "Babylonia," KJV keeps Shinar, as here): the narrator files this empire, from its first verse, under Babel. One more quiet fact: the divine name never appears in this chapter — "the Lord" (Adonai) and "God" (Elohim) only; Jehovah enters Daniel only in the prayer of chapter 9.

Verses 3–7 · וַיָּשֶׂם לָהֶם … שֵׁמוֹת vayasem lahem … shemot

The empire harvests elites. Royal seed, nobles, "youths in whom was no blemish" (KJV "children in whom was no blemish" — the vocabulary of sacrificial flawlessness, applied to boys), handsome, brilliant — and enrolled in a three-year course in "the writing and tongue of the Chaldeans": cuneiform — Akkadian, plus the scholars' dead Sumerian — the hardest scribal curriculum on earth, all tablet-lists, omen-series and mathematics. Babylon absorbed conquered nations by educating their best (and feeding them: the daily pat-bag, "the king's food" — a Persian loanword sitting in the Hebrew, one of this book's tell-tale foreign words; KJV "the king's meat" — meat = food, the archaism flagged since Genesis 1 — ASV, charmingly, "the king's dainties").

The renamings. Each of the four names carries God: Dani-El, "El is my judge"; Chanan-Yah, "Yah has been gracious"; Misha-El, "who is what El is?"; Azar-Yah, "Yah has helped." Each replacement smuggles in a Babylonian god: Belteshazzar (Bel, protect his life!), Abednego — "servant of Nebo," with the god's name apparently garbled to Nego (scribes misspelling idols on purpose is a real and delicious habit), Shadrach and Meshach obscure. Readers of this site have watched renaming since "your name shall be Abraham" (Genesis 17:5) — and this is that thread, inverted: God renames to bless; the empire renames to erase. Watch the narrator's quiet resistance: the book goes right on calling them Daniel, Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah.

Verses 8–14 · וַיָּשֶׂם דָּנִיֵּאל עַל-לִבּוֹ vayasem Daniel al-libbo

"Daniel set on his heart." (KJV "purposed in his heart.") The book's hinge sentence — and its puzzle: why would the food defile (yitga'al)? Kosher law does not ban wine, and vegetables were not the only clean option on a royal menu. The readings: meat from idol-sacrifices; unclean species; or — perhaps likeliest — the politics of the table: to eat the king's portion is to be the king's man, and Daniel draws the line at dependence, not diet. Whatever the reason, watch the manner: no protest, no bid for martyrdom — a polite request, and "God gave Daniel kindness and compassion" — chesed (the covenant word of Genesis 19:19) and rachamim, womb-feeling, granted this time in a foreign court.

Ten days of vegetables — history's first controlled trial. A test group, a control group ("the youths who eat the king's food"), a fixed duration, an observable endpoint: "as you see, deal with your servants." The design is so clean that medical historians regularly cite it as the earliest recorded comparative dietary experiment. The word for the diet, zero'im — seed-food — occurs only here in the Bible, and the KJV gave English one of its best archaisms for it: "give us pulse to eat." And "ten days… that you may be tested" has a long echo: when Smyrna is promised "affliction ten days, that you may be tested" (Revelation 2:10), it is speaking Daniel's dialect — the bounded trial, counted and survivable.

Verses 15–17 · נָתַן לָהֶם הָאֱלֹהִים natan lahem ha-Elohim

The verdict, with a straight face. Ten days on, the resistance diet has out-fattened the imperial table — "their appearance was seen to be better, and fuller in flesh" — the chapter's dry humor, and its point: the outcome was never nutritional. Then natan sounds its third beat: "God gave them knowledge and skill in all writing and wisdom" — the curriculum was Babylon's, the aptitude was God's — "and Daniel had understanding in every vision and dream": half a verse, and it is the job description for the entire book (the dream of the statue, the tree, the writing on the wall, and the visions of chapters 7–12 all hang from this line).

Verses 18–21 · עֶשֶׂר יָדוֹת eser yadot

"Ten hands better." The idiom is kept as the Hebrew has it (the shelf smooths to "ten times better" — KJV NIV alike; the hands stay here): in every matter of wisdom the king finds the four exiles ten hands above "all the magicians and enchanters" — and "magicians" is chartummim, an Egyptian loanword: the very court-magician title of Pharaoh's men in the Joseph story. The narrator is quietly filing Babylon's court under Egypt's — Israel has stood before a foreign king's diviners before, and out-dreamed them. (Douay, following the Greek and Latin, calls the king "Nabuchodonosor" throughout — same man, longer journey through the alphabets.)

One verse, one lifetime. "And Daniel continued until the first year of King Cyrus" — from 605 to 539 BC in a single sentence: the boy deported before the temple fell outlives the empire that took him. Babylon's own fall (chapter 5) and Cyrus's first year are pinned by the Nabonidus Chronicle and the Cyrus Cylinder — the exile's end-date is posted at its beginning, and the whole captivity is bracketed inside one chapter. The open break {פ} closes the overture; the curtain rises next on a dream the king refuses to tell.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation sides with the literal shelf: "youths in whom was no blemish" (the sacrificial vocabulary kept), "set on his heart" (with KJV's "purposed"), and "Shinar" kept archaic with KJV, against the shelf's "Babylonia" glosses.

Where it goes its own way: "ten hands better" kept literal against the whole shelf's "ten times"; "the king's food" for the Persian loanword pat-bag (KJV's "meat" and ASV's "dainties" both saluted); and the book-level apparatus — the mid-verse language switch ahead at 2:4, the canon-placement split, the dating debate — filed openly in the first note.

Echoes paid and planted. Paid: Shinar — the temple vessels carried to the tower's own land (11:2); the renaming thread inverted — the God who renamed to bless (17:5), an empire renaming to erase; ten days of testing, the bounded trial Smyrna's letter will speak again (Rev 2:10); and chesed in a foreign court (19:19). Planted: the Aramaic switch (2:4); the temple vessels, waiting for Belshazzar's feast (ch. 5); "every vision and dream," the whole book's warrant; the chartummim, Pharaoh's word, waiting for Joseph (Genesis 41); and Cyrus, named before his story — the exile's terminus posted at its start.
