Translator's Notes

Verses 1–6 · a state funeral, in Egyptian

Before anything else, a son on his father's face. Three verbs — fell, wept, kissed — and then the machinery of an empire starts up. It is the last time Joseph weeps in this book, and the fifth: turning away (42:24), locked in a room (43:30), heard through the palace wall (45:2), on his father's neck at Goshen (46:29), and now on his father's face.

⚠️ The embalming is genuinely Egyptian, and it is one of only two in the Bible. Jacob here and Joseph in verse 26 — both in Egypt, both at the end of the book, and nowhere else in the Hebrew Bible does anyone get mummified. The numbers fit what is known of the practice: Herodotus reports a seventy-day process, and Egyptian sources describe a natron-drying stage of roughly forty days inside a longer ritual period. Genesis gives forty for the embalming and seventy for the national mourning, which is the same arithmetic seen from the outside. The narrator is not explaining Egypt to Egyptians; he is reporting a foreign custom accurately to readers who do not practise it. KJV "and the Egyptians mourned for him threescore and ten days"; ASV the same.

Why Joseph does not simply ask Pharaoh himself. ⚠️ Verse 4 is odd on its face: the second most powerful man in Egypt asks courtiers to pass a message to the king he sees regularly. The likeliest explanation is ritual — a mourner in Egypt went unshaven and unkempt, and appearing before the king in that state was not done (compare 41:14, where Joseph is shaved before being brought out of the pit-prison to Pharaoh). The man who once had to be groomed to enter the throne room now cannot enter it at all.

"The grave which I dug for myself." Karithi — dug, hewed out. ⚠️ Nothing in Genesis records Jacob digging a grave; the cave at Machpelah was bought by Abraham (23:16) and Jacob's own instruction two chapters ago recited that purchase carefully. The verb can carry the sense of acquiring or preparing a burial place rather than excavating one, and some read it of a grave dug within the existing cave. It may also be Joseph's diplomatic compression for a foreign court — "my own grave" is simply easier to explain to Pharaoh's household than a title deed from Ephron the Hittite. This translation keeps the literal verb and flags the tension.

Verses 7–14 · Egypt buries a shepherd

Look at who goes. All Pharaoh's servants, the elders of his household, all the elders of the land of Egypt, chariots and horsemen. This is a state cortège for a foreign herdsman — the same trade the Egyptians are said to find detestable (46:34) — travelling several hundred miles to bury him in another country. The honour is really being paid to Joseph, and Genesis lets the size of it speak. Kaved me'od, "very heavy," is the same word used of the famine's weight and of the mourning in verse 10; the procession is heavy in the way grief is.

⚠️ And look at what stays behind. "Only their little ones and their flocks and their herds they left in the land of Goshen." The children are hostages in all but name. Egypt is lending an army's worth of chariots to a funeral in Canaan and keeping the family's next generation where it can be found. Nothing in the text says so; the sentence simply sits there, and it is the first faint chill of Exodus 1.

Abel-Mizraim. The Canaanites, watching, name the place from what they see: evel mitzrayim, "mourning of Egypt" — heard as avel mitzrayim, "meadow of Egypt," the two words being identical in the consonants. The joke of it is that they are burying an Israelite and the observers cannot tell; from the roadside it is Egypt that has come to grieve. ⚠️ The location "beyond the Jordan" is a real puzzle, since the natural route from Egypt to Hebron does not cross the Jordan at all. It may reflect the perspective of a writer west of the river, or a longer detour, or a site whose location is simply lost.

Verses 15–21 · the message their father never sent

Seventeen years, and they still think he might kill them. That is the situation of verse 15, and it is worth sitting with. They have lived in Goshen under his protection since chapter 47. He has fed them through five years of famine. And the moment the old man dies they conclude, immediately and unanimously, that the only thing holding him back was their father. The Hebrew is conditional and frightened — lu yistemenu, "what if he hates us" — and the verb for repayment is doubled: pay us back in full.

⚠️ And the message is almost certainly invented. Genesis records no such instruction from Jacob. Two chapters of deathbed speech are given in detail — the adoption, the poem, the burial charge — and none of it contains a word to Joseph about forgiving his brothers. More than that: nothing in the book ever says Jacob learned what they did. It is possible he knew and it went unrecorded; it is possible they are telling the truth about a private conversation. But the plain reading is that ten frightened men have put words in their dead father's mouth, because they do not believe their own case will hold without him. Note also that they send word rather than come — they negotiate at a distance first, and only appear in person in verse 18, after they hear how he answered. Genesis reports the manoeuvre without a syllable of comment, exactly as it reported the pit.

"Lift away, please." Sa na — the ordinary verb for carrying, used of forgiveness: take this weight off us. KJV "forgive the trespass"; ESV "please forgive the transgression." Keeping the physical sense matters here, because what they are asking him to carry is real and the next verse prices it.

"And Joseph wept when they spoke to him." Not at the memory of the crime. At the message. After everything — the reveal, the embrace, the seventeen years — they are still managing him, still bargaining, still calling themselves "the servants of the God of your father" rather than his brothers. The weeping is the whole answer to their fear, and it arrives before he says a word.

"Am I in the place of God?" ⚠️ The same phrase Jacob threw at Rachel in anger when she demanded children (30:2) — am I in the place of God? — meaning there it was a refusal of power he did not have. Here it is a refusal of a role he does have. He is the second man in Egypt; he could do exactly what they fear, that afternoon, lawfully. He declines on the grounds that judgement is not his office. It is also, incidentally, the correct answer to what they have just tried: they wanted a verdict of acquittal, and he tells them he is not the judge.

Verse 20 is the sentence the book has been walking toward — and the grammar matters. "You devised evil against me; God devised it for good." The same verb, chashav, twice — to plan, to reckon, to intend. Notice what the sentence does not say. It does not say the evil was secretly good. It does not say they did not do it — the object of the first verb is evil, flatly, and verse 17 has just used their own words: "the transgression of your brothers and their sin." And it does not say God made them do it. Two agents, one event, opposite intentions, and both intentions real. This is the fully developed form of what Joseph said five chapters earlier in the heat of the reveal — "it was not you who sent me here, but God" — and it is stated here more carefully, with the guilt left in. KJV "ye thought evil against me; but God meant it unto good"; ASV the same; the ESV "you meant evil against me, but God meant it for good"; the NWT "you had evil in mind against me. God had good in mind." Every version keeps both agents; the point is unmissable in the Hebrew and none of them has tried to soften it.

"To keep a numerous people alive." Lehachayot — the same root as the michyah of 45:5 and the "you have kept us alive" of the Egyptians selling themselves at 47:25. Three times now, and each time it means survival bought at a price somebody paid.

"And spoke to their heart." Vaydabber al libbam — an idiom of tenderness, used elsewhere of reassuring the frightened and of courtship. Most versions render the sense rather than the words (KJV "spake kindly unto them"; NIV "reassured them"); this keeps the heart in it. It is the last thing he does for them, and it answers a fear he could simply have dismissed.

Verses 22–26 · a hundred and ten years, and a coffin in Egypt

⚠️ A hundred and ten is not a round number by accident. It is the Egyptian ideal lifespan — the figure that appears repeatedly in Egyptian wisdom literature and funerary inscriptions as the perfect span a well-lived life should reach. Hebrew ideals run differently (Abraham 175, Isaac 180, Jacob 147; Psalm 90 sets the human limit at seventy or eighty). Genesis gives Joseph the Egyptian number. He is buried in the Egyptian manner, he holds an Egyptian title, his sons have an Egyptian mother — and the book closes his life in the vocabulary of the country he served. Whether that is honour or irony is not stated.

Great-grandchildren on his knees. The same gesture of acknowledgement Jacob used on Ephraim and Manasseh (48:12). Joseph, the son who was taken from his father, ends surrounded by four generations — and the last recorded thing he does with his hands is claim children.

"God will surely visit you." Paqod yifqod — the infinitive doubled for certainty, and he says it twice, in verses 24 and 25, in the last words he speaks. Paqad means to attend to, to take notice of, to call to account; it is what a commander does at a muster and what a shepherd does at a count. KJV "God will surely visit you"; ASV the same; the ESV "God will visit you," dropping the doubled infinitive that makes it a certainty. ⚠️ These exact words are picked up at the burning bush and again on the night of the exodus — Moses is told to tell the elders "I have surely visited you" (Exodus 3:16), and when Israel finally leaves, the narrator stops the departure to say that Moses took the bones of Joseph with him, "for he had solemnly sworn the sons of Israel, saying: God will surely visit you" (Exodus 13:19). The oath is honoured four hundred years later, and the bones are finally buried at Shechem, in ground Jacob bought (Joshua 24:32). Joseph does not ask to be taken home now. He asks to be carried out when — and stakes his body on a promise he will not see kept.

The last word of Genesis. Ba-aron be-mitzrayim — "in a coffin, in Egypt." Aron is simply a chest or box; it is the same word that will later name the ark of the covenant, the chest carried through the wilderness. KJV "he was put in a coffin in Egypt"; NWT "he was placed in a coffin in Egypt" — every version keeps the box. So the book that opens "in the beginning God created the heavens and the earth" ends with an embalmed man in a box in a foreign country. ⚠️ And that is deliberately not a resolution. Every promise in the book is outstanding: the land is not held, the nation is seventy people in a border province, and the last image is a container waiting to be picked up. The final Hebrew word is the name of the wrong country. Genesis ends the way it does so that Exodus has to begin.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the words exact / distinct: "devised… devised" for the doubled chashav in verse 20, so the two agents share one verb; "lift away" for nasa in the plea for forgiveness, which is what the word does — carry a burden off someone; "am I in the place of God?" kept identical to Jacob's phrase at 30:2; "spoke to their heart" left as the idiom; "surely visit" for paqod yifqod, the doubled infinitive Exodus quotes back; and "coffin" for aron, with the note that it is the ark's own word.

Echoes paid. The pit answered at last, by the man who was in it; "it was not you who sent me here, but God" restated with the guilt left in; the oath about the burial discharged with an Egyptian army as escort; Abraham's purchase at Machpelah used for the fourth and last time; the knees that claimed Ephraim and Manasseh claiming their grandchildren; and the dream of brothers bowing completed in verse 18 — where Joseph, who had it, refuses to enjoy it.

Echoes planted. "God will surely visit you", quoted verbatim at the bush and on the night Israel leaves; Joseph's bones, carried out by Moses and buried at Shechem four hundred years later; the children left behind in Goshen; and a family of seventy that Exodus will open by calling too numerous to be safe.

📖 That is the end of Genesis. Fifty chapters — the first complete book in this translation. It began with a formless earth and ends with a box in Egypt, and almost nothing promised in it has arrived: the land is a grave-plot, the nation is a household, and the blessing that was supposed to reach all the families of the earth has so far reached one Egyptian province during a famine. What the book does deliver is a method — a God who works through people who lie, cheat, favour the wrong child and sell their brother, without at any point pretending they did not. The last thing anyone says in it is a request to be carried home later.

Next in this translation: the story picks up in Exodus, which opens by repeating Genesis 46's list of names and then says they filled the land — and a new king arises who did not know Joseph. Exodus 1–3 are already translated here; the work continues from chapter 4.
