Translator's Notes

Verses 1–7 · the last stop, and permission to leave

Why he stops at Beersheba. An old man given wagons and told to hurry does not pause to sacrifice unless something is troubling him — and Beersheba is not a random halt. It is the southern edge of the land, the last place in Canaan before the road becomes the road to Egypt; it is where Abraham planted a tamarisk and called on the everlasting God (21:33); and it is where Isaac received his own night visit and built his own altar (26:24-25). Jacob sacrifices there specifically "to the God of his father Isaac" — not "of Abraham and Isaac," the usual pairing. There is a reason for the narrowing, and it is in the next paragraph.

The thing he is afraid of. ⚠️ Every previous descent into Egypt in this book has gone badly or been forbidden outright. Abraham went down because of a famine and immediately lied about his wife (12:10-20). And when a famine came in Isaac's day and Isaac moved to go down, God stopped him flat: "Do not go down to Egypt" (26:2). That is the prohibition Jacob is carrying, spoken to the very father whose God he is now sacrificing to. He is at the border asking whether the family is permitted to cross it. The answer, in verse 3, uses the same words and reverses them: al tira me-redah mitzrayemah — "do not fear to go down to Egypt." This is not a generic reassurance. It is a specific licence, and Genesis has spent thirty-four chapters making it necessary.

"Jacob, Jacob." The doubled name is rare and it is always the same kind of moment: "Abraham, Abraham" at the knife (22:11), and later "Moses, Moses" at the bush and "Samuel, Samuel" in the dark. The answer is always the one word — hineni, "here I am" — which Abraham said on the mountain and which Joseph said when his father sent him to find his brothers (37:13), the errand that ended in the pit. The old man now says it himself, at the border, in the dark.

The two anokhis. Verse 4 is emphatic twice over — "I myself will go down with you… and I myself will surely bring you up." The promise is not that the family will be spared the descent but that God is going into it with them; and a'alkha gam aloh, the doubled "bring up," is the verb the whole book of Exodus will be named by. Israel goes down to Egypt with the return already promised in the same breath. ⚠️ It is fulfilled twice over and neither reading cancels the other: personally, when Jacob's body is carried back to the cave at Machpelah (50:13), and nationally, four hundred years later.

"Joseph will lay his hand on your eyes." The office of closing a dead man's eyes belonged to his nearest son. It is the gentlest sentence God speaks in Genesis, and it answers the fear Jacob has actually been living with since chapter 37 — not that he will die in Egypt, but that he would die without that son in the room. KJV "and Joseph shall put his hand upon thine eyes."

Verses 8–27 · seventy souls, and what a list is for

Why the list is here at all. A modern reader skims it; ancient readers did not. This is the roster of the nation at its smallest — the moment before the multiplication — and it exists so that Exodus 1 can open by repeating its first words ("these are the names of the sons of Israel who came to Egypt") and then say that they filled the land. The list is the "before" photograph. It is also, quietly, the ledger of everything that has happened in this book: the deaths, the irregular marriages, the one daughter anyone bothered to name.

The things it refuses to leave out. Verse 12 records Judah's sons and then adds, flatly, "and Er and Onan died in the land of Canaan" — chapter 38 acknowledged in a subordinate clause, in a document meant to honour the family. Verse 10 names "Shaul the son of the Canaanite woman," marking one of Simeon's sons as born of exactly the marriage the patriarchs kept sending servants a thousand miles to avoid. Verse 15 counts Dinah among Leah's children, so that chapter 34 is not quietly dropped. And verse 17 names Serah, the sister — a granddaughter listed by name for no stated reason, one of the very few women in the roster, which is precisely why later Jewish tradition grew a whole legend around her. Genesis does not explain her. It just declines to lose her.

⚠️ The count does not add up cleanly, and pretending otherwise would be dishonest. Verse 15 says Leah's line is thirty-three, but the names given total thirty-two (thirty-three only if you count Jacob himself, or Dinah, or read Er and Onan differently). Verses 26 and 27 give sixty-six who came, then seventy for the whole house — which works if you add Joseph, his two sons, and Jacob (66 + 3 + 1 = 70), but the arithmetic depends on which people the "sixty-six" is already excluding. Ancient readers noticed too: the Septuagint reads seventy-five, adding grandsons of Joseph, and that is the number Stephen quotes in Acts 7:14 — so the New Testament and the Hebrew text disagree on the total because they are quoting different editions. A Dead Sea Scrolls fragment of Exodus supports the seventy-five reading. This translation follows the Masoretic seventy, as it does throughout, and notes the variant rather than harmonising it. Seventy is in any case doing more than counting: it is the number of the nations in chapter 10, and Israel goes down into Egypt as one small world.

"Souls." Nefesh, kept literal here as it is everywhere in this translation. The count is not of "persons" in the abstract but of living breathing throats — the same word used of the animals on the fifth day and of Judah's father whose nefesh is bound to the boy's. KJV and ASV both keep "souls"; the NIV and ESV render "persons," which is accurate to the sense and loses the thread.

Verses 28–30 · Judah goes first, and an old man is finished

Judah is sent ahead. Of eleven available sons, this is the one entrusted to go find Joseph and point the road. Nothing is said about it; the narrative simply hands him the job. It is the same man who proposed the sale (37:26), stood surety for Benjamin (43:9), and offered to stay a slave in his place (44:33). Genesis promotes Judah by giving him errands, not speeches — and it will end by giving him the sceptre.

Joseph harnesses his own chariot. The second man in Egypt, who has people for this, does it himself and drives out to meet an old shepherd. And then a striking asymmetry: "he fell on his neck and wept on his neck a long while." The weeping is all Joseph's — Jacob is not said to weep at all, here or anywhere in the reunion. ⚠️ A famous rabbinic reading fills the silence by saying Jacob was reciting the Shema; that is commentary, not text. What the text gives is one man sobbing on another man's neck and the other man saying nothing until verse 30. The KJV has "wept on his neck a good while," which catches the duration; the Hebrew's final word is simply od, "still, yet, more" — he kept on.

"Let me die this time." Amutah ha-pa'am — awkward in English and worth keeping awkward. Not "now I can die happy"; something closer to this time I can die, as against all the times he said he would go down to Sheol mourning (37:35, 42:38, 44:31). He is not asking to die. He is saying the thing that made death unbearable has been removed. KJV "Now let me die, since I have seen thy face"; the NWT "Now I am ready to die," which is the sense, at the cost of the strangeness.

Verses 31–34 · the coaching, and the usefulness of being despised

Joseph writes their script. He tells them exactly what to say and exactly which word to lead with: not "shepherds" but "men of livestock" — anshei mikneh, a phrase with more standing to it, the language of owners rather than hired hands — while he himself uses the blunter "shepherds of flocks" when briefing them. He is a court operator managing an audience, and Genesis lets you watch him do it.

The strategy is the opposite of assimilation. Read the logic of verse 34 slowly: say you are herdsmen in order that you may live in Goshen, because every shepherd is an abomination to Egypt. He is deliberately leading with the fact that will disgust his hosts, so that they will be housed at arm's length on the frontier instead of absorbed into the centre of Egyptian life. The thing that makes his family despised is the thing that will keep them a family. ⚠️ Whether that separation is a mercy or the seedbed of Exodus 1's "there arose a new king who did not know Joseph" is a question this chapter opens and does not answer.

Was Egypt really revolted by shepherds? ⚠️ Honest answer: not in any blanket way we can document. Egyptians kept enormous herds themselves, and tomb art shows herdsmen everywhere. Proposals for what lies behind the line include class contempt for a low-status trade, an ethnic distaste for Asiatic pastoralists drifting in from the east, and — most often repeated, least supported — a memory of the Hyksos. No Egyptian text says it. The narrator states it as a plain fact about the country his readers are being told about, and it is doing narrative work regardless of what stands behind it. KJV "for every shepherd is an abomination unto the Egyptians"; the NWT "detestable thing," which is what to'evah weighs.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the words exact / distinct: "Do not fear to go down to Egypt" kept in the same words as the prohibition to Isaac at 26:2, so the licence is visibly a reversal; "I myself… and I myself" for the doubled anokhi; "surely bring you up" for the doubled aloh, the Exodus verb; "souls" for nefesh throughout the census; "Let me die this time" for amutah ha-pa'am, left as strange as it is; and "abomination" for to'evah, unsoftened.

Echoes paid. Hineni — Abraham's word at the binding and Joseph's at 37:13 — now Jacob's; the prohibition to Isaac lifted at the same border; the wagons that persuaded him, now carrying him; Er and Onan and Dinah both refusing to disappear from the family record; and "let me go and see him before I die" answered in verse 30.

Echoes planted. "These are the names of the sons of Israel who came to Egypt" — the sentence Exodus opens by repeating, with the nation grown; "I will surely bring you up," the promise the whole exodus discharges; Goshen chosen precisely because it is the far edge; and the separation strategy of verse 34, which keeps them a people and eventually marks them as a target.

Next installment in this book: Genesis 47 — five brothers stand in front of the most powerful man on earth and say they are shepherds, and Jacob, asked his age, answers that his years have been "few and evil." Then the famine grinds on, and Joseph buys Egypt — its silver, its livestock, its land, and finally its people — for Pharaoh.
