The Passover instituted and the tenth blow struck at midnight; Pharaoh surrenders everything and asks for a blessing; Israel leaves Raamses for Succoth with a mixed multitude, having stripped Egypt — and the chapter ends with one law for the native and the sojourner alike.
c. 1446 BC on the early-date chronology this project foregrounds — the fourteenth of Abib, the month the ripening barley of 9:31 had been counting toward. The text dates it three times as 'this very day' (12:17, 41, 51).

Exodus 12 — The Mister Translation

1
וַיֹּאמֶר יְהוָה אֶל-מֹשֶׁה וְאֶל-אַהֲרֹן, בְּאֶרֶץ מִצְרַיִם לֵאמֹר.
And Jehovah said to Moses and to Aaron in the land of Egypt:note
Y Jehová dijo a Moisés y a Aarón en la tierra de Egipto:
2
הַחֹדֶשׁ הַזֶּה לָכֶם, רֹאשׁ חֳדָשִׁים: רִאשׁוֹן הוּא לָכֶם, לְחָדְשֵׁי הַשָּׁנָה.
“This month shall be for you the head of months; it shall be the first for you of the months of the year.note
«Este mes os será principio de los meses; será para vosotros el primero de los meses del año.
cross-refs⤷ 9:31
3
דַּבְּרוּ, אֶל-כָּל-עֲדַת יִשְׂרָאֵל לֵאמֹר, בֶּעָשֹׂר, לַחֹדֶשׁ הַזֶּה: וְיִקְחוּ לָהֶם, אִישׁ שֶׂה לְבֵית-אָבֹת--שֶׂה לַבָּיִת.
Speak to all the assembly of Israel, saying: On the tenth of this month let them take for themselves, each man, a lamb for a fathers’ house, a lamb for a household.note
Hablad a toda la asamblea de Israel, diciendo: El día diez de este mes tome cada uno un cordero por casa paterna, un cordero por familia.
4
וְאִם-יִמְעַט הַבַּיִת, מִהְיוֹת מִשֶּׂה--וְלָקַח הוּא וּשְׁכֵנוֹ הַקָּרֹב אֶל-בֵּיתוֹ, בְּמִכְסַת נְפָשֹׁת: אִישׁ לְפִי אָכְלוֹ, תָּכֹסּוּ עַל-הַשֶּׂה.
And if the household is too small for a lamb, then he and his neighbor nearest to his house shall take one, according to the number of persons; each according to what he eats, you shall count for the lamb.note
Y si la familia es demasiado pequeña para un cordero, que lo tomen él y su vecino más cercano a su casa, según el número de las personas; cada uno conforme a lo que coma, contaréis para el cordero.
5
שֶׂה תָמִים זָכָר בֶּן-שָׁנָה, יִהְיֶה לָכֶם; מִן-הַכְּבָשִׂים וּמִן-הָעִזִּים, תִּקָּחוּ.
A lamb without blemish, a male, a year old, shall be yours; from the sheep or from the goats you may take it.note
El cordero será sin defecto, macho, de un año; de las ovejas o de las cabras lo tomaréis.
6
וְהָיָה לָכֶם לְמִשְׁמֶרֶת, עַד אַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר יוֹם לַחֹדֶשׁ הַזֶּה; וְשָׁחֲטוּ אֹתוֹ, כֹּל קְהַל עֲדַת-יִשְׂרָאֵל--בֵּין הָעַרְבָּיִם.
And it shall be kept by you until the fourteenth day of this month; and the whole congregation of the assembly of Israel shall slaughter it between the two evenings.note
Y lo guardaréis hasta el día catorce de este mes; y toda la congregación de la asamblea de Israel lo degollará entre las dos tardes.
7
וְלָקְחוּ, מִן-הַדָּם, וְנָתְנוּ עַל-שְׁתֵּי הַמְּזוּזֹת, וְעַל-הַמַּשְׁקוֹף--עַל, הַבָּתִּים, אֲשֶׁר-יֹאכְלוּ אֹתוֹ, בָּהֶם.
And they shall take some of the blood and put it on the two doorposts and on the lintel, on the houses in which they eat it.note
Y tomarán de la sangre y la pondrán en los dos postes y en el dintel, en las casas en que lo coman.
8
וְאָכְלוּ אֶת-הַבָּשָׂר, בַּלַּיְלָה הַזֶּה: צְלִי-אֵשׁ וּמַצּוֹת, עַל-מְרֹרִים יֹאכְלֻהוּ.
And they shall eat the flesh that night, roasted with fire; with unleavened bread and bitter herbs they shall eat it.note
Y comerán la carne aquella noche, asada al fuego; con panes sin levadura y hierbas amargas la comerán.
9
אַל-תֹּאכְלוּ מִמֶּנּוּ נָא, וּבָשֵׁל מְבֻשָּׁל בַּמָּיִם: כִּי אִם-צְלִי-אֵשׁ, רֹאשׁוֹ עַל-כְּרָעָיו וְעַל-קִרְבּוֹ.
Do not eat any of it raw, or boiled at all in water, but roasted with fire — its head with its legs and with its inner parts.note
No comáis de ella cruda, ni cocida en agua, sino asada al fuego — su cabeza con sus patas y sus entrañas.
10
וְלֹא-תוֹתִירוּ מִמֶּנּוּ, עַד-בֹּקֶר; וְהַנֹּתָר מִמֶּנּוּ עַד-בֹּקֶר, בָּאֵשׁ תִּשְׂרֹפוּ.
And you shall leave none of it until morning; and what remains of it until morning you shall burn with fire.note
Y no dejaréis nada de ella hasta la mañana; y lo que quede hasta la mañana lo quemaréis en el fuego.
11
וְכָכָה, תֹּאכְלוּ אֹתוֹ--מָתְנֵיכֶם חֲגֻרִים, נַעֲלֵיכֶם בְּרַגְלֵיכֶם וּמַקֶּלְכֶם בְּיֶדְכֶם; וַאֲכַלְתֶּם אֹתוֹ בְּחִפָּזוֹן, פֶּסַח הוּא לַיהוָה.
And this is how you shall eat it: your loins girded, your sandals on your feet, and your staff in your hand; and you shall eat it in haste. It is a Passover to Jehovah.note
Y así la comeréis: ceñidos vuestros lomos, vuestras sandalias en los pies y vuestro bastón en la mano; y la comeréis de prisa. Es la Pascua de Jehová.
12
וְעָבַרְתִּי בְאֶרֶץ-מִצְרַיִם, בַּלַּיְלָה הַזֶּה, וְהִכֵּיתִי כָל-בְּכוֹר בְּאֶרֶץ מִצְרַיִם, מֵאָדָם וְעַד-בְּהֵמָה; וּבְכָל-אֱלֹהֵי מִצְרַיִם אֶעֱשֶׂה שְׁפָטִים, אֲנִי יְהוָה.
And I will pass through the land of Egypt that night and strike every firstborn in the land of Egypt, from man to beast; and against all the gods of Egypt I will execute judgments. I am Jehovah.note
Y yo pasaré aquella noche por la tierra de Egipto y heriré a todo primogénito en la tierra de Egipto, desde el hombre hasta la bestia; y contra todos los dioses de Egipto ejecutaré juicios. Yo soy Jehová.

13
וְהָיָה הַדָּם לָכֶם לְאֹת, עַל הַבָּתִּים אֲשֶׁר אַתֶּם שָׁם, וְרָאִיתִי אֶת-הַדָּם, וּפָסַחְתִּי עֲלֵכֶם; וְלֹא-יִהְיֶה בָכֶם נֶגֶף לְמַשְׁחִית, בְּהַכֹּתִי בְּאֶרֶץ מִצְרָיִם.
And the blood shall be a sign for you on the houses where you are; and I will see the blood and pass over you, and there shall be no plague upon you to destroy, when I strike in the land of Egypt.note
Y la sangre os será por señal en las casas donde estéis; y veré la sangre y pasaré de largo sobre vosotros, y no habrá entre vosotros plaga de destrucción cuando yo hiera la tierra de Egipto.
14
וְהָיָה הַיּוֹם הַזֶּה לָכֶם לְזִכָּרוֹן, וְחַגֹּתֶם אֹתוֹ חַג לַיהוָה: לְדֹרֹתֵיכֶם, חֻקַּת עוֹלָם תְּחָגֻּהוּ.
And this day shall be for you a memorial, and you shall keep it as a festival to Jehovah; throughout your generations, as an everlasting statute, you shall keep it.note
Y este día os será memorial, y lo celebraréis como fiesta a Jehová; por vuestras generaciones, como estatuto perpetuo, lo celebraréis.
15

Seven days you shall eat unleavened bread; on the very first day you shall remove leaven from your houses, for whoever eats what is leavened, that soul shall be cut off from Israel, from the first day until the seventh day.note
Siete días comeréis panes sin levadura; ya el primer día quitaréis la levadura de vuestras casas, porque cualquiera que coma algo leudado, esa alma será cortada de Israel, desde el primer día hasta el séptimo.
16
וּבַיּוֹם הָרִאשׁוֹן, מִקְרָא-קֹדֶשׁ, וּבַיּוֹם הַשְּׁבִיעִי, מִקְרָא-קֹדֶשׁ יִהְיֶה לָכֶם: כָּל-מְלָאכָה, לֹא-יֵעָשֶׂה בָהֶם--אַךְ אֲשֶׁר יֵאָכֵל לְכָל-נֶפֶשׁ, הוּא לְבַדּוֹ יֵעָשֶׂה לָכֶם.
And on the first day there shall be a holy convocation, and on the seventh day a holy convocation for you; no work at all shall be done on them, except what must be eaten by every soul — that alone may be prepared by you.note
Y el primer día habrá convocación santa, y el séptimo día convocación santa tendréis; ninguna obra se hará en ellos, salvo lo que haya de comer cada alma; solo eso podréis preparar.
17
וּשְׁמַרְתֶּם, אֶת-הַמַּצּוֹת, כִּי בְּעֶצֶם הַיּוֹם הַזֶּה, הוֹצֵאתִי אֶת-צִבְאוֹתֵיכֶם מֵאֶרֶץ מִצְרָיִם; וּשְׁמַרְתֶּם אֶת-הַיּוֹם הַזֶּה, לְדֹרֹתֵיכֶם--חֻקַּת עוֹלָם.
And you shall keep the unleavened bread, for on this very day I brought your armies out of the land of Egypt; and you shall keep this day throughout your generations, as an everlasting statute.note
Y guardaréis los panes sin levadura, porque en este mismo día saqué vuestros ejércitos de la tierra de Egipto; y guardaréis este día por vuestras generaciones, como estatuto perpetuo.
18
בָּרִאשֹׁן בְּאַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר יוֹם לַחֹדֶשׁ, בָּעֶרֶב, תֹּאכְלוּ, מַצֹּת: עַד יוֹם הָאֶחָד וְעֶשְׂרִים, לַחֹדֶשׁ--בָּעָרֶב.
In the first month, on the fourteenth day of the month, in the evening, you shall eat unleavened bread, until the twenty-first day of the month, in the evening.note
En el mes primero, el día catorce del mes, por la tarde, comeréis panes sin levadura, hasta el día veintiuno del mes, por la tarde.
19
שִׁבְעַת יָמִים--שְׂאֹר, לֹא יִמָּצֵא בְּבָתֵּיכֶם: כִּי כָּל-אֹכֵל מַחְמֶצֶת, וְנִכְרְתָה הַנֶּפֶשׁ הַהִוא מֵעֲדַת יִשְׂרָאֵל--בַּגֵּר, וּבְאֶזְרַח הָאָרֶץ.
Seven days no leaven shall be found in your houses, for whoever eats what is leavened, that soul shall be cut off from the assembly of Israel — whether a sojourner or a native of the land.note
Siete días no se hallará levadura en vuestras casas, porque cualquiera que coma algo leudado, esa alma será cortada de la asamblea de Israel — sea extranjero o natural de la tierra.
20

You shall eat nothing leavened; in all your dwellings you shall eat unleavened bread.”note
No comeréis nada leudado; en todas vuestras moradas comeréis panes sin levadura».
21

Y Moisés llamó a todos los ancianos de Israel y les dijo: «Sacad y tomaos corderos según vuestras familias, y degollad la pascua.
22
וּלְקַחְתֶּם אֲגֻדַּת אֵזוֹב, וּטְבַלְתֶּם בַּדָּם אֲשֶׁר-בַּסַּף, וְהִגַּעְתֶּם אֶל-הַמַּשְׁקוֹף וְאֶל-שְׁתֵּי הַמְּזוּזֹת, מִן-הַדָּם אֲשֶׁר בַּסָּף; וְאַתֶּם, לֹא תֵצְאוּ אִישׁ מִפֶּתַח-בֵּיתוֹ--עַד-בֹּקֶר.
And you shall take a bunch of hyssop and dip it in the blood that is in the basin, and touch the lintel and the two doorposts with the blood that is in the basin; and none of you shall go out from the door of his house until morning.note
Y tomaréis un manojo de hisopo y lo mojaréis en la sangre que está en la vasija, y tocaréis el dintel y los dos postes con la sangre que está en la vasija; y ninguno de vosotros saldrá de la puerta de su casa hasta la mañana.
23

Porque Jehová pasará hiriendo a Egipto, y verá la sangre en el dintel y en los dos postes, y Jehová pasará de largo por aquella puerta y no dejará que el destructor entre en vuestras casas a herir.
24
וּשְׁמַרְתֶּם, אֶת-הַדָּבָר הַזֶּה, לְחָק-לְךָ וּלְבָנֶיךָ, עַד-עוֹלָם.
And you shall keep this thing as a decree for you and for your sons forever.note
Y guardaréis esto como estatuto para vosotros y para vuestros hijos, para siempre.
25
וְהָיָה כִּי-תָבֹאוּ אֶל-הָאָרֶץ, אֲשֶׁר יִתֵּן יְהוָה לָכֶם--כַּאֲשֶׁר דִּבֵּר; וּשְׁמַרְתֶּם, אֶת-הָעֲבֹדָה הַזֹּאת.
And when you come into the land that Jehovah will give you, just as he has spoken, you shall keep this service.note
Y cuando entréis en la tierra que Jehová os dará, tal como ha dicho, guardaréis este servicio.
26
וְהָיָה, כִּי-יֹאמְרוּ אֲלֵיכֶם בְּנֵיכֶם: מָה הָעֲבֹדָה הַזֹּאת, לָכֶם.

Y cuando vuestros hijos os digan: ‘¿Qué significa para vosotros este servicio?’,

28
וַיֵּלְכוּ וַיַּעֲשׂוּ, בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל: כַּאֲשֶׁר צִוָּה יְהוָה אֶת-מֹשֶׁה וְאַהֲרֹן, כֵּן עָשׂוּ. {ס}

Y los hijos de Israel fueron e hicieron tal como Jehová había mandado a Moisés y a Aarón; así lo hicieron.

Translator’s Notes

Verses 1–13 · a calendar, a lamb, and a door

⚠ After nine plagues and ten chapters of argument, the answer is not another plague. It is a set of instructions, and the first of them resets TIME: “this month shall be for you the HEAD OF MONTHS.” Israel’s year is re-founded on the night it was rescued — the calendar itself becomes a witness. (The civil year continued to begin in the autumn, which is why Judaism keeps two new years to this day.) The month is the one 9:31 was quietly counting toward, Abib, the month of the ripening ear.

The lamb is chosen on the tenth and kept until the fourteenth — four days in the house before it is killed. It is a male without blemish, and it may be a sheep or a goat (the Hebrew seh covers both; “lamb” in English quietly narrows it). ⚠ And note verse 4, which is easy to read past: a household too small for a whole lamb shares with the nearest neighbor, counted “according to what each eats.” Nobody is left out for being too few, and nothing is wasted. The ordinance is built for poor and small families first.

“Between the two evenings” (v6) is an old ambiguity: between sunset and full dark, or from the sun’s decline to sunset (the later, mid-afternoon reckoning, which is how the temple came to do it). KJV keeps the Hebrew idiom, “in the evening” with a margin note; the ESV reads “at twilight”; the NWT “between the two evenings.” And it is slaughtered by “the whole congregation” — thousands of households acting at one hour, which is itself the making of a nation.

⚠ The blood goes on the OUTSIDE of the door — the two doorposts and the lintel — where the family eating inside cannot see it. Whatever else Passover is, it begins as a night of trusting a mark you are not looking at. The sign is not for them; verse 13 says so plainly: “when I SEE the blood.”

Eaten in haste, loins girded and sandals on and staff in hand — a meal eaten standing, dressed for the road, by people who are still slaves at the moment they eat it. Roasted, not boiled; nothing left till morning.

⚠ «It is a PASSOVER to Jehovah.» The word pesach is famously uncertain. Three readings, all old: (1) to PASS OVER, skip, spare — the traditional rendering, and the Greek Old Testament’s; (2) to PROTECT, hover over defensively — supported by Isaiah 31:5, where Jehovah “protects and delivers, PASSES OVER and rescues” Jerusalem like a bird over its nest; (3) to LIMP or leap, the verb’s ordinary sense elsewhere (1 Kings 18:21, 26). The difference matters: reading (1) has God stepping past a house; reading (2) has him standing over its door. This translation keeps the traditional “pass over” and flags the alternative rather than deciding it.

And verse 12 finally states outright what the plagues have been doing: “against all the gods of Egypt I will execute judgments.” The whole cycle has been a lawsuit against a pantheon, and the last defendant is the god-king’s own house.

Verses 14–20 · seven days without leaven

The instructions jump forward before the night has even happened. Verses 14-20 legislate an annual festival — the Feast of Unleavened Bread — for a people who are not out of Egypt yet. The text is deliberately arranged this way: the rescue and the remembering of the rescue are given together, as one thing.

Why unleavened? Verse 39 gives the historical reason — there was no time to let dough rise. But the command here precedes the haste, and goes further than an absence: leaven must be actively REMOVED from the houses, and for seven days none may be found. ⚠ That is the origin of the household search still carried out before Passover, and of leaven’s standing New Testament use as an image of what spreads invisibly through a whole batch — “a little leaven leavens the whole lump” (1 Corinthians 5:6-8, where Paul goes straight on to call Christ “our Passover”).

«That soul shall be CUT OFF from Israel.» The penalty (karet) is severe and its exact force is debated in the tradition itself — expulsion from the community, childlessness, or a divine rather than judicial punishment. What is clear is the logic: a member of a redeemed people who will not keep the sign of the redemption removes himself from it. KJV “that soul shall be cut off from Israel”; the NIV “must be cut off from Israel.”

⚠ And already, in verse 19, the door is open: the rule binds “the SOJOURNER and the native of the land” alike. The founding festival of Israel is legislated from the first day with the resident foreigner inside its frame — a thread this chapter picks up again at its very end (vv48-49). KJV “whether he be a stranger, or born in the land”; the ESV “whether he is a sojourner or a native.”

Verses 21–28 · hyssop, the destroyer, and a child’s question

Moses passes the instructions to the elders, and adds the practical detail the divine speech did not: a bunch of hyssop as the brush, the blood held in a basin — though the word saf can equally mean the THRESHOLD, so that some have read the blood as caught at the doorway itself rather than in a bowl (KJV “the bason”; the NWT the same, with the alternative noted) — and an order that sounds small and is not — nobody goes outside until morning. The households are shut in with the mark on the door and the night outside. Hyssop reappears wherever blood or water is applied for cleansing (Leviticus 14; Numbers 19; Psalm 51:7, “purge me with hyssop”), and at the cross a sponge is lifted on a hyssop stalk (John 19:29).

⚠ «He will not allow THE DESTROYER to come into your houses.» Verse 23 introduces a destroyer (ha-mashchit) — and the chapter does not harmonize this with verse 12, where Jehovah says “I will pass through… I will strike.” Both are left standing: God himself strikes, and a destroyer is restrained at the door. Ancient and modern readers have taken it as a distinct agent (compare the destroying angel of 2 Samuel 24:16), as a personification of the plague, or as a way of speaking about God’s own act at one remove. The library reports the double statement and does not resolve it. KJV and the ESV both read “the destroyer”; the NWT renders it “the ruination.”

⚠ «And when your sons say to you, what does this service mean to you?» There it is again — and this is the second time in three chapters. At 10:2 the plagues were said to exist so that a father could RECOUNT them to a son and a grandson; here the rite is built around the child’s question, with the answer scripted into the law itself. This verse is the direct ancestor of the Four Questions of the Passover seder, and the reason that meal is conducted as an interrogation rather than a lecture. The instruction to explain is older than the event being explained.

“And the people bowed low and prostrated themselves” — the first act of worship in the book by the whole people, and it comes before anything has been delivered. Then, quietly: “so they did.”

Verses 29–36 · midnight, and a king asking for a blessing

It happens exactly as announced, at the hour named in 11:4 — and the roll of the dead repeats the merism of 11:5 with one change: there the bottom of the range was the slave girl at the millstones, here it is “the firstborn of the CAPTIVE who was in the dungeon.” Even the prisoners of Egypt are counted as Egypt.

⚠ And there is a tomb. On the early-date chronology this translation follows, the pharaoh of the exodus is Amenhotep II — who, Scripture being careful that the ARMY drowned and not the king (14:28), was buried in the Valley of the Kings. Buried with him in KV35 is a son, WEBENSENU, whose organs were found in canopic jars inscribed with his name: a son who demonstrably died before his father did. It is suggestive rather than probative — nothing establishes that he was the crown prince or how he died — and Joel Kramer, who makes the case, is careful to say he does not want to make too much of it, “but I also don’t want to make too little of it either.” The encyclopedia entry carries his film on the tomb.

«There was no house where there was not someone dead.» The narration is flat and short; the book spends no words on the scene. And the sound that fills it is the great OUTCRY promised in 11:6 — the same word this book opened with when Israel cried out under the bondage (2:23).

⚠ Pharaoh’s surrender is total, and its last three words are the most surprising in the chapter. He summons them at night, concedes everything he has spent five chapters withholding — the people, the children, the flocks and the herds — and then says: “AND BLESS ME ALSO.” The man who opened this contest with “Who is Jehovah, that I should obey his voice? I do not know Jehovah” (5:2) ends it asking the God he did not know for a blessing.

“We are all dying!” The Egyptians press Israel out — the same verb-root that has described Pharaoh’s hardened HOLDING ON now describes Egypt pressing them to GO. The expulsion promised in 11:1 arrives.

⚠ And the asking is fulfilled (vv35-36): they ask, the Egyptians give, “and they STRIPPED Egypt.” The verb (natzal) is the plunder-word, and it is the same root used all through the book for God DELIVERING his people — the rescue and the spoil named by one verb. KJV “they spoiled the Egyptians”; the ESV and NIV “plundered.” What Abraham was promised in Genesis 15:14 is now in their hands, and what it will become is a golden calf and a tabernacle.

Verses 37–42 · the numbers, the mixed multitude, and a night of watching

⚠ «About six hundred thousand on foot — the men — besides the little ones.» Read plainly this implies a nation of roughly two million people, and it is one of the most discussed figures in the Bible, because the logistics in the Sinai are formidable and the same books elsewhere describe Israel as small (Deuteronomy 7:7). The readings, each with a real pedigree: (1) the plain sense — the figure is what it says, and the wilderness provision is precisely the point the narrative keeps making; (2) the elef reading — the Hebrew word for “thousand” (elef) also means a CLAN or a military unit, so “600 elef” could be 600 fighting units of unstated size; the same ambiguity turns up in Judges and 1 Samuel; (3) a later census figure carried back into the exodus account. The library lays them out and takes no vote — but the elef reading is a real feature of the language, not a modern escape hatch.

⚠ «And a great MIXED MULTITUDE also went up with them.» This is one of the quietest and most important sentences in Exodus. Erev rav means a mingled crowd — non-Israelites who walked out of Egypt with Israel. Who were they? The book has already told us: at the hail, “the one who FEARED the word of Jehovah among the servants of Pharaoh” brought his household indoors (9:20). Egypt was never a solid block; some of it believed, and some of it left. KJV “a mixed multitude”; the ESV “a mixed multitude”; the NIV flattens it to “many other people,” losing the mingling the Hebrew names. Israel, from its first hour as a free people, is not ethnically pure — which is exactly why the chapter ends by legislating for the foreigner who wants in.

⚠ «Four hundred and thirty years.» Another old crux, because Genesis 15:13 told Abraham four hundred, and Paul, at Galatians 3:17, counts the 430 from the PROMISE to the law — which would place the patriarchs inside the span. The Greek Old Testament and the Samaritan Pentateuch resolve it in the text itself, reading “in Egypt AND IN CANAAN”; the Hebrew here says simply “in Egypt.” Both totals are standing witnesses and both are old; this translation follows the Hebrew and notes the variant rather than importing it. KJV “the sojourning of the children of Israel, who dwelt in Egypt, was four hundred and thirty years”; the NWT reads the same and footnotes the Greek and Samaritan expansion.

“On that very day” — twice (vv41, 51), and once more at v17. The exodus is dated as precisely as a legal instrument.

⚠ «A NIGHT OF WATCHING for Jehovah.» The Hebrew (leil shimmurim) works in both directions and the verse says both: it is a night Jehovah KEPT WATCH to bring them out, and a night to BE KEPT by all Israel through their generations. God’s vigil becomes the people’s vigil — and Jewish tradition has kept it as a night of staying up ever since. KJV “a night to be much observed”; the ESV “a night of watching.”

Verses 43–51 · one law

The chapter closes by drawing the boundary of the meal — and the surprise is where it falls. Not around a bloodline. The foreigner’s son may not eat; but a slave bought with money MAY, once circumcised, and so may any sojourner who takes the covenant sign on himself and his household. ⚠ The line is drawn around the COVENANT, not around ancestry — which is why the mixed multitude of verse 38 is not a problem the text has to explain away. It was legislated for on the same night.

«ONE LAW shall there be for the native and for the sojourner who sojourns among you.» This is stated at the founding moment of the nation, before Sinai, before any of the rest of the legislation — and it is repeated through the Torah (Leviticus 24:22; Numbers 15:15-16). Whatever else Israel is being made into, it is not being made into a closed people.

⚠ «You shall not break a bone of it.» A rule about butchering — the lamb is kept whole, eaten in one house, nothing carried out. It becomes one of the New Testament’s most exact citations: at the crucifixion the soldiers break the legs of the two men beside Jesus and find him already dead, and John writes, “these things happened so that the Scripture might be fulfilled: NOT A BONE OF HIM SHALL BE BROKEN” (19:33-36), quoting this verse (with Psalm 34:20 behind it). KJV “neither shall ye break a bone thereof”; the ESV “you shall not break any of its bones.” Paul makes the identification directly: “Christ our PASSOVER has been sacrificed” (1 Corinthians 5:7).

And the book’s longest chapter ends with a sentence it has been trying to write since chapter 1: “on that very day Jehovah brought the sons of Israel out from the land of Egypt by their armies.” Not “he did not send them” — which is how every chapter since the fifth has ended — but OUT.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the exact / distinct words: “Passover” for pesach with its three old senses flagged rather than decided; “a lamb” for seh, noting the Hebrew covers a kid as well; “between the two evenings” left as the idiom it is; “the destroyer” kept alongside “I will strike,” unharmonized; “a great mixed multitude” for erev rav, not softened to “many other people”; “six hundred thousand” printed as the Hebrew reads, with the elef ambiguity noted; and “a night of watching” for leil shimmurim, which the verse itself uses in both directions.

Echoes paid. The sentence of 4:22-23, executed at midnight; the outcry of 2:23, now Egypt’s (11:6; 12:30); the silver and gold promised to Abraham (Genesis 15:14), asked at 11:2 and handed over here; the Egyptians who feared the word at the hail (9:20), walking out in the mixed multitude; the telling to a child commanded at 10:2, now built into the rite; the month the ripening barley of 9:31 was counting toward; and Pharaoh’s “I do not know Jehovah” (5:2), ending in “bless me also.”

Echoes planted. The lamb without blemish and the unbroken bone, which John will quote at a crucifixion (19:36) and Paul will name outright (1 Corinthians 5:7); the unleavened bread of a Passover table (Luke 22:19); the leaven that spreads through a whole lump, for good and ill; the firstborn, who from this night belong to Jehovah (13:2); and “one law for the native and the sojourner,” stated before Sinai and repeated through the Torah.
