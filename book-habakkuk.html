📖 Habakkuk

חֲבַקּוּק Chavaqquq — 'Embrace' or, more forcefully, 'wrestle' — most likely from chabaq, 'to embrace, clasp, grapple.' ⚠ The name fits the book to perfection: Habakkuk is the man who takes hold of God and will not let go until he gets an answer. Luther called him 'the embracer'; 'the wrestler' catches what he actually does. (An older guess links it to an Akkadian garden-plant name, hambaququ, but the 'embrace' reading is the one that means something.)
Ἀμβακούμ (Ambakoum) — The Septuagint keeps Habakkuk eighth among the Twelve, and spells the name Ambakoum. ⚠ It is under that Greek name that Habakkuk appears in the apocryphal story 'Bel and the Dragon' (an addition to Daniel), carried by an angel by the hair of his head to bring a meal to Daniel in the lions' den in Babylon — a legend, but a sign of how firmly tradition tied Habakkuk to the age of the Chaldeans. The Vulgate and Douay call the book Habacuc.

The prophet who argues back. Alone among the Twelve, Habakkuk's book is not a message from God to the people but a DIALOGUE with God — a two-round dispute over the oldest hard question there is: if God is just, why does injustice win, and why would a holy God use a nation even more wicked (Babylon) to punish a wicked one (Judah)? It ends not with an answer that solves the problem but with a decision to trust in the dark — and gives the New Testament its most quoted line, 'the righteous will live by his faith.'

Chapter by chapter

Habakkuk 1The prophet who ARGUES back. Alone among the Twelve, Habakkuk's book is not a message from God to the people but a DIALOGUE with God — a two-round dispute over the oldest hard question there is. First complaint: how long, Jehovah, will you tolerate the violence rotting Judah, the paralyzed law, the perverted courts? God's answer is the shock the whole book turns on — “I am raising up the CHALDEANS,” the Babylonian war-machine (horses swifter than leopards, who “laugh at every fortress” and whose “own strength is their god”) — the cure worse than the disease. Which triggers the harder, second complaint, the theodicy problem stated as cleanly as it ever has been: “you are of purer eyes than to look on evil… why then do you look on the treacherous, and stay silent while the wicked swallows one more righteous than himself?” The chapter ends with the question hanging in the air and the prophet climbing his watchtower to wait — and the answer, in chapter 2, will be the New Testament's most quoted line: “the righteous will live by his faith.” ⚠ A running commentary on this very book was among the first Dead Sea Scrolls found in 1947.

About the book

Where it sits
EIGHTH of THE TWELVE in the Hebrew order (after Nahum, before Zephaniah). Three chapters, and no versification offset from the English — a rarity in this neighbourhood. ⚠ Chapter 3 has the character of a separate composition (its own heading and musical notes), and — tellingly — it is ABSENT from the Dead Sea Scrolls' Habakkuk Commentary (1QpHab), which stops at the end of chapter 2, one of the clues in the debate over whether the psalm was original to the book.
Genre
A DISPUTE / DIALOGUE, followed by a PSALM. Chapters 1-2 are a structured argument (complaint, answer, complaint, answer) shading into five woe-oracles against the tyrant; chapter 3 is a self-standing liturgical poem — a theophany with a musical superscription ('on shigionoth'), the term Selah three times, and a closing note 'to the choirmaster, on my stringed instruments,' which suggests it was sung in temple worship. ⚠ So Habakkuk is part lawsuit, part hymn — the theodicy stated as a legal brief and then answered by an act of praise. Some of its Hebrew (chapter 3 especially) is archaic and among the hardest in the Bible.
Author
HABAKKUK 'THE PROPHET,' of whom nothing else is known — no father, no home town, only the title (repeated at 3:1). The temple-worship notes of chapter 3 have led some to think he was a levitical singer attached to the sanctuary, which would make him a professional in a way Amos and Micah were not. He wrote as Babylon was rising, a contemporary of Jeremiah and of the young Daniel.
Date written
c. 605 BC, close-fixed by the historical crisis the book turns on. Habakkuk speaks of the CHALDEANS (neo-Babylonians) as a rising, terrifying power that God is about to unleash (1:6) — which points to the years just after Nebuchadnezzar's victory at Carchemish (605) made Babylon the master of the west, and before the deportations began. A minority dates the first complaint slightly earlier (to the internal violence of Jehoiakim's reign) with the Babylon material added as events unfolded; either way the book stands at the very end of Judah's independence.
Place
JUDAH in its last free years — a society, on the prophet's own account, rotting from inside with 'violence,' a paralyzed law, and perverted courts. The threat on the horizon is BABYLON (the Chaldeans), sweeping across 'the breadth of the earth.' No specific town is named; the drama is between a prophet, his God, and an empire.
Audience
Anyone who has looked at the triumph of the wicked and asked where God is. Habakkuk does not scold the people; he SPEAKS FOR them, taking their unanswered questions up the mountain and putting them to God directly. His book is written for the believer in a dark time — which is why it has been read, ever since, by people watching evil win and wondering how long.

How it's laid out

1:1-2:1THE DIALOGUE — first complaint (how long will you tolerate the violence in Judah?); God's answer (I am raising up the Chaldeans); second, harder complaint (how can holy eyes use a nation more wicked than the one it punishes?); and the prophet climbing his watchtower to wait for the reply.
2:2-20THE ANSWER AND THE FIVE WOES — 'write the vision, make it plain… the righteous will live by his faithfulness' (2:4); then five 'woe' oracles on the Chaldean tyrant (the plunderer, the exploiter, the builder-by-bloodshed, the drunk-maker, the idolater), and the vision that the whole earth 'will be filled with the knowledge of the glory of Jehovah as the waters cover the sea.'
3THE PSALM — 'Jehovah, I have heard your report, and I fear.' A theophany of God marching out of the south in storm to save his people, ending on the highest note of faith in the Bible: 'though the fig tree does not blossom and there is no fruit on the vines… yet I will exult in Jehovah, I will rejoice in the God of my salvation.'

What it's about

  • THEODICY — the problem of evil, stated more nakedly here than anywhere else in the prophets: 'you are of purer eyes than to look on evil… why then do you look on the treacherous and stay silent?' (1:13). Habakkuk does not solve it; he holds God's holiness and God's apparent inaction together and refuses to drop either.
  • FAITH IN THE DARK — the book's answer is not an explanation but a posture: 'the righteous will live by his FAITHFULNESS' (2:4). To live by faith is to keep trusting the God who has not yet acted, on the strength of who he is — which is exactly what chapter 3 enacts, rejoicing when the fig tree fails.
  • THE ARGUING PROPHET — Habakkuk legitimizes the honest complaint. He is not punished for cross-examining God; he is answered. The book stands with Job and the lament-psalms as Scripture's permission to bring God your hardest questions rather than swallow them.
  • GOD'S SOVEREIGNTY OVER EMPIRE — the terrifying claim of 1:6, 'I am raising up the Chaldeans': the God of a small nation is the God of the great predator-empires too, using even Babylon as his rod — and holding Babylon guilty for its own arrogance (1:11; the woes of ch 2). History is not out of his hands, however it looks.
  • THE WEAPON-WORSHIPPER — Babylon 'sacrifices to his net' (1:16), the empire on its knees before its own military might; 'his own strength is his god' (1:11). Habakkuk names the specific sin of great power: the deification of force, which the woes of chapter 2 then dismantle.

The source text

Translated from the pointed Hebrew Masoretic Text (as printed by Mechon-Mamre). ⚠ Habakkuk's Hebrew ranges from clear (the dialogue of chapters 1-2) to among the most difficult in the Bible (the archaic poetry of chapter 3, and the notorious crux of 1:11). This translation follows the Masoretic reading where it is hard rather than emending to a smoother sense (so 1:11 keeps 'whose own strength is his god' rather than the KJV's 'his mind change / imputing… unto his god'), and flags the uncertainty. The famous 2:4 will be handled at its own chapter, where the choice between 'faith' and 'faithfulness' (the same Hebrew word, emunah) is itself the interpretive question.

Looking forward

Habakkuk gives the New Testament its theological keystone. ⚠ 'The righteous will live by his faith' (2:4) is quoted THREE times — Romans 1:17 (the thesis of the whole letter, 'the righteous shall live by faith'), Galatians 3:11, and Hebrews 10:38 — and Luther's rediscovery of it lit the Reformation. Paul also quotes 1:5 ('behold, you scoffers… a work is being done in your days you would not believe') at Antioch (Acts 13:41) as a warning to those who reject the gospel. Beyond the quotations, the book's whole movement — from the honest cry 'how long?' through the answer that God is at work in ways beyond belief, to the closing 'yet I will rejoice in the God of my salvation' — traces the shape of faith the New Testament calls for: trust in a God whose deliverance is certain but not yet seen. Habakkuk 3's God 'marching out for the salvation of your people, for the salvation of your anointed (mashiach)' is read by the church as pointing beyond any historical rescue to the ultimate one.

Where the debates are

THE UNITY OF THE BOOK — whether chapter 3's psalm was original to Habakkuk or a later liturgical addition (its separate heading, its archaic language, and its absence from the Qumran commentary are the evidence on the 'added' side). THE DATE and sequence of the complaints — whether the Chaldeans are the answer to the violence, or whether the first complaint is ABOUT the Chaldeans and the wicked/righteous are nations rather than groups within Judah. THE MEANING OF 2:4 — 'faith' (trust) or 'faithfulness' (steadfast loyalty), the same Hebrew emunah, and whose faith is meant. And the crux of 1:11, one of the hardest half-verses in the prophets.
