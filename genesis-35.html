Jacob keeps his Bethel vow — the foreign gods buried, his name sealed Israel a second time under El Shaddai — then the road takes Deborah, Rachel (bearing Benjamin at Bethlehem), and at last old Isaac, buried by Esau and Jacob together.
AM ≈ 2207 · c. 1739 BC — God calls Jacob back to Bethel to keep the vow he made fleeing; he purges the camp of its foreign gods, builds the altar, and is confirmed as Israel, heir of El Shaddai's covenant. On the road south Rachel dies bearing Benjamin and is buried by Ephrath — Bethlehem; Reuben sins against his father's bed; the twelve sons are all named. Isaac's death at Hebron (age 180, buried by both his sons) is recorded here to close his generation, though by the numbers he outlives Joseph's sale by years.

Translator's Notes — verse by verse

Jacob keeps the vow he made fleeing this land — back to Bethel, the foreign gods buried on the way — and God seals his new name a second time. Then the road exacts its price: Deborah's oak, Rachel's grave at Bethlehem, and old Isaac laid to rest by the two brothers together. Quotations from copyrighted versions (NIV, TLB, NWT) are kept to short phrases for comparison; KJV, Geneva, Douay-Rheims and ASV are public domain.

Verses 1–8 · the vow comes due — the gods buried, and a terror of God

Twenty years, and the stone at Bethel is remembered. "Arise, go up to Bethel and dwell there" — God calls Jacob back to the exact place where, a fugitive with a stone for a pillow, he had dreamed the stairway and made his first vow: "if God will be with me… and I return in peace… then Jehovah shall be my God, and this stone shall be God's house, and of all you give me I will give a tenth" (28:20-22). He has returned, in peace and with a limp; now the vow is called due, and he goes to keep it. It is the whole arc of his exile closing on itself.

A house cleansed before it meets God. First he purges his own camp: "Put away the foreign gods (elohei nekhar)… purify yourselves, and change your garments" — the earliest form of the consecration Israel will make at Sinai ("wash your garments," Exodus 19:10-14). Among those household idols, surely, are the teraphim Rachel stole from Laban (31:19) and hid so cleverly; the text left their fate hanging, and here they are given up. Jacob buries them "under the terebinth by Shechem" — the very town his sons had just drenched in blood (ch. 34), now a grave for dead gods. The KJV "strange gods," the ASV and NWT "foreign gods."

Protected by fear, not by the sword. Then the family that expects reprisal for Shechem is met by something else: "a terror of God (chittat elohim) was on the cities around, and they did not pursue the sons of Jacob." The safety Simeon and Levi thought they had won with swords is given instead as a gift — a quiet verdict on their method. Jacob builds his altar and names it El-Bethel, "the God of Bethel" — not just the place but its God, now owned. And a tender footnote closes the scene: Deborah, Rebekah's old nurse (unnamed when she left home with the young bride, 24:59), dies and is buried under an oak called Allon-bacuth, "the oak of weeping." Rebekah herself gets no death-scene in Genesis; her nurse gets a named tree.

Verses 9–15 · Israel, again — אֵל שַׁדַּי El Shaddai and the pillar

The name sealed a second time. "Your name shall no longer be Jacob, but Israel" — but the reader has heard this before, wrestled out of the dark at Peniel (32:28). Here it is given again, in daylight, by God directly and in blessing at the altar. The two accounts sit side by side; source-critics read them as two traditions of one renaming (this the Priestly telling, the Jabbok the older), and this translation shows the doubling rather than smoothing it away — the new name is sealed twice, once striven for by night and once bestowed by day. What was won by struggle is now confirmed by grace.

El Shaddai, and the creation-blessing aimed at a nation. "I am El Shaddai" — the covenant-name God used with Abraham at the covenant of circumcision (17:1) — "be fruitful and multiply; a nation and a company of nations shall come from you, and kings shall come from your loins." The very words spoken over Adam and Noah (1:28; 9:1) are now narrowed onto one family and stretched forward: not just a nation but nations, and — for the first time — kings, the monarchy foreseen generations before there is one. The KJV renders the name "God Almighty" (following the Greek and Latin); the NWT "God Almighty" as well, footnoting the Hebrew. Jacob answers with worship: a pillar of stone (his second at Bethel, after his stone pillow, 28:18), and over it he pours a drink-offering (nesekh) — the Bible's first — and oil, and names the place Bethel once more.

Verses 16–22 · Rachel's grave — בֶּן-אוֹנִי Ben-oni and Benjamin

The added son, and the price. On the road to Ephrath Rachel goes into hard labor and dies bearing her second son. Long ago she had cried "give me children, or I die" (30:1), and named her first "may he add" (Joseph, 30:24) in hope of another; now the added son comes, and the old plea turns literal — she has her son and she dies. With her last breath she names him Ben-oni — "son of my sorrow" (from on/aven, trouble) — but "his father called him Benjamin," "son of the right hand" (yamin, the hand of strength and favor). He is the only son in Genesis whose name a parent overrides: Jacob will not let the last child of his beloved wife carry "my sorrow" for a lifetime, and turns a dying grief into a name of blessing. The KJV keeps both — "Benoni… Benjamin" — as this translation does; the meanings are the whole point.

Rachel at Bethlehem. She is buried "on the way to Ephrath — that is, Bethlehem," the town's first naming in the Bible, and Jacob sets a pillar that stands "to this day." Centuries on, Jeremiah will hear "Rachel weeping for her children… because they are no more" (Jeremiah 31:15), and Matthew will read that weeping over the children of Bethlehem killed by Herod (Matthew 2:18) — the mother buried by Bethlehem, weeping for Bethlehem's sons. Rachel dies at the door of the town where the Messiah will be born.

A firstborn's grab, and a father's silence. Then, in a single hard line: "Reuben went and lay with Bilhah his father's concubine; and Israel heard." It is more than lust — to take a father's concubine was to reach for a father's authority (as Absalom will later take David's, 2 Samuel 16:22). And Israel, as with Dinah, says nothing here; the scribes even drew a paragraph break in the middle of the verse, a veil across the shameful line. But the silence is not the end of it: at his deathbed Jacob strips Reuben the firstborn of his birthright — "unstable as water, you shall not have the excellency, because you went up to your father's bed" (49:3-4). The verdict only waits.

Verses 22–26 · the twelve, complete

The house assembled. "Now the sons of Jacob were twelve" — and the roster is read out by mother: Leah's six (Reuben, Simeon, Levi, Judah, Issachar, Zebulun), Rachel's two (Joseph and Benjamin), Bilhah's two (Dan and Naphtali), Zilpah's two (Gad and Asher). These are the twelve tribes-to-be, named at last in one place. The Hebrew strings them with an unbroken "and… and… and" (the polysyndeton this translation keeps, where the NIV tidies them into a comma list) — a deliberate piling-up, the family counted like treasure. One small seam: the summary calls them all "born in Paddan-aram," though Benjamin was just born in Canaan a few verses back (v. 18) — a rounded-off formula for the household as a whole, not a strict claim about the last son. The full family stands complete on the page for the first time — just before the next chapter but one begins to tear it apart.

Verses 27–29 · Isaac laid to rest — the brothers at the grave

Home at last, and a father buried. Jacob reaches Isaac at Mamre / Hebron — the place of the family's one owned patch of ground, the cave of Machpelah — and Isaac dies at 180, "old and full of days," and is "gathered to his people," the death-formula that quietly reaches past the grave to the fathers. And the last line heals an old wound: "Esau and Jacob his sons buried him" — the twins who fought in the womb and over the birthright and the blessing stand together at the tomb, exactly as Isaac and Ishmael, the heir and the sent-away son, had stood together to bury Abraham (25:9). Genesis buries its patriarchs to the sound of estranged brothers making peace.

An obituary out of order. Isaac does not really die yet — he is 180 here, which means he outlives the sale of Joseph (ch. 37) by a dozen years and more. The narrator gives his death now, early, to close his generation cleanly before the book turns wholly to the next — the same thing it did with Abraham, whose death (25:8) is told before the Jacob-and-Esau story it actually precedes. It is a storyteller's mercy: clear the stage of the old man in peace, before the years of grief over Joseph begin.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the words exact / distinct: "foreign gods" for elohei nekhar; "El-Bethel" left in the Hebrew; "El Shaddai" (dictionary shaddai) rather than the Greek-derived "Almighty"; Ben-oni and Benjamin kept apart so the mother's grief and the father's blessing both sound; "pillar" for matzevah and "drink-offering" for nesekh; and "gathered to his people," the death-formula, unflattened.

Echoes paid. The Bethel vow (28:20-22) kept — Jacob returns, buries the idols, builds the altar; the terror of God protecting the family, exactly as planted at Shechem (ch. 34); the name Israel (32:28) sealed a second time; the second pillar at Bethel (28:18); the buried foreign gods answering Rachel's stolen teraphim (31:19); Rachel's old plea for "another son" (30:24) answered and fatal; and Esau and Jacob at the grave mirroring Isaac and Ishmael burying Abraham (25:9). Echoes planted: Rachel's tomb and "Rachel weeping" (Jeremiah 31:15 → Matthew 2:18); Reuben's sin and the birthright he will lose (49:3-4); the twelve complete, waiting for the coat and Joseph (ch. 37); and Bethlehem, first named here, waiting for its king.

Next installment in this book: Genesis 36 — the book pauses to catalogue Esau: his Canaanite wives, his move to Seir, and the long roll of the clans, chiefs, and kings of Edom "before any king reigned over Israel" — tying off the brother's line before ch. 37 turns to Joseph and the coat that tears the family apart.
