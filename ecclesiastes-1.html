A king in Jerusalem takes the whole world as his subject and reports back: the sun, the wind, the rivers, the generations — all of them in motion, none of them arriving anywhere.
⚠ Filed under the monarchy because the speaker claims it — 'son of David, king in Jerusalem,' traditionally SOLOMON (reigned c. 970–931 BC) — but the book's own language argues otherwise. Its Hebrew is demonstrably late, carrying Aramaic (ra'yon, 1:17, the ordinary Aramaic word for 'thought,' found in Hebrew only here) and Persian loanwords (pardes, 2:5; pitgam, 8:11), which puts the WRITING in the Persian or early Hellenistic period, 4th–3rd century BC. The oldest surviving copy, 4QQoh-a from Qumran, is mid-2nd century, which fixes the latest possible date. Setting and composition are two different questions, and here they are centuries apart.

Translator’s Notes

Verses 1–3 · a title nobody can translate, a word everybody mistranslates, and a question from a ledger

⚠ QOHELET is not a name. Qohelet (קֹהֶלֶת) is a participle built on qahal, “to assemble” — so, roughly, the one who convenes an assembly. It is used of no one else in the Bible, and it is grammatically feminine while taking masculine verbs, which nobody has satisfactorily explained; the likeliest account is that it began as a title of office (Hebrew forms offices that way — compare Sophereth and Pochereth in Ezra 2) and hardened into a name. The Greek translators rendered it Ekklēsiastēs, from ekklēsia — the very word this translation renders “congregation” in the New Testament — and Latin carried that through to the English title Ecclesiastes. So the book’s familiar name means, almost exactly, the Congregator.

The versions divide on what to do with it, and the choice colors the whole book. KJV, GNV, DRB and TLB all say “the Preacher”, which imports a pulpit the Hebrew does not have. NIV has “the Teacher”. NWT is the most literal of the shelf here — “the congregator” — and is exactly right about the root, at the cost of sounding like a job posting. ASV keeps “the Preacher” too. We simply leave the word standing: Qohelet. It is the honest option, and it keeps the reader from assuming a sermon is coming.

⚠ «Vapor of vapors» — and here is where nearly every English Bible has shaped how this book is read. The word is hevel (הֶבֶל): a breath, a puff of vapor, mist on a cold morning. It is a concrete, physical word. Jerome rendered it vanitas, and the KJV followed him with “Vanity of vanities” — as do ASV, GNV and DRB. But vanitas in Latin means emptiness; English “vanity” has since drifted toward conceit, so modern readers hear a moral charge that is not in the Hebrew at all. The modern versions abstract it a different way: NIV “Meaningless! Meaningless!”, NWT “The greatest futility! … Everything is futile”, TLB “nothing is worthwhile”. Each of those is an interpretation — a verdict about worth — where the Hebrew gives an image. Vapor is not worthless. Vapor is real, and you cannot hold it. We keep the image and let the reader do the work: vapor of vapors.

The echo pays off in Genesis. Hevel is also a man’s name — it is the name of Abel, Genesis 4:2, which that chapter never pauses to explain. A man called Breath, who is alive for six verses. The book that turns this word into a refrain is reaching for something already sitting in the Bible’s fourth chapter.

«What profit» is yitron, and it is an accountant’s word: surplus, what is left over after the reckoning. It occurs ten times in this book and nowhere else in the Hebrew Bible. The question of Ecclesiastes is therefore posed, from its third verse, in the language of a balance sheet: after all the amal — the grinding, wearing labor — what is the net? KJV “What profit hath a man”, NWT “What profit does a man have”, NIV softens to “What do people gain”.

«Under the sun» — twenty-nine times in this book, and nowhere else in the Bible. It is the book’s frame and its restriction: everything Qohelet says he says about the world as observed from inside it. Whatever may be true above the sun is, by his own terms, outside the report.

Verses 4–8 · the machine that never arrives anywhere

Four systems in four verses — generations, sun, wind, water — and every one of them is in constant motion and getting nowhere. This is not a poem about decay. Nothing here is breaking down; the machine runs perfectly, forever, and that is the problem.

«The earth stands forever» is the fixed point, and it is placed deliberately: people are what pass through. The generations are the moving part.

⚠ The sun PANTS. Verse 5’s verb is sho’ef (שׁוֹאֵף), from a root meaning to gasp, pant, suck in air — used of a woman in labor (Isaiah 42:14) and of a wild donkey sniffing the wind (Jeremiah 2:24). The sun does not glide back to its starting line; it drags itself there out of breath, and then has to do it again tomorrow. Nearly every version files the effort off: KJV and ASV “hasteth to his place”, NIV “hurries back”, NWT “it returns panting to the place where it rises” — NWT alone keeps the gasp, and is right to. We keep it too.

⚠ A trick of word order in verse 6 that no English version reproduces. The Hebrew runs: going to the south, and turning to the north, turning turning going — and only then, after five participles, arrives at its subject: ha-ruach, the wind. For half a verse you do not know what is moving. The sentence circles before it tells you what is circling, which is the point of the verse performed in its grammar. We keep the delay with a dash. And the word is ruach — wind, breath, spirit — which is worth holding onto for eight more verses.

«All things are wearisome» — devarim means both things and words, and the ambiguity is live. Read as “things,” verse 8 continues the cosmology; read as “words,” it makes a triad of the three faculties — speaking, seeing, hearing — each of them inexhaustible. The shelf goes with things: KJV “All things are full of labour”, NIV and NWT “All things are wearisome”. So do we, but the other reading is not a stretch and the triad that follows is its evidence.

Verses 9–11 · nothing new, and nobody remembered

«There is nothing new under the sun» is the most quoted line in the book and it is usually quoted as weary cynicism. In context it is the conclusion of the nature poem: the closed circuit of verses 4–8 applied to human affairs. If the sun and the wind and the rivers only ever return, so do we.

Verse 11 is the harder blow, and it is often mistranslated into something milder. The word is zikkaron, remembrance, and the claim is not that the past is forgotten but that it works in both directions: there is no remembrance of the former people, and there will be none of the later ones either, by those who come after them. Qohelet includes his own readers, and himself, in the amnesia. KJV “There is no remembrance of former things”, ASV “of the former generations”, NIV “No one remembers the former generations”. The Hebrew rishonim/acharonim can be either people or things; the parallel with the generations of verse 4 argues for people, and we translate it that way.

The scroll mark after verse 11 — a petuchah, an open paragraph break — falls exactly where the book changes register. Everything to this point is anonymous poetry about the world. Everything after it is a man saying I. The scribes who set that space were reading the seam correctly.

Verses 12–15 · «I WAS king» — the tense that started a legend

⚠ The verb in verse 12 is past. Ani Qohelet hayiti melekh — “I, Qohelet, was king over Israel in Jerusalem.” This is a genuine problem for the traditional identification with Solomon, who did not stop being king and then write about it. KJV and ASV print the past plainly, “I the Preacher was king”. NIV keeps it: “I, the Teacher, was king”. NWT renders it “I, the congregator, was king”. The rabbis felt the difficulty and answered it with a story: that Solomon was deposed by the demon Ashmedai and wandered as a beggar saying “I was king” (Talmud, Gittin 68b). It is a wonderful legend and it is an admission that the tense is strange. The simpler reading is that a later writer has adopted a Solomonic persona in order to run the experiment that only a king could afford — and then, having spent it, drops the mask; after chapter 2 the royal fiction is never used again.

«An evil business» — inyan ra. Inyan is a task, occupation, something to be busy with; it appears eight times in this book and nowhere else in the Bible. The sentence is startling and every version has to decide how hard to hit it: KJV and ASV “sore travail”, NIV “What a heavy burden God has laid on mankind!”, NWT “It is a calamitous occupation that God has given”. The Hebrew ra is the ordinary word for evil or bad, and Qohelet says God gave it. He does not soften that, and neither should a translation.

⚠ Verse 15 is the whole book in twelve Hebrew words. What is twisted cannot be made straight, and what is missing cannot be counted. The second clause is the sharper one: chesron (chesron), a deficit, a shortfall, occurs only here in the entire Hebrew Bible — and it is again a bookkeeping word. You cannot audit an absence. Whatever is not there cannot even be entered in the ledger, let alone recovered. KJV “that which is wanting cannot be numbered”, NIV “what is lacking cannot be counted”.

Verses 16–18 · chasing wind, and the price of knowing

«A chasing of wind» — re’ut ruach in verse 14, and it is the book’s second refrain, paired with hevel nine times. The noun re’ut is disputed: from ra’ah, to shepherd or pasture (so: herding the wind), or from an Aramaic root meaning desire, striving. KJV and GNV took the second and made it psychological — “vexation of spirit”, reading ruach as the human spirit; ASV corrected to “striving after wind”, and NIV and NWT both have “chasing after the wind”. The modern reading is almost certainly right, and it is better poetry besides: the same ruach that spent verse 6 going in circles is now the thing he is trying to catch.

⚠ A single word in verse 17 is a piece of hard evidence about when this book was written. There the phrase shifts to ra’yon ruach, and ra’yon is Aramaic. It is the ordinary Aramaic word for thought — it is what Nebuchadnezzar’s thoughts are called in Daniel 2:29–30 — and in Hebrew it occurs only in Ecclesiastes. This is the kind of detail that carries the dating argument: the book’s Hebrew is demonstrably late, full of Aramaic and (in chapter 2) Persian, and no theory of authorship can wish that away.

«Madness and folly» — holelot and sikhlut, an odd pair, the first from a root meaning to rave. Qohelet does not merely note them; he says he set his heart to know them, deliberately, as part of the same investigation as wisdom. That is a research programme, not a moral lapse.

Verse 18 is the chapter’s last word and its bill. In much wisdom is much vexation, and whoever adds knowledge adds pain. The two nouns are ka’as — irritation, grief, the same word used of provoking someone to anger — and makh’ov, physical pain, the word for a wound. KJV and ASV “much grief … increaseth sorrow”, NIV “much sorrow … more grief”, NWT “much frustration … increases pain”. Set beside Proverbs, which spends thirty-one chapters commending the pursuit of wisdom, this is a deliberate and uncomfortable counter-statement — and the canon kept both.

One more absence worth naming at the start. Verse 13 says God — Elohim — and that is the only way this book ever refers to him. Across twelve chapters the divine name never appears; there is no covenant, no Israel’s history, no temple, no law. Coming immediately after Esther, which does not mention God at all, it is worth saying plainly that the Hebrew canon has room for two consecutive scrolls that decline to talk the way the rest of it talks.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the exact / distinct words: “vapor” for hevel — the concrete image, not the Latin verdict “vanity” nor the modern abstraction “meaningless” — and kept identical every one of the five times it falls in verse 2; “Qohelet” left untranslated rather than turned into a Preacher; “profit” for yitron, holding the ledger metaphor; “toil” for amal, distinct from ordinary work; “pants” for the sun in verse 5, because the verb really is a gasp; the delayed subject of verse 6 preserved with a dash, so the sentence circles before it says what is circling; and “evil business” in verse 13, with ra left as the plain word for evil.

Echoes paid. Hevel is the name of Abel in Genesis 4:2 — a man called Breath who lasts six verses; this book takes that word and says it thirty-eight times. Ruach, the wind of verse 6 and the same word as the breath over the waters in Genesis 1:2, becomes by verse 14 the thing that cannot be caught. And the silence about the divine name arrives directly out of Esther: two consecutive scrolls in the Writings that decline to speak the way the rest of the canon speaks.

Echoes planted. «Under the sun», the frame that will restrict every observation in the book to what can be seen from inside the world. «I was king», a royal persona that is spent by the end of chapter 2 and then quietly dropped. The ledger words — yitron and chesron, profit and deficit — which are the terms Qohelet will keep trying to balance and never can. And verse 18, the price of knowing, which the book has eleven more chapters to argue with.
