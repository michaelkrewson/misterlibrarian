The whole book: a single oracle against EDOM for standing by — and cheering — while Babylon sacked Jerusalem, and a closing promise that Zion's exiles will return and 'the kingdom will belong to Jehovah.'
⚠ DISPUTED, but the internal evidence points hard at just after 586 BC. Verses 11-14 describe Edom standing aside while foreigners entered Jerusalem's gates and cast lots over the city — which reads like an eyewitness of the Babylonian sack, and matches the bitter memory of Psalm 137:7 and Lamentations 4:21-22. A minority dates the book far earlier, to a 9th-century raid under Jehoram (2 Kings 8:20-22; 2 Chronicles 21:16-17). The shared material with Jeremiah 49 (Jeremiah preached through the fall of Jerusalem) fits the exilic setting. Filed here just after the fall.

Translator’s Notes

Verse 1 · the shortest book in the Hebrew Bible, and a report already circulating

Twenty-one verses, one chapter, no second page — this is the smallest book in the Old Testament, and every word of it is aimed at a single target. Edom. There is no call narrative, no biography, no date-line, no second subject. Amos condemned Edom in one oracle out of eight (Amos 1:11–12); Obadiah is that oracle grown into a whole book.

The name is a sentence: Oved-Yah, “servant / worshipper of Jehovah.” It was a common enough name — there are about a dozen Obadiahs in the Hebrew Bible, including the God-fearing palace steward who hid a hundred prophets from Jezebel (1 Kings 18) — and none of them can be securely matched to this one. We know nothing about the author but his name, and his name is a job description. DRB, following the Vulgate and the Greek, even spells it differently: “the vision of Abdias.”

⚠ And the opening is not the prophet’s own voice but a bulletin he has overheard. “We have heard a report from Jehovah, and a herald has been sent among the nations” — the summons to war is already going out, and the prophet has simply caught it in transit. The word tsir is a messenger or envoy; KJV and ASV have “an ambassador is sent among the heathen”; NIV “an envoy was sent to the nations.”

⚠ It opens with Amos’s own name for God. Adonai YHVH, “the Lord Jehovah” — the double title that runs some twenty times through Amos and that Amos alone on the shelf (via ASV) prints in full. Its appearance here, in the first three words, is one of several threads tying Obadiah to Amos, whose book sits just before it in the scroll of the Twelve.

⚠ And verses 1–9 are also, almost line for line, in another prophet. Jeremiah 49:7–22, the oracle against Edom, shares whole clauses with Obadiah 1–9 — the eagle’s nest among the stars, the thieves who leave gleanings, the wise men of Teman destroyed. Three explanations have been argued for centuries: Jeremiah borrowed Obadiah; Obadiah borrowed Jeremiah; or both drew on an older oracle now lost, circulating perhaps by word of mouth. The majority view today is the third — a common source — and if forced to rank the two, most place Obadiah first. The library sets the parallel out and does not vote.

Verses 2–4 · a nation living in the rock, and the eagle brought down

⚠ Zadon is not ordinary pride — it is the arrogance that overreaches, the presumption that thinks itself untouchable. The same root gives the word for a deliberate, high-handed sin, and for the “proud” waters that would have drowned the psalmist (Psalm 124:5). Edom’s sin is not idolatry here; it is a state of mind, a security so complete that it has curdled into contempt. KJV “the pride of thine heart hath deceived thee”; NWT “the presumptuousness of your heart”; DRB “the pride of thy heart hath lifted thee up.” We keep the sharper word: the insolence of your heart.

⚠ «You who dwell in the clefts of the rock» is a description and a pun at once. The word is sela, which means “rock, crag” — and is also the name of Edom’s cliff-fortress capital, Sela. Edom’s heartland is the red-sandstone highlands south-east of the Dead Sea, a country of gorges and rock-cut strongholds reached through narrow ravines; the later Nabateans carved Petra into exactly this stone, in exactly this region, and travellers ever since have read Obadiah with Petra in front of them. ⚠ The Edomite Sela is probably an earlier fortress-summit nearby rather than Nabatean Petra itself, but the geography is the same: a people who had made a home where no army could follow.

The reply is the tallest image in the book. “Though you set your nest among the stars” — the eagle nests where nothing can climb to it, and it is still not high enough. KJV “though thou exalt thyself as the eagle, and though thou set thy nest among the stars”; NIV “though you soar like the eagle and make your nest among the stars.” The eagle here is the nesher, which can also mean the griffon-vulture — the great soaring bird of the crags — the same word as Exodus 19’s “eagles’ wings.”

Verses 5–7 · plundered worse than a thief would, and betrayed by the men at his own table

The taunt of verse 5 turns on what robbers LEAVE. A thief takes what he wants and goes; grape-pickers always miss a few clusters — Israelite law actually commanded them to (Leviticus 19:10). Edom will be stripped more thoroughly than either: not robbed but ransacked, its very hiding-places turned out. ASV “how are the things of Esau searched! how are his hidden treasures sought out!” The interjection “how you are ruined!” is jammed into the middle of verse 5, a gasp interrupting the prophet’s own sentence.

⚠ Verse 7 is the cruellest line in the book, and it is about hospitality. The betrayers are named three ways: the men of your covenant, the men at peace with you, and — the phrase that lands hardest — those who eat your bread. In the ancient Near East, to share a man’s bread was to be bound to him; to set a trap for the man whose table you had eaten at was the deepest treachery there was. Edom, who stood by while her brother was betrayed, is repaid by her own allies in exactly that coin. ⚠ The last three words — “there is no understanding in him” — are grammatically abrupt and much discussed; some read them as the trap itself (Edom never saw it coming), some as a closing verdict. KJV “they that eat thy bread have laid a wound under thee: there is none understanding in him”; NWT “those eating your bread will place a net under you where there is no discernment.”

Verses 8–9 · destroying wisdom in the one place famous for it

⚠ The specific thing God promises to destroy in Edom is its WISDOM — and that is a pointed choice, because Edom was known for it. Edomite wisdom was proverbial: “Is there no longer wisdom in Teman?” asks Jeremiah 49:7, and the book of Job locates its conventional wisdom in Edomite country — Job’s first and most confident friend, Eliphaz, is “the Temanite.” So the sentence lands on the nation’s pride a second time: not only its rock-fortress but its reputation for counsel will be taken away. NIV “will I not destroy the wise men of Edom, those of understanding in the mountains of Esau?”

Teman is both a grandson of Esau (Genesis 36:11) and the northern district of Edom his clan named — already an entry in this library from Amos, who burned its citadels (Amos 1:12). Here its “warriors are shattered.” And the phrase “the mountain of Esau,” which recurs like a drumbeat — five times in this short book — keeps the whole quarrel personal: not “Edom” the state but Esau the brother, still being addressed a thousand years after Genesis.

Verses 10–14 · the charge at last — and the day it names

⚠ Now the reason arrives, and it is one word: chamas, VIOLENCE — “for the violence done to your BROTHER Jacob.” Chamas is a heavy word: it is the word for the ruin that filled the earth before the flood (Genesis 6:11–13, “the earth was filled with chamas”), the lawless cruelty that pulls a society down. And it is done to a brother — Edom is Esau, Judah is Jacob, and the twins of Genesis are still the frame. ASV “for the violence done to thy brother Jacob.”

⚠ What Edom actually DID is described with terrible restraint: he STOOD THERE. “On the day you stood aside … you too were like one of them.” The crime is not that Edom sacked Jerusalem — strangers and foreigners did that — but that Edom watched, and by watching joined them. The scene is unmistakable: foreigners entering the gates, casting lots over the city, carrying off its wealth. This is the fall of Jerusalem, and the natural reading dates the whole book to just after 586 BC, when Babylon burned the city and Edom, on the record of Psalm 137:7 (“remember, Jehovah, against the sons of Edom the day of Jerusalem, who said, ‘Strip it, strip it, to its foundation!’”) and Lamentations 4:21, stood by and cheered. A minority dates Obadiah far earlier, to a ninth-century raid; the library leads with 586 because verses 11–14 read like an eyewitness, and records the older view in the book introduction.

⚠ Then comes the most sustained thing in the book: eight commands, each pinned to “the day.” Do not gaze … do not rejoice … do not open your mouth wide … do not enter … do not look … do not lay hands … do not stand at the crossroads … do not hand over. And running under them, ten times in four verses, the drumbeat “on the day” (b’yom) — the day of his misfortune, of his ruin, of distress, of calamity. ⚠ The commands are grammatically forbidding a thing already done: a Hebrew way of replaying the crime in slow motion so the reader watches each step. The escalation is exact — from looking, to gloating, to speaking, to entering the gate, to looting, to blocking the escape routes and rounding up refugees. The gloater becomes an accomplice becomes a slave-catcher, one clause at a time.

Verses 15–16 · the Day of Jehovah, measure for measure, and the cup

⚠ «As you have done, it will be done to you» is the hinge of the whole book — the principle of exact recompense, the deed circling back onto the head that did it. Gemul is a “dealing” or “recompense” that returns, and the image is a boomerang of justice: your dealing will return upon your own head. KJV “as thou hast done, it shall be done unto thee: thy reward shall return upon thine own head”; NIV “your deeds will return upon your own head.”

⚠ And the phrase that carries it is the day of Jehovah — the one Amos first turned around and Joel made a doctrine. Here it is aimed outward again, “near against all the nations,” and Edom is only the first of them. Obadiah stands in a line: Amos pointed the day at Israel, Joel at Judah, Obadiah at the nations — the same phrase, three prophets, three targets.

⚠ The image in verse 16 is the CUP. “As you drank on my holy mountain” — Edom drank, reveled, at Jerusalem’s fall, on Zion itself — “so all the nations will drink continually.” But the second drinking is different: it is the cup of God’s wrath, a standing prophetic image (Jeremiah 25:15–29; Isaiah 51:17), and the nations “drink and gulp it down and are as though they had never been.” The verb la’u, “gulp / swallow noisily,” is rare and vivid. One cup poured at a celebration; another cup that empties the celebrant out of existence.

Verses 17–21 · fire and stubble, a place nobody can find, and the last word

The book turns, in its last five verses, from Edom’s fall to Zion’s survival. “On Mount Zion there will be a peletah” — a remnant of escapees, the survivors — “and it will be holy.” The very word for the fugitives Edom hunted down in verse 14 is now given to the ones who get away on Zion.

⚠ Verse 18 is the sentence Amos’s own book keeps threatening, carried to its end: «no survivor for the house of Esau.» The house of Jacob a fire, the house of Joseph a flame, the house of Esau stubble — dry stalks that a spark finishes. ⚠ And history did roughly this: the Edomites were pushed out of Seir by the Nabateans, drifted into southern Judah (which became “Idumea”), and were forcibly converted to Judaism by John Hyrcanus around 125 BC, after which they simply cease to exist as a separate people. Herod the Great, an Idumean, is the last flare of the line — so that Esau’s house ends, of all ways, absorbed into Jacob’s.

The geography of verses 19–20 is a map of return: the Negev (the southern desert) takes Esau’s mountain, the Shephelah (the lowland) takes Philistia, and the exiles spread out to the old borders — Zarephath, the Phoenician coast town between Tyre and Sidon where Elijah lodged with the widow (1 Kings 17), marking the far north.

⚠ And then SEPHARAD — a place nobody has ever securely located, whose misidentification became the name of a people. The Jerusalem exiles are said to be “in Sepharad.” The best guesses are Sardis in Asia Minor (an Aramaic inscription there names a place “Sfrd”) or a Median district the Assyrians called Saparda. But the Aramaic Targum, paraphrasing centuries later, rendered Sepharad as “Spain” — and that guess stuck so hard that Spanish Jews have been called Sephardi ever since, and are to this day. A single translator’s hunch about an unknown word became an ethnonym for half of world Jewry.

⚠ The last word of the shortest book is the whole point of it. “And deliverers (moshi’im) will go up on Mount Zion” — the word is the one the book of Judges uses for its rescuer-judges — “to judge the mountain of Esau; and the kingdom will belong to Jehovah.” A book that began with a war-summons among the nations ends by handing the whole contested map back to its owner. ⚠ Note the closing scroll mark {ש} — and here it marks the end not just of a chapter but of the entire book, since Obadiah has only one.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the exact / distinct words: “the insolence of your heart” for zadon, the overreaching arrogance that thinks itself untouchable, not ordinary pride; “the clefts of the rock” keeping sela as both crag and the place-name Sela; “those who eat your bread” kept literal, because the betrayal is a betrayal of the TABLE; “the violence done to your brother Jacob” for chamas, the flood-word for society-wrecking cruelty, done to a brother; the ten repetitions of “on the day” in verses 11–14 kept as the hammering drumbeat they are; “your dealing will return upon your own head” for gemul, the boomerang of exact recompense; and “deliverers” for moshi’im, the rescuer-word of the book of Judges, in the final verse.

Echoes paid. Esau and Jacob, the quarrelling twins of Genesis 27, still the whole frame — “your brother Jacob,” “the mountain of Esau” five times. Chamas, the “violence” that filled the earth before the flood in Genesis 6, now Edom’s single named crime. Teman and its shattered warriors — the Edomite district Amos had just burned. Adonai YHVH, Amos’s own title for God, opening the book. And the day of Jehovah — pointed at Israel by Amos, made doctrine by Joel, and here aimed at all the nations.

Echoes planted. The oracle against Edom, which reaches the New Testament’s world through Herod the Idumean — the last Edomite, ruling Jacob’s land at the birth of Jesus. The cup of the nations (v16), the wrath-cup that runs through Jeremiah and Isaiah and into the Passion (“let this cup pass”) and Revelation. “As you have done, it will be done to you” (v15), the measure-for-measure principle the Sermon on the Mount will restate. And the final line — “the kingdom will belong to Jehovah” — the note the whole prophetic hope is tuned to, and the one Revelation ends on: “the kingdom of the world has become the kingdom of our Lord.”
