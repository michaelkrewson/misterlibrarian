The sea. Israel is told to camp with the water at its back so Pharaoh will think them trapped; six hundred chariots overtake them; a strong east wind blows all night, Israel crosses on dry ground, and the returning water destroys the army — after which Israel believes for the first time.
c. 1446 BC on the early-date chronology this project foregrounds — days after the Passover, at a place the text names precisely and no one can now locate.

Exodus 14 — The Mister Translation

1
וַיְדַבֵּר יְהוָה, אֶל-מֹשֶׁה לֵּאמֹר.
And Jehovah spoke to Moses, saying,note
Y Jehová habló a Moisés, diciendo:
2
דַּבֵּר, אֶל-בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל, וְיָשֻׁבוּ וְיַחֲנוּ לִפְנֵי פִּי הַחִירֹת, בֵּין מִגְדֹּל וּבֵין הַיָּם: לִפְנֵי בַּעַל צְפֹן, נִכְחוֹ תַחֲנוּ עַל-הַיָּם.
“Speak to the sons of Israel, that they turn back and camp before Pi-hahiroth, between Migdol and the sea, before Baal-zephon; opposite it you shall camp, by the sea.note
«Di a los hijos de Israel que vuelvan atrás y acampen delante de Pi-hahirot, entre Migdol y el mar; delante de Baal-zefón, frente a él acamparéis, junto al mar.
3
וְאָמַר פַּרְעֹה לִבְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל, נְבֻכִים הֵם בָּאָרֶץ; סָגַר עֲלֵיהֶם, הַמִּדְבָּר.
And Pharaoh will say of the sons of Israel, ‘They are wandering in confusion in the land; the wilderness has shut them in.’note
Y el faraón dirá de los hijos de Israel: ‘Andan errantes en la tierra; el desierto los ha encerrado’.
4
וְחִזַּקְתִּי אֶת-לֵב-פַּרְעֹה, וְרָדַף אַחֲרֵיהֶם, וְאִכָּבְדָה בְּפַרְעֹה וּבְכָל-חֵילוֹ, וְיָדְעוּ מִצְרַיִם כִּי-אֲנִי יְהוָה; וַיַּעֲשׂוּ-כֵן.
And I will make strong Pharaoh’s heart, and he will pursue them, and I will get glory over Pharaoh and over all his army; and Egypt will know that I am Jehovah.” And they did so.note
Y yo endureceré el corazón del faraón, y los perseguirá; y me glorificaré en el faraón y en todo su ejército, y sabrán los egipcios que yo soy Jehová». Y ellos lo hicieron así.
5
וַיֻּגַּד לְמֶלֶךְ מִצְרַיִם, כִּי בָרַח הָעָם; וַיֵּהָפֵךְ לְבַב פַּרְעֹה וַעֲבָדָיו, אֶל-הָעָם, וַיֹּאמְרוּ מַה-זֹּאת עָשִׂינוּ, כִּי-שִׁלַּחְנוּ אֶת-יִשְׂרָאֵל מֵעָבְדֵנוּ.
And it was told to the king of Egypt that the people had fled, and the heart of Pharaoh and of his servants was turned against the people, and they said, “What is this we have done, that we have sent Israel away from serving us?”note
Y se le dio aviso al rey de Egipto de que el pueblo había huido, y el corazón del faraón y de sus siervos se volvió contra el pueblo, y dijeron: «¿Qué es esto que hemos hecho, que hemos dejado ir a Israel de servirnos?».
6
וַיֶּאְסֹר, אֶת-רִכְבּוֹ; וְאֶת-עַמּוֹ, לָקַח עִמּוֹ.
And he harnessed his chariot and took his people with him.note
Y unció su carro y tomó consigo a su gente.
7
וַיִּקַּח, שֵׁשׁ-מֵאוֹת רֶכֶב בָּחוּר, וְכֹל, רֶכֶב מִצְרָיִם; וְשָׁלִשִׁם, עַל-כֻּלּוֹ.
And he took six hundred chosen chariots and all the chariots of Egypt, with officers over all of them.note
Y tomó seiscientos carros escogidos y todos los carros de Egipto, con oficiales sobre todos ellos.
8
וַיְחַזֵּק יְהוָה, אֶת-לֵב פַּרְעֹה מֶלֶךְ מִצְרַיִם, וַיִּרְדֹּף, אַחֲרֵי בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל; וּבְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל, יֹצְאִים בְּיָד רָמָה.
And Jehovah made strong the heart of Pharaoh king of Egypt, and he pursued the sons of Israel — and the sons of Israel were going out with a high hand.note
Y Jehová endureció el corazón del faraón, rey de Egipto, y persiguió a los hijos de Israel; y los hijos de Israel salían con mano alzada.
9
וַיִּרְדְּפוּ מִצְרַיִם אַחֲרֵיהֶם, וַיַּשִּׂיגוּ אוֹתָם חֹנִים עַל-הַיָּם, כָּל-סוּס רֶכֶב פַּרְעֹה, וּפָרָשָׁיו וְחֵילוֹ--עַל-פִּי, הַחִירֹת, לִפְנֵי, בַּעַל צְפֹן.
And the Egyptians pursued them and overtook them camping by the sea — all the horses and chariots of Pharaoh and his horsemen and his army — by Pi-hahiroth, before Baal-zephon.note
Y los egipcios los persiguieron y los alcanzaron acampados junto al mar — todos los caballos y carros del faraón, y sus jinetes y su ejército —, junto a Pi-hahirot, delante de Baal-zefón.
10
וּפַרְעֹה, הִקְרִיב; וַיִּשְׂאוּ בְנֵי-יִשְׂרָאֵל אֶת-עֵינֵיהֶם וְהִנֵּה מִצְרַיִם נֹסֵעַ אַחֲרֵיהֶם, וַיִּירְאוּ מְאֹד, וַיִּצְעֲקוּ בְנֵי-יִשְׂרָאֵל, אֶל-יְהוָה.
And Pharaoh drew near, and the sons of Israel lifted up their eyes, and behold, Egypt was marching after them; and they were very afraid, and the sons of Israel cried out to Jehovah.note
Y el faraón se acercó, y los hijos de Israel alzaron sus ojos, y he aquí que los egipcios marchaban tras ellos; y tuvieron mucho miedo, y los hijos de Israel clamaron a Jehová.
11
וַיֹּאמְרוּ, אֶל-מֹשֶׁה, הֲמִבְּלִי אֵין-קְבָרִים בְּמִצְרַיִם, לְקַחְתָּנוּ לָמוּת בַּמִּדְבָּר: מַה-זֹּאת עָשִׂיתָ לָּנוּ, לְהוֹצִיאָנוּ מִמִּצְרָיִם.
And they said to Moses, “Was it because there were no graves in Egypt that you took us away to die in the wilderness? What is this you have done to us, bringing us out of Egypt?note
Y dijeron a Moisés: «¿Acaso no había sepulcros en Egipto, que nos has sacado para morir en el desierto? ¿Qué es esto que nos has hecho, sacándonos de Egipto?
12
הֲלֹא-זֶה הַדָּבָר, אֲשֶׁר דִּבַּרְנוּ אֵלֶיךָ בְמִצְרַיִם לֵאמֹר, חֲדַל מִמֶּנּוּ, וְנַעַבְדָה אֶת-מִצְרָיִם: כִּי טוֹב לָנוּ עֲבֹד אֶת-מִצְרַיִם, מִמֻּתֵנוּ בַּמִּדְבָּר.
Is this not the word we spoke to you in Egypt, saying, ‘Leave us alone, that we may serve the Egyptians’? For it is better for us to serve the Egyptians than to die in the wilderness.”note
¿No es esto lo que te dijimos en Egipto: ‘Déjanos en paz, que sirvamos a los egipcios’? Porque mejor nos era servir a los egipcios que morir en el desierto».
13
וַיֹּאמֶר מֹשֶׁה אֶל-הָעָם, אַל-תִּירָאוּ--הִתְיַצְּבוּ וּרְאוּ אֶת-יְשׁוּעַת יְהוָה, אֲשֶׁר-יַעֲשֶׂה לָכֶם הַיּוֹם: כִּי, אֲשֶׁר רְאִיתֶם אֶת-מִצְרַיִם הַיּוֹם--לֹא תֹסִפוּ לִרְאֹתָם עוֹד, עַד-עוֹלָם.
And Moses said to the people, “Do not be afraid. Stand firm and see the salvation of Jehovah, which he will work for you today; for the Egyptians whom you see today, you will never see again, ever.note
Y Moisés dijo al pueblo: «No temáis. Estad firmes y ved la salvación de Jehová, que él os hará hoy; porque a los egipcios que hoy veis, no los volveréis a ver jamás.
14
יְהוָה, יִלָּחֵם לָכֶם; וְאַתֶּם, תַּחֲרִשׁוּן. {פ}
Jehovah will fight for you, and you shall keep silent.”note
Jehová peleará por vosotros, y vosotros callaréis».
15
וַיֹּאמֶר יְהוָה אֶל-מֹשֶׁה, מַה-תִּצְעַק אֵלָי; דַּבֵּר אֶל-בְּנֵי-יִשְׂרָאֵל, וְיִסָּעוּ.
And Jehovah said to Moses, “Why do you cry out to me? Speak to the sons of Israel, that they set out.note
Y Jehová dijo a Moisés: «¿Por qué clamas a mí? Di a los hijos de Israel que se pongan en marcha.
16
וְאַתָּה הָרֵם אֶת-מַטְּךָ, וּנְטֵה אֶת-יָדְךָ עַל-הַיָּם--וּבְקָעֵהוּ; וְיָבֹאוּ בְנֵי-יִשְׂרָאֵל בְּתוֹךְ הַיָּם, בַּיַּבָּשָׁה.
And you, lift up your staff and stretch out your hand over the sea and split it, and let the sons of Israel go into the midst of the sea on dry ground.note
Y tú, alza tu vara y extiende tu mano sobre el mar y divídelo, y entren los hijos de Israel por en medio del mar, en seco.
17
וַאֲנִי, הִנְנִי מְחַזֵּק אֶת-לֵב מִצְרַיִם, וְיָבֹאוּ, אַחֲרֵיהֶם; וְאִכָּבְדָה בְּפַרְעֹה וּבְכָל-חֵילוֹ, בְּרִכְבּוֹ וּבְפָרָשָׁיו.
And I, behold, I am making strong the heart of the Egyptians, and they will go in after them; and I will get glory over Pharaoh and over all his army, over his chariots and over his horsemen.note
Y yo, he aquí que endurezco el corazón de los egipcios, y entrarán tras ellos; y me glorificaré en el faraón y en todo su ejército, en sus carros y en sus jinetes.
18
וְיָדְעוּ מִצְרַיִם, כִּי-אֲנִי יְהוָה, בְּהִכָּבְדִי בְּפַרְעֹה, בְּרִכְבּוֹ וּבְפָרָשָׁיו.
And Egypt will know that I am Jehovah, when I get glory over Pharaoh, over his chariots and over his horsemen.”note
Y sabrán los egipcios que yo soy Jehová, cuando me glorifique en el faraón, en sus carros y en sus jinetes».
19
וַיִּסַּע מַלְאַךְ הָאֱלֹהִים, הַהֹלֵךְ לִפְנֵי מַחֲנֵה יִשְׂרָאֵל, וַיֵּלֶךְ, מֵאַחֲרֵיהֶם; וַיִּסַּע עַמּוּד הֶעָנָן, מִפְּנֵיהֶם, וַיַּעֲמֹד, מֵאַחֲרֵיהֶם.
And the angel of God who was going before the camp of Israel moved and went behind them; and the pillar of cloud moved from before them and stood behind them,note
Y el ángel de Dios que iba delante del campamento de Israel se trasladó y fue detrás de ellos; y la columna de nube se movió de delante de ellos y se puso detrás,

y vino a estar entre el campamento de Egipto y el campamento de Israel. Y era nube y tinieblas, y alumbraba la noche; y en toda la noche no se acercaron los unos a los otros.
21
וַיֵּט מֹשֶׁה אֶת-יָדוֹ, עַל-הַיָּם, וַיּוֹלֶךְ יְהוָה אֶת-הַיָּם בְּרוּחַ קָדִים עַזָּה כָּל-הַלַּיְלָה, וַיָּשֶׂם אֶת-הַיָּם לֶחָרָבָה; וַיִּבָּקְעוּ, הַמָּיִם.

Y Moisés extendió su mano sobre el mar, y Jehová hizo retroceder el mar con un fuerte viento del este toda la noche, y convirtió el mar en tierra seca; y las aguas se dividieron.

23

24
וַיְהִי, בְּאַשְׁמֹרֶת הַבֹּקֶר, וַיַּשְׁקֵף יְהוָה אֶל-מַחֲנֵה מִצְרַיִם, בְּעַמּוּד אֵשׁ וְעָנָן; וַיָּהָם, אֵת מַחֲנֵה מִצְרָיִם.

Y sucedió que a la vigilia de la mañana, Jehová miró hacia el campamento de los egipcios desde la columna de fuego y de nube, y sembró la confusión en el campamento de los egipcios.
25
וַיָּסַר, אֵת אֹפַן מַרְכְּבֹתָיו, וַיְנַהֲגֵהוּ, בִּכְבֵדֻת; וַיֹּאמֶר מִצְרַיִם, אָנוּסָה מִפְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל--כִּי יְהוָה, נִלְחָם לָהֶם בְּמִצְרָיִם. {פ}
And he took off the wheels of their chariots and made them drive heavily; and Egypt said, “Let me flee from before Israel, for Jehovah is fighting for them against Egypt.”note
Y quitó las ruedas de sus carros y los hizo avanzar pesadamente; y dijeron los egipcios: «Huyamos de delante de Israel, porque Jehová pelea por ellos contra Egipto».

וַיֹּאמֶר יְהוָה אֶל-מֹשֶׁה, נְטֵה אֶת-יָדְךָ עַל-הַיָּם; וְיָשֻׁבוּ הַמַּיִם עַל-מִצְרַיִם, עַל-רִכְבּוֹ וְעַל-פָּרָשָׁיו.
And Jehovah said to Moses, “Stretch out your hand over the sea, and let the waters return upon the Egyptians, upon their chariots and upon their horsemen.”note
Y Jehová dijo a Moisés: «Extiende tu mano sobre el mar, y vuelvan las aguas sobre los egipcios, sobre sus carros y sobre sus jinetes».
27
וַיֵּט מֹשֶׁה אֶת-יָדוֹ עַל-הַיָּם, וַיָּשָׁב הַיָּם לִפְנוֹת בֹּקֶר לְאֵיתָנוֹ, וּמִצְרַיִם, נָסִים לִקְרָאתוֹ; וַיְנַעֵר יְהוָה אֶת-מִצְרַיִם, בְּתוֹךְ הַיָּם.

Y Moisés extendió su mano sobre el mar, y al rayar el alba el mar volvió a su cauce permanente, mientras los egipcios huían hacia él; y Jehová sacudió a los egipcios en medio del mar.
28
וַיָּשֻׁבוּ הַמַּיִם, וַיְכַסּוּ אֶת-הָרֶכֶב וְאֶת-הַפָּרָשִׁים, לְכֹל חֵיל פַּרְעֹה, הַבָּאִים אַחֲרֵיהֶם בַּיָּם: לֹא-נִשְׁאַר בָּהֶם, עַד-אֶחָד.
And the waters returned and covered the chariots and the horsemen, all the army of Pharaoh that had gone in after them into the sea; not one of them remained.note
Y volvieron las aguas y cubrieron los carros y los jinetes, todo el ejército del faraón que había entrado tras ellos en el mar; no quedó de ellos ni uno solo.
29
וּבְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל הָלְכוּ בַיַּבָּשָׁה, בְּתוֹךְ הַיָּם; וְהַמַּיִם לָהֶם חֹמָה, מִימִינָם וּמִשְּׂמֹאלָם.
But the sons of Israel walked on dry ground in the midst of the sea, and the waters were a wall for them on their right and on their left.note

Translator’s Notes

Verses 1–14 · a deliberate trap, and a people who want their chains back

⚠ The first thing to notice is that the route is BAIT. Israel is told to TURN BACK and camp with the water at their backs — a militarily absurd position — precisely so that “Pharaoh will say, they are wandering in confusion; the wilderness has SHUT THEM IN.” The trap is set with Israel as the lure, and the text says so in advance. The three place-names (Pi-hahiroth, Migdol, Baal-zephon) are given with unusual precision and yet none can be located with confidence today; see the Sea of Reeds for the geography and its long argument.

“I will get GLORY over Pharaoh.” The verb is kaved — to be HEAVY, and so to be honored, weighty, glorious. It is the same root the book has been using all along for Pharaoh’s heart: the heart made heavy is answered by a God who makes himself weighty. KJV “I will be honoured upon Pharaoh”; the ESV “I will get glory over Pharaoh.”

“What is this we have done, that we have SENT ISRAEL AWAY FROM SERVING US?” Pharaoh’s regret is economic and it is put in the exact vocabulary the whole book has been fighting over — the verb avad, to SERVE. The question of the book is never whether Israel will serve, but whom. Six hundred chosen chariots go out to settle it.

⚠ And then Israel opens its mouth for the first time as a free people, and what comes out is the sentence that will define the wilderness: “It is BETTER FOR US TO SERVE THE EGYPTIANS THAN TO DIE IN THE WILDERNESS.” Note the bitter joke they lead with — “was it because there were no GRAVES in Egypt?” — aimed at a country famous above all others for its tombs. They are three days out of slavery and already asking to go back; and they claim, plausibly enough, that they said so at the time.

«STAND FIRM and see the salvation of Jehovah… Jehovah will fight for you, and you shall keep silent.» This is the classic formula of what scholars call holy war, in which the decisive action is not Israel’s. Yeshuah here means rescue in the concrete sense — being got out of danger — and the name JOSHUA, and later JESUS, are built from the same root. KJV “stand still, and see the salvation of the LORD”; the NIV “stand firm and you will see the deliverance the LORD will bring.”

Verses 15–25 · “Why do you cry to me? Go forward.”

⚠ The most quoted tension in the chapter is between verses 13 and 15, and it is real: Moses has just said STAND STILL, and God immediately says “Why do you cry out to me? Tell them to SET OUT.” The two are not a contradiction so much as a sequence — stop panicking, then walk — but the sharpness is worth keeping. Standing still is the posture toward the enemy; moving is the posture toward the sea. The one thing not permitted is prayer as a substitute for obedience.

The pillar changes station. For a chapter it has gone BEFORE them as a guide; now it moves BEHIND them as a rear-guard, and what it does there is the strangest sentence in the passage: “there was the cloud and the darkness, and it lit up the night.” The Hebrew is compressed and old, and the standard reading is that the same pillar was dark toward Egypt and light toward Israel — the Greek Old Testament and the ancient versions fill it out that way. KJV “it was a cloud and darkness to them, but it gave light by night to these” (the pronouns are supplied by the translators); the ESV keeps it terse.

⚠ «Jehovah drove the sea back by a strong EAST WIND all night.» This is worth stating plainly because it is often missed: the text supplies a MECHANISM, and it is a natural one — the same east wind that carried in the locusts (10:13). What Exodus claims is not that nature was suspended but that it was COMMANDED: the wind blows on cue, all night, and stops on cue. That pattern — ordinary means, impossible timing — runs through the whole plague cycle. It also means the older debate about “natural explanations” is somewhat beside the point; the narrative offers one itself and still calls the result the great hand of Jehovah (v31).

“The waters were a WALL for them on their right and on their left.” Chomah is the ordinary word for a city wall, which is why the picture in every illustration is two standing cliffs of water. A minority reading takes it as a metaphor of PROTECTION — a defensive wall on either flank — and points out that a wind strong enough to dry a seabed would not leave water standing in vertical faces. The library notes the reading; the plain sense of chomah is a wall, and that is how it is translated here.

⚠ The chariot wheels (v25): the Hebrew says Jehovah REMOVED them (vayasar). But the Greek Old Testament and the Samaritan text read a nearly identical verb, vaye’esor, “he BOUND / clogged” them — one consonant apart, and closer to “made them drive heavily.” KJV “took off their chariot wheels”; the NIV follows the variant with “jammed the wheels.” This translation keeps the Masoretic reading and flags the alternative.

And the confession comes from the Egyptians themselves — the third time in the book Egypt says the true thing before Israel does (compare the magicians at 8:15 and the officials at 10:7): “Jehovah is fighting for them.”

Verses 26–31 · the sea comes back, and Israel believes

“The sea returned to its STEADY FLOW.” The word (eitan) means permanent, perennial, the state a thing sits in when nothing is holding it otherwise. The miracle is described as the removal of a restraint rather than the addition of a force.

“Jehovah SHOOK the Egyptians off into the midst of the sea” (v27) is a startlingly physical verb (na’ar) — used of shaking out a garment. KJV “overthrew the Egyptians”; the ESV “threw the Egyptians into the midst of the sea.”

⚠ What the text says drowned, and what it does not. Verse 28 is careful: the waters covered “the chariots and the horsemen, ALL THE ARMY OF PHARAOH that had gone in after them.” It is the army that is named, not the king — and the Bible never states anywhere that Pharaoh himself died in the sea. ⚠ This is not a trivial point: it is one leg of the argument (see the pharaoh of the Exodus) that the Exodus pharaoh reigned on afterward and has a known tomb. ⚠ In fairness the other way: Psalm 136:15 says God “shook off Pharaoh and his army in the Sea of Reeds” using this chapter’s own verb, and many readers take that as including him. The library reports both and does not settle it.

“Israel saw the Egyptians dead on the shore of the sea.” The deliverance is confirmed not by the crossing but by the bodies — a grim, concrete detail the narrative does not soften, and the reason the next chapter can be sung at all.

⚠ «And they BELIEVED in Jehovah and in Moses his servant.» This is the first time in the book that Israel is said to believe, and it is the hinge the whole exodus has been driving at — back at the burning bush the question was precisely whether they would (4:1, 4:31). ⚠ It is also, read forward, a heartbreaking sentence: the belief lasts three days, until the water at Marah is bitter (15:24). Faith produced by rescue turns out to have a short half-life, which is the honest subject of everything from here to the Jordan.

⚠ A note on the scroll marks at verse 31. The signs printed here, {ר} and {ש}, are not letters of the text: they are layout marks in the Masoretic tradition. The SONG OF THE SEA in the next chapter is written in a special stichographic pattern — short lines set out like courses of brickwork rather than as running prose — and these marks bracket it. The {ר} recurs at nearly every line of the song itself. (The same {ש} also closes each book of the scroll, which is how it appears at the end of Genesis and of Daniel.)

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the exact / distinct words: “get glory” for kaved, the same HEAVY root as Pharaoh’s heart; “serving” kept for avad on both sides of the argument, since the book’s question is never whether Israel serves but whom; “salvation” for yeshuah in its concrete sense of rescue; “a wall” for chomah, with the protection reading noted not adopted; “took off the wheels” following the Masoretic text, with the Greek and Samaritan “clogged” flagged; and “shook off” for the garment-verb na’ar.

Echoes paid. The east wind that brought the locusts (10:13), now driving back a sea; the pillar of 13:21, moving from guide to rear-guard; the hardening, one last time, and still stated both ways; Egypt confessing first, as the magicians did at the gnats and the officials before the locusts; and the question of the burning bush — will they believe? (4:1, 31) — answered in the last verse.

Echoes planted. The Song of the Sea, which the scroll is already laying out in its brick pattern before this chapter ends; “better to serve the Egyptians”, the wilderness complaint in its first and purest form; the belief of verse 31, which lasts until the bitter water three days later (15:24); and a sea that becomes Israel’s standing image of rescue, from the Psalms to Paul’s “baptized into Moses in the cloud and in the sea” (1 Corinthians 10:1-2).

Next in this book: Exodus 15 — the Song of the Sea, the oldest poetry in the book and among the oldest in the Bible; then Miriam with a tambourine, and three days later a well of water too bitter to drink.
