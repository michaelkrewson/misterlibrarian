Genesis 37 — The Mister Translation

1
וַיֵּשֶׁב יַעֲקֹב, בְּאֶרֶץ מְגוּרֵי אָבִיו--בְּאֶרֶץ, כְּנָעַן.
And Jacob settled in the land of his father's sojournings, in the land of Canaan.note
Y Jacob se asentó en la tierra de las peregrinaciones de su padre, en la tierra de Canaán.
cross-refs⤷ 23:4
2
אֵלֶּה תֹּלְדוֹת יַעֲקֹב, יוֹסֵף בֶּן-שְׁבַע-עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה הָיָה רֹעֶה אֶת-אֶחָיו בַּצֹּאן, וְהוּא נַעַר אֶת-בְּנֵי בִלְהָה וְאֶת-בְּנֵי זִלְפָּה, נְשֵׁי אָבִיו; וַיָּבֵא יוֹסֵף אֶת-דִּבָּתָם רָעָה, אֶל-אֲבִיהֶם.
These are the generations of Jacob. Joseph, seventeen years old, was shepherding the flock with his brothers — he was a lad, with the sons of Bilhah and the sons of Zilpah, his father's wives — and Joseph brought a bad report of them to their father.note
Estas son las generaciones de Jacob. José, de diecisiete años, apacentaba el rebaño con sus hermanos — era un muchacho, con los hijos de Bilhá y los hijos de Zilpá, mujeres de su padre — y José trajo un mal informe de ellos a su padre.
cross-refs⤷ 36:1⤷ Jer 20:10
3
וְיִשְׂרָאֵל, אָהַב אֶת-יוֹסֵף מִכָּל-בָּנָיו--כִּי-בֶן-זְקֻנִים הוּא, לוֹ; וְעָשָׂה לוֹ, כְּתֹנֶת פַּסִּים.
And Israel loved Joseph more than all his sons, for he was a son of his old age to him; and he made him a long ornamented tunic.note
E Israel amaba a José más que a todos sus hijos, porque era para él un hijo de su vejez; y le hizo una túnica larga y ornamentada.
4
וַיִּרְאוּ אֶחָיו, כִּי-אֹתוֹ אָהַב אֲבִיהֶם מִכָּל-אֶחָיו--וַיִּשְׂנְאוּ, אֹתוֹ; וְלֹא יָכְלוּ, דַּבְּרוֹ לְשָׁלֹם.
And his brothers saw that their father loved him more than all his brothers, and they hated him, and they could not speak to him in peace.note
Y vieron sus hermanos que su padre lo amaba más que a todos sus hermanos, y lo odiaron, y no podían hablarle en paz.
5
וַיַּחֲלֹם יוֹסֵף חֲלוֹם, וַיַּגֵּד לְאֶחָיו; וַיּוֹסִפוּ עוֹד, שְׂנֹא אֹתוֹ.
And Joseph dreamed a dream and told it to his brothers; and they hated him still more.note
Y José soñó un sueño y lo contó a sus hermanos; y lo odiaron todavía más.
6
וַיֹּאמֶר, אֲלֵיהֶם: שִׁמְעוּ-נָא, הַחֲלוֹם הַזֶּה אֲשֶׁר חָלָמְתִּי.
And he said to them, "Listen, please, to this dream that I have dreamed.note
Y les dijo: «Escuchen, por favor, este sueño que he soñado.
7
וְהִנֵּה אֲנַחְנוּ מְאַלְּמִים אֲלֻמִּים, בְּתוֹךְ הַשָּׂדֶה, וְהִנֵּה קָמָה אֲלֻמָּתִי, וְגַם-נִצָּבָה; וְהִנֵּה תְסֻבֶּינָה אֲלֻמֹּתֵיכֶם, וַתִּשְׁתַּחֲוֶיןָ לַאֲלֻמָּתִי.
Look — we were binding sheaves in the middle of the field, and look, my sheaf rose and even stood upright; and look, your sheaves came around and bowed down to my sheaf."note
Mira — estábamos atando gavillas en medio del campo, y mira, mi gavilla se levantó y hasta se quedó erguida; y mira, las gavillas de ustedes la rodearon y se inclinaron ante mi gavilla».
8
וַיֹּאמְרוּ לוֹ, אֶחָיו, הֲמָלֹךְ תִּמְלֹךְ עָלֵינוּ, אִם-מָשׁוֹל תִּמְשֹׁל בָּנוּ; וַיּוֹסִפוּ עוֹד שְׂנֹא אֹתוֹ, עַל-חֲלֹמֹתָיו וְעַל-דְּבָרָיו.
And his brothers said to him, "Will you really be king over us? Will you really rule over us?" And they hated him still more, for his dreams and for his words.note
Y sus hermanos le dijeron: «¿De veras reinarás sobre nosotros? ¿De veras nos dominarás?» Y lo odiaron todavía más, por sus sueños y por sus palabras.
cross-refs⤷ 3:16
9
וַיַּחֲלֹם עוֹד חֲלוֹם אַחֵר, וַיְסַפֵּר אֹתוֹ לְאֶחָיו; וַיֹּאמֶר, הִנֵּה חָלַמְתִּי חֲלוֹם עוֹד, וְהִנֵּה הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ וְהַיָּרֵחַ וְאַחַד עָשָׂר כּוֹכָבִים, מִשְׁתַּחֲוִים לִי.
And he dreamed yet another dream and recounted it to his brothers, and said, "Look, I have dreamed another dream: and look, the sun and the moon and eleven stars were bowing down to me."note
Y soñó todavía otro sueño y lo relató a sus hermanos, y dijo: «Mira, he soñado otro sueño: y mira, el sol y la luna y once estrellas se inclinaban ante mí».
10
וַיְסַפֵּר אֶל-אָבִיו, וְאֶל-אֶחָיו, וַיִּגְעַר-בּוֹ אָבִיו, וַיֹּאמֶר לוֹ מָה הַחֲלוֹם הַזֶּה אֲשֶׁר חָלָמְתָּ: הֲבוֹא נָבוֹא, אֲנִי וְאִמְּךָ וְאַחֶיךָ, לְהִשְׁתַּחֲוֺת לְךָ, אָרְצָה.
And he recounted it to his father and to his brothers; and his father rebuked him and said to him, "What is this dream that you have dreamed? Shall we really come — I and your mother and your brothers — to bow down to you to the ground?"note
Y lo relató a su padre y a sus hermanos; y su padre lo reprendió y le dijo: «¿Qué sueño es este que has soñado? ¿De veras vendremos — yo y tu madre y tus hermanos — a inclinarnos ante ti hasta la tierra?»
11
וַיְקַנְאוּ-בוֹ, אֶחָיו; וְאָבִיו, שָׁמַר אֶת-הַדָּבָר.
And his brothers were jealous of him; but his father kept the matter.note
Y sus hermanos le tuvieron envidia; pero su padre guardó el asunto.
12
וַיֵּלְכוּ, אֶחָיו, לִרְעוֹת אֶת-צֹאן אֲבִיהֶם, בִּשְׁכֶם.
And his brothers went to shepherd their father's flock at Shechem.note
Y sus hermanos fueron a apacentar el rebaño de su padre en Siquem.
cross-refs⤷ 34:25
13
וַיֹּאמֶר יִשְׂרָאֵל אֶל-יוֹסֵף, הֲלוֹא אַחֶיךָ רֹעִים בִּשְׁכֶם--לְכָה, וְאֶשְׁלָחֲךָ אֲלֵיהֶם; וַיֹּאמֶר לוֹ, הִנֵּנִי.
And Israel said to Joseph, "Are your brothers not shepherding at Shechem? Come, and I will send you to them." And he said to him, "Here I am."note
Y dijo Israel a José: «¿No están tus hermanos apacentando en Siquem? Ven, y te enviaré a ellos». Y él le dijo: «Heme aquí».
cross-refs⤷ 22:1
14
וַיֹּאמֶר לוֹ, לֶךְ-נָא רְאֵה אֶת-שְׁלוֹם אַחֶיךָ וְאֶת-שְׁלוֹם הַצֹּאן, וַהֲשִׁבֵנִי, דָּבָר; וַיִּשְׁלָחֵהוּ מֵעֵמֶק חֶבְרוֹן, וַיָּבֹא שְׁכֶמָה.
And he said to him, "Go now, see whether your brothers are at peace, and whether the flock is, and bring me back word." So he sent him from the valley of Hebron, and he came to Shechem.note
Y le dijo: «Ve ahora, mira si tus hermanos están en paz, y si lo está el rebaño, y tráeme respuesta». Y lo envió del valle de Hebrón, y llegó a Siquem.
15
וַיִּמְצָאֵהוּ אִישׁ, וְהִנֵּה תֹעֶה בַּשָּׂדֶה; וַיִּשְׁאָלֵהוּ הָאִישׁ לֵאמֹר, מַה-תְּבַקֵּשׁ.
And a man found him, and look — he was wandering in the field; and the man asked him, "What are you looking for?"note
Y lo halló un hombre, y mira — andaba errante por el campo; y el hombre le preguntó: «¿Qué buscas?»
16
וַיֹּאמֶר, אֶת-אַחַי אָנֹכִי מְבַקֵּשׁ; הַגִּידָה-נָּא לִי, אֵיפֹה הֵם רֹעִים.
And he said, "I am looking for my brothers. Tell me, please, where they are shepherding."note
Y él dijo: «Busco a mis hermanos. Dime, por favor, dónde están apacentando».
17
וַיֹּאמֶר הָאִישׁ, נָסְעוּ מִזֶּה--כִּי שָׁמַעְתִּי אֹמְרִים, נֵלְכָה דֹּתָיְנָה; וַיֵּלֶךְ יוֹסֵף אַחַר אֶחָיו, וַיִּמְצָאֵם בְּדֹתָן.
And the man said, "They have moved on from here, for I heard them saying, 'Let us go to Dothan.'" And Joseph went after his brothers and found them at Dothan.note
Y el hombre dijo: «Se han ido de aquí, porque los oí decir: "Vamos a Dotán"». Y José fue tras sus hermanos y los halló en Dotán.
18
וַיִּרְאוּ אֹתוֹ, מֵרָחֹק; וּבְטֶרֶם יִקְרַב אֲלֵיהֶם, וַיִּתְנַכְּלוּ אֹתוֹ לַהֲמִיתוֹ.
And they saw him from far off, and before he came near to them, they schemed against him to put him to death.note
Y lo vieron desde lejos, y antes de que se acercara a ellos, tramaron contra él para darle muerte.
19
וַיֹּאמְרוּ, אִישׁ אֶל-אָחִיו: הִנֵּה, בַּעַל הַחֲלֹמוֹת הַלָּזֶה--בָּא.
And they said, each to his brother, "Look — this master of dreams is coming!note
Y se dijeron cada uno a su hermano: «Mira — ahí viene este señor de los sueños.
20
וְעַתָּה לְכוּ וְנַהַרְגֵהוּ, וְנַשְׁלִכֵהוּ בְּאַחַד הַבֹּרוֹת, וְאָמַרְנוּ, חַיָּה רָעָה אֲכָלָתְהוּ; וְנִרְאֶה, מַה-יִּהְיוּ חֲלֹמֹתָיו.
And now, come, let us kill him and throw him into one of the pits, and we will say, 'An evil beast has devoured him.' And we shall see what becomes of his dreams."note
Y ahora, vengan, matémoslo y echémoslo en una de las cisternas, y diremos: "Una mala bestia lo devoró". Y veremos en qué paran sus sueños».
21
וַיִּשְׁמַע רְאוּבֵן, וַיַּצִּלֵהוּ מִיָּדָם; וַיֹּאמֶר, לֹא נַכֶּנּוּ נָפֶשׁ.
And Reuben heard, and rescued him from their hand, and said, "Let us not strike down a life."note
Y lo oyó Rubén, y lo libró de sus manos, y dijo: «No hiramos de muerte una vida».
22
וַיֹּאמֶר אֲלֵהֶם רְאוּבֵן, אַל-תִּשְׁפְּכוּ-דָם--הַשְׁלִיכוּ אֹתוֹ אֶל-הַבּוֹר הַזֶּה אֲשֶׁר בַּמִּדְבָּר, וְיָד אַל-תִּשְׁלְחוּ-בוֹ: לְמַעַן, הַצִּיל אֹתוֹ מִיָּדָם, לַהֲשִׁיבוֹ, אֶל-אָבִיו.
And Reuben said to them, "Do not shed blood! Throw him into this pit that is in the wilderness, but do not lay a hand on him" — so that he might rescue him from their hand, to bring him back to his father.note
Y les dijo Rubén: «¡No derramen sangre! Échenlo en esta cisterna que está en el desierto, pero no le pongan la mano encima» — para librarlo de sus manos y devolverlo a su padre.
23
וַיְהִי, כַּאֲשֶׁר-בָּא יוֹסֵף אֶל-אֶחָיו; וַיַּפְשִׁיטוּ אֶת-יוֹסֵף אֶת-כֻּתָּנְתּוֹ, אֶת-כְּתֹנֶת הַפַּסִּים אֲשֶׁר עָלָיו.
And so it was, when Joseph came to his brothers, that they stripped Joseph of his tunic — the long ornamented tunic that was on him —note
Y así fue que, cuando José llegó a sus hermanos, lo despojaron de su túnica — la túnica larga y ornamentada que llevaba puesta —
24
וַיִּקָּחֻהוּ--וַיַּשְׁלִכוּ אֹתוֹ, הַבֹּרָה; וְהַבּוֹר רֵק, אֵין בּוֹ מָיִם.
and they took him and threw him into the pit; and the pit was empty — there was no water in it.note
y lo tomaron y lo echaron en la cisterna; y la cisterna estaba vacía — no había agua en ella.
25
וַיֵּשְׁבוּ, לֶאֱכָל-לֶחֶם, וַיִּשְׂאוּ עֵינֵיהֶם וַיִּרְאוּ, וְהִנֵּה אֹרְחַת יִשְׁמְעֵאלִים בָּאָה מִגִּלְעָד; וּגְמַלֵּיהֶם נֹשְׂאִים, נְכֹאת וּצְרִי וָלֹט--הוֹלְכִים, לְהוֹרִיד מִצְרָיְמָה.
And they sat down to eat bread; and they lifted their eyes and looked, and look — a caravan of Ishmaelites coming from Gilead, their camels bearing gum and balm and ladanum, going to bring them down to Egypt.note
Y se sentaron a comer pan; y alzaron los ojos y miraron, y mira — una caravana de ismaelitas venía de Galaad, con sus camellos cargados de goma, bálsamo y ládano, y bajaban para llevarlo a Egipto.
26
וַיֹּאמֶר יְהוּדָה, אֶל-אֶחָיו: מַה-בֶּצַע, כִּי נַהֲרֹג אֶת-אָחִינוּ, וְכִסִּינוּ, אֶת-דָּמוֹ.
And Judah said to his brothers, "What gain is it if we kill our brother and cover his blood?note
Y dijo Judá a sus hermanos: «¿Qué ganancia hay en que matemos a nuestro hermano y cubramos su sangre?
cross-refs⤷ Jer 22:17⤷ 4:10
27
לְכוּ וְנִמְכְּרֶנּוּ לַיִּשְׁמְעֵאלִים, וְיָדֵנוּ אַל-תְּהִי-בוֹ, כִּי-אָחִינוּ בְשָׂרֵנוּ, הוּא; וַיִּשְׁמְעוּ, אֶחָיו.
Come, let us sell him to the Ishmaelites, and let our hand not be on him — for he is our brother, our flesh." And his brothers listened.note
Vengan, vendámoslo a los ismaelitas, y no esté nuestra mano sobre él — porque es nuestro hermano, nuestra carne». Y sus hermanos lo escucharon.
28
וַיַּעַבְרוּ אֲנָשִׁים מִדְיָנִים סֹחֲרִים, וַיִּמְשְׁכוּ וַיַּעֲלוּ אֶת-יוֹסֵף מִן-הַבּוֹר, וַיִּמְכְּרוּ אֶת-יוֹסֵף לַיִּשְׁמְעֵאלִים, בְּעֶשְׂרִים כָּסֶף; וַיָּבִיאוּ אֶת-יוֹסֵף, מִצְרָיְמָה.
And Midianite men, traders, passed by; and they drew Joseph up and lifted him out of the pit, and sold Joseph to the Ishmaelites for twenty pieces of silver. And they brought Joseph to Egypt.note
Y pasaron unos hombres madianitas, mercaderes; y sacaron y subieron a José de la cisterna, y vendieron a José a los ismaelitas por veinte piezas de plata. Y llevaron a José a Egipto.
29
וַיָּשָׁב רְאוּבֵן אֶל-הַבּוֹר, וְהִנֵּה אֵין-יוֹסֵף בַּבּוֹר; וַיִּקְרַע, אֶת-בְּגָדָיו.
And Reuben returned to the pit, and look — Joseph was not in the pit! And he tore his clothes.note
Y volvió Rubén a la cisterna, y mira — ¡José no estaba en la cisterna! Y rasgó sus vestidos.
30
וַיָּשָׁב אֶל-אֶחָיו, וַיֹּאמַר: הַיֶּלֶד אֵינֶנּוּ, וַאֲנִי אָנָה אֲנִי-בָא.
And he returned to his brothers and said, "The boy is not there! And I — where can I go?"note
Y volvió a sus hermanos y dijo: «¡El muchacho no está! Y yo — ¿adónde iré?»
31
וַיִּקְחוּ, אֶת-כְּתֹנֶת יוֹסֵף; וַיִּשְׁחֲטוּ שְׂעִיר עִזִּים, וַיִּטְבְּלוּ אֶת-הַכֻּתֹּנֶת בַּדָּם.
And they took Joseph's tunic, and slaughtered a male goat, and dipped the tunic in the blood.note
Y tomaron la túnica de José, y degollaron un macho cabrío, y mojaron la túnica en la sangre.
cross-refs⤷ 27:16
32
וַיְשַׁלְּחוּ אֶת-כְּתֹנֶת הַפַּסִּים, וַיָּבִיאוּ אֶל-אֲבִיהֶם, וַיֹּאמְרוּ, זֹאת מָצָאנוּ: הַכֶּר-נָא, הַכְּתֹנֶת בִּנְךָ הִוא--אִם-לֹא.
And they sent the long ornamented tunic and brought it to their father and said, "This we found. Recognize, please — is it your son's tunic, or not?"note
Y enviaron la túnica larga y ornamentada, y la trajeron a su padre y dijeron: «Esto hemos hallado. Reconoce, por favor — ¿es la túnica de tu hijo, o no?»
cross-refs⤷ 27:23

Translator's Notes

Verses 1–4 · the generations that are a story, and the tunic

A settler in a land of un-settledness. The chapter opens with a deliberate contrast to the one just closed. Esau "settled" (vayyeshev) in the hill country of Seir — land he owns (36:8). Jacob "settled" too, but in "the land of his father's sojournings" — megurim, the noun from ger, "resident alien." He settles in a land of not-settling. It is the same ache the book has been pressing since Abraham buried Sarah in the only plot he ever owned (23:4): the brother who took a different country has a home; the brother who has the promise has a lease.

A "generations" formula with no genealogy under it. "These are the toledot of Jacob" — and then, instead of the roll of names the formula has produced nine times before, comes a boy of seventeen and a grudge. This is the last of the book's structural headings and the one that breaks its own pattern: Jacob's "generations" are not a list, they are a plot. Genesis has been narrowing all along — clearing the side line before following the chosen one, Ishmael before Isaac, Esau before Jacob — and here the narrowing finishes and the book simply becomes a story, running unbroken now to its last verse.

The report, and a word with a future. Joseph brings his father dibbatam ra'ah — their bad report. Dibbah is not neutral news; it is the word for a whispering campaign, and the Bible uses it of the ten spies who bring back "an evil report of the land" that costs a generation the promise (Numbers 13:32), and of Jeremiah hearing "the whispering of many" from the men plotting his death (Jeremiah 20:10). The text does not say whether the report was true. It says what it did.

The most famous garment in the Bible, and nobody knows what it was. Ketonet passim is two words: a ketonet is a tunic (the same garment God makes for the man and his wife in 3:21), and passim is a puzzle. The KJV's beloved "coat of many colours" is inherited, not translated: the Septuagint guessed poikilos, "many-hued," the Vulgate followed with tunica polymita, "a many-threaded coat," and the Douay — translating that Latin — has "a coat of divers colours." But Hebrew pas elsewhere means the flat of the hand or foot (it is the word in Daniel's court for the palm of a hand that writes on a wall, Daniel 5:5), which yields a tunic reaching to the extremities — long-sleeved and ankle-length, the robe of someone who plainly is not going to be doing any work. The ASV keeps "many colours" in the line but concedes "a long garment with sleeves" in its margin; the NWT reads "a long striped garment"; the NIV has settled on "an ornate robe" and notes the meaning is uncertain; the TLB simply calls it "a brightly colored coat." The one hard clue is that the phrase occurs in only one other story in scripture: it is what Tamar, David's daughter, is wearing — "for so were the virgin daughters of the king robed" — when her brother rapes her and she tears it (2 Samuel 13:18-19). Both garments mark royal favor; both end torn in a story about a violated sibling. This translation reads Mister "a long ornamented tunic" — a garment of rank, with the doubt flagged rather than painted over. What matters to the plot is not its color but its visibility: you can see it coming from far off (v. 18).

The word they could not say. "They could not speak to him in peace" — le-shalom. Most versions smooth it to an adverb (KJV and ASV "speak peaceably unto him"), which is good English and loses the noun. Keep the noun, because the plot turns on it: in eleven verses their father will send Joseph out to inspect their shalom (v. 14). The errand is named with the very word they cannot bring themselves to speak.

Verses 5–11 · two dreams, and a father who says nothing

Told twice, because it is settled. Joseph dreams the same message in two registers — agricultural, then astronomical — and Genesis itself will later supply the rule for reading that: "the dream was doubled to Pharaoh because the thing is established by God, and God is hastening to do it" (41:32). The doubling is not adolescent repetition; it is the text's own signature for certainty. Everything the brothers do to stop these dreams becomes the machinery that fulfills them — they will bow to him, in Egypt, because they sold him there.

"Will you really be king over us?" The brothers answer with two Hebrew infinitive absolutes stacked for scorn — hamalokh timlokh… im-mashol timshol, "reigning will you reign… ruling will you rule?" And their second verb is a loaded one: mashal is the verb God used over the woman in the garden ("he will rule over you," 3:16) and over Cain's crouching sin ("you must rule over it," 4:7). It has been, from the beginning, a word about who is on top of whom — and the brothers spit it back as an accusation. They have understood the dream exactly. That is precisely why they hate it.

The mother who is not there. Jacob's rebuke assumes a cast of three: "I and your mother and your brothers." But Rachel died two chapters ago, on the road to Bethlehem (35:19). Readers have handled the wrinkle in three ways: Rashi took Jacob to be arguing from the impossible ("your mother is dead — so much for your dream"); others read Bilhah, the foster-mother who raised Joseph after Rachel died, as the "mother" meant; others take the sun, moon and eleven stars as a fixed astronomical figure — a complete household, schematic rather than a headcount. The text does not choose, and neither does this translation.

Two reactions, and only one of them is spoken. "His brothers were jealous of him; but his father kept the matter" — shamar et-hadavar, the verb for guarding, watching, keeping a charge. Jacob rebukes the boy out loud and files the dream away in silence. It is the first hint that the father half-believes it. Luke will use the identical gesture, in Greek, of another parent holding a son's inexplicable destiny without comment: "his mother kept all these sayings in her heart" (Luke 2:51). The KJV renders our line "observed the saying," the NIV "kept the matter in mind," the NWT "kept the saying" — all reaching for the same restraint.

Verses 12–17 · sent to Shechem — and a stranger in a field

Of all the places to pasture a flock. The brothers are grazing "at Shechem" — the town where Simeon and Levi killed every male and plundered the city three chapters ago (34:25-29), and after which Jacob said in plain terror, "you have made me a stench to the inhabitants of the land… I am few in number, and they will gather against me and strike me" (34:30). They have gone straight back to the scene. The text offers no comment at all — it simply puts the name there and lets the reader remember. And into that place the father sends his favorite, alone, unarmed.

"Here I am." Joseph's single word of reply is hineni — the answer of total availability that this book has used at exactly one other hinge: the Aqedah, where Abraham says it three times, and where a father takes the beloved son, the son he loves, up a mountain to kill him (22:1, 22:7, 22:11). Here a father sends a beloved son on a journey the reader already knows will end with his clothes soaked in blood and brought back to that father. The difference is the one that matters: at Moriah a voice from heaven stopped the knife. In this chapter heaven says nothing whatever. God is not named once in Genesis 37 — the only chapter of the Joseph story where the narrator lets him disappear entirely, exactly where you would most want him.

The errand. "See whether your brothers are at shalom" — the word from verse 4, now placed in the father's mouth as an innocent question. He is asking the one thing that has already been answered.

The man in the field. Joseph is to'eh — "wandering," straying, lost; it is the participle used of a sheep that has strayed, and of Hagar "wandering in the wilderness of Beersheba" (21:14). And then, for three verses, the story stops for an anonymous stranger. He has no name, no lineage, and no function except to redirect the plot: without his overheard scrap of conversation Joseph goes home, and there is no Egypt, no famine relief, no exodus. Jewish tradition, uneasy with an accident that large, identified him with the angel Gabriel (so Rashi, following the Midrash). The Hebrew says only ish, "a man." This translation leaves him a man, because the plainness is the point: the whole future of the family turns on a passerby who happened to be listening.

Dothan. A real place — Tell Dothan, about thirteen miles north of Shechem, excavated in the 1950s and 60s and occupied continuously through the Bronze and Iron Ages. It sits on the caravan road running from Gilead down toward the coast and Egypt, which is exactly why a spice caravan turns up in verse 25. It is also where Elisha's servant will one day wake to find the hills full of horses and chariots of fire (2 Kings 6:13-17) — the same town, and the other kind of morning.

Verses 18–24 · the plot, the dream-lord, and the empty pit

Scheming, in a family that has done this before. Vayyitnakkelu — "they schemed, dealt craftily" — is a rare and ugly verb. This household has a long record with cunning: Jacob took the blessing be-mirmah, "with deceit" (27:35); Laban paid him back with the same word at the wedding (29:25); Jacob's own sons answered Shechem "with deceit" (34:13). It has passed down like an inheritance, and now it is aimed at the family's own child.

"This master of dreams." Ba'al ha-chalomot ha-lazeh — literally "the owner of the dreams, this one here." Ba'al means possessor or proprietor; ha-lazeh is a demonstrative with a curl of contempt in it, roughly "this specimen." The KJV flattens it to "this dreamer," the NIV to "here comes that dreamer," the Geneva to "beholde, this dreamer commeth" — all readable, all a little too polite. The Hebrew is a sneer at a job title, and this translation keeps it: Mister "this master of dreams."

The lie they invent, and will hear again. "We will say, 'An evil beast has devoured him'" — and thirteen verses later the father says it back to them, word for word, as his own conclusion (v. 33). They do not even have to tell the lie; they only have to leave the coat where it will do the work, and the grieving man supplies the sentence himself. The text sets the two lines close enough that the reader hears the echo and the brothers, standing there, must hear it too.

Reuben's half-rescue. The firstborn intervenes — "let us not strike down a life" (nefesh, the same word as the "living creature" of the creation account) — but he intervenes by compromise: don't kill him, just throw him in a pit, and I will come back for him later. He never says "let him go." He is also, the reader remembers, the son who slept with his father's concubine (35:22) and has ground to make up; the narrator quietly gives us his private motive — "to bring him back to his father" — and then arranges for him to be absent at the one moment it mattered. The rescue attempt that talks instead of acting is one of the most recognizable failures in the book.

"And the pit was empty; there was no water in it." A famously redundant sentence — if it is empty, of course there is no water — and one the tradition has never been able to leave alone. The Talmud draws the grim practical inference: no water, but the reader should not assume nothing was in it (Shabbat 22a). The plainer reading is that the second clause is the narrator making sure we understand what kind of hole this is: a dry cistern, a bottle-shaped cut in the rock, smooth-sided, several men deep, impossible to climb out of. He is not in danger of drowning. He is in a stone jar. And the brothers, verse 25, sit down beside it to have lunch.

Verses 25–30 · what gain? — and the sale nobody can fully untangle

A caravan of cousins. The traders who come over the hill are Ishmaelites — descendants of Ishmael, Abraham's other son, the boy who was also put out into a wilderness by a family that preferred the younger brother (21:14). Their camels carry nekhot, tsori, va-lot — gum tragacanth, balm, and ladanum: resins, the luxury goods of the incense road. This is the freight of the Gilead-to-Egypt trade, and Jacob will one day send the very same three items back down that road as a gift to the Egyptian official he does not know is his son (43:11). The reader is watching a boy leave in a cargo he will later be sent presents in.

"What gain is it?" Judah's opening word is betsa — not neutral profit but the loaded noun for cut, take, unjust gain, the word the prophets use for graft. Jeremiah will aim it at a king of Judah whose "eyes and heart are on nothing but your unjust gain" (Jeremiah 22:17). Judah's proposal is often read as mercy — and it does save Joseph's life, and the brothers do stop short of murder — but the sentence he uses to sell it is an accountant's. He does not say "he is our brother, we cannot"; he says the killing has no margin in it. The KJV catches this exactly with "what profit is it," the NIV with "what will we gain"; the TLB's "what will we gain by killing him" is the same instinct in plainer clothes. Then he adds the pious clause — "for he is our brother, our flesh" — the language of kinship deployed as the closing argument for a sale.

And covering the blood. "…and cover his blood" — the phrase reaches back to the first murder in this book, where blood that was not covered cried out from the ground (4:10). Job says it as a prayer: "O earth, do not cover my blood" (Job 16:18). Judah's plan is precisely to make the ground keep quiet.

The seam this translation does not sand. Verse 28 is the most argued-over sentence in the chapter, and the difficulty is real: Midianite traders pass by; they draw Joseph out of the pit and sell him to the Ishmaelites. Grammatically the nearest subject for "drew" and "sold" is the Midianites, not the brothers — yet Joseph later tells his brothers flatly, "I am Joseph your brother, whom you sold into Egypt" (45:4). Then verse 36 has the Medanites — a third spelling — selling him to Potiphar, while 39:1 says Potiphar bought him "from the hand of the Ishmaelites." Two readings, both serious: (a) the standard source-critical account sees two tellings braided together — one in which Judah persuades his brothers to sell Joseph to Ishmaelites, another in which Reuben plans a rescue and passing Midianites steal the boy out of the pit before the brothers can act, which would make the brothers guilty of the intent and not the transaction. (b) The harmonizing reading treats the two names as overlapping terms for the same caravan traders — and it has one genuinely strong piece of evidence: Judges 8 tells of an army explicitly called Midianites, then explains their gold earrings with "because they were Ishmaelites" (Judges 8:24), using the words interchangeably in a single breath. On that reading the brothers pull Joseph up and sell him to a mixed caravan the text names loosely. The KJV and ASV render the ambiguity as it stands and leave the reader to it; the NIV quietly resolves it by supplying "his brothers pulled Joseph up." This translation keeps the Hebrew's unspecified "they," because the ambiguity is in the text and taking it out is a decision, not a translation.

Twenty pieces of silver. Not an arbitrary figure: Leviticus later fixes the valuation of a male between five and twenty years old at exactly twenty shekels (Leviticus 27:5). Joseph is seventeen. He is sold for the going institutional rate for a boy his age — the price is correct, and that is the horror of it. Readers have long heard the later rhyme in it, a descendant of Judah priced at thirty pieces (Zechariah 11:12; Matthew 26:15); Genesis makes nothing of it, and neither will we.

"And I — where can I go?" Reuben comes back to an empty hole and tears his clothes — the first of the chapter's two garment-tearings, and the reader should note that the second is his father's (v. 34), for the same boy, over a lie Reuben could have stopped. His cry is four Hebrew words and almost unpunctuatable: va-ani anah ani-va, "and I, where am I going?" It is not grief for Joseph. It is the eldest son calculating what this will cost him.

Verses 31–36 · "recognize, please" — the deceiver deceived, and Sheol

A goat, a garment, and a blind old man: it has happened before in this house. This is the chapter's masterstroke, and it is built entirely out of repeated props. To steal his brother's blessing, Jacob took his brother's garments and covered himself in the skins of goat kids, and went in to a father whose eyes were too dim to see (27:15-16). Now his sons take his favorite son's garment, slaughter a male goat, dip the one in the other's blood, and carry it to a father who cannot see what happened. The rabbis put it bluntly (Genesis Rabbah 84:19): with a kid he deceived his father, and with a kid he was deceived. Genesis never says it. It just hands him the goat.

The two most dangerous words in the family's vocabulary. "Haker-na — recognize, please." The verb is nakar, to recognize, identify, acknowledge — and it is the exact verb that failed at the earlier bedside: Isaac "did not recognize him, because his hands were hairy like his brother Esau's hands" (27:23). There the father failed to recognize a son who was present; here the father recognizes a son who is not. And the words come back one chapter later with terrible precision: when Judah — the brother who proposed the sale — is confronted by Tamar with the pledge he left her, she sends it to him with the identical phrase, "haker-na, recognize, please, whose these are" (38:25). The man who sent haker-na to his father receives haker-na from his daughter-in-law. That is why chapter 38, so often dismissed as an interruption of the Joseph story, sits exactly where it sits: it is the book's own comment on this scene.

The lie completes itself. The brothers ask a question and volunteer nothing — "this we found." They never say the word "beast." Jacob does, quoting their invention of verse 20 back at them unprompted, and then adds his own flourish: tarof toraf Yosef, "torn, torn is Joseph," another infinitive absolute — the same doubling-for-certainty the dreams used, now serving a falsehood. He is more certain than anyone in the chapter, and he is wrong.

Sackcloth, first worn here. Saq — coarse dark goat-hair cloth — makes its first appearance in the Bible in this verse, on Jacob, and will be the standard dress of grief and repentance for the whole rest of the canon, from Nineveh's king to the two witnesses of Revelation (Jonah 3:6; Revelation 11:3). The mourning garment enters scripture as a father's response to a lie.

The first Sheol. "I will go down to my son, mourning, to Sheol" — and this is the word's first occurrence in the Bible, which makes how we translate it a decision about a great deal more than one verse. Sheol is the Hebrew realm of the dead: down, dark, silent, and — crucially — where everyone goes, righteous and wicked alike; it is not a place of punishment, and the Hebrew Bible has no other word to set against it. The KJV renders it here "the grave," but renders the same word "hell" in thirty-one other places and "the pit" in three — so an English reader of the KJV cannot tell that Jacob, Job, David and Isaiah are all talking about one thing, and would reasonably conclude that Jacob expects to descend to his son in the grave while the wicked go somewhere else. That is not a small distortion; it is a whole doctrine assembled out of an inconsistent gloss. The Douay, following the Vulgate's infernum, has "into hell" right here — the strangest sentence on the shelf, since it has a patriarch expecting to join his beloved son there. The ASV and the NWT both do the honest thing and simply transliterate — "Sheol" — refusing to decide with a translation what the Hebrew leaves undecided, and this translation follows them on the merits. It is the same principle as "vault" and "side": where the familiar English word imports an idea the Hebrew does not have, keep the Hebrew and explain it.

He refused to be comforted. Vayema'en lehitnachem — from nacham, the verb this book has been turning over since Lamech named his son Noah, "this one will bring us comfort" (5:29), and since God himself was said to nacham, to be sorry that he had made humankind (6:6). Here it is refused. And the refusal has one more life in it: Jeremiah will see Rachel — Joseph's own mother, buried on the Bethlehem road two chapters ago (35:19) — "weeping for her children, refusing to be comforted, because they are not" (Jeremiah 31:15), and Matthew will hear that verse again over the children of Bethlehem (Matthew 2:18). The mother refusing comfort and the father refusing comfort are the same family, in the same words, over the same son's line.

A court official, and a title with a smell of blood. The last verse hands Joseph to Potiphar, seris Par'oh, sar ha-tabbachim. Saris is the court-official word — it can mean a literal eunuch, and often does, though it is also used broadly of any high functionary (Potiphar has a wife, which is why translators divide: the KJV reads "an officer," the NWT "a court official"). Sar ha-tabbachim is stranger: tabbach is a butcher or slaughterer, so the phrase is literally "chief of the slaughterers" — which the versions render "captain of the guard" on the reasonable ground that a royal bodyguard's business is executions. This translation reads Mister "chief of the executioners," because that is what the Aramaic equivalent plainly means when the same office appears in Daniel's Babylon — Arioch, "chief of the executioners," who comes to kill the wise men (Daniel 2:14). And with that title the two stories line up: a Hebrew boy, taken young into a world empire, handed over to the chief official of a foreign king, who will read dreams nobody else can read and rise to stand second in the kingdom. Genesis has just written the opening of Daniel.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the words exact / distinct: "a long ornamented tunic" for ketonet passim, with the coat-of-many-colours pedigree given and the uncertainty flagged rather than painted over; "Sheol" left untranslated at its first appearance in the Bible (with the ASV and NWT, against the KJV's grave/hell/pit shuffle); "speak to him in peace" keeping shalom a noun so verse 14's errand can land on it; "bad report" for dibbah; "this master of dreams" for the brothers' sneer; "what gain" for Judah's betsa; "chief of the executioners" for sar ha-tabbachim; and verse 28's unspecified "they" left unspecified.

Echoes paid. Hineni — "Here I am" — from the Aqedah to another beloved son sent toward death, this time with no voice from heaven; toledot, the book's structural beam, used for the last time and broken open into a story; mashal, "rule," carried from 3:16 and 4:7 to the brothers' sneer; the goat and the garment of 27:15-16 turned back on Jacob; nakar, the verb that failed at Isaac's bedside, succeeding here on a bloody tunic; nacham, the comfort promised at Noah's naming, refused; Shechem, the killing-field of chapter 34, chosen as a pasture; and Judah's betsa answering Jeremiah's indictment of his royal line.

Echoes planted. Haker-na, "recognize, please" — returning in one chapter to trap Judah himself (38:25); the dreams that must be doubled "because the thing is established by God" (41:32); the gum, balm and ladanum going down to Egypt that will come back up as a gift (43:11); the brothers who will bow (42:6); Reuben's forfeited birthright and Judah's scepter (49:3-10); the sackcloth that will clothe every mourner from Nineveh to Revelation; Rachel refusing comfort (Jeremiah 31:15 → Matthew 2:18); and a Hebrew youth in a foreign court reading dreams — Daniel's whole book, sketched in this chapter's last line.

Next installment in this book: Genesis 38 — the story stops dead and turns to Judah: his Canaanite marriage, the deaths of Er and Onan, and Tamar at the roadside in a veil, who takes his seal, his cord and his staff, and sends them back with the two words he taught his father — "recognize, please."
