Translator’s Notes

Verses 1–3 · a forty-year word

The superscription frames the longest and most turbulent prophetic career in the Bible. Jeremiah’s word comes “in the thirteenth year of Josiah” (627 BC), runs on through the reign of Jehoiakim, and does not stop “until the eleventh year of Zedekiah… until the exile of Jerusalem, in the fifth month” (586 BC). Forty years, five kings, and one direction: down toward the fall. The book’s frame is a nation’s collapse, and the man appointed to say it was coming.

«Of the priests who were in Anathoth.» Jeremiah is a priest by birth. Anathoth — a Levitical town about three miles northeast of Jerusalem in the territory of Benjamin — was very likely the village to which Solomon banished the priest Abiathar (1 Kings 2:26). A son of a sidelined priestly line, from a small town, called to indict the temple and the throne it stood beside.

Verses 4–5 · known before the womb

«Before I formed you… I knew you.» The call reaches back before Jeremiah exists. “Formed” is yatsar — the potter’s verb, the word for God shaping the man from the dust (Genesis 2:7), and the very sign Jeremiah will later act out at the potter’s house (ch 18). “Knew” (yada) is not information but the intimate, covenant knowing that means chosen. And “set you apart” (qadash) — consecrated, devoted to a purpose — all before birth. KJV “I knew thee… I sanctified thee, and I ordained thee a prophet unto the nations.”

«A prophet to the nations.» Not to Judah only — goyim, the nations; Jeremiah’s oracles will reach Egypt, Moab, Ammon, Edom, and Babylon (chs 46–51). The scope is set in the first sentence God speaks to him. Paul heard his own calling in these very words — “he who set me apart before I was born” (Galatians 1:15).

Verses 6–8 · I am only a youth

«Ah, Lord Jehovah!» Jeremiah’s first word back to God is a groan (ahah) and the reluctant prophet’s objection: “I do not know how to speak, for I am only a youth (na’ar).” He joins a line — Moses “I am not eloquent,” Gideon “my clan is the weakest,” Isaiah “I am a man of unclean lips.” The called protest their inadequacy; it is always true, and it is never the point.

God does not argue the weakness — he overrides it with two commands and a promise. “Do not say ‘I am a youth.’” “Do not be afraid before them.” And the promise that will bookend the whole call: “I am with you to deliver you” (repeated word for word at v19). “The utterance of Jehovah” (ne’um YHWH) is the prophet’s signature stamp, sounded all through the book.

Verses 9–10 · the hand on the mouth, the six verbs

«He touched my mouth.» God stretches out his hand and touches Jeremiah’s lips — the commissioning gesture. Where Isaiah’s mouth was cleansed by a burning coal (Isaiah 6:7), Jeremiah’s is filled: “I have put my words in your mouth” — the mark of the true prophet, who speaks not his own message but God’s (Deuteronomy 18:18).

The six verbs. Verse 10 is the mission statement of the whole book: “to uproot and to tear down, to destroy and to overthrow, to build and to plant.” Four verbs of demolition, two of construction — and in that proportion. Jeremiah’s is mostly a ministry of tearing down (a doomed nation, warned to the very end), but it does not end there: “to build and to plant” is the seed of the hope that flowers in the new covenant (ch 31). The wrecking clears the ground for the planting. KJV “to root out, and to pull down, and to destroy, and to throw down, to build, and to plant.”

Verses 11–12 · the almond and the Watcher

A pun you can only half-carry into English. Jeremiah sees “a branch of an almond tree” — maqqel shaqed; and God answers, “You have seen well, for I am watching (shoqed) over my word to do it.” Shaqed (“almond”) and shoqed (“watching, wakeful”) are nearly the same sound. The almond is the first tree to wake and blossom in the Judean spring — the “watcher tree” — and God is awake over his word, watching to perform it. The vision’s whole point is certainty: what God has said, he is wakeful to do. ⚠ The KJV catches the wordplay differently — “I will hasten my word” — reading the root as speed rather than watchfulness; the ESV and most modern versions read “watching.”

Verses 13–16 · the pot from the north

The second vision. “A boiling pot, its face turned away from the north” — a cauldron tilting so its scalding contents pour down toward the south, toward Judah. “Out of the north the disaster (ra’ah) shall break loose.” The enemy from the north is Babylon, whose armies came down the fertile-crescent road from the north though Babylon itself lay to the east; their kings “set each his throne at the entrance of the gates of Jerusalem” — the picture of conquest, the invader holding court where the city once judged.

The reason, stated plainly (v16): “for all their evil — in forsaking me, and burning incense to other gods, and bowing down to the works of their own hands.” Idolatry is the root charge of the whole book: a people who traded the living God for gods they made with their hands. The judgment is not arbitrary; it is a marriage betrayed.

Verses 17–19 · an iron pillar

«Gird up your loins.» The charge to the prophet: tuck up your robe to work or run, stand, and speak everything — and “do not be shattered (chatat) before them, lest I shatter you before them.” The pun is a warning wrapped in a command: fear them, and you will be broken; the only safety is obedience. KJV “be not dismayed at their faces, lest I confound thee before them.”

«A fortified city, an iron pillar, bronze walls.» God makes the reluctant youth a fortress against the whole establishment — kings, princes, priests, and people alike. ⚠ Note carefully what the promise is and is not: “they will fight against you” — and they will (Jeremiah will be beaten, put in the stocks, thrown down a cistern, and left to die) — “but they will not prevail against you, for I am with you… to deliver you.” Not a life without attack, but a life that cannot be destroyed. The call closes on the very promise it opened with (v8): I am with you.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the exact / distinct words: “formed” for yatsar, the potter’s verb that will become a whole sign-act (ch 18); “knew” for yada and “set apart” for qadash, the covenant-and-consecration words of the prenatal call; “a youth” for na’ar, the reluctant prophet’s objection; the six verbs “uproot / tear down / destroy / overthrow / build / plant” kept four-to-two; “watching” for shoqed, held against the “almond” (shaqed) so the pun is at least visible; and “shattered” for chatat, kept the same in the warning “do not be shattered… lest I shatter you.”

Echoes paid. Yatsar forming the man from the dust (Genesis 2:7); the reluctant-prophet line of Moses, Gideon, and Isaiah; the mouth touched, as Isaiah’s was by the coal (Isaiah 6), and filled as the prophet-like-Moses is (Deuteronomy 18:18); the “enemy from the north” that will be named Babylon; and the indictment — forsaking Jehovah for other gods — that the tariff of the last kings turns on (Jer 22:9).

Echoes planted. The six verbs, the program of the whole book, whose two hopeful ones flower in the new covenant (ch 31); the promise “I am with you to deliver you,” which bookends this call (vv8, 19) and is the rope Jeremiah clings to in his darkest confession (20:11); and the iron pillar made of a frightened boy — a man who will be beaten, jailed, and thrown in a cistern, and never destroyed.

This library already carries Jeremiah 20–22 (Pashhur and the stocks, the final siege, the tariff of the last kings). Next in sequence: Jeremiah 2 — the great opening indictment, “my people have committed two evils: they have forsaken me, the fountain of living waters, and hewn out for themselves broken cisterns that can hold no water.”
