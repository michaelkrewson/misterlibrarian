Sarah dies at Hebron; Abraham buys the cave of Machpelah — the first parcel of the Promised Land is a grave.
AM 2085 · c. 1859 BC traditional — Sarah dies at 127, thirty-seven years after Isaac's birth (Isaac is now 37). The purchase gives the patriarchs their one indisputable foothold in Canaan; the tomb at Hebron is venerated to this day.

Translator's Notes — verse by verse

Each note explains this translation's choice and compares the seven versions on the shelf. Quotations from copyrighted versions (NIV, TLB, NWT) are kept to short phrases for comparison; KJV, Geneva, Douay-Rheims and ASV are public domain.

Verses 1–6 · גֵּר-וְתוֹשָׁב אָנֹכִי ger-vetoshav anokhi

The only matriarch whose age and death the Torah records. Sarah dies at 127 — scripture gives no other woman's lifespan — and the chapter opens the parashah the tradition calls Chayei Sarah, "the Life of Sarah," a title that names a death. She dies at Kiriath-arba ("town of Arba," a giant of the Anakim, or "town of the four") — the narrator adds, as he does with Ur and the Philistines, the name the reader knows: "that is, Hebron." And Abraham "came to lament for Sarah and to weep for her" — two distinct words this translation keeps apart: sapad, the formal, public mourning-rite (the eulogy, the wailing), and bakah, plain tears. The Masoretes even wrote the kaf of "weep" (velivkotah) as a miniature letter in the scroll — a scribal whisper the sages read as private grief, the tears kept small behind the public lament.

"A resident alien and a settler." Abraham's self-description is two precise legal words, and the shelf mostly blurs them: ger and toshav (KJV "a stranger and a sojourner"; NWT "an alien resident and a settler," keeping them two). A ger is a resident foreigner — living among a people, protected, but with no citizenship and no right to own land; a toshav is a settled outsider, a step more rooted, still landless. That is the whole problem of the chapter stated in a breath: the man to whom God has promised all this land as an everlasting holding (17:8) cannot legally bury his wife in it — he owns not one square foot. And the word he uses for what he needs is loaded: achuzzah, a holding — the permanent, inheritable land-possession (from achaz, "to grasp, hold fast"), the very term 17:8 used for the promised land itself. The sons of Heth answer with Eastern courtesy and real respect: "you are a prince of God among us" — nesi Elohim (NWT "a chieftain of God"; KJV flattens the divine name to a superlative, "a mighty prince"). Both readings live in the phrase — "a prince of God" and "a godlike, mighty prince" — and this translation keeps the literal one, since the whole point is that these Hittites can see whose man Abraham is.

Verses 7–16 · בְּכֶסֶף מָלֵא … עֹבֵר לַסֹּחֵר bekhesef male … over lassocher

A masterclass in Near-Eastern bargaining. Every move here is ritual, and to a modern ear it reads like theater — because it is. Abraham bows (twice), speaks only through intermediaries at first, and insists on paying full silver (kesef male, v9) — no gift, no debt, no lien a future heir could contest. Ephron plays his part with lavish generosity: "the field I give you, and the cave — I give it you!" (v11) — said three times, in public, at the city gate. It is not an offer to donate; it is the opening of a negotiation, the seller's flourish before the number. And the number, when it slips out, is dressed as a trifle between friends: "land worth four hundred shekels of silver — between me and you, what is that?" (v15). Four hundred shekels was a small fortune (Jeremiah later buys a whole field for seventeen, 32:9); Ephron names a steep price with a shrug, and Abraham, who wanted no favors, pays it without a word of haggling. He "weighed out" the silver — coinage was centuries away; you cut and weighed metal — "four hundred shekels, at the merchant's standard" (over lassocher, KJV "current money with the merchant"): full weight, trade-grade, no thumb on the scale. The scene is a legal record disguised as a courtesy.

Verses 17–20 · וַיָּקָם שְׂדֵה עֶפְרוֹן vayyaqom sedeh Efron

The deed, itemized — and the first foothold. The transfer is recorded with a surveyor's care: the field, the cave, "and every tree in the field, within all its border round about" — Hittite and Mesopotamian land-deeds itemized the trees exactly so, because trees were taxable and disputable. The verb for what happened to the property is a legal one: it "rose" — vayyaqom — meaning it stood confirmed, was made over, passed valid title (KJV "were made sure"; the same root that says a covenant is "established"). Witnessed by the whole assembly "at the gate of his city" — the ancient courthouse — it is a transaction no one could ever overturn. And that is the quiet thunder of this chapter: the man promised an entire land dies owning a single field with a cave in it — a grave. The whole inheritance of Israel, in Abraham's lifetime, comes down to Machpelah ("the double" — from kaphal, "to fold, to double") — the first legally-held parcel of the Promised Land, and the down payment on all the rest. It becomes the patriarchal tomb: Abraham himself, Isaac and Rebekah, Jacob and Leah are all laid here (25:9; 49:31; 50:13), and the Herodian enclosure over the cave at Hebron still stands, one of the oldest continuously-venerated sites on earth. Faith buries its dead in the ground of the promise — and holds the receipt.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the words distinct: "a resident alien and a settler" for ger and toshav (with the NWT, against KJV's merged "stranger and sojourner") — two different legal statuses, both landless; "lament" and "weep" for sapad and bakah (the public rite and the private tears); and "a prince of God" for nesi Elohim (with the NWT's "chieftain of God," against KJV's "mighty prince"), keeping the divine name the Hittites actually spoke.

Where it goes its own way: "a holding" for achuzzah (KJV "possession"), to let it ring against 17:8's promised holding; "was made over" for the legal vayyaqom (KJV "were made sure"); "at the merchant's standard" for over lassocher (KJV "current money with the merchant"); and numerals for Sarah's 127 years.

Echoes paid and planted. Paid: achuzzah, the "everlasting holding" God promised at (17:8) — and here, in Abraham's lifetime, a burial holding is all of it he owns; Mamre, the oaks where Abraham pitched his tent (13:18), now facing the family tomb; "I will make your name great" (12:2), visible in the Hittites' "prince of God"; and the land promised "to your seed" (12:7), of which this single field is the first legal parcel. Planted: Machpelah, the patriarchal tomb (Isaac and Jacob will come here); Rebekah, named two chapters back, whom the next chapter goes to fetch for the now-motherless Isaac; and Hebron, which will be Israel's first royal capital under David.
