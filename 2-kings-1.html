Samaria, in the two-year reign of Ahaziah son of Ahab — a king injured by a fall who sends to a Philistine god for a prognosis, and the last appearance of Elijah before he is taken up.
c. 852 BC. ⚠ The verse that dates the succession (1:17, 'the second year of Jehoram son of Jehoshaphat') does not sit easily with the synchronisms at 3:1 and 8:16 — a small sample of the largest technical problem in Kings, whose cross-dated regnal notices are the backbone of Old Testament chronology and do not add up on a plain reading.

Translator’s Notes

Verses 1–2 · a book that starts in the middle — and the lord of the flies

⚠ This chapter is the clearest proof that 1 and 2 Kings are one book. In the Hebrew Bible Melakhim is a single scroll; the division is the Greek Septuagint’s, made for length, and it fell here — in the middle of a reign. Ahaziah became king at the end of 1 Kings 22; he falls through his lattice in the second verse of 2 Kings and is dead by the seventeenth. A reader who starts a “new book” here is starting midway through somebody’s obituary.

And the very first sentence belongs to a different story. “Moab rebelled against Israel after the death of Ahab” is dropped in and then abandoned; the Moabite war is not taken up until chapter 3. It reads like a note pinned to the front of the file — KJV, ASV and NIV all print it as a flat standalone sentence, because that is what it is — and it is worth knowing that the Moabite side of this same revolt survives independently, inscribed on the Mesha Stele (the Moabite Stone, found in 1868), where King Mesha gives his own account of throwing off Israel and credits his god Chemosh. It is one of the few places where a party on the other side of a biblical war left us a statement.

«Fell through the lattice.» Sevakhah is a mesh or network — the wooden screen closing the upper chamber, the cool room on the roof where a king would sit. He leans on it and it gives way. KJV “fell down through a lattice in his upper chamber”; NIV “fell through the lattice of his upper room.” The book of Kings opens its second half the way it opened its first: with a royal body that has stopped working.

⚠ «Baal-zebub the god of Ekron» — and the joke is almost certainly in the vowels. Ba’al Zevuv means, flatly, “lord of flies.” No people is likely to have worshiped a god under that name. What the Ugaritic texts do attest, several centuries earlier, is zbl b’l — “Baal the Prince,” zevul meaning prince or exalted dwelling. The Hebrew looks like a deliberate one-consonant slander: Prince Baal becomes Fly-lord. The strongest evidence is that the New Testament, written in Greek centuries later, still knows the un-mocked form: it reads Beelzeboul, with the L, as a name for the prince of the demons (Matthew 12:24; Mark 3:22) — which is how a Philistine city god ends up, by way of a Hebrew pun, as a name for the devil in English literature.

Ekron is one of the five Philistine cities, and the nearest of them to Samaria. A king of Israel sending a delegation there for a medical prognosis is not merely impious; it is a public statement about where authority lies, and everyone on the road would have seen them go.

Verses 3–8 · messengers intercepted by a Messenger, and a question asked three times

The pun that organizes the scene is invisible in English. The king sends mal’akhim — messengers. And the mal’akh of Jehovah — the same word, which English renders “angel” — sends Elijah to head them off. The king’s messengers are intercepted by God’s Messenger, and they never reach Ekron at all. We print “messenger” in both places so the collision is visible; KJV, ASV and NIV all print “angel” for one and “messengers” for the other, and the joke is gone.

⚠ «Is it because there is no God in Israel?» The question is asked three times, word for word (vv 3, 6, 16) — once by the angel, once repeated back by the messengers, once delivered to the king’s face. That is the indictment, and note how it is framed: not Baal-zebub is powerless, and not you have broken a commandment. It is is there nobody home? The charge is that the king of Israel has behaved as though his own country were vacant.

«The bed to which you have GONE UP — you shall not COME DOWN from it.» Up and down run the whole chapter, and this translation keeps every one of them in the same words: Elijah is told to go up; the messengers come up and turn back; Elijah sits on the top of a mountain; three captains come up; three times a king orders him come down; fire comes down; the angel tells him to go down; and the king who went up to bed never comes down from it. It is worth carrying into the next chapter, where Elijah goes up in a whirlwind and does not come down at all.

«What was the manner of the man?» Mishpat here is not “judgment” but form, custom, look. KJV “What manner of man was he?” is exactly right and has passed into the language.

⚠ «A man possessed of hair» — and the Hebrew genuinely will not decide. Ish ba’al se’ar can mean a hairy man (so KJV “an hairy man”, ASV the same) or a man wearing a hair garment (so NIV “with a garment of hair”). Both are defensible; we keep the Hebrew’s own noncommittal phrasing. Either way, the leather belt makes it an identification: the king needs only the description to name the man. And this outfit — rough hair, leather belt at the waist — is copied exactly, seven or eight centuries later, onto John the Baptist (Mark 1:6; Matthew 3:4). It is a costume, and it is a claim: the man in it is saying whose successor he is.

Verses 9–12 · a pun, and a hundred and two dead men

⚠ The exchange turns on two Hebrew words that are one letter apart. The captain addresses Elijah by his title: ish ha-Elohim, man of God. Elijah answers, “if I am a man of God (ish), let fire (esh) come down.” Ish and esh — the man and the fire — are near-identical in sound, and verse 12 fuses them outright: what falls is esh Elohim, the fire of God. The captains use the title as a formula, the way one says your excellency; Elijah takes it literally, and the pun is the argument. (The same phrase, “the fire of God,” falls from the sky in Job 1:16, where a messenger uses it of a disaster and has no idea what he is saying.)

The text does distinguish the three captains, and it is careful about it. The first arrives and issues an order: the king has said, come down. The second issues it faster and harder: come down QUICKLY. Fifty armed men each time, sent to bring in a prophet. The third kneels. The narrator is not describing three identical men struck at random; he is describing an escalating attempt to compel a prophet by force, and then a man who stops trying.

⚠ And it should be said plainly that this is one of the hardest passages in the prophets. A hundred and two men die, most of them soldiers under orders, and the book offers no word of comment. Readers have taken it several ways — as a contest over who commands whom, in which a king who has already been told he will die keeps sending troops; as a story shaped, like much of the Elijah cycle, in the idiom of legend rather than chronicle; and as a passage many readers of every era have simply found indefensible. The most interesting response to it is inside the Bible: in Luke 9:54 James and John, refused hospitality by a Samaritan village, ask Jesus, “Lord, do you want us to tell fire to come down from heaven and consume them — as Elijah did?” And he turns and rebukes them. The New Testament raises the objection itself, and does not resolve the Old Testament passage so much as decline to repeat it. The library prints the chapter and does not cast a vote.

Verses 13–16 · the third captain, and a prophet told not to be afraid

The third man changes only two things, and they are the whole difference. He does not quote the king, and he does not stand. “Please let my life be precious in your eyes” — tiqar-na nafshi, let my nefesh be weighty, costly to you — and he asks for his fifty men as well as himself. He has watched a hundred men die and he still comes up the hill, because he was ordered to; and then he does the one thing available to him. He is the only person in the chapter who addresses Elijah without an imperative in the sentence, and he is the only one who survives.

«Go down with him; do not be afraid of him.» A small line with a long memory. This is the same Elijah who, after the greatest victory of his life on Carmel, ran a day into the wilderness from one threat from Jezebel and asked to die (1 Kings 19). The angel does not tell him the king is harmless. It tells him not to be afraid — which is the sort of thing one says to someone who has been afraid before.

And the verdict is finally delivered in person. Three times the sentence has been spoken — to the angel’s messenger, by the messengers, and now at the bedside. The chapter has spent fifteen verses getting one man into one room to say to the king’s face what was already true in verse 4. Nothing changes; nothing is negotiated. It is simply said where it cannot be relayed, misheard, or ignored.

Verses 17–18 · two men named Jehoram, and a book we do not have

«And he died, according to the word of Jehovah that Elijah had spoken.» This is the engine of the whole book stated in one clause: a word is spoken, and the narrator comes back to note that it happened. Kings does this dozens of times, and it is the reason the book bothers with the reigns at all.

⚠ A genuine chronological knot, and it is fair to say so. Ahaziah is succeeded by his brother Jehoram of Israel — and the verse dates that to the second year of Jehoram of Judah, son of Jehoshaphat. Two kings, two kingdoms, the same name, reigning at the same time. Worse, the synchronism does not sit easily with the ones at 3:1 (“the eighteenth year of Jehoshaphat”) and 8:16. This is a small sample of the largest technical problem in Kings: the cross-dated regnal notices are the backbone of Old Testament chronology and they do not add up on a plain reading. The standard reconciliation invokes co-regencies — a father and son reigning jointly, so that a reign can be counted from two different starting points — which is a real ancient practice and also, conveniently, a solution flexible enough to absorb almost any discrepancy. It is widely used and not universally accepted. The Greek Septuagint, faced with the same difficulty, moves the numbers around.

«For he had no son.» Four words explaining why a brother inherits — and a quiet closing of the account on a king who consulted a foreign god about whether he would live.

⚠ A rarity in the Hebrew text, and the second in two chapters on this site: the Masoretes place a paragraph mark inside verse 17, between the accession and the date. The same feature appeared at 1 Kings 1:19. Nobody has satisfactorily explained it; we print it where they put it.

«The book of the Chronicles of the Kings of Israel.» Not the biblical book of Chronicles — a royal annal, cited here and some seventeen more times for Israel (and a matching one for Judah), and lost. Kings openly tells you it is a compilation and names its sources; the writer expects a reader who might go and look, and everything he chose not to copy out of those annals is a decision about what the reigns were actually for.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the exact / distinct words: “messenger” for mal’akh in both directions, so the king’s messengers are visibly intercepted by God’s Messenger; “go up” and “come down” held identical throughout, because the chapter is built on them; “inquire of” for darash, the consulting-word, kept the same in all four places; “man of God” for ish ha-Elohim, the title the captains use as a formula and Elijah takes literally; “the fire of God” for esh Elohim; “a man possessed of hair” keeping the Hebrew’s refusal to say whether the hair is his or his coat’s; and “let my life be precious in your eyes” for the third captain’s tiqar-na nafshi.

Echoes paid. The prophet who fled from Jezebel (1 Kings 19) now told by an angel not to be afraid of her son; the “fire of God” that fell on Job’s flocks (Job 1:16), the same phrase in a mouth that did not understand it; and the up-and-down of 1 Kings 1, where the whole succession turned on who went down to which spring.

Echoes planted. Elijah’s costume — hair and a leather belt — worn again by John the Baptist (Mark 1:6), a deliberate claim about whose successor he is; Baal-zebub, which travels through the Greek Beelzeboul into the Gospels as a name for the prince of the demons; the going up, which in the very next chapter takes Elijah into a whirlwind; and Moab’s rebellion, left hanging in verse 1 and picked up in chapter 3.

2 Kings is a new book on the site. Next in sequence: 2 Kings 2 — the day Jehovah takes Elijah up. He and Elisha walk from Gilgal to Bethel to Jericho to the Jordan, and three times Elijah tells him to stay behind and three times he refuses; the water is parted with a rolled-up cloak; Elisha asks for a double portion of his spirit; and a chariot of fire and horses of fire separate them, and Elijah goes up in a whirlwind, and the younger man is left holding the coat.
