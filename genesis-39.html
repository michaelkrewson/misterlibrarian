Translator's Notes

Verses 1–6 · the sentence chapter 37 refused to say

"And Jehovah was with Joseph." Read the previous two chapters again and notice what is missing from them: in chapter 37 — the coat, the pit, the sale, a father destroyed by a lie — God is never named once. Not a word from heaven, not a line of comment, nothing. Then the boy is sold into a foreign house as property, and the narrator, who has been silent through everything that could be called a tragedy, finally speaks: vayehi YHVH et-Yosef. And having said it, he will not stop saying it — four times in twenty-three verses (vv. 2, 3, 21, 23), twice on either side of the disaster in the middle. The chapter is built as a frame, and the frame is the sentence. Where a reader most wants to hear that God is present, Genesis says nothing; when everything has already gone wrong, it says it four times.

"A man who prospered" — in slavery. The word is matsliach, from tsalach, to succeed, to be made to go well — and the chapter uses it of a man who has been robbed of his family, his freedom, and his name. It is not a promise that things are pleasant; Joseph is still a slave in verse 6 and will be a prisoner by verse 20. Whatever "prospering" means here, it plainly does not mean "getting what he wanted." The KJV has "a prosperous man," the ASV the same, the NIV "he prospered," the NWT "a successful man" — all fine; the discomfort is in the Hebrew, not the English.

An Egyptian's house, blessed for a slave's sake. Verse 5 is quietly enormous: the blessing runs through Joseph into a foreign, idol-worshipping household — "in the house and in the field," which is to say the whole estate. This is the Abrahamic promise operating in miniature and out of sight: "in you all the families of the earth will be blessed" (12:3). The first person we ever see it happen to is a slave-owner in Egypt, and he has no idea why his accounts have improved.

"He knew nothing except the bread he ate." An odd little clause, and the versions divide on whether it is bookkeeping or delicacy. Read plainly, Potiphar handed over everything and stopped paying attention to anything but his own dinner. But some readers hear a euphemism (his wife, discreetly), and others a cultural note — Egyptians would not eat with Hebrews, a rule Genesis itself states outright later (43:32), so the master's food was the one thing a Hebrew steward could not touch. The KJV's "he knew not aught he had, save the bread which he did eat" keeps the ambiguity; the NIV resolves it toward bookkeeping ("he did not concern himself with anything except the food he ate"). This translation keeps it as it stands.

His mother's face. The chapter ends its first movement with a description that ought to stop a careful reader: Joseph was beautiful in form and lovely to look at. That is not a general compliment — it is, word for word, the Hebrew used of exactly one other person in the Bible, Rachel (yefat to'ar vifat mar'eh, 29:17), Joseph's own mother, whose beauty set the whole Jacob-Laban story in motion. This translation renders both with the same English, because the Hebrew is the same and the echo is the point; the KJV splits them ("beautiful and well favoured" for Rachel, "a goodly person, and well favoured" for Joseph), so an English reader cannot see that the son has his mother's face. And the sentence is placed here, immediately before verse 7, for a reason: it is not admiration, it is the hinge of the plot.

Verses 7–10 · two words, and a long answer

She speaks in two words; he answers in fifty. The Hebrew of her proposition is shikhvah immi — an imperative and a pronoun, blunt as a command from an owner to a piece of property, which is exactly the power relation in the room. Joseph's reply runs three full verses and is the longest speech he has made in his life. He argues the trust ("he has given all he has into my hand… he has withheld nothing from me except you"), and then he stops arguing about Potiphar altogether. "How then could I do this great evil, and sin against God?" The wrong he names last is not the wrong to his master or to himself — it is a wrong against someone who is not in the room and whose name has just been said four times over his life. This is the boy who told his brothers his dreams; he has learned to speak.

"Day after day." Verse 10 quietly refuses the version of this story where a man is ambushed once by temptation and heroically resists. Yom yom — it went on. The text says he did not listen, and then adds the smaller and harder detail: not "to lie beside her" and not even "to be with her." He would not be in the room. The narrative is describing a siege, not a moment.

A story the Egyptians were also telling. Honesty requires the comparison: a strikingly similar tale survives in Egyptian, the Tale of Two Brothers (Papyrus d'Orbiney, written down around 1200 BC), in which a man's wife propositions his younger brother, is refused, and accuses him to her husband. Folklorists file the shape under its own type-name — the spurned-wife accusation — and it turns up in Greek myth too (Phaedra and Hippolytus). What that means is argued: borrowing, a shared stock of story-shapes, or simply that this is a thing that happens to powerless men in powerful houses and always has. Genesis is not embarrassed by the resemblance, and neither is this translation; it is worth knowing that the ancient world had a name for what happened to Joseph.

Verses 11–18 · the second garment

It happens to him twice. This is the detail the chapter is built to make you feel. Joseph's brothers stripped him of his ornamented tunic, soaked it in goat's blood, and carried it to their father as evidence of a death that never happened. Now a second garment is taken off him, kept back deliberately (v. 16 — she laid it beside her and waited for her husband to come home), and produced as evidence of a crime that never happened. Twice Joseph is stripped; twice the cloth is left in someone else's hand; twice it is used to tell a lie about him to the man who owns his future. He does not get to speak in his own defence either time — in fact the chapter never records Joseph saying a single word after verse 9.

The word repeated until you notice. "He left his garment… and fled and went outside" is said four times in seven verses (vv. 12, 13, 15, 18), twice by the narrator and twice by her. Hebrew narrative repeats deliberately, and trimming it is editing — but watch what she changes. The narrator says he left it in her hand (be-yadah) — because she was gripping it. In her retellings it becomes beside me (etsli), which removes her hand from the story entirely. One preposition, and the seizing disappears.

"A Hebrew man" — and who she says it to. She does not begin with her husband. She calls the household staff first and builds a coalition, and her opening word to them is ethnic: "he has brought us in a Hebrew man to laugh at us." Ivri is the outsider-word, used in the Bible mostly by foreigners or to foreigners — first of Abram the Hebrew, and later all through the Egyptian scenes. To her husband, one verse later, the phrase upgrades to "the Hebrew slave whom you brought to us" — the blame quietly relocated onto the man she is complaining to. It is a small masterpiece of accusation, and Genesis reports it without a syllable of comment.

"To laugh at us." The verb she uses is letsacheq — the laughter root that has run through this book since Abraham and Sarah laughed at the promise, that named Isaac, and that turned nasty at 21:9, where Ishmael was seen metsacheq and Sarah demanded he be thrown out. It can mean play, mockery, or something coarser depending on where you stand — and she is exploiting exactly that range. The KJV renders it "to mock us," the ASV the same; the NIV reads "to make sport of us" and the NWT "to make us a laughingstock." The family's own laughter-word, aimed at Rachel's son by a stranger.

Verses 19–20 · what Potiphar does not do

The punishment is the loudest evidence in the chapter. A slave accused of assaulting his owner's wife, by an Egyptian officer whose actual job title is chief of the executioners, does not go to prison. He is killed, that day, and nobody writes it down. Potiphar's anger burns — and then he files him into the royal detention house and, as far as the text lets us see, never speaks of it again. Genesis does not explain this. But it does place the sentence next to the man's job description, and it does have his wife say "the Hebrew slave whom you brought to us," which is a complaint aimed at her husband; a reader is left to wonder how much of this Potiphar believed, and whether the cell was a punishment or the cheapest way to keep a valuable steward alive while saving a marriage. The chapter's silence is not an oversight — it is the same restraint that let the brothers stand in front of a father saying "an evil beast has devoured him."

The prison-house. Bet ha-sohar — "the house of the sohar," and nobody is quite sure what sohar means. It occurs only in the Joseph story, never elsewhere in the Bible; guesses run from a root meaning "round" (a fortress-tower or round-house) to an Egyptian loanword. This translation keeps "prison-house" so that bet, "house," stays audible — because that is the chapter's own bitter joke. Joseph is put in charge of a house (v. 4), loses it, and is handed a second house to run (v. 22). The KJV and ASV read simply "the prison"; the NWT "the prison house," keeping the same seam. ⚠️ Verse 20 also carries a ketiv/qere — Mechon-Mamre prints the unpointed written form beside the pointed one it is read as — kept here as received.

Verses 21–23 · the frame closes

"But Jehovah was with Joseph." The same four words as verse 2, now on the far side of the false accusation, and the chapter's architecture is complete: God with him in the house of the man who bought him, God with him in the jail of the man who framed him. Nothing external has improved — he has gone from slave to prisoner, which is downward — and the sentence does not change. It is the plainest statement Genesis makes about what "God is with you" does and does not promise.

Kindness, and favour. Two words worth keeping distinct. Chesed is covenant loyalty — the faithful, committed kindness this book has used of Lot's rescue (19:19) and of the servant's prayer at the well (24:12) — and it is God's, "extended" to Joseph like a hand reaching down into a cell. Chen is favour, the charm that makes someone want to help you, and it is what the keeper feels. The one produces the other. The versions reach variously: the KJV "shewed him mercy," the ASV "showed kindness unto him," the NWT "showed him loyal love" — and the NWT's compound is arguably the most exact of the three, since chesed's whole freight is loyalty rather than mere niceness. This translation reads "kindness" for continuity with 19:19 and 24:12, with the loyalty in the dictionary where it can be explained.

The same man, in a smaller room. Verses 22-23 are verses 4-6 again, deliberately: everything given into his hand, the keeper looking at nothing, Jehovah making it prosper. Joseph does in the jail exactly what he did in the house — he runs it. Whatever else this chapter is about, it is about a man whose competence and character are not situational, and the narrator makes the point by repeating himself rather than praising him. Note what has been quietly established by the end: Joseph is now the trusted manager of a prison full of the king's prisoners. The next two men through that door will be Pharaoh's cupbearer and Pharaoh's baker.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the words exact / distinct: "beautiful in form and lovely to look at" for yefeh to'ar vifeh mar'eh — rendered identically to Rachel's description because the Hebrew is identical and the KJV's split hides it; "prospered" for tsalach, uncomfortable as it is of a slave; "prison-house" for bet ha-sohar, keeping bet audible against the two other houses Joseph is given to run; "kindness" for chesed (continuous with 19:19 and 24:12) kept distinct from "favor" for chen; "to laugh at us" keeping the Isaac laughter-root audible in her accusation; and the fourfold "he left his garment… and fled and went outside" left repeated, as Hebrew narrative intends.

Echoes paid. The silence of chapter 37 — where God is never named — answered here four times over; the stripped tunic of 37:23 and the second garment, both taken off him and both used as false evidence; Rachel's beauty (29:17) reappearing on her son's face and endangering him; tsachaq, the laughter that named Isaac and turned cruel at 21:9, now weaponised by a stranger; Ivri, "Hebrew," last heard of Abram; the promise that "all the families of the earth will be blessed" (12:3) quietly enriching an Egyptian who never learns why; and chesed extended into a prison.

Echoes planted. A prison full of "the king's prisoners" and a Hebrew who runs it — the cupbearer and the baker are one chapter away (40); dreams, which Joseph has neither had nor interpreted since his brothers threw him in a pit, about to become the thing that lifts him out (40-41); "Jehovah was with him" becoming the formula the whole Bible uses for its unlikely deliverers; and Joseph's own verdict on all of it, still twelve chapters off — "you meant evil against me, but God meant it for good" (50:20).

Next installment in this book: Genesis 40 — two royal servants dream on the same night in Joseph's prison, and the man whose own dreams put him there discovers he can read other people's: three branches and three baskets, one restored and one hanged — and "do not interpretations belong to God?" Then the cupbearer forgets him for two full years.
