The angel's scroll of wars — from Persia through Alexander to the abomination that desolates.
The vision stands in Cyrus's first Persian years (ch. 10 dates it ~536 BC) and sweeps 370 years forward — to Antiochus IV's desecration, 15 Kislev 167 BC. Where its accuracy stops (v40) is the hinge of the whole dating debate.

Daniel 11 — The MiSTeR Translation

1
וַאֲנִי בִּשְׁנַת אַחַת, לְדָרְיָוֶשׁ הַמָּדִי--עָמְדִי לְמַחֲזִיק וּלְמָעוֹז, לוֹ.
"And I — in the first year of Darius the Mede, I stood up to strengthen and protect him.note
2
וְעַתָּה, אֱמֶת אַגִּיד לָךְ; הִנֵּה-עוֹד שְׁלֹשָׁה מְלָכִים עֹמְדִים לְפָרַס, וְהָרְבִיעִי יַעֲשִׁיר עֹשֶׁר-גָּדוֹל מִכֹּל, וּכְחֶזְקָתוֹ בְעָשְׁרוֹ, יָעִיר הַכֹּל אֵת מַלְכוּת יָוָן.
And now I will tell you the truth. Look — three more kings shall arise for Persia, and the fourth shall grow richer than all of them; and when he is strong through his riches, he shall stir up everything against the kingdom of Greece.note
3
וְעָמַד, מֶלֶךְ גִּבּוֹר; וּמָשַׁל מִמְשָׁל רַב, וְעָשָׂה כִּרְצוֹנוֹ.
Then a mighty king shall arise, and rule a great dominion, and do as he pleases.note
4
וּכְעָמְדוֹ, תִּשָּׁבֵר מַלְכוּתוֹ, וְתֵחָץ, לְאַרְבַּע רוּחוֹת הַשָּׁמָיִם; וְלֹא לְאַחֲרִיתוֹ, וְלֹא כְמָשְׁלוֹ אֲשֶׁר מָשָׁל--כִּי תִנָּתֵשׁ מַלְכוּתוֹ, וְלַאֲחֵרִים מִלְּבַד-אֵלֶּה.
And as he arises, his kingdom shall be broken, and divided toward the four winds of the heavens — not to his posterity, and not like the dominion he ruled; for his kingdom shall be plucked up, and go to others besides these.note
5
וְיֶחֱזַק מֶלֶךְ-הַנֶּגֶב, וּמִן-שָׂרָיו; וְיֶחֱזַק עָלָיו וּמָשָׁל, מִמְשָׁל רַב מֶמְשַׁלְתּוֹ.
And the king of the south shall grow strong — yet one of his commanders shall grow stronger still, and rule; his dominion shall be a great dominion.note
6
וּלְקֵץ שָׁנִים, יִתְחַבָּרוּ, וּבַת מֶלֶךְ-הַנֶּגֶב תָּבוֹא אֶל-מֶלֶךְ הַצָּפוֹן, לַעֲשׂוֹת מֵישָׁרִים; וְלֹא-תַעְצֹר כּוֹחַ הַזְּרוֹעַ, וְלֹא יַעֲמֹד וּזְרֹעוֹ, וְתִנָּתֵן הִיא וּמְבִיאֶיהָ וְהַיֹּלְדָהּ, וּמַחֲזִקָהּ בָּעִתִּים.
And at the end of some years they shall ally themselves, and the daughter of the king of the south shall come to the king of the north to seal fair terms. But she shall not retain the strength of her arm, and he shall not stand, nor his arm; and she shall be given up — she, and those who brought her, and he who fathered her, and he who supported her in those times.note
7
וְעָמַד מִנֵּצֶר שָׁרָשֶׁיהָ, כַּנּוֹ; וְיָבֹא אֶל-הַחַיִל, וְיָבֹא בְּמָעוֹז מֶלֶךְ הַצָּפוֹן, וְעָשָׂה בָהֶם, וְהֶחֱזִיק.
But from a branch of her own roots one shall arise in his place; and he shall come against the army, and enter the fortress of the king of the north, and deal with them, and prevail.note
8
וְגַם אֱלֹהֵיהֶם עִם-נְסִכֵיהֶם עִם-כְּלֵי חֶמְדָּתָם כֶּסֶף וְזָהָב, בַּשְּׁבִי--יָבִא מִצְרָיִם; וְהוּא שָׁנִים יַעֲמֹד, מִמֶּלֶךְ הַצָּפוֹן.
Even their gods, with their cast images and their precious vessels of silver and gold, he shall carry captive into Egypt; then for some years he shall stand off from the king of the north.note
cross-refs⤷ 1:2
9
וּבָא, בְּמַלְכוּת מֶלֶךְ הַנֶּגֶב, וְשָׁב, אֶל-אַדְמָתוֹ.
And he shall come into the kingdom of the king of the south, but shall return to his own land.note
10
וּבָנָו יִתְגָּרוּ, וְאָסְפוּ הֲמוֹן חֲיָלִים רַבִּים, וּבָא בוֹא, וְשָׁטַף וְעָבָר; וְיָשֹׁב ויתגרו וְיִתְגָּרֶה, עַד-מָעֻזֹּה.
And his sons shall stir themselves for war and gather a multitude of great forces; and one shall surely come, and flood through, and pass over — and he shall return, and wage war, up to his fortress.note
11
וְיִתְמַרְמַר, מֶלֶךְ הַנֶּגֶב, וְיָצָא, וְנִלְחַם עִמּוֹ עִם-מֶלֶךְ הַצָּפוֹן; וְהֶעֱמִיד הָמוֹן רָב, וְנִתַּן הֶהָמוֹן בְּיָדוֹ.
Then the king of the south, embittered, shall go out and fight with him — with the king of the north; and he shall raise a great multitude, and the multitude shall be given into his hand.note
12
וְנִשָּׂא הֶהָמוֹן, ירום וְרָם לְבָבוֹ; וְהִפִּיל רִבֹּאוֹת, וְלֹא יָעוֹז.
And when the multitude is carried off, his heart shall be lifted high; he shall bring down tens of thousands — but he shall not prevail.note
13
וְשָׁב, מֶלֶךְ הַצָּפוֹן, וְהֶעֱמִיד הָמוֹן, רַב מִן-הָרִאשׁוֹן; וּלְקֵץ הָעִתִּים שָׁנִים יָבוֹא בוֹא, בְּחַיִל גָּדוֹל וּבִרְכוּשׁ רָב.
For the king of the north shall return and raise a multitude greater than the first; and at the end of the times — years — he shall surely come, with a great army and abundant supplies.note
14
וּבָעִתִּים הָהֵם, רַבִּים יַעַמְדוּ עַל-מֶלֶךְ הַנֶּגֶב; וּבְנֵי פָּרִיצֵי עַמְּךָ, יִנַּשְּׂאוּ לְהַעֲמִיד חָזוֹן--וְנִכְשָׁלוּ.
And in those times many shall rise against the king of the south; and the sons of the violent among your own people shall lift themselves up, to establish the vision — and they shall stumble.note
15
וְיָבֹא, מֶלֶךְ הַצָּפוֹן, וְיִשְׁפֹּךְ סוֹלְלָה, וְלָכַד עִיר מִבְצָרוֹת; וּזְרֹעוֹת הַנֶּגֶב, לֹא יַעֲמֹדוּ, וְעַם מִבְחָרָיו, וְאֵין כֹּחַ לַעֲמֹד.
And the king of the north shall come and pour out a siege-ramp and capture a fortified city; and the arms of the south shall not stand — not even his picked troops; there shall be no strength to stand.note
16
וְיַעַשׂ הַבָּא אֵלָיו כִּרְצוֹנוֹ, וְאֵין עוֹמֵד לְפָנָיו; וְיַעֲמֹד בְּאֶרֶץ-הַצְּבִי, וְכָלָה בְיָדוֹ.
And he who comes against him shall do as he pleases, and none shall stand before him; and he shall stand in the Beautiful Land, with destruction in his hand.note

17
וְיָשֵׂם פָּנָיו לָבוֹא בְּתֹקֶף כָּל-מַלְכוּתוֹ, וִישָׁרִים עִמּוֹ--וְעָשָׂה; וּבַת הַנָּשִׁים יִתֶּן-לוֹ לְהַשְׁחִיתָהּ, וְלֹא תַעֲמֹד וְלֹא-לוֹ תִהְיֶה.
And he shall set his face to come with the strength of his whole kingdom, bringing fair terms — and he shall carry them out: he shall give him the daughter of women, to ruin it; but she shall not stand for him, and shall not be his.note
18
וישב וְיָשֵׂם פָּנָיו לְאִיִּים, וְלָכַד רַבִּים; וְהִשְׁבִּית קָצִין חֶרְפָּתוֹ לוֹ, בִּלְתִּי חֶרְפָּתוֹ יָשִׁיב לוֹ.
Then he shall turn his face to the coastlands, and capture many; but a commander shall put a stop to his insolence — more, he shall turn his insolence back upon him.note
19
וְיָשֵׁב פָּנָיו, לְמָעוּזֵּי אַרְצוֹ; וְנִכְשַׁל וְנָפַל, וְלֹא יִמָּצֵא.
So he shall turn his face back to the fortresses of his own land — and he shall stumble, and fall, and be found no more.note
20
וְעָמַד עַל-כַּנּוֹ מַעֲבִיר נוֹגֵשׂ, הֶדֶר מַלְכוּת; וּבְיָמִים אֲחָדִים יִשָּׁבֵר, וְלֹא בְאַפַּיִם וְלֹא בְמִלְחָמָה.
Then in his place shall arise one who sends an exactor of tribute through the glory of the kingdom; but within a few days he shall be broken — not in anger, and not in battle.note
21
וְעָמַד עַל-כַּנּוֹ נִבְזֶה, וְלֹא-נָתְנוּ עָלָיו הוֹד מַלְכוּת; וּבָא בְשַׁלְוָה, וְהֶחֱזִיק מַלְכוּת בַּחֲלַקְלַקּוֹת.
And in his place shall arise a contemptible man, on whom royal majesty was never conferred; he shall come in quietly, and seize the kingdom by smooth intrigues.note
22
וּזְרֹעוֹת הַשֶּׁטֶף יִשָּׁטְפוּ מִלְּפָנָיו, וְיִשָּׁבֵרוּ; וְגַם, נְגִיד בְּרִית.
And the arms of the flood shall be flooded away before him, and broken — and also the prince of the covenant.note
23
וּמִן-הִתְחַבְּרוּת אֵלָיו, יַעֲשֶׂה מִרְמָה; וְעָלָה וְעָצַם, בִּמְעַט-גּוֹי.
And from the time an alliance is made with him, he shall work deceit; and he shall rise, and grow strong, with a small nation.note
24
בְּשַׁלְוָה וּבְמִשְׁמַנֵּי מְדִינָה, יָבוֹא, וְעָשָׂה אֲשֶׁר לֹא-עָשׂוּ אֲבֹתָיו וַאֲבוֹת אֲבֹתָיו, בִּזָּה וְשָׁלָל וּרְכוּשׁ לָהֶם יִבְזוֹר; וְעַל מִבְצָרִים יְחַשֵּׁב מַחְשְׁבֹתָיו, וְעַד-עֵת.
Quietly he shall come into the richest parts of the province, and do what his fathers never did, nor his fathers' fathers: plunder and spoil and goods he shall scatter among his followers; and against the strongholds he shall devise his schemes — but only for a time.note
25
וְיָעֵר כֹּחוֹ וּלְבָבוֹ עַל-מֶלֶךְ הַנֶּגֶב, בְּחַיִל גָּדוֹל, וּמֶלֶךְ הַנֶּגֶב יִתְגָּרֶה לַמִּלְחָמָה, בְּחַיִל-גָּדוֹל וְעָצוּם עַד-מְאֹד; וְלֹא יַעֲמֹד, כִּי-יַחְשְׁבוּ עָלָיו מַחֲשָׁבוֹת.
And he shall stir up his power and his heart against the king of the south with a great army; and the king of the south shall be roused for war with a very great and mighty army — but he shall not stand, for schemes shall be devised against him.note
26
וְאֹכְלֵי פַת-בָּגוֹ יִשְׁבְּרוּהוּ, וְחֵילוֹ יִשְׁטוֹף; וְנָפְלוּ, חֲלָלִים רַבִּים.
The eaters of his own food-portion shall break him; his army shall be flooded away, and many shall fall slain.note
cross-refs⤷ 1:8
27
וּשְׁנֵיהֶם הַמְּלָכִים לְבָבָם לְמֵרָע, וְעַל-שֻׁלְחָן אֶחָד כָּזָב יְדַבֵּרוּ; וְלֹא תִצְלָח, כִּי-עוֹד קֵץ לַמּוֹעֵד.
And the two kings — their hearts set on evil, at one table they shall speak lies; but it shall not succeed, for the end is still for the appointed time.note
28
וְיָשֹׁב אַרְצוֹ בִּרְכוּשׁ גָּדוֹל, וּלְבָבוֹ עַל-בְּרִית קֹדֶשׁ; וְעָשָׂה, וְשָׁב לְאַרְצוֹ.
Then he shall return to his land with great goods, and his heart set against the holy covenant; and he shall act — and return to his own land.note
29
לַמּוֹעֵד יָשׁוּב, וּבָא בַנֶּגֶב; וְלֹא-תִהְיֶה כָרִאשֹׁנָה, וְכָאַחֲרוֹנָה.
At the appointed time he shall return, and come into the south — but this last time shall not be as the first.note
30
וּבָאוּ בוֹ צִיִּים כִּתִּים, וְנִכְאָה, וְשָׁב וְזָעַם עַל-בְּרִית-קוֹדֶשׁ, וְעָשָׂה; וְשָׁב וְיָבֵן, עַל-עֹזְבֵי בְּרִית קֹדֶשׁ.
For ships of Kittim shall come against him, and he shall lose heart; and he shall turn back, and rage against the holy covenant, and act — he shall turn back, and give heed to those who forsake the holy covenant.note
cross-refs⤷ Gen 10:4
31
וּזְרֹעִים, מִמֶּנּוּ יַעֲמֹדוּ; וְחִלְּלוּ הַמִּקְדָּשׁ הַמָּעוֹז, וְהֵסִירוּ הַתָּמִיד, וְנָתְנוּ, הַשִּׁקּוּץ מְשֹׁמֵם.
And forces from him shall arise and profane the sanctuary, the fortress; and they shall remove the daily offering, and set up the abomination that desolates.note
32
וּמַרְשִׁיעֵי בְרִית, יַחֲנִיף בַּחֲלַקּוֹת; וְעַם יֹדְעֵי אֱלֹהָיו, יַחֲזִקוּ וְעָשׂוּ.
Those who act wickedly against the covenant he shall corrupt with smooth words — but the people who know their God shall stand firm, and act.note
33
וּמַשְׂכִּילֵי עָם, יָבִינוּ לָרַבִּים; וְנִכְשְׁלוּ בְּחֶרֶב וּבְלֶהָבָה, בִּשְׁבִי וּבְבִזָּה--יָמִים.
And the wise among the people shall give understanding to many; yet they shall stumble by sword and by flame, by captivity and by plunder — for days.note
34
וּבְהִכָּשְׁלָם, יֵעָזְרוּ עֵזֶר מְעָט; וְנִלְווּ עֲלֵיהֶם רַבִּים, בַּחֲלַקְלַקּוֹת.
And when they stumble, they shall be helped with a little help; and many shall join them with smooth talk.note
35
וּמִן-הַמַּשְׂכִּילִים יִכָּשְׁלוּ, לִצְרוֹף בָּהֶם וּלְבָרֵר וְלַלְבֵּן--עַד-עֵת קֵץ: כִּי-עוֹד, לַמּוֹעֵד.
And some of the wise shall stumble — to refine among them, and to purify, and to make white, until the time of the end; for it is still for the appointed time.note
36
וְעָשָׂה כִרְצֹנוֹ הַמֶּלֶךְ, וְיִתְרוֹמֵם וְיִתְגַּדֵּל עַל-כָּל-אֵל, וְעַל אֵל אֵלִים, יְדַבֵּר נִפְלָאוֹת; וְהִצְלִיחַ עַד-כָּלָה זַעַם, כִּי נֶחֱרָצָה נֶעֱשָׂתָה.
And the king shall do as he pleases; he shall exalt himself and magnify himself above every god, and against the God of gods he shall speak astonishing things; and he shall prosper — until the indignation is finished, for what is decreed shall be done.note
37
וְעַל-אֱלֹהֵי אֲבֹתָיו לֹא יָבִין, וְעַל-חֶמְדַּת נָשִׁים וְעַל-כָּל-אֱלוֹהַּ לֹא יָבִין: כִּי עַל-כֹּל, יִתְגַּדָּל.

Translator's Notes — verse by verse

Each note explains this translation's choice and compares the seven versions on the shelf. Quotations from copyrighted versions (NIV, TLB, NWT) are kept to short phrases for comparison; KJV, Geneva, Douay-Rheims and ASV are public domain.

Verses 1–4 · מַלְכוּת יָוָן malkhut Yavan

You are entering a vision in progress. Chapters 10–12 of Daniel are a single scene, and this page opens at its middle: back in chapter 10 (not yet on this site), Daniel — in "the third year of Cyrus" — has fasted three weeks; a shining figure has arrived, apologizing for the delay ("the prince of Persia withstood me twenty-one days, and Michael… came to help me"); and it is that angel who speaks every verse of this chapter. His opening "I stood up to strengthen him" (v1) reaches back into that frame — and "the first year of Darius the Mede" names this book's famous puzzle: no such king appears in the Babylonian or Persian records between Belshazzar and Cyrus. The proposals: a throne-name of Cyrus himself; Gubaru, the governor who took Babylon the night it fell; or an error of the tradition — reported, not adjudicated. (A quibble readers catch: 1:21 said Daniel continued "until the first year of Cyrus," yet chapter 10 has him active in Cyrus's third — 1:21 marks the exile's end-point he lived to see, not his last day at work.)

Persia in one verse, Alexander in two. "Three more kings… and the fourth, richer than all, shall stir up everything against the kingdom of Greece" — Yavan, Javan, the Table of Nations' own name for the Greek world (Genesis 10:2; KJV "Grecia") — the fourth being Xerxes, whose invasion of Greece (480 BC) bought a grudge Greece never dropped. Then "a mighty king… and as he arises, his kingdom shall be broken toward the four winds — not to his posterity": Alexander, dead at 32 in Babylon, his empire quartered among his generals, his heirs murdered — the chapter's method in miniature: no one after Darius is ever named; a century and a half of history proceeds entirely in pronouns.

Verses 5–20 · מֶלֶךְ-הַנֶּגֶב … מֶלֶךְ הַצָּפוֹן melekh-hanegev … melekh hatsafon

The chess match — and where the reader lives. "The king of the south" is the house of Ptolemy in Egypt; "the king of the north," the house of Seleucus in Syria — and the Beautiful Land lies exactly between, so every march in this chapter goes past Jerusalem's door. The correspondences here are of a density unmatched anywhere in prophecy — a partial register, in order: the strong commander who outgrows his king (v5: Seleucus I, once Ptolemy I's officer); the daughter sent north to seal terms and "given up — she, and those who brought her, and he who fathered her" (v6: Berenice, married to Antiochus II, murdered with her child and household — some read "he who fathered her" as "her child"; the historical fit is grimly good either way); the "branch of her roots" who storms north in revenge and hauls home "even their gods… captive into Egypt" (vv 7-8: Ptolemy III, whose own inscriptions boast of bringing the gods back — the trophy-theology of Daniel 1:2, replayed between pagans); the raging counter-multitude "given into his hand" (v11: Raphia, 217 BC, the great elephant battle); the return "with a great army" that the south cannot stand (vv 13-16: Antiochus III, Panium, 200 BC — the year Judea passed from south to north); the "daughter of women" given "to ruin it" who "shall not stand for him" (v17: Cleopatra I, planted as a Trojan bride — who sided with her husband); the turn to the coastlands, stopped by "a commander" who turned his insolence home (v18: Rome's Scipio, Magnesia, 190 BC); the ignominious stumble (v19: Antiochus III, killed plundering a temple for tribute money); and the successor who "sends an exactor of tribute" and is broken "not in anger, and not in battle" (v20: Seleucus IV, whose tax-man Heliodorus raided Jerusalem's temple — the story of 2 Maccabees 3 — and then, it seems, poisoned his king). The dating debate planted at Daniel 1 lives exactly here: this density is either prophecy of stupefying precision, or history told as prophecy up to the writer's own day — hold the question; verse 40 will sharpen it.

The scribes' double track. Several verses in this stretch carry a ketiv/qere — the Masoretes' "written / read" apparatus: where tradition read a word differently than the consonants write it, they left the written letters standing and put the reading beside them (vv 10, 12, 18 here, 39 below — visible in the Hebrew column as an unpointed word beside its pointed twin). Printed as received: the manuscript's own honesty, kept.

Verses 21–28 · וְעָמַד עַל-כַּנּוֹ נִבְזֶה ve'amad al-kanno nivzeh

"A contemptible man." Enter the chapter's center of gravity — never named, like everyone here, but wearing one king's biography: Antiochus IV, who styled himself Epiphanes, "God Manifest" (contemporaries punned Epimanes, "the madman"). Not the heir — "royal majesty was never conferred on him" — he slid onto the throne "by smooth intrigues" while the true heir sat hostage in Rome. "The prince of the covenant" broken (v22) is read as Onias III, the legitimate high priest, deposed and later murdered (171 BC; 2 Maccabees 4 tells it) — the empire now selling Israel's priesthood to the highest bidder. The first Egyptian war (vv 25-27) comes off exactly as written: the south's army vast and useless, broken from inside — "the eaters of his own food-portion shall break him": pat-bag, the king's-table word of Daniel 1:5 — the table Daniel refused becomes the table that devours a king. "Two kings at one table, speaking lies" (v27): Antiochus and his puppet-nephew Ptolemy, uncle and ward toasting each other's ruin. And coming home heavy with Egyptian plunder, "his heart set against the holy covenant… and he shall act" (v28) — the temple treasury stripped, 169 BC.

Verses 29–35 · צִיִּים כִּתִּים … הַשִּׁקּוּץ מְשֹׁמֵם tsiyyim Kittim … hashiqquts meshomem

The ships of Kittim, and a circle in the sand. Kittim is Javan's son from the Table of Nations (Genesis 10:4) — first Cyprus, then the Greek west, and by now the newest western power: Rome (the Dead Sea Scrolls read Kittim as the Romans too; KJV "Chittim," while NIV translates the name away as "western coastlands"). The scene behind the verse is one of ancient history's greatest: Antiochus, mid-conquest of Egypt, 168 BC, met at Eleusis by the Roman envoy Popillius Laenas, who handed him the Senate's ultimatum, drew a circle around him in the sand with his stick, and told him to answer before stepping out of it. He stepped out a client, "and he shall lose heart" — and turned his humiliation on Jerusalem.

The abomination that desolates. December 167 BC: the garrison in the temple-fortress, the daily offering halted, and on the altar of burnt offering "the abomination that desolates" (KJV "the abomination that maketh desolate") — an altar to Olympian Zeus; swine offered, Torah scrolls burned, the covenant made a capital crime. When 1 Maccabees 1:54 records the day, it reaches for this verse's own phrase — "they erected a desolating abomination upon the altar" — the earliest reader of Daniel we can watch reading Daniel. (Jesus will lift the phrase again toward a horizon of his own: "the abomination of desolation, spoken of by Daniel the prophet… let the reader understand," Matthew 24:15.) Against the terror the chapter sets not warriors but "the people who know their God" and the maskilim, "the wise," who "give understanding to many" and fall by sword and flame — the martyrs of the persecution; the armed Maccabean revolt itself appears, on the common reading, only as "a little help" (v34) — the book's cool, deliberate distance from the sword. Their falling "refines, purifies, makes white" — the metallurgy of martyrdom, until the appointed time.

Verses 36–39 · וְעַל אֵל אֵלִים יְדַבֵּר נִפְלָאוֹת ve'al El elim yedabber niflaot

The king above every god. Self-exaltation "above every god," "astonishing things against the God of gods" — Antiochus's own coins escalate to THEOU EPIPHANOUS, "of God Manifest," and his program made him, for the tradition, the very type of the final blasphemer. The details keep their Hellenistic fit: "no heed… to the one beloved of women" — Tammuz-Adonis, the dying god the women of the Levant wept for (Ezekiel 8:14 shows the weeping at Jerusalem's own gate); honor instead to "a god of fortresses" (KJV "the god of forces," ASV "the god of fortresses") — read as Zeus Olympios of the citadel cult, or Jupiter Capitolinus whose temple he lavished, or, unsettlingly, simply war itself, honored "with gold and silver" — garrisons endowed like shrines, loyalty bought with parceled land "for a price" (v39): the Akra citadel planted in Jerusalem, and confiscated Judean land in its gift.

Verses 40–45 · וּבְעֵת קֵץ uve'et qets

The seam — said plainly. "At the time of the end" a last campaign unrolls: a whirlwind victory through the lands, Egypt looted, "Libyans and Cushites at his steps" (KJV "the Libyans and the Ethiopians"), alarms from east and north, and the king's end "between the sea and the beautiful holy mountain… with none to help him." And here the astonishing historical fit stops: Antiochus IV mounted no such final conquest of Egypt, and he died at Tabae in Persia, late 164 BC, campaigning eastward — not encamped in the Land at all. This seam is the oldest argument about the book. The pagan philosopher Porphyry (3rd century AD) read it exactly as a modern critic does: accurate "prophecy" up to the author's own moment (~166 BC), genuine — and unfulfilled — expectation beyond it; we know his argument because Jerome preserved it in his Daniel commentary in order to fight it, answering that from verse 36 (or here at 40) the subject quietly shifts from Antiochus to a final enemy at the end of days — the "gap" reading held by traditional interpreters ever since, with Antiochus as the near figure and type. One reading hears a fingerprint; the other, a telescope. Both are stated here at full strength, per this project's standing rule — and the verse itself is translated as it stands, matching neither side's convenience.

Old neighbors on a late map. Who escapes the last king's hand? "Edom, and Moab, and the chief part of the sons of Ammon" — Esau's nation and the two born in Lot's cave (Genesis 19:37-38), still on the map, still next door, four centuries after Genesis closed. "He shall plant his palace-tents" — appadno, a Persian loanword so unfamiliar that the Vulgate took it for a place-name, and Douay duly prints "he shall fix his tabernacle Apadno between the seas" — a phantom town born of one hard word, sitting on the shelf to this day. "And he shall come to his end, with none to help him" — on either reading of the seam, the chapter's verdict on every empire in it. The next page of the book is the reason all this was worth telling: "at that time Michael shall stand up… and many who sleep in the dust of the earth shall awake."

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation sides with the literal shelf: "the abomination that desolates" (with the KJV family), "a god of fortresses" (with ASV, against KJV's vaguer "forces"), "Kittim" kept as the name it is (against NIV's "western coastlands"), and the pronouns left unnamed — the chapter's own method — with the identifications confined to the notes.

Where it goes its own way: "the Beautiful Land" for erets ha-tsvi (KJV "the glorious land"; NWT, memorably, "the land of the Decoration"), "palace-tents" for the loanword that became Douay's phantom town Apadno, "lock horns" for the goring-verb of verse 40, and the seam of verses 40-45 reported with both readings at full strength rather than smoothed toward either.

Echoes paid and planted. Paid: Kittim and Javan, sons of the Table of Nations (10:4, 10:2), grown into Rome and Greece; the Beautiful Land — the everlasting possession of (17:8) as a chessboard square between empires; gods and vessels carried off as trophies, the theology of (1:2); the pat-bag — Daniel's refused portion (1:8) breaking a king at his own table; and Edom, Moab, and Ammon — Lot's cave-born kin (19:37) — still next door at history's end. Planted: "Michael shall stand up," the dust that wakes, and the sealed book — Daniel 12, this book's next page; and the abomination-phrase, waiting on the Mount of Olives (Matthew 24:15).
