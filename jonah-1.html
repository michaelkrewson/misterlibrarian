A prophet flees his commission by sea; God hurls a storm; pagan sailors, more merciful than the prophet, at last throw him overboard and the sea calms — and they worship the God he was running from.
⚠ TWO dates, one for the man and one for the book. The prophet Jonah son of Amittai is REAL and 8th-century: 2 Kings 14:25 sets him under Jeroboam II (c. 780 BC), a contemporary of Amos and Hosea — which is why the chapter is filed here in the monarchy. But the BOOK that tells his story is widely thought to be POST-EXILIC (5th-4th c.), a later, pointed tale built around the old northern prophet's name, aimed at a narrow nationalism the returned community had to unlearn. Nineveh, its setting, fell in 612 BC. The two datings sit side by side without contradiction: an 8th-century figure, a later book.

Translator’s Notes

Verses 1–3 · the only prophet who runs — and the direction he runs

⚠ Jonah is the odd book of the Twelve in almost every way, and the first way is that it is a STORY, not a collection of oracles. The other eleven Minor Prophets are books of God’s speeches; Jonah is a narrative with a plot, and the one line of actual preaching it contains (3:4) is five Hebrew words long. It is also the only prophetic book whose prophet is the problem — the anti-hero of his own book — and the only one in which the prophet flatly DISOBEYS.

⚠ And he is a real, datable prophet. Jonah son of Amittai appears once elsewhere in the Bible: 2 Kings 14:25 names him as the prophet from Gath-hepher (a village in Galilee, a few miles from what would later be Nazareth) whose word Jeroboam II fulfilled by restoring Israel’s northern borders in the mid-eighth century. ⚠ That is the sharp point of the whole book: the historical Jonah was a nationalist good-news prophet, the man who told Israel its borders would grow — and this book takes that same prophet and sends him to save the capital of the empire that would one day destroy Israel. The name helps: Yonah means “dove,” and ben Amittai means “son of my faithfulness” (from emet, truth). The runaway is “Dove, son of Faithfulness.” DRB, from the Vulgate, calls him Jonas, son of Amathi.

⚠ Nineveh is “the great city” — and great (gadol) is about to become the drumbeat of the book. A great city, a great wind, a great storm, a great fear, a great fish: the word tolls through all four chapters. Nineveh was the future capital of ASSYRIA — the empire whose armies would obliterate the northern kingdom in 722 BC and deport the ten tribes — so “go and cry against Nineveh” is, to an Israelite ear, an order to go and warn the executioner. “Their evil has come up before me” — ra’ah, “evil / calamity,” another word that will run the whole book, meaning by turns their wickedness, the disaster it earns, and (astonishingly, at the end) the disaster God relents of.

⚠ He runs to the exact opposite end of the map, and the text tracks him with one word: DOWN. Tarshish is the far western edge of the known world — most likely Tartessos in southern Spain, a Phoenician metal-trading port — the maximum distance a man could put between himself and Nineveh in the east. And the verb yarad, “go down,” begins its relentless run here: Jonah goes down to Joppa, down into the ship; he will go down into the hold (v5), down into the sea, down into the fish, and down to “the roots of the mountains” (2:6). The whole first half of the book is a descent. Twice in one verse he flees “away from before Jehovah” (millifnei YHWH) — as if a man could leave God’s presence, which is exactly what his own creed in verse 9 will deny. KJV and ASV render it “from the presence of Jehovah”; DRB, from the Vulgate, has the more vivid “from the FACE of the Lord” — which is closer to the Hebrew, where lifnei is literally “to the face of.”

Verses 4–6 · the storm, and a prophet asleep while pagans pray

⚠ God’s counter-move is the same verb Jonah’s will keep meeting: he HURLED a wind. Heteel, “to hurl, fling,” is used three times in this chapter — God hurls the wind (v4), the sailors hurl the cargo (v5), and finally they hurl Jonah (v15). The runaway who wanted only to be left alone becomes the thing thrown. KJV and ASV soften it to “sent out a great wind”; NWT “hurled a great wind” keeps the force of the verb, and so do we.

The scene is built on a contrast the reader is meant to feel. The pagan sailors are in a frenzy of prayer — “each cried out to his own god” — and of action, throwing the freight overboard to save the ship and everyone on it. And where is the prophet of the true God? Below decks, asleep. He “had gone down into the innermost part of the vessel, and lay down, and fell into a deep sleep” — nirdam, the heavy, God-sent kind of sleep, the same root as the tardemah that falls on Adam in Genesis 2:21. The men fighting for their lives worship gods who cannot help; the man who could pray to the God of the sea is unconscious.

⚠ And the captain, a pagan, unknowingly repeats God’s own words. “Arise, call out to your god!” — qum qera — is exactly the command God gave in verse 2 (“arise … call out against it”). The order Jonah fled from is put back to him by a heathen ship’s captain in the middle of the sea. “Perhaps the god will give us a thought” — the captain is not even sure which god, and hopes only that some deity might notice; his tentative “perhaps” is the book’s recurring word for the slim hope of mercy, the same “who knows?” the king of Nineveh will voice in 3:9. KJV “What meanest thou, O sleeper?”; NIV “How can you sleep?”

Verses 7–10 · the lot, the interrogation, and a creed the man is busy contradicting

The sailors do what ancient people did when a disaster had a hidden cause: they cast lots — the “let God point” procedure that fingers Achan (Joshua 7), nearly kills Jonathan (1 Samuel 14), and had just been cast over Jerusalem by Edom in the book before this (Obadiah 11). The lot falls on Jonah, and the sailors’ response is not a lynching but a barrage of polite questions: what is your work, where are you from, what is your country, what is your people?

⚠ Jonah’s answer is one of the great ironies of the Bible. “I am a Hebrew, and I fear Jehovah, the God of the heavens, who made the sea and the dry land.” Every clause is orthodox and every clause condemns him. He calls himself a Ivri, “Hebrew” — the word Israelites used of themselves to foreigners (Joseph in Egypt, the Hebrews before Pharaoh). He says he “fears” — worships — Jehovah, while doing the one thing that proves he is not letting that fear govern him. And he confesses God as the maker of the sea — the very sea he is at that moment trying to use as a road out of God’s reach. His theology is faultless; his obedience is nil. KJV keeps the older idiom, “I am an Hebrew”; ASV “I fear Jehovah, the God of heaven, who hath made the sea and the dry land”; NWT “I am worshiping Jehovah the God of the heavens.”

⚠ The sailors’ fear is the real thing. “The men feared a great fear” (yir’ah gedolah) — the noun of the very verb Jonah had just claimed for himself. He says he fears God; the pagans actually do. It is the first of three risings of their fear in the chapter (v5, v10, v16), and each rise is a step toward the God Jonah is running from.

Verses 11–16 · the pagans who out-mercy the prophet — and where the fish actually is

⚠ Jonah’s solution — «lift me up and hurl me into the sea» — is left deliberately unreadable. Is it repentance, offering his life to save the crew? Is it self-sacrifice? Or is it a death-wish — a man who would rather drown than go to Nineveh, still choosing down over obedience? The book will not say, and it matters, because in chapter 4 Jonah asks to die twice more. What the verse does make plain is that he knows the storm is his fault (“on my account”).

⚠ And now the sailors do the thing that turns the whole chapter into an indictment: they try to SAVE him. “The men rowed hard” — the verb chatar means literally to dig, and they dig at the oars — risking their own lives to get the guilty man safely back to shore rather than throw him over. ⚠ Hold this next to the rest of the book: the pagan crew will not sacrifice one endangering stranger to save themselves, while the prophet Jonah, in chapter 4, will rage that God spared a whole city of a hundred and twenty thousand. The heathens are more merciful than the man of God, and the book knows exactly what it is doing.

When they finally give in, they pray — and the prayer is theologically perfect. “Do not let us perish for this man’s life, and do not lay innocent blood upon us — for you, Jehovah, have done as you pleased.” The sailors, who an hour ago were each shouting at his own god, now pray to Jehovah by name, worry about bloodguilt, and confess God’s sovereignty, all before they have been taught a word of it. They throw Jonah over; “the sea stood still from its raging” — the Hebrew literally has the sea “stand” (amad) from its rage, an odd, arresting verb for water. KJV “the sea ceased from her raging”; NIV “the raging sea grew calm”; NWT “the sea ceased its raging” — all smoothing the strange “stood” that this translation keeps. And then verse 16: “the men feared Jehovah with a great fear, and sacrificed a sacrifice to Jehovah, and made vows.” ⚠ The first converts in the book of Jonah are the pagan sailors — and the prophet, who has not preached a single word to them, has been dragged into being God’s instrument entirely against his will.

⚠ And here is where a reader raised on Sunday school gets a surprise: THE FISH IS NOT IN THIS CHAPTER. In the Hebrew Bible, Jonah 1 ends at verse 16, with the sailors worshipping on the calmed sea. “Now Jehovah appointed a great fish to swallow up Jonah” is Hebrew 2:1 — the first verse of the next chapter — which English Bibles renumber as 1:17, folding it back into chapter 1. This site follows the Masoretic numbering, as everywhere, so the famous line opens chapter 2 here. ⚠ And when it comes, notice what the Hebrew does not say: it is a dag gadol, a “great fish” — not a whale. No species is named; the whale is an accretion of later art and translation. Even Jesus, invoking “the sign of Jonah” (Matthew 12:40), uses the Greek ketos, “sea-monster / huge fish,” not a word for whale.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the exact / distinct words: “great” (gadol) kept on all of it — great city, great wind, great storm, great fear, great fish — because the repetition is the book’s pulse; “hurled” for heteel, the same verb for the wind God throws, the cargo the sailors throw, and Jonah himself; “went down” for every yarad, tracking the descent (Joppa, ship, hold, and on into the sea and the fish); “away from before Jehovah” for millifnei YHWH, the impossible flight his own creed refutes; “I am a Hebrew” for Ivri, the outsiders’ word for an Israelite; “fear / feared a great fear” for yare / yir’ah, the same root Jonah claims and the sailors actually live; and “a great fish” for dag gadol — not a whale — when it arrives at the head of chapter 2.

Echoes paid. Jonah son of Amittai, the border-expanding nationalist prophet of 2 Kings 14:25, sent here to the enemy’s capital. The deep sleep of Genesis 2, fallen on a prophet in a crisis. Casting lots to find the guilty man — the same procedure Edom used over Jerusalem in Obadiah 11, the book just before this. And the “perhaps” of the captain (v6), the slim hope of mercy that the whole book turns on.

Echoes planted. The descent — down to Joppa, down to the hold, down to the sea, and in chapter 2 down to the roots of the mountains, before the fish brings him back up. The great fish of 2:1, and the psalm Jonah prays from inside it. The pagans who fear God — the sailors here, the whole city of Nineveh in chapter 3, and a king who says “who knows?” the way this captain does. The mercy Jonah cannot stand — the crew’s desperate rowing to save one man foreshadows God’s sparing of a hundred and twenty thousand, and Jonah’s fury at it in chapter 4. And “the sea and the dry land” (v9), the God of both, who will command a fish, a plant, a worm and a wind before the book is done.
