Judges 1 — The Mister Translation

1
וַיְהִי, אַחֲרֵי מוֹת יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, וַיִּשְׁאֲלוּ בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל, בַּיהוָה לֵאמֹר: מִי יַעֲלֶה-לָּנוּ אֶל-הַכְּנַעֲנִי בַּתְּחִלָּה, לְהִלָּחֶם בּוֹ.
And it happened, after the death of Joshua, that the sons of Israel inquired of Jehovah, saying, "Who will go up for us against the Canaanite first, to fight against him?"note
Y sucedió, después de la muerte de Josué, que los hijos de Israel consultaron a Jehová, diciendo: «¿Quién subirá por nosotros contra el cananeo primero, para pelear contra él?»
cross-refs⤷ Joshua 1:1
2
וַיֹּאמֶר יְהוָה, יְהוּדָה יַעֲלֶה: הִנֵּה נָתַתִּי אֶת-הָאָרֶץ, בְּיָדוֹ.
And Jehovah said, "Judah will go up. Look — I have given the land into his hand."note
Y Jehová dijo: «Judá subirá. Mira — he dado la tierra en su mano».
cross-refs⤷ Gen 49:8
3
וַיֹּאמֶר יְהוּדָה לְשִׁמְעוֹן אָחִיו עֲלֵה אִתִּי בְגֹרָלִי, וְנִלָּחֲמָה בַּכְּנַעֲנִי, וְהָלַכְתִּי גַם-אֲנִי אִתְּךָ, בְּגוֹרָלֶךָ; וַיֵּלֶךְ אִתּוֹ, שִׁמְעוֹן.
And Judah said to Simeon his brother, "Go up with me into my allotted portion, and let us fight against the Canaanite; and I too will go with you into yours." And Simeon went with him.note
Y Judá dijo a Simeón su hermano: «Sube conmigo a mi porción, y peleemos contra el cananeo; y yo también iré contigo a la tuya». Y Simeón fue con él.
4
וַיַּעַל יְהוּדָה, וַיִּתֵּן יְהוָה אֶת-הַכְּנַעֲנִי וְהַפְּרִזִּי בְּיָדָם; וַיַּכּוּם בְּבֶזֶק, עֲשֶׂרֶת אֲלָפִים אִישׁ.
And Judah went up, and Jehovah gave the Canaanite and the Perizzite into their hand; and they struck down ten thousand men at Bezek.note
Y subió Judá, y Jehová dio al cananeo y al ferezeo en su mano; y derrotaron a diez mil hombres en Bézec.
5
וַיִּמְצְאוּ אֶת-אֲדֹנִי בֶזֶק, בְּבֶזֶק, וַיִּלָּחֲמוּ, בּוֹ; וַיַּכּוּ, אֶת-הַכְּנַעֲנִי וְאֶת-הַפְּרִזִּי.
And they found Adoni-bezek at Bezek and fought against him, and they struck down the Canaanite and the Perizzite.note
Y hallaron a Adoni-bézec en Bézec y pelearon contra él, y derrotaron al cananeo y al ferezeo.
6
וַיָּנָס אֲדֹנִי בֶזֶק, וַיִּרְדְּפוּ אַחֲרָיו; וַיֹּאחֲזוּ אוֹתוֹ--וַיְקַצְּצוּ, אֶת-בְּהֹנוֹת יָדָיו וְרַגְלָיו.
And Adoni-bezek fled, and they pursued him and caught him and cut off his thumbs and his big toes.note
Y Adoni-bézec huyó, y lo persiguieron y lo prendieron y le cortaron los pulgares de sus manos y los dedos gordos de sus pies.
cross-refs⤷ 1:7
7
וַיֹּאמֶר אֲדֹנִי-בֶזֶק, שִׁבְעִים מְלָכִים בְּהֹנוֹת יְדֵיהֶם וְרַגְלֵיהֶם מְקֻצָּצִים הָיוּ מְלַקְּטִים תַּחַת שֻׁלְחָנִי--כַּאֲשֶׁר עָשִׂיתִי, כֵּן שִׁלַּם-לִי אֱלֹהִים; וַיְבִיאֻהוּ יְרוּשָׁלִַם, וַיָּמָת שָׁם.{פ}
And Adoni-bezek said, "Seventy kings with their thumbs and big toes cut off used to gather under my table. As I have done, so God has repaid me." And they brought him to Jerusalem, and he died there.note
Y Adoni-bézec dijo: «Setenta reyes, con los pulgares de sus manos y los dedos gordos de sus pies cortados, recogían las migajas debajo de mi mesa. Como yo hice, así me ha pagado Dios». Y lo llevaron a Jerusalén, y allí murió.
cross-refs⤷ 1:6
8
וַיִּלָּחֲמוּ בְנֵי-יְהוּדָה, בִּירוּשָׁלִַם, וַיִּלְכְּדוּ אוֹתָהּ, וַיַּכּוּהָ לְפִי-חָרֶב; וְאֶת-הָעִיר, שִׁלְּחוּ בָאֵשׁ.
And the sons of Judah fought against Jerusalem and captured it, and struck it with the edge of the sword, and sent the city up in fire.note
Y los hijos de Judá pelearon contra Jerusalén y la tomaron, y la hirieron a filo de espada; y prendieron fuego a la ciudad.
9
וְאַחַר, יָרְדוּ בְּנֵי יְהוּדָה, לְהִלָּחֵם, בַּכְּנַעֲנִי--יוֹשֵׁב הָהָר, וְהַנֶּגֶב וְהַשְּׁפֵלָה.
And afterward the sons of Judah went down to fight against the Canaanite who dwelt in the hill country and the Negev and the lowland.note
Y después los hijos de Judá bajaron a pelear contra el cananeo que habitaba en la montaña y el Négueb y la Sefela.
10
וַיֵּלֶךְ יְהוּדָה, אֶל-הַכְּנַעֲנִי הַיּוֹשֵׁב בְּחֶבְרוֹן, וְשֵׁם-חֶבְרוֹן לְפָנִים, קִרְיַת אַרְבַּע; וַיַּכּוּ אֶת-שֵׁשַׁי וְאֶת-אֲחִימַן, וְאֶת-תַּלְמָי.
And Judah went against the Canaanite who dwelt in Hebron — the name of Hebron formerly was Kiriath-arba — and they struck down Sheshai and Ahiman and Talmai.note
Y Judá fue contra el cananeo que habitaba en Hebrón — el nombre de Hebrón antes era Quiriat-arba — y derrotaron a Sesai y a Ahimán y a Talmai.
11
וַיֵּלֶךְ מִשָּׁם, אֶל-יוֹשְׁבֵי דְּבִיר; וְשֵׁם-דְּבִיר לְפָנִים, קִרְיַת-סֵפֶר.
And from there he went against the inhabitants of Debir — the name of Debir formerly was Kiriath-sepher.note
Y de allí fue contra los habitantes de Debir — el nombre de Debir antes era Quiriat-séfer.
12
וַיֹּאמֶר כָּלֵב, אֲשֶׁר-יַכֶּה אֶת-קִרְיַת-סֵפֶר וּלְכָדָהּ--וְנָתַתִּי לוֹ אֶת-עַכְסָה בִתִּי, לְאִשָּׁה.
And Caleb said, "Whoever strikes Kiriath-sepher and captures it, to him I will give Achsah my daughter as a wife."note
Y Caleb dijo: «Al que hiera a Quiriat-séfer y la tome, le daré por mujer a Acsa mi hija».
13
וַיִּלְכְּדָהּ עָתְנִיאֵל בֶּן-קְנַז, אֲחִי כָלֵב הַקָּטֹן מִמֶּנּוּ; וַיִּתֶּן-לוֹ אֶת-עַכְסָה בִתּוֹ, לְאִשָּׁה.
And Othniel son of Kenaz, Caleb's younger brother, captured it; and he gave him Achsah his daughter as a wife.note
Y la tomó Otoniel hijo de Cenaz, hermano menor de Caleb; y le dio por mujer a Acsa su hija.
14
וַיְהִי בְּבוֹאָהּ, וַתְּסִיתֵהוּ לִשְׁאֹל מֵאֵת-אָבִיהָ הַשָּׂדֶה, וַתִּצְנַח, מֵעַל הַחֲמוֹר; וַיֹּאמֶר-לָהּ כָּלֵב, מַה-לָּךְ.
And it happened, when she came to him, that she urged him to ask her father for a field. And she slid down from the donkey, and Caleb said to her, "What do you want?"note
Y sucedió, cuando ella vino a él, que lo instó a pedir a su padre un campo. Y se bajó del asno, y Caleb le dijo: «¿Qué quieres?»
15
וַתֹּאמֶר לוֹ הָבָה-לִּי בְרָכָה, כִּי אֶרֶץ הַנֶּגֶב נְתַתָּנִי, וְנָתַתָּה לִי, גֻּלֹּת מָיִם; וַיִּתֶּן-לָהּ כָּלֵב, אֵת גֻּלֹּת עִלִּית, וְאֵת, גֻּלֹּת תַּחְתִּית.{פ}
And she said to him, "Give me a blessing. Since you have given me the land of the Negev, give me also springs of water." And Caleb gave her the upper springs and the lower springs.note
Y ella le dijo: «Dame una bendición. Ya que me has dado la tierra del Négueb, dame también manantiales de agua». Y Caleb le dio los manantiales de arriba y los manantiales de abajo.
16
וּבְנֵי קֵינִי חֹתֵן מֹשֶׁה עָלוּ מֵעִיר הַתְּמָרִים, אֶת-בְּנֵי יְהוּדָה, מִדְבַּר יְהוּדָה, אֲשֶׁר בְּנֶגֶב עֲרָד; וַיֵּלֶךְ, וַיֵּשֶׁב אֶת-הָעָם.
And the sons of the Kenite, Moses' father-in-law, went up from the City of Palms with the sons of Judah to the wilderness of Judah, which is in the Negev of Arad; and they went and dwelt with the people.note
Y los hijos del ceneo, suegro de Moisés, subieron de la Ciudad de las Palmeras con los hijos de Judá al desierto de Judá, que está en el Négueb de Arad; y fueron y habitaron con el pueblo.
17
וַיֵּלֶךְ יְהוּדָה, אֶת-שִׁמְעוֹן אָחִיו, וַיַּכּוּ, אֶת-הַכְּנַעֲנִי יוֹשֵׁב צְפַת; וַיַּחֲרִימוּ אוֹתָהּ, וַיִּקְרָא אֶת-שֵׁם-הָעִיר חָרְמָה.
And Judah went with Simeon his brother, and they struck down the Canaanite who dwelt in Zephath, and put it to the ban; and the name of the city was called Hormah.note
Y Judá fue con Simeón su hermano, y derrotaron al cananeo que habitaba en Sefat, y la pusieron al anatema; y el nombre de la ciudad se llamó Horma.
18
וַיִּלְכֹּד יְהוּדָה אֶת-עַזָּה וְאֶת-גְּבוּלָהּ, וְאֶת-אַשְׁקְלוֹן וְאֶת-גְּבוּלָהּ, וְאֶת-עֶקְרוֹן, וְאֶת-גְּבוּלָהּ.
And Judah captured Gaza and its territory, and Ashkelon and its territory, and Ekron and its territory.note
Y Judá tomó a Gaza y su territorio, y a Ascalón y su territorio, y a Ecrón y su territorio.
19
וַיְהִי יְהוָה אֶת-יְהוּדָה, וַיֹּרֶשׁ אֶת-הָהָר: כִּי לֹא לְהוֹרִישׁ אֶת-יֹשְׁבֵי הָעֵמֶק, כִּי-רֶכֶב בַּרְזֶל לָהֶם.
And Jehovah was with Judah, and he took possession of the hill country; but he could not drive out the inhabitants of the valley, because they had chariots of iron.note
Y Jehová estuvo con Judá, y tomó posesión de la montaña; pero no pudo expulsar a los habitantes del valle, porque tenían carros de hierro.
20
וַיִּתְּנוּ לְכָלֵב אֶת-חֶבְרוֹן, כַּאֲשֶׁר דִּבֶּר מֹשֶׁה; וַיּוֹרֶשׁ מִשָּׁם, אֶת-שְׁלֹשָׁה בְּנֵי הָעֲנָק.
And they gave Hebron to Caleb, as Moses had spoken; and he drove out from there the three sons of the Anak.note
Y dieron Hebrón a Caleb, como había dicho Moisés; y expulsó de allí a los tres hijos de Anac.
21
וְאֶת-הַיְבוּסִי יֹשֵׁב יְרוּשָׁלִַם, לֹא הוֹרִישׁוּ בְּנֵי בִנְיָמִן; וַיֵּשֶׁב הַיְבוּסִי אֶת-בְּנֵי בִנְיָמִן, בִּירוּשָׁלִַם, עַד, הַיּוֹם הַזֶּה.{פ}
But the Jebusite who dwelt in Jerusalem the sons of Benjamin did not drive out; and the Jebusite has dwelt with the sons of Benjamin in Jerusalem to this day.note
Pero al jebuseo que habitaba en Jerusalén no lo expulsaron los hijos de Benjamín; y el jebuseo ha habitado con los hijos de Benjamín en Jerusalén hasta el día de hoy.
cross-refs⤷ Joshua 1:1
22
וַיַּעֲלוּ בֵית-יוֹסֵף גַּם-הֵם, בֵּית-אֵל; וַיהוָה, עִמָּם.
And the house of Joseph, they too, went up against Bethel; and Jehovah was with them.note
Y la casa de José, ellos también, subieron contra Betel; y Jehová estuvo con ellos.
23
וַיָּתִירוּ בֵית-יוֹסֵף, בְּבֵית-אֵל; וְשֵׁם-הָעִיר לְפָנִים, לוּז.
And the house of Joseph spied out Bethel — the name of the city formerly was Luz.note
Y la casa de José exploró Betel — el nombre de la ciudad antes era Luz.
24
וַיִּרְאוּ, הַשֹּׁמְרִים, אִישׁ, יוֹצֵא מִן-הָעִיר; וַיֹּאמְרוּ לוֹ, הַרְאֵנוּ נָא אֶת-מְבוֹא הָעִיר, וְעָשִׂינוּ עִמְּךָ, חָסֶד.
And the watchers saw a man coming out of the city, and they said to him, "Show us, please, the way into the city, and we will deal kindly with you."note
Y los vigías vieron a un hombre que salía de la ciudad, y le dijeron: «Muéstranos, por favor, la entrada de la ciudad, y trataremos contigo con bondad».
25
וַיַּרְאֵם אֶת-מְבוֹא הָעִיר, וַיַּכּוּ אֶת-הָעִיר לְפִי-חָרֶב; וְאֶת-הָאִישׁ וְאֶת-כָּל-מִשְׁפַּחְתּוֹ, שִׁלֵּחוּ.
And he showed them the way into the city, and they struck the city with the edge of the sword; but the man and all his family they let go.note
Y él les mostró la entrada de la ciudad, e hirieron la ciudad a filo de espada; pero al hombre y a toda su familia los dejaron ir.
26
וַיֵּלֶךְ הָאִישׁ, אֶרֶץ הַחִתִּים; וַיִּבֶן עִיר, וַיִּקְרָא שְׁמָהּ לוּז--הוּא שְׁמָהּ, עַד הַיּוֹם הַזֶּה.{פ}
And the man went to the land of the Hittites and built a city and called its name Luz — that is its name to this day.note
Y el hombre fue a la tierra de los heteos y edificó una ciudad y la llamó Luz — ese es su nombre hasta el día de hoy.
27
וְלֹא-הוֹרִישׁ מְנַשֶּׁה, אֶת-בֵּית-שְׁאָן וְאֶת-בְּנוֹתֶיהָ וְאֶת-תַּעְנַךְ וְאֶת-בְּנֹתֶיהָ, וְאֶת-יֹשְׁבֵי דוֹר וְאֶת-בְּנוֹתֶיהָ וְאֶת-יוֹשְׁבֵי יִבְלְעָם וְאֶת-בְּנֹתֶיהָ, וְאֶת-יוֹשְׁבֵי מְגִדּוֹ וְאֶת-בְּנוֹתֶיהָ; וַיּוֹאֶל, הַכְּנַעֲנִי, לָשֶׁבֶת, בָּאָרֶץ הַזֹּאת.
And Manasseh did not drive out Beth-shean and its daughter-towns, nor Taanach and its daughter-towns, nor the inhabitants of Dor and its daughter-towns, nor the inhabitants of Ibleam and its daughter-towns, nor the inhabitants of Megiddo and its daughter-towns; and the Canaanite was determined to dwell in that land.note
Y Manasés no expulsó a Bet-seán y sus aldeas, ni a Taanac y sus aldeas, ni a los habitantes de Dor y sus aldeas, ni a los habitantes de Ibleam y sus aldeas, ni a los habitantes de Meguido y sus aldeas; y el cananeo estaba resuelto a habitar en aquella tierra.
28
וַיְהִי כִּי-חָזַק יִשְׂרָאֵל, וַיָּשֶׂם אֶת-הַכְּנַעֲנִי לָמַס; וְהוֹרֵישׁ, לֹא הוֹרִישׁוֹ.{ס}
And it happened, when Israel grew strong, that they put the Canaanite to forced labour; but they did not utterly drive him out. note
Y sucedió, cuando Israel se hizo fuerte, que puso al cananeo a trabajos forzados; pero no lo expulsó del todo.
cross-refs⤷ Exod 1:11
29
וְאֶפְרַיִם לֹא הוֹרִישׁ, אֶת-הַכְּנַעֲנִי הַיּוֹשֵׁב בְּגָזֶר; וַיֵּשֶׁב הַכְּנַעֲנִי בְּקִרְבּוֹ, בְּגָזֶר.{ס}
And Ephraim did not drive out the Canaanite who dwelt in Gezer; and the Canaanite dwelt in his midst, in Gezer.note
Y Efraín no expulsó al cananeo que habitaba en Gézer; y el cananeo habitó en medio de él, en Gézer.
30
זְבוּלֻן, לֹא הוֹרִישׁ אֶת-יוֹשְׁבֵי קִטְרוֹן, וְאֶת-יוֹשְׁבֵי, נַהֲלֹל; וַיֵּשֶׁב הַכְּנַעֲנִי בְּקִרְבּוֹ, וַיִּהְיוּ לָמַס.{ס}
Zebulun did not drive out the inhabitants of Kitron or the inhabitants of Nahalol; and the Canaanite dwelt in his midst and became forced labour.note

Translator's Notes

Verses 1–7 · after Joshua, and a king who did the same

The book begins the way Joshua did — "after the death of…" — and that is the point. Joshua opened "after the death of Moses"; Judges opens "after the death of Joshua." ⚠️ The pattern is deliberate and the book will make it terrible: Israel's story has become a sequence of leaders dying and a people left to see whether they will hold. This first chapter is the record of the generation right after Joshua trying to finish the conquest — and, tribe by tribe, failing to. It is the honest, unheroic underside of the book of Joshua, and the two books were almost certainly meant to be read against each other.

They ask before they act. "The sons of Israel inquired of Jehovah" — who goes up first? — and the answer is Judah, with the land declared already given ("I have given the land into his hand," the same settled-fact perfect as Joshua 1). The book opens well: they consult God, God answers, Judah wins. Watch how far it has fallen by the last verse of the chapter, and how much further by the last chapter of the book.

⚠️ Adoni-bezek, and a rough justice the text lets stand. A Canaanite king is caught and his thumbs and big toes are cut off — a real and horrible mutilation that disables a warrior (no grip, no balance) and humiliates a king. And then the man convicts himself: seventy kings with their thumbs and toes cut off used to gather scraps under my table; as I have done, so God has repaid me. KJV "as I have done, so God hath requited me"; the ASV the same. ⚠️ The narrator does not moralise; he lets a pagan king pronounce the sentence on his own cruelty and name the God of Israel as the one who balanced it. It is a grim opening note — Israel does to him exactly what he had done to others, and the victim calls it justice — and the book is signalling early that it will not clean up what its people do.

Verses 8–10 · Jerusalem taken — and not

⚠️ Verse 8 says Judah burned Jerusalem; verse 21 says the Jebusites still live there. Both stand. This is not a contradiction to hide but a seam to understand. Taking a city in ancient warfare — burning it, striking its fighters — is not the same as holding it and driving out its people; a raided city is reoccupied. Jerusalem is the perfect example: Judah strikes it here, Benjamin fails to dispossess it in verse 21, and it will not truly become Israelite until David takes the stronghold generations later (2 Samuel 5). The chapter is built on exactly this distinction — the difference between a victory and a possession — and Jerusalem is where it is starkest.

"Formerly Kiriath-arba." Hebron's older name means "the town of Arba," a giant, the ancestor of the Anakim — which is why the three names struck down here (Sheshai, Ahiman, Talmai) are the giants Caleb had marked out. The scene is a compressed retelling of material also in Joshua's conquest account; the two books overlap on purpose.

Verses 11–15 · Achsah, who asked for water

A whole marriage-and-inheritance story in five verses, and its hero is a woman. Caleb offers his daughter Achsah to whoever takes Kiriath-sepher; his younger kinsman Othniel does it (he becomes the first of the judges in chapter 3, so the book is quietly introducing its first deliverer as a footnote to a wedding). But the story is hers. ⚠️ She is the one who acts. She urges Othniel to ask her father for a field; then, when that is not enough, she goes to Caleb herself, slides down off her donkey — a gesture of respect, or of urgency — and asks outright: you have given me the dry Negev; give me springs of water too. And he does — the upper springs and the lower. In a chapter that is otherwise a catalogue of men failing to take land, a woman successfully negotiates for the one thing that makes land worth having, and gets both halves of what she asks.

"Give me a blessing." Havah li verakhah — berakhah, "blessing," used here concretely of a gift, a favour granted, and specifically of well-watered land. It is the same word that runs through the patriarchal stories; here it is a daughter claiming her portion, and she is right to, and her father agrees.

Verses 16–18 · the Kenites, Hormah, and the coast

The Kenites come in with Israel, not against it. The "sons of the Kenite, Moses' father-in-law" — the family of Jethro/Hobab — travel up from the City of Palms (Jericho) and settle peacefully among Judah. ⚠️ It is a small, important counter-note in a chapter about clearing out Canaanites: a non-Israelite people is absorbed by welcome rather than the sword, remembered as kin because of Moses' marriage. The Kenites matter later — it is a Kenite woman, Jael, who kills Sisera in chapter 4.

"Put it to the ban… called Hormah." Vayacharimu — from cherem, the devoted-to-destruction, the hardest concept in the conquest: a thing so given over to God that it is destroyed rather than taken as plunder. The town's new name, Hormah, is built from the same root — "Destruction" — so the naming freezes the act into the map. ⚠️ The cherem is the sharpest ethical difficulty in these books and this translation neither softens it nor explains it away; it renders it as what it is and leaves the weight of it in the note.

⚠️ Verse 18 says Judah took Gaza, Ashkelon and Ekron — three of the great Philistine cities — and it is a genuine tension. Everywhere else, including verse 19 immediately after, those coastal cities stay firmly out of Israel's hands (the Philistines are the great unconquered enemy of the whole book and of Samuel). The KJV and NWT follow the Masoretic "took Gaza… Ashkelon… Ekron"; the Septuagint resolves the tension by reading the opposite — "Judah did not take Gaza…" — and many take that as the original. This translation prints the Masoretic text as it stands and flags the variant; it may record a temporary raid that did not hold, which would fit the chapter's whole theme, or it may be a textual slip. The reading is genuinely unsettled.

Verses 19–21 · "but he could not, because they had iron"

⚠️ Here is the theological knot of the chapter, stated in one verse. "Jehovah was with Judah, and he took the hill country; but he could not drive out the inhabitants of the valley, because they had chariots of iron." KJV "but could not drive out the inhabitants of the valley, because they had chariots of iron"; the NIV the same. Read the two halves together: God was with them, and they could not win, because of military hardware. ⚠️ The book does not resolve which it means — that God's presence has limits against iron (which the rest of scripture flatly denies; God routs chariots elsewhere without trouble), or, far more likely given where the book is going, that the failure was Israel's, not God's, and "iron chariots" is the excuse Israel told itself. The very next chapters make the second reading unavoidable: Judges 2 says plainly that the tribes did not drive out the nations because they disobeyed, not because they were outgunned. The "could not" of verse 19 is the beginning of a self-deception the book is documenting.

"To this day." The Jebusites are still in Jerusalem "to this day" (v. 21) — a narrator's aside written from a time when it was still true, i.e. before David. It is one of the small phrases that dates the writing from the inside, and it is honest bookkeeping: the conquest is on the record as unfinished.

Verses 22–26 · Bethel, and a bargain at the gate

An unsettling little episode, and the text knows it. The house of Joseph takes Bethel not by assault but by turning an informer: they catch a man leaving the city and promise to "deal kindly" with him — chesed, covenant-loyalty — if he shows them the way in. He does; they destroy the city; they keep their word to him and let his family go. ⚠️ It is a deliberate echo of Rahab at Jericho, and also its shadow: there a Canaanite risked her life to help Israel out of faith and was spared; here a Canaanite betrays his own city to save his skin and is spared. The word chesed sits uncomfortably over a transaction that is really just a deal with a traitor. And then the sting: the man goes off and founds another Luz in Hittite territory — the old Canaanite culture is not destroyed, it relocates and rebuilds. Nothing is fully finished here either.

Verses 27–33 · "did not drive out" — seven times

This is the engine of the chapter, and it is a drumbeat. Read the refrain down the tribes: Manasseh did not drive out (v. 27), Ephraim did not drive out (v. 29), Zebulun did not drive out (v. 30), Asher did not drive out (v. 31), Naphtali did not drive out (v. 33). ⚠️ The verb horish, "dispossess," tolls again and again, each time with a negative, and the chapter has quietly inverted itself: it opened with Judah's victories and ends with a litany of failures marching north up the map. The list is geographical — the further from Judah, the worse it gets — and it is building toward a verdict the next chapter will deliver.

"Forced labour," not conquest. Where the tribes are strong enough, they do not expel the Canaanites but put them to mas — corvée, forced labour (KJV "put the Canaanites to tribute"; the ESV "put the Canaanites to forced labor"), the same word used of what Egypt did to Israel and of what Solomon later did to the survivors. ⚠️ It is presented as strength ("when Israel grew strong") but it is the opposite of the command: they were told to drive out, and instead they kept and exploited. Slavery is the compromise they reach with disobedience, and the irony is unmissable — a people redeemed from forced labour imposing it. The whole book of Judges is about to show what living beside the people you were told to remove actually does to you.

"The Canaanite was determined to dwell there." Vayo'el — he was willing, resolved, undeterred. The verb quietly credits the Canaanites with the resolve Israel lacked: they meant to stay, and did.

Verses 34–36 · Dan, pushed off its own land

The bottom of the slide. The chapter began with a tribe (Judah) driving Canaanites out of the hill country; it ends with a tribe (Dan) being driven by the Amorites into the hill country and not even allowed down into its own valley. ⚠️ The reversal is complete and deliberate: the conqueror has become the confined. Dan's failure here is the seed of one of the book's darkest later stories — in Judges 18 the tribe gives up on its assigned land altogether and migrates north to seize a quiet, unsuspecting town instead. The last named place in the chapter, the Ascent of Scorpions, is a fittingly bleak note to end on: a boundary line marking how far the enemy's territory reached, in a chapter about how much of the land Israel never took.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the words exact / distinct: "did not drive out" for horish negated, kept identical down the tribal list so the drumbeat is audible; "put it to the ban" / "Hormah" for cherem and its name, unsoftened; "forced labour" for mas, the same word as Egypt's slavery; "deal kindly" for chesed, left as covenant-loyalty even where it covers a deal with a traitor; "was determined to dwell" for vayo'el; and Gaza/Ashkelon/Ekron printed per the Masoretic text with the Septuagint's negative in the note.

Echoes paid. Joshua's own opening, "after the death of…," repeated over Joshua himself; the conquest of Joshua retold from underneath, as the part that did not get finished; Judah given the first place in Jacob's blessing, made good here at the head of the fighting; and Rahab's rescue at Jericho, shadowed by the informer at Bethel.

Echoes planted. Othniel, the first judge, slipped in as a bridegroom; the Kenites, whose kinship will matter when Jael drives a tent-peg through Sisera; the Jebusites in Jerusalem, waiting for David; Dan pushed off its land, on the road to Judges 18; and the whole refrain of "did not drive out," which Judges 2 will turn from a report into an indictment.

Next installment in this book: Judges 2 — the angel of Jehovah comes up from Gilgal to Bochim and reads the verdict this chapter has been building toward, the people weep, Joshua's generation dies, and "another generation arose after them who did not know Jehovah" — and the long, terrible cycle of the judges begins.
