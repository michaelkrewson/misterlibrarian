Translator’s Notes

Verse 1 · a prophet with no date, and a book with four chapters

⚠ This heading is missing the one thing every other prophetic heading has: a KING. Isaiah, Hosea, Amos and Micah all date themselves by reigns; Joel gives a name, a father’s name, and nothing else. Nothing else in the book helps either — no king, no foreign empire named as the current threat, no datable event. The result is that Joel’s date is the most disputed in the Bible: serious proposals run from the ninth century to the fourth, a spread of five hundred years, and they are argued from the same evidence read in opposite directions (no king mentioned — because there wasn’t one yet, or because there wasn’t one any more). The library sets out the arguments in the book introduction and does not pick.

⚠ And this book has FOUR chapters, not three. The Hebrew runs 1:1–20, 2:1–27, 3:1–5, 4:1–21; English Bibles fold Hebrew chapter 3 into chapter 2 (Hebrew 3:1–5 = English 2:28–32) and renumber Hebrew 4 as English 3. That matters because the passage Peter quotes at Pentecost — “I will pour out my spirit on all flesh” — is Hebrew 3:1, which most readers know as 2:28. We follow the Masoretic numbering, as everywhere on this site. It is the second such offset in two books: Hosea 1 has the same problem in the other direction.

Verses 2–4 · four words for locust, and nobody knows what they are

The opening is a summons to remember. “Has anything like this happened in your days, or your fathers’?” and then: tell your children, and their children, and the generation after that. Whatever has happened, its first significance is that it must be reported forward — which is the reason there is a book.

⚠ Verse 4 uses FOUR different Hebrew words, and the truth is that nobody knows what distinguishes them. They are gazam, arbeh, yeleq and chasil. The three main proposals: four species of locust; four growth stages of the one insect (a locust moults five times, and each stage eats); or four poetic names piled up for sheer weight, the way English might say swarm, plague, host, cloud. Only arbeh is common — it is the word for the eighth plague of Egypt — and the other three are rare enough that their meanings are reconstructed from context and cognates.

The versions show the confusion plainly. KJV has “palmerworm … locust … cankerworm … caterpiller”; ASV keeps that quartet; NIV reads them as stages — “the locust swarm … great locusts … young locusts … other locusts”; NWT renders “caterpillar … locust swarm … creeping unwinged locust … cockroach.” The Septuagint used four different Greek words too, and did not agree with any of them. We translate by what each root seems to do — shearer, swarmer, licker, finisher — which preserves the one thing the verse certainly conveys: four waves, each finishing what the last one left.

Verses 5–7 · an army with a lion’s teeth, and whose vine it is

Wake the drunkards first. It is a deliberately undignified place to start — not the priests, not the elders, but the men who will notice the disaster when the wine runs out. Asis is the sweet new wine of the fresh pressing, and the point is that there will not be a pressing. KJV “howl, all ye drinkers of wine, because of the new wine”; NIV “wail, all you drinkers of wine; wail because of the new wine”; NWT “because of the sweet wine, for it has been taken from your mouths.”

⚠ «A nation has come up against my land» is the sentence the whole book turns on. The word is goy, the ordinary word for a foreign nation, applied to insects — with a lion’s teeth, past counting, coming up against the land the way an army does. So which is it? Three readings, all ancient: (1) a literal locust plague, described in military language; (2) a literal army, described as locusts; (3) a locust plague that Joel reads as the advance sign of the day of Jehovah, so that the insects and the army are the same event seen at two depths. The third is the majority view now, and it is the one that makes chapter 2 legible. The library records all three.

Notice the possessive. “My land,” “my vine,” “my fig tree” — the speaker has shifted mid-sentence from the prophet to God, without announcement, and the loss is described as God’s own property being stripped. KJV “He hath laid my vine waste, and barked my fig tree”; NWT “It has made my vine an object of horror.”

Verses 8–12 · the loss measured in liturgy, not in bushels

«Wail like a young woman … for the husband of her youth» — the imperative is feminine singular, addressed to the land or the city rather than to the crowd, and the noun for husband is ba’al in its plain sense of master of a household. Betulah here is a young woman betrothed but not yet living with her husband: the grief is for something that had barely started.

⚠ Verse 9 is the theological centre of the chapter, and it is easy to read past. The catastrophe is not measured in tonnage. It is measured by what has stopped at the temple: grain offering and drink offering are cut off from the house of Jehovah. Those two are the daily offerings — flour, oil and wine, presented every morning and evening (Exodus 29:38–42). Every version keeps the two technical terms (KJV “the meat offering and the drink offering”; ASV “the meal-offering and the drink-offering”; NWT “grain offering and drink offering”), and they are right to. When the harvest fails, the daily service simply cannot be performed. The locusts have not merely made people hungry; they have severed the country’s standing conversation with God, and that is why the priests are the ones told to mourn.

The vocabulary of verses 10–12 is agricultural and exact: dagan (threshed grain), tirosh (new wine, unfermented), yitzhar (fresh-pressed oil) — the standard triad of the land’s produce, all three failed in one line; then wheat and barley, then vine, fig, pomegranate, palm and apple. Joel names them individually rather than saying “the crops,” and the effect is a list of specific, familiar things gone.

The last clause of verse 12 turns the same verb on the people. The trees hovish, dried up — and then “joy has hovish from the sons of man.” The word means both to dry out and to be ashamed, and Joel uses it four times in the chapter, letting the two senses run together. ASV “joy is withered away from the sons of men”; NIV “the people’s joy is withered away.”

Verses 13–14 · the instruction the whole book exists to give

«Sanctify a fast; call a solemn assembly» — an atzarah is a formal convocation, the word used for the closing day of a festival. Joel takes the machinery of celebration and reassigns it to catastrophe: the same summons that gathers the nation for a feast is used to gather it to cry out. This is the book’s practical demand, and it will be repeated at 2:15 in almost identical words. KJV “Sanctify ye a fast, call a solemn assembly”; ASV the same; NIV “Declare a holy fast; call a sacred assembly”; NWT “Sanctify a fast. Call for a solemn assembly.”

«Spend the night in sackcloth» is not a figure of speech. The priests are told to sleep in it — to stay in mourning through the dark hours, at the sanctuary, rather than perform a daytime ritual and go home.

Note who is summoned: the elders and all the inhabitants of the land, the same phrase as verse 2. Joel never addresses a king, a court, an army or a policy. His audience is a population and a priesthood, which is one of the few genuine clues to when and where he wrote.

Verses 15–17 · the Day of Jehovah, a pun that cannot be carried, and the hardest verse in the book

⚠ «The day of Jehovah» enters here, and Joel is the book that makes it a doctrine. The phrase occurs five times in these four chapters — more concentrated than anywhere else in the Bible — and Joel does something with it that a reader should not miss: the Day is not only for Israel’s enemies. Amos had already made that point bitterly (5:18, “why do you want the day of Jehovah? it is darkness and not light”), and here the locusts arriving on Judah’s own fields are its near edge.

⚠ And the second half of verse 15 is a pun no translation can carry. Ke-shod mi-Shaddai yavo — like SHOD from SHADDAI it comes. Shod means violent destruction; Shaddai is the ancient divine title of Genesis 17 whose meaning is itself uncertain. The two words sound almost identical and are probably unrelated, and Joel puts them side by side anyway. KJV “as a destruction from the Almighty shall it come”; ASV the same; NWT “It will come as a destruction from the Almighty.” Every version gives the sense and loses the sound; we render Shaddai as the Destroyer to keep at least the echo, and flag that this is a choice about sound, not a claim about the name’s etymology.

⚠ Verse 17 contains THREE words that occur nowhere else in the Hebrew Bible — avshu, perudot and megrefoteihem — in a single clause. That is an extraordinary concentration, and it means every English rendering of this verse is a reasoned guess. KJV “The seed is rotten under their clods”; ASV “The seeds rot under their clods”; NIV “The seeds are shriveled beneath the clods”; NWT “The shriveled figs have shrunk beneath their shovels.” The versions agree on the gist — something in the ground has failed — and on almost nothing else. We follow the majority and say plainly that it is uncertain.

Verses 18–20 · the animals, and a verb from a psalm

The chapter ends with the cattle. After the drunkards, the farmers, the priests and the whole population, Joel turns to the herds — groaning, milling about with nowhere to graze — and this is not sentimentality. In a famine the livestock are the last reserve, and their distress is the point past which nothing is left. KJV “How do the beasts groan! the herds of cattle are perplexed”; NIV “How the cattle moan! The herds mill about.”

⚠ And the last verse borrows a verb from one of the best-known lines in the Psalms. Ta’arog occurs only three times in the Hebrew Bible: twice in Psalm 42:2 — as the deer PANTS for streams of water, so my soul pants for you, God — and here. Joel gives the psalm’s verb to the animals: even the beasts of the field pant for you, because the water channels have dried up. The most famous image of spiritual longing in the Bible turns out to have been, in its other occurrence, a literal thirsty animal at an empty streambed.

Verses 19–20 also introduce fire, which has not been mentioned before — drought and scorching on top of the locusts, or the same devastation described in a second image. Either way the chapter closes on the prophet doing what he told everyone else to do in verse 14: to you, Jehovah, I call. The book’s answer begins in the next chapter.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the exact / distinct words: four different English words for the four locusts of verse 4 — shearer, swarmer, licker, finisher — chosen for what each root seems to DO, since nobody knows whether they are species, stages or synonyms, and levelling them to “locusts” would erase the one thing the verse certainly says; “grain offering and drink offering” kept as the technical pair, because the disaster is being measured liturgically; “a nation” for goy in verse 6, the ordinary word for a foreign people, applied to insects; “the Destroyer” for Shaddai in verse 15, to keep the shod/Shaddai echo — flagged as a choice about SOUND, not a claim about the name’s meaning; and “pant” in verse 20, the rare verb this translation also owes to Psalm 42.

Echoes paid. Arbeh, the locust of the eighth plague in Exodus 10, now one of four. Shaddai, the name announced in Genesis 17:1, punned against destruction. The daily grain and drink offerings of Exodus 29, stopped. And ta’arog, the panting deer of Psalm 42, given here to real animals at a dry streambed.

Echoes planted. The day of Jehovah, five times in four chapters — Joel is where the phrase becomes a doctrine, and where it is aimed at Judah before anyone else. «Sanctify a fast, call a solemn assembly», repeated at 2:15. The locusts, which chapter 2 turns into a described army and God then promises to repay — “I will restore to you the years the locust has eaten” (2:25). And the outpoured spirit of Hebrew 3:1 — the verse most readers know as 2:28, and the one Peter stands up and quotes at Pentecost.
