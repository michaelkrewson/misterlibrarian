Genesis 43 — The Mister Translation

1
וְהָרָעָב, כָּבֵד בָּאָרֶץ.
And the famine was heavy in the land.note
Y el hambre era pesada en la tierra.
2
וַיְהִי, כַּאֲשֶׁר כִּלּוּ לֶאֱכֹל אֶת-הַשֶּׁבֶר, אֲשֶׁר הֵבִיאוּ, מִמִּצְרָיִם; וַיֹּאמֶר אֲלֵיהֶם אֲבִיהֶם, שֻׁבוּ שִׁבְרוּ-לָנוּ מְעַט-אֹכֶל.
And so it was, when they had finished eating the grain they had brought from Egypt, that their father said to them, "Go back, buy us a little food."note
Y así fue que, cuando acabaron de comer el grano que habían traído de Egipto, su padre les dijo: «Vuelvan, cómprennos un poco de alimento».
3
וַיֹּאמֶר אֵלָיו יְהוּדָה, לֵאמֹר: הָעֵד הֵעִד בָּנוּ הָאִישׁ לֵאמֹר לֹא-תִרְאוּ פָנַי, בִּלְתִּי אֲחִיכֶם אִתְּכֶם.
And Judah said to him, "The man solemnly warned us, saying, 'You will not see my face unless your brother is with you.'note
Y Judá le dijo: «El hombre nos advirtió solemnemente: "No verán mi rostro si su hermano no está con ustedes".
4
אִם-יֶשְׁךָ מְשַׁלֵּחַ אֶת-אָחִינוּ, אִתָּנוּ--נֵרְדָה, וְנִשְׁבְּרָה לְךָ אֹכֶל.
If you will send our brother with us, we will go down and buy you food.note
Si envías a nuestro hermano con nosotros, bajaremos y te compraremos alimento.
5
וְאִם-אֵינְךָ מְשַׁלֵּחַ, לֹא נֵרֵד: כִּי-הָאִישׁ אָמַר אֵלֵינוּ, לֹא-תִרְאוּ פָנַי, בִּלְתִּי, אֲחִיכֶם אִתְּכֶם.
But if you will not send him, we will not go down — for the man said to us, 'You will not see my face unless your brother is with you.'"note
Pero si no lo envías, no bajaremos — porque el hombre nos dijo: "No verán mi rostro si su hermano no está con ustedes"».
6
וַיֹּאמֶר, יִשְׂרָאֵל, לָמָה הֲרֵעֹתֶם, לִי--לְהַגִּיד לָאִישׁ, הַעוֹד לָכֶם אָח.
And Israel said, "Why did you deal badly with me, to tell the man you had another brother?"note
Y dijo Israel: «¿Por qué me hicieron este mal, contándole al hombre que tenían otro hermano?»
7
וַיֹּאמְרוּ שָׁאוֹל שָׁאַל-הָאִישׁ לָנוּ וּלְמוֹלַדְתֵּנוּ לֵאמֹר, הַעוֹד אֲבִיכֶם חַי הֲיֵשׁ לָכֶם אָח, וַנַּגֶּד-לוֹ, עַל-פִּי הַדְּבָרִים הָאֵלֶּה; הֲיָדוֹעַ נֵדַע--כִּי יֹאמַר, הוֹרִידוּ אֶת-אֲחִיכֶם.
And they said, "The man asked and asked about us and about our kindred, saying, 'Is your father still alive? Do you have a brother?' And we told him according to these words. Could we possibly know that he would say, 'Bring your brother down'?"note
Y ellos dijeron: «El hombre preguntó y preguntó por nosotros y por nuestra parentela: "¿Vive aún su padre? ¿Tienen un hermano?" Y se lo dijimos conforme a estas palabras. ¿Cómo íbamos a saber que diría: "Traigan a su hermano"?»
8
וַיֹּאמֶר יְהוּדָה אֶל-יִשְׂרָאֵל אָבִיו, שִׁלְחָה הַנַּעַר אִתִּי--וְנָקוּמָה וְנֵלֵכָה; וְנִחְיֶה וְלֹא נָמוּת, גַּם-אֲנַחְנוּ גַם-אַתָּה גַּם-טַפֵּנוּ.
And Judah said to Israel his father, "Send the boy with me, and we will rise and go — that we may live and not die, we and you and our little ones.note
Y Judá dijo a Israel su padre: «Envía al muchacho conmigo, y nos levantaremos e iremos — para que vivamos y no muramos, nosotros y tú y nuestros pequeños.
9
אָנֹכִי, אֶעֶרְבֶנּוּ--מִיָּדִי, תְּבַקְשֶׁנּוּ: אִם-לֹא הֲבִיאֹתִיו אֵלֶיךָ וְהִצַּגְתִּיו לְפָנֶיךָ, וְחָטָאתִי לְךָ כָּל-הַיָּמִים.
I myself will be surety for him — from my hand you shall require him. If I do not bring him to you and set him before you, then I will have sinned against you all my days.note
Yo mismo saldré por fiador de él — de mi mano lo reclamarás. Si no te lo traigo y lo pongo delante de ti, habré pecado contra ti todos los días.
cross-refs⤷ 44:32⤷ 42:37
10
כִּי, לוּלֵא הִתְמַהְמָהְנוּ--כִּי-עַתָּה שַׁבְנוּ, זֶה פַעֲמָיִם.
For if we had not delayed, by now we would have returned twice over."note
Porque si no nos hubiéramos demorado, ya habríamos vuelto dos veces».
11
וַיֹּאמֶר אֲלֵהֶם יִשְׂרָאֵל אֲבִיהֶם, אִם-כֵּן אֵפוֹא זֹאת עֲשׂוּ--קְחוּ מִזִּמְרַת הָאָרֶץ בִּכְלֵיכֶם, וְהוֹרִידוּ לָאִישׁ מִנְחָה: מְעַט צֳרִי, וּמְעַט דְּבַשׁ, נְכֹאת וָלֹט, בָּטְנִים וּשְׁקֵדִים.
And Israel their father said to them, "If it must be so, then do this: take some of the land's best in your vessels and carry down a gift to the man — a little balm and a little honey, gum and ladanum, pistachios and almonds.note
Y les dijo Israel su padre: «Si así ha de ser, hagan esto: tomen de lo mejor de la tierra en sus recipientes y bajen al hombre un regalo — un poco de bálsamo y un poco de miel, goma y ládano, pistachos y almendras.
cross-refs⤷ 37:25
12
וְכֶסֶף מִשְׁנֶה, קְחוּ בְיֶדְכֶם; וְאֶת-הַכֶּסֶף הַמּוּשָׁב בְּפִי אַמְתְּחֹתֵיכֶם, תָּשִׁיבוּ בְיֶדְכֶם--אוּלַי מִשְׁגֶּה, הוּא.
And take double silver in your hand; and the silver that was returned in the mouth of your bags, take back in your hand — perhaps it was a mistake.note
Y tomen en su mano el doble de plata; y la plata que les fue devuelta en la boca de sus sacos, llévenla de vuelta en su mano — quizá fue un error.
13
וְאֶת-אֲחִיכֶם, קָחוּ; וְקוּמוּ, שׁוּבוּ אֶל-הָאִישׁ.
And take your brother, and rise, go back to the man.note
Y tomen a su hermano, y levántense, vuelvan al hombre.
14
וְאֵל שַׁדַּי, יִתֵּן לָכֶם רַחֲמִים לִפְנֵי הָאִישׁ, וְשִׁלַּח לָכֶם אֶת-אֲחִיכֶם אַחֵר, וְאֶת-בִּנְיָמִין; וַאֲנִי, כַּאֲשֶׁר שָׁכֹלְתִּי שָׁכָלְתִּי.
And may El Shaddai give you compassion before the man, that he may send back to you your other brother, and Benjamin. And as for me — if I am bereaved, I am bereaved."note
Y que El Shaddai les dé compasión delante del hombre, para que les devuelva a su otro hermano y a Benjamín. Y en cuanto a mí — si he de quedar sin hijos, quedaré sin hijos».
cross-refs⤷ 43:30⤷ 17:1
15
וַיִּקְחוּ הָאֲנָשִׁים אֶת-הַמִּנְחָה הַזֹּאת, וּמִשְׁנֶה-כֶּסֶף לָקְחוּ בְיָדָם וְאֶת-בִּנְיָמִן; וַיָּקֻמוּ וַיֵּרְדוּ מִצְרַיִם, וַיַּעַמְדוּ לִפְנֵי יוֹסֵף.
And the men took this gift, and they took double silver in their hand, and Benjamin; and they rose and went down to Egypt and stood before Joseph.note
Y los hombres tomaron este regalo, y tomaron en su mano el doble de plata, y a Benjamín; y se levantaron y bajaron a Egipto y se presentaron delante de José.
16
וַיַּרְא יוֹסֵף אִתָּם, אֶת-בִּנְיָמִין, וַיֹּאמֶר לַאֲשֶׁר עַל-בֵּיתוֹ, הָבֵא אֶת-הָאֲנָשִׁים הַבָּיְתָה; וּטְבֹחַ טֶבַח וְהָכֵן, כִּי אִתִּי יֹאכְלוּ הָאֲנָשִׁים בַּצָּהֳרָיִם.
And Joseph saw Benjamin with them, and he said to the man over his house, "Bring the men into the house, and slaughter an animal and prepare it, for the men will eat with me at noon."note
Y José vio a Benjamín con ellos, y dijo al que estaba sobre su casa: «Lleva a estos hombres a la casa, y degüella una res y prepárala, porque estos hombres comerán conmigo al mediodía».
17
וַיַּעַשׂ הָאִישׁ, כַּאֲשֶׁר אָמַר יוֹסֵף; וַיָּבֵא הָאִישׁ אֶת-הָאֲנָשִׁים, בֵּיתָה יוֹסֵף.
And the man did as Joseph had said, and the man brought the men into Joseph's house.note
Y el hombre hizo como José había dicho, y llevó a los hombres a la casa de José.
18
וַיִּירְאוּ הָאֲנָשִׁים, כִּי הוּבְאוּ בֵּית יוֹסֵף, וַיֹּאמְרוּ עַל-דְּבַר הַכֶּסֶף הַשָּׁב בְּאַמְתְּחֹתֵינוּ בַּתְּחִלָּה, אֲנַחְנוּ מוּבָאִים--לְהִתְגֹּלֵל עָלֵינוּ וּלְהִתְנַפֵּל עָלֵינוּ, וְלָקַחַת אֹתָנוּ לַעֲבָדִים וְאֶת-חֲמֹרֵינוּ.
And the men were afraid because they were brought into Joseph's house, and they said, "It is because of the silver that was returned in our bags the first time that we are being brought in — to roll himself upon us and fall upon us, and take us as slaves, and our donkeys."note
Y los hombres tuvieron miedo porque los llevaban a la casa de José, y dijeron: «Es por la plata que nos fue devuelta en nuestros sacos la primera vez que nos traen aquí — para echarse sobre nosotros y caer sobre nosotros, y tomarnos por esclavos, y a nuestros asnos».
19
וַיִּגְּשׁוּ, אֶל-הָאִישׁ, אֲשֶׁר, עַל-בֵּית יוֹסֵף; וַיְדַבְּרוּ אֵלָיו, פֶּתַח הַבָּיִת.
And they came near to the man who was over Joseph's house and spoke to him at the entrance of the house,note
Y se acercaron al hombre que estaba sobre la casa de José y le hablaron a la entrada de la casa,
20
וַיֹּאמְרוּ, בִּי אֲדֹנִי; יָרֹד יָרַדְנוּ בַּתְּחִלָּה, לִשְׁבָּר-אֹכֶל.
and they said, "Please, my lord — we came down, indeed came down, the first time to buy food.note
y dijeron: «Por favor, señor mío — bajamos, sí bajamos, la primera vez a comprar alimento.
21
וַיְהִי כִּי-בָאנוּ אֶל-הַמָּלוֹן, וַנִּפְתְּחָה אֶת-אַמְתְּחֹתֵינוּ, וְהִנֵּה כֶסֶף-אִישׁ בְּפִי אַמְתַּחְתּוֹ, כַּסְפֵּנוּ בְּמִשְׁקָלוֹ; וַנָּשֶׁב אֹתוֹ, בְּיָדֵנוּ.
And so it was, when we came to the lodging place and opened our bags — look, each man's silver was in the mouth of his bag, our silver in its full weight; and we have brought it back in our hand.note
Y así fue que, al llegar al mesón y abrir nuestros sacos — mira, la plata de cada uno estaba en la boca de su saco, nuestra plata en su peso completo; y la hemos traído de vuelta en nuestra mano.
22
וְכֶסֶף אַחֵר הוֹרַדְנוּ בְיָדֵנוּ, לִשְׁבָּר-אֹכֶל; לֹא יָדַעְנוּ, מִי-שָׂם כַּסְפֵּנוּ בְּאַמְתְּחֹתֵינוּ.
And we have brought down other silver in our hand to buy food. We do not know who put our silver in our bags."note
Y hemos bajado otra plata en nuestra mano para comprar alimento. No sabemos quién puso nuestra plata en nuestros sacos».
23
וַיֹּאמֶר שָׁלוֹם לָכֶם אַל-תִּירָאוּ, אֱלֹהֵיכֶם וֵאלֹהֵי אֲבִיכֶם נָתַן לָכֶם מַטְמוֹן בְּאַמְתְּחֹתֵיכֶם--כַּסְפְּכֶם, בָּא אֵלָי; וַיּוֹצֵא אֲלֵהֶם, אֶת-שִׁמְעוֹן.
And he said, "Peace to you — do not be afraid. Your God and the God of your father gave you treasure in your bags; your silver came to me." And he brought Simeon out to them.note
Y él dijo: «Paz a ustedes — no teman. Su Dios y el Dios de su padre les dio un tesoro en sus sacos; la plata de ustedes llegó a mí». Y les sacó a Simeón.

24
וַיָּבֵא הָאִישׁ אֶת-הָאֲנָשִׁים, בֵּיתָה יוֹסֵף; וַיִּתֶּן-מַיִם וַיִּרְחֲצוּ רַגְלֵיהֶם, וַיִּתֵּן מִסְפּוֹא לַחֲמֹרֵיהֶם.
And the man brought the men into Joseph's house and gave them water, and they washed their feet; and he gave fodder to their donkeys.note
Y el hombre llevó a los hombres a la casa de José y les dio agua, y lavaron sus pies; y dio forraje a sus asnos.
25
וַיָּכִינוּ, אֶת-הַמִּנְחָה, עַד-בּוֹא יוֹסֵף, בַּצָּהֳרָיִם: כִּי שָׁמְעוּ, כִּי-שָׁם יֹאכְלוּ לָחֶם.
And they prepared the gift for Joseph's coming at noon, for they had heard that they would eat bread there.note
Y prepararon el regalo para la llegada de José al mediodía, porque habían oído que allí comerían pan.
26
וַיָּבֹא יוֹסֵף הַבַּיְתָה, וַיָּבִיאּוּ לוֹ אֶת-הַמִּנְחָה אֲשֶׁר-בְּיָדָם הַבָּיְתָה; וַיִּשְׁתַּחֲווּ-לוֹ, אָרְצָה.
And Joseph came into the house, and they brought him the gift that was in their hand, into the house, and bowed down to him to the ground.note
Y José entró en la casa, y le trajeron a la casa el regalo que tenían en su mano, y se inclinaron ante él hasta la tierra.

27
וַיִּשְׁאַל לָהֶם, לְשָׁלוֹם, וַיֹּאמֶר הֲשָׁלוֹם אֲבִיכֶם הַזָּקֵן, אֲשֶׁר אֲמַרְתֶּם--הַעוֹדֶנּוּ, חָי.
And he asked after their peace, and said, "Is your father well — the old man of whom you spoke? Is he still alive?"note
Y les preguntó por su paz, y dijo: «¿Está bien su padre, el anciano de quien hablaron? ¿Vive todavía?»
28
וַיֹּאמְרוּ, שָׁלוֹם לְעַבְדְּךָ לְאָבִינוּ--עוֹדֶנּוּ חָי; וַיִּקְּדוּ, וישתחו וַיִּשְׁתַּחֲווּ.
And they said, "Your servant our father is well; he is still alive." And they knelt and bowed down.note
Y ellos dijeron: «Tu siervo nuestro padre está bien; todavía vive». Y se arrodillaron y se inclinaron.
29
וַיִּשָּׂא עֵינָיו, וַיַּרְא אֶת-בִּנְיָמִין אָחִיו בֶּן-אִמּוֹ, וַיֹּאמֶר הֲזֶה אֲחִיכֶם הַקָּטֹן, אֲשֶׁר אֲמַרְתֶּם אֵלָי; וַיֹּאמַר, אֱלֹהִים יָחְנְךָ בְּנִי.
And he lifted his eyes and saw Benjamin his brother, his mother's son, and said, "Is this your youngest brother, of whom you spoke to me?" And he said, "God be gracious to you, my son."note
Y alzó sus ojos y vio a Benjamín su hermano, hijo de su madre, y dijo: «¿Es este su hermano menor, del que me hablaron?» Y dijo: «Dios te sea propicio, hijo mío».
30
וַיְמַהֵר יוֹסֵף, כִּי-נִכְמְרוּ רַחֲמָיו אֶל-אָחִיו, וַיְבַקֵּשׁ, לִבְכּוֹת; וַיָּבֹא הַחַדְרָה, וַיֵּבְךְּ שָׁמָּה.
And Joseph hurried out, for his compassion grew warm toward his brother, and he sought to weep; and he went into the inner room and wept there.note
Y José salió de prisa, porque su compasión se encendió hacia su hermano, y buscó dónde llorar; y entró en el aposento y lloró allí.

31
וַיִּרְחַץ פָּנָיו, וַיֵּצֵא; וַיִּתְאַפַּק--וַיֹּאמֶר, שִׂימוּ לָחֶם.
And he washed his face and came out, and held himself in, and said, "Serve the bread."note
Y se lavó el rostro y salió, y se contuvo, y dijo: «Sirvan el pan».

Translator's Notes

Verses 1–10 · Judah offers himself

The famine outlasts the food. Nothing has been resolved since ch. 42 — Simeon is still in Egypt, Jacob has still refused, and the family simply eats until the sacks are empty. The father's opening line is a masterpiece of avoidance: "Go back, buy us a little food," as though this were a small errand and not the thing he has forbidden. Judah has to say the quiet part out loud.

"Why did you deal badly with me?" Jacob's instinct under pressure is to find whose fault it is. The brothers' answer — "the man asked and asked about us and about our kindred" — is defensive and, as far as ch. 42 records, not quite true: the man asked whether they had a father and a brother only after they volunteered "we are twelve brothers… the one is not" (42:13). They are shading the account to their father again, exactly as they did on the first trip.

The sentence Reuben could not find. Here is the chapter's hinge, and it should be read against 42:37. Reuben's offer was "you may put my two sons to death if I do not bring him back" — a man proposing his own children as collateral, which is monstrous and also costs him nothing he has to feel. Judah says: "I myself will be surety for him — from my hand you shall require him." Arav is the commercial word for standing as guarantor of a debt: if the thing is lost, the surety pays it. He is putting himself up. And Jacob, who said an immediate and absolute no to Reuben, says yes to this. The KJV's "I will be surety for him; of my hand shalt thou require him" is exact and has never been bettered.

This is the same man from chapter 38. Judah proposed selling Joseph for a profit (37:26); Judah withheld Shelah from Tamar and would have burned her for it; and Judah is the one who said, out loud and in public, "she is more righteous than I." Genesis has been building this character quietly for five chapters, and the reader who wondered why chapter 38 interrupted the Joseph story gets the answer here: this is what the interruption was for. The man who learned to say "I was wrong" is the only one who can now say "hold me responsible."

Verses 11–14 · a gift that has been down this road before

Look at what is in the bags. Jacob sends "a little balm and a little honey, gum and ladanum, pistachios and almonds." The shelf wanders on the spice names — the KJV reads "spices, and myrrh, nuts, and almonds," the ASV "spicery and myrrh," and the NIV "spices and myrrh, some pistachio nuts and almonds." Three of those are not a random hamper: nekhot, tsori and lot — gum, balm and ladanum — are precisely, word for word, the cargo the Ishmaelite caravan was carrying down to Egypt on the day his brothers sold Joseph into it. The old man is loading the same three commodities onto donkeys and sending them down the same road, as a present, to the son he thinks is dead. He has no idea. Genesis does not say a word about it; it simply lists the items twice, six chapters apart, and trusts you.

"Perhaps it was a mistake." Jacob's instruction about the returned silver is the most ordinary sentence in the chapter and quietly reveals him: take it back, and let us assume an accounting error. It is the one explanation that requires nobody to have intended anything.

El Shaddai, and a word from the womb. He prays by the covenant name last heard when God renamed Abram (17:1) — the name for the God who gives children to people who have none — and he asks for rachamim. Keep hold of that word: it is built on rechem, the womb, and means the ache of a parent for a child, mother-love used as the standard word for mercy. Jacob asks God to put womb-feeling in a hard Egyptian official. Sixteen verses later the text will say, of that official, that his rachamim grew warm and he had to leave the room. The prayer is answered before it finishes travelling, in a body Jacob does not know is his son's.

"If I am bereaved, I am bereaved." Ka'asher shakholti shakhalti — a resigned, fatalistic doubling, and the same construction Esther will use walking in to the king unsummoned ("if I perish, I perish," Esther 4:16). The KJV's "if I be bereaved of my children, I am bereaved" catches the tone: not faith, exactly. A man out of options.

Verses 15–25 · terror at the door, and a steward who says the word

Kindness read as ambush. Invited to the governor's own house — an enormous honour — the brothers panic, and the reason is the silver. Their reasoning is grimly practical: they will be "taken as slaves." These are men who once sold a brother into slavery for silver, now convinced that unexplained silver is about to make slaves of them. Genesis lets the symmetry sit there without a word of comment.

"Peace to you — do not be afraid." And here the chapter does something remarkable. The steward's first word is shalom — the word this family has been unable to say since 37:4, when the brothers "could not speak to him in peace," and the word their father put in the errand that got Joseph sold: "go, see whether your brothers are at peace" (37:14). It has been withheld for six chapters. It comes back, in Egypt, from a servant, to the men who could not pronounce it. The KJV's "Peace be to you, fear not" keeps the noun where a modern ear might expect "it's all right"; the NIV's "It's all right… don't be afraid" is exactly that smoothing, and the thread disappears with it. Then he tells them their God gave them treasure — a household servant handing ten frightened Hebrews a theological explanation of something his master did — and brings out Simeon.

"Your silver came to me." Which is true, and hides everything. The steward is in on it.

Verses 26–31 · the dream again, and the second weeping

They bow twice more. Verse 26 and verse 28, and by now the reader has stopped being surprised — which is itself the point. What was a boy's insufferable dream in 37:7 has become the ordinary daily posture of ten adult men doing business. Prophecy fulfilled does not announce itself; it just becomes the way things are.

He asks after their peace. Three times in two verses the root shalom: he asked after their shalom, "is your father shalom," "your servant our father is shalom." The man who was sent to inspect his brothers' shalom and was thrown into a pit for it is now the one asking — and getting an answer. English cannot keep the repetition without sounding odd ("is your father at peace?"), so this translation uses "asked after their peace" for the frame and natural English inside it, with the thread flagged here.

"His mother's son." The narrator adds three words that no one at the table could have said: Benjamin is not just his youngest brother, he is ben immo — Rachel's other son, the only full brother he has. Joseph asks a formal question, receives no answer, and blesses the boy anyway ("God be gracious to you, my son" — yechonkha, from chanan, the root of Hannah, John, and "grace"). Then he stops being able to do this.

"His compassion grew warm." Nikhmeru rachamav — the verb means to grow hot, to be kindled, and the noun is Jacob's word from verse 14. Hebrew locates feeling in the body, and this is the body reporting: the womb-love heated up and the man had to leave. The KJV's "his bowels did yearn upon his brother" is a genuinely faithful rendering of the physicality that has simply aged into comedy for modern ears; the NIV reads "deeply moved," which is decorous and loses the heat. This is Joseph's second weeping (after 42:24) and he still hides it — washes his face, comes out, and held himself in (vayyit'appaq, to restrain, force oneself). Count them as they come: the restraint is going to fail.

Verses 32–34 · three tables, and a portion five times over

Three separate tables, and a note that pays an old debt. Joseph alone; the brothers alone; the Egyptians alone — "because the Egyptians cannot eat bread with the Hebrews, for that is an abomination to Egypt." Genesis states the custom flatly, without mockery — the KJV "for that is an abomination unto the Egyptians," the ASV the same, the NWT "a detestable thing" — and it lands on something left open four chapters ago: Potiphar "knew nothing of what he had except the bread he ate" — a clause the note there said might be bookkeeping, delicacy, or a dietary rule. Here is the dietary rule, stated outright by the narrator. And notice where it leaves Joseph: not at the Egyptians' table and not at the Hebrews'. He eats alone, between two peoples, which is a fair description of his whole life.

The seating. They are placed in exact birth order, "the firstborn according to his birthright and the youngest according to his youth" — ten men who cannot imagine how an Egyptian could know it, staring at each other. It is a small controlled miracle designed to unsettle them, and it does.

Five portions — and the last test being set up. Benjamin gets five times what anyone else does — the KJV famously calls it a "mess" ("Benjamin's mess was five times so much as any of theirs"), an archaism for a served portion that has drifted into meaning a muddle — which is not generosity so much as an experiment. Joseph is recreating the original conditions with clinical precision: a father's favourite, one brother conspicuously preferred in front of the others, at a table where they can all see it. The last time these men watched a father lavish a preference on Rachel's son, they stripped him and sold him. The narrator's final word on the meal is the answer to the experiment, and it is not what happened in 37:4: they drank and grew merry with him. Nobody resents it. The trap for that, with a silver cup, is one chapter away.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the words exact / distinct: "I myself will be surety for him" for arav, the guarantor's commercial word, so Judah's offer reads as the legal undertaking it is; "compassion" for rachamim in both Jacob's prayer (v. 14) and Joseph's body (v. 30), because they must be the same word for the answer to be visible; "peace" keeping shalom audible in vv. 23, 27 and 28; "abomination" for to'evah; and the gift's gum, balm and ladanum rendered exactly as at 37:25 so the cargo can be recognised.

Echoes paid. Reuben's grotesque offer answered by Judah putting up himself — and the character built in chapter 38 finally doing the work it was built for; the caravan's gum, balm and ladanum loaded by Jacob as a present and sent back down the road his son left on; shalom, unspeakable at 37:4 and the subject of the errand at 37:14, spoken over them in Egypt; rachamim, asked of God in v. 14 and reported in a man's body in v. 30; the bread Potiphar kept to himself explained by the Egyptian table rule; and the bowing, twice more, now unremarkable.

Echoes planted. Benjamin's five portions and a favoured youngest son at a table — the conditions of the original crime rebuilt on purpose, with the silver cup about to be planted in his sack (44:2); Judah's surety, which he will have to make good on in the longest speech in Genesis ("let your servant remain instead of the boy," 44:33) — and that is the moment Joseph stops being able to hold himself in; and the restraint of v. 31, counted twice now and failing.

Next installment in this book: Genesis 44 — the silver cup is hidden in Benjamin's sack and the brothers are overtaken on the road; the test is complete, the favoured youngest is condemned, and the others are free to walk away exactly as they once did. Then Judah steps forward and speaks for eighteen verses.
