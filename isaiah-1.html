A covenant lawsuit against Judah — heaven and earth summoned as witnesses, the sacrifices refused, the courts indicted for taking bribes, and Jerusalem left standing like a watchman's hut in a stripped field.
c. 740-700 BC, the reigns of Uzziah, Jotham, Ahaz and Hezekiah (v1) — the decades in which ASSYRIA destroyed the northern kingdom (722) and besieged Jerusalem (701). ⚠ Verse 8's 'city under siege' is usually read as 701, and Sennacherib's own prism describes the same event: he shut Hezekiah up in Jerusalem 'like a bird in a cage' — one of the closest correspondences between the Bible and an independent ancient record. The chapter is often called the overture to the whole book, and may have been placed first rather than spoken first.

Translator’s Notes

Verse 1 · four kings, and the manuscript that changed how we read this book

«A vision» — chazon, from a verb meaning to see, not to hear. It is the standard heading for a prophetic book and it covers the whole of what follows, which is mostly speech. The prophet is a person who is shown something.

The four kings are a period, not a list. Uzziah, Jotham, Ahaz and Hezekiah span roughly 740–700 BC — from the year Uzziah died (the vision of chapter 6) through the Assyrian catastrophe. In those decades Assyria destroyed the northern kingdom of Israel (722 BC) and came within a wall of taking Jerusalem (701 BC). Everything in this chapter is written from inside that emergency.

⚠ Isaiah is the book with the best manuscript evidence in the Hebrew Bible. The Great Isaiah Scroll (1QIsaa), found at Qumran in 1947, is a complete copy of all sixty-six chapters dated to about 125 BC — roughly a thousand years older than the oldest complete Masoretic manuscripts. It differs from the received text in spelling and in hundreds of small readings, and it agrees with it in substance throughout. When this translation notes a textual question in Isaiah, that scroll is usually one of the witnesses being weighed. Its readings are given in the notes when they matter.

Verses 2–4 · a lawsuit, an ox, and the title that runs through the whole book

⚠ This is not a sermon. It is a legal summons, and the first line proves it. “Hear, heavens, and give ear, earth” calls the two oldest witnesses in the covenant to court — the same pair Moses calls in Deuteronomy 32:1 (“Give ear, heavens … hear, earth”) and in the covenant oath of Deuteronomy 30:19. Isaiah reverses the order and keeps the function. The technical name for the form is a rib, a covenant lawsuit: witnesses summoned, charge stated, evidence given, verdict and sentence. Reading chapter 1 as a courtroom scene explains its shape, and it explains verse 18.

«Sons I raised and brought up» — the charge is not irreligion but ingratitude in a family. The verb pasha, translated “rebelled,” is a political word for breaking with a sovereign, and Isaiah puts it in the mouth of a father.

The ox and the donkey have had a long afterlife. The point is brutally simple: livestock know who feeds them. The two animals are not in the birth narratives of Matthew or Luke — they walked into every nativity scene in Christian art from this verse, by way of early commentators who read it alongside Habakkuk 3:2 in an old Greek rendering. KJV “the ox knoweth his owner, and the ass his master’s crib”; NWT “A bull well knows its buyer, and a donkey the manger of its owner”; NIV “the donkey its owner’s manger.” The Hebrew evus is a feeding trough — the manger word.

«Woe» is hoy, the cry shouted at a funeral. Isaiah uses it more than any other prophet, and he is about to use it again in verse 24 — where, startlingly, it is God who says it.

⚠ «The Holy One of Israel» is Isaiah’s signature. The title appears about twenty-five times in this book and only a handful of times in the whole rest of the Bible — and it is distributed evenly across chapters 1–39 and 40–66, which is one of the strongest arguments that whatever their origins, the parts of Isaiah were shaped into one book by people who shared a vocabulary. Every version keeps it (KJV, ASV, NIV, NWT), and it is worth noticing what it holds together: holy, the word for absolute separateness, bolted directly onto the name of a particular small nation.

Verses 5–9 · a beaten body, a hut in a field, and a siege we can date

The nation is described as a body that has been beaten and not treated. Verse 6 is medical: petsa (an open wound), chabburah (a bruise or welt), makkah teriyah (a fresh, wet gash). The three things you do for a wound — press it out, bind it, soften it with oil — are each named and each denied. KJV “wounds, and bruises, and putrifying sores”; ASV “fresh stripes”; NIV “welts and open sores”; NWT “wounds and bruises and fresh gashes.”

⚠ Verse 7 has an old textual question. The Hebrew ends “like an overthrow of zarim”, strangers — but the noun mahpekhah, “overthrow,” is used almost everywhere else in the Bible of exactly one event: the destruction of Sodom (Deuteronomy 29:23; Isaiah 13:19; Jeremiah 49:18; Amos 4:11). Many read zarim here as a copyist’s slip for Sedom, which would fit the Sodom of verses 9–10 exactly. KJV and ASV keep the Hebrew (“overthrown by strangers”); NIV paraphrases past the problem. We keep the Masoretic reading and record the alternative.

⚠ Verse 8 can be dated, and there is a document on the other side of it. “Like a booth in a vineyard, like a shelter in a cucumber field” — the flimsy watchman’s hut left standing when the harvest is over and everything around it has been stripped. That is Jerusalem in 701 BC, when Sennacherib of Assyria took the Judean countryside and left the capital isolated. Sennacherib’s own annals, on a clay prism now in the British Museum, say he shut Hezekiah up in Jerusalem “like a bird in a cage” — the Assyrian king’s image for the same siege, and one of the closest correspondences anywhere between the Bible and an independent ancient record. Melunah, the second word for hut, occurs only here and in Isaiah 24:20.

«Survivors» is sarid, and this is the first appearance of what becomes one of Isaiah’s central ideas: a remnant. It is not yet consoling here — the sentence is if there were not a handful left, we would already be Sodom. Isaiah will name his own son after it (7:3, Shear-Yashub, “a remnant will return”).

Verses 10–17 · God says he is sick of the worship he commanded

⚠ The insult in verse 10 is deliberate and it is aimed at the congregation. Verse 9 said we were nearly Sodom; verse 10 turns and addresses them as if they already are: rulers of Sodom, people of Gomorrah. This is being said to people on their way to the temple.

What follows is one of the hardest passages in the prophets, and it must not be softened. The sacrifices God rejects here are the ones he commanded in Leviticus: the burnt offering, the grain offering, incense, new moon, sabbath, the appointed feasts. He does not say they are being done badly. He says: I have had my fill; who asked this of you; my soul hates them; I am weary of carrying them. KJV “I am full of the burnt offerings of rams … I delight not in the blood of bullocks”; NWT “I have had enough of whole burnt offerings of rams”; NIV “I have more than enough.”

The readings divide, honestly, and this translation does not choose. One: the prophets reject the cult itself as a late and mistaken institution — a reading that has to answer Jeremiah 7:22 and the fact that Isaiah still calls the temple “my courts.” Two, and more commonly held: this is prophetic hyperbole aimed at worship offered by hands full of blood, and verses 16–17 are the proof, since they do not abolish the offerings, they name what was missing. Either way the text’s force is the same, and it is directed at people who were, by their own lights, devout.

⚠ A scribal fingerprint in verse 12. The consonants can be read “when you come to see my face” — but the Masoretes pointed the verb as a passive, “to be seen before me,” because seeing God’s face was theologically intolerable. The same adjustment is made all through the pilgrimage laws (Exodus 23:15; 34:23-24), and it belongs to the family of deliberate scribal softenings the tradition itself catalogued as tiqqun soferim. Every English version follows the pointing, and so do we — with the note.

«Incense is a detestable thing to me» uses to’evah, the word Leviticus reserves for what is ritually abhorrent. The prophet has taken the vocabulary of the priesthood and turned it against the priesthood’s own practice.

Verses 16–17 are nine imperatives in a row, and the hinge of the chapter: wash, be clean, remove, stop, learn, seek justice, set right, judge, plead. Five of the nine are about court procedure, and two of them name the two people in an ancient society with no one to speak for them — the orphan and the widow. ⚠ One word is genuinely ambiguous: ashsheru chamotz can mean “set the oppressed right” or “correct the oppressor” — the noun is a passive participle that can point either way. KJV “relieve the oppressed”, ASV “relieve the oppressed”, NIV “defend the oppressed”, NWT “set right the oppressor” — the shelf itself splits down the middle, which is the honest state of the Hebrew.

Verses 18–20 · scarlet and snow — and whether it is a promise or a question

«Let us settle it in court» — the verb is yakach in the reciprocal form, and it is a legal word: to argue a case out, to reach a judgment between two parties. The KJV’s famous “let us reason together” has given generations a picture of a gentle conversation over a table; the courtroom that has been running since verse 2 is still in session. NIV “Come now, let us settle the matter”; NWT “Come, now, and let us set matters straight”; ASV keeps “let us reason together.”

⚠ And now the real question, which most English Bibles decide silently. Hebrew marks a question by context far more often than by any particle, and verse 18 has none. So the two clauses can be read as a promise — though your sins are like scarlet, they shall be white as snow (so KJV, ASV, NIV, NWT, and the entire hymn tradition) — or as an incredulous question put by a prosecutor: shall they become white as snow?, with verses 19–20 supplying the answer: only if you are willing. A number of modern commentators take the second reading precisely because the surrounding verses are a summons, a charge, and a sentence, and because an unconditional pardon dropped into the middle of them has to be explained. We print the question and record the tradition, because the Hebrew genuinely permits both and the choice is an interpretation, not a translation.

The dye is an insect. Shani and tola are both words for the crimson of the ancient world, and tola is literally a worm — the kermes scale insect, crushed for the fastest and most expensive red available. The image is chosen for that reason: it was known as a dye that does not wash out.

⚠ Verses 19–20 turn on a pun that no English can carry. “You will eat the good of the land” (tokhelu) against “you will be eaten by the sword” (te’ukkelu) — the same verb, active and passive, one letter apart. Eat, or be eaten. KJV “ye shall be devoured with the sword” gets the sense and loses the sound.

Verses 21–26 · a lament for a city, and a furnace that is meant to save it

⚠ The first word of verse 21 is eikhah, “How…!” — and it is not an ordinary exclamation. It is the opening word and the Hebrew title of the book of Lamentations, and the standard first word of a funeral dirge. Isaiah is singing over Jerusalem while it is still standing. KJV “How is the faithful city become an harlot!” catches it; several modern versions flatten it to “See how…”.

The charge is delivered in three metaphors of adulteration and one of betrayal. Silver has become sigim, the dross skimmed off in smelting; the drink is cut with water; and the town that was faithful has gone to other men. Note what is not said: nothing about idolatry until verse 29. The city’s infidelity, in this chapter, is corruption in its courts — bribes, and cases the orphan and the widow cannot get heard. The word for the officials’ payoffs, shalmonim, occurs nowhere else in the Bible.

⚠ Verse 24 is one of the most disturbing sentences in the prophets, and the versions do not disguise it. God, titled three ways at once — ha-Adon, the Lord; Jehovah of armies; the Mighty One of Israel — says hoy, the funeral cry, and then: I will get relief from my adversaries and take vengeance on my enemies. The verb is nacham, to be comforted or eased. And the adversaries are his own people. KJV “Ah, I will ease me of mine adversaries”; ASV the same; NIV “I will vent my wrath on my foes”; NWT “I will rid myself of my adversaries.”

And then the metaphor turns. Verse 25 is the same smelting that produced the dross in verse 22, run in reverse — the hand raised against the city is a refiner’s hand, burning out the alloy so that what is left is silver. The point of the fire is verse 26: the judges restored, and the city given back the name it lost in verse 21 — Faithful Town. The chapter’s indictment and its hope use the same furnace.

Verses 27–31 · two words for what saves a city, and a garden that burns

«Zion will be redeemed by justice, and those in her who return, by righteousness» — and these are the two words the whole chapter has been circling. Mishpat is judgment rightly executed, the thing the courts of verse 23 were selling; tzedaqah is rightness in relationship, the thing verse 21 says used to spend the night in the city. Isaiah pairs them again and again, most famously in the vineyard song of 5:7, where he says God looked for mishpat and found mispach, bloodshed — another pun the English cannot hold.

«Those in her who return» is shaveha, from shuv, to turn back — the word that means both repent and come home from exile. Both senses are live in Isaiah, and the ambiguity is not a problem to be solved.

⚠ The last three verses name the religion the chapter has not yet mentioned. The terebinths (elim, great spreading trees) and the gardens are not landscape: they are the open-air shrines of Canaanite worship, sacred groves and planted enclosures that the historical books keep reporting and the kings keep failing to remove. So the indictment closes with both halves of it side by side — corrupt courts and country shrines — and the sentence is an image of exact reversal: the people who chose the trees become a tree with dead leaves, and the people who chose the gardens become a garden with no water.

Verse 31 ends the chapter on a spark. Chason is the strong one — the powerful man of the preceding verses — and ne’oret is tow, the combed-out flax waste that catches instantly. His work becomes the nitsots, the spark (a word found only here), and the strong man is the tinder for his own deeds. KJV “the strong shall be as tow, and the maker of it as a spark”; NWT “the strong man will become tow, and his work a spark.” No one puts it out. That is the last word of Isaiah’s first chapter, and the book has sixty-five more.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the exact / distinct words: “Jehovah” wherever the divine name stands, and “the Lord, Jehovah of armies” in verse 24 where three titles are stacked; “Woe” for hoy, the funeral cry, kept identical in verse 4 and verse 24 so the reader can see God use the word himself; “the Holy One of Israel,” Isaiah’s own title, untouched; “to be seen before me” in verse 12 following the Masoretic pointing, with the scribal adjustment noted rather than reversed; a question at verse 18, because the Hebrew has no particle either way and the promise is an interpretation; “set the oppressed right” in verse 17 with the opposite reading recorded; and “eat … be eaten” in verses 19–20, where the two verbs really are the same verb.

Echoes paid. Heaven and earth summoned as witnesses — the covenant court of Deuteronomy 32:1 and 30:19, with the two witnesses in reverse order. Sodom and Gomorrah (Genesis 19), used twice in two verses: first as what nearly happened, then as who they already are. Hoy, mishpat, tzedaqah, olah and minchah — the vocabulary of the Law and the funeral, turned on the people who kept both. And the scribal softening of verse 12, the same instinct that produced the tradition’s own catalogue of corrections.

Echoes planted. The Holy One of Israel, which will return about twenty-five times and is one of the threads tying the far ends of this book together. The remnant — the few survivors of verse 9, who become a doctrine, and a son’s name, in chapter 7. The vineyard, glimpsed here as a hut in a stripped field and given a whole song in chapter 5. Zion, named for the first time in the book. Justice and righteousness, the pair Isaiah will keep welding together. And the furnace, which in verse 25 is already doing the double work — punishment and refining — that the rest of the book will need it to do.
