The book pauses to catalogue Esau — his Canaanite wives, his move to Seir, the clans and chiefs of Edom, the Horites they displaced, and eight kings who reigned in Edom before Israel had any king.
An undated genealogical interlude. Esau settles the red highlands of Seir 'away from his brother Jacob' — a peaceful separation, like Abraham and Lot's — and his line becomes the nation of Edom, with clan-chiefs and a non-dynastic line of kings. Amalek, Israel's perennial enemy, is born in it (36:12). The chapter clears the brother's account with honor before Genesis turns, in ch. 37, wholly to Joseph.

Translator's Notes — grouped for a catalogue

A whole chapter of names: the family, chiefs, and kings of Edom. Genealogy chapters get fewer, wider notes — the shape and the placement are the point, not each name — so these zoom out. Quotations from copyrighted versions (NIV, TLB, NWT) are kept to short phrases for comparison; KJV, Geneva, Douay-Rheims and ASV are public domain.

Verses 1–8 · the other brother's book, and a peaceful parting

Genesis honors the son it did not choose. "These are the generations (toledot) of Esau" — the formula that frames the whole book (the heavens and earth, Adam, Noah, Shem, Isaac…) turns, before it will settle on Jacob and Joseph, to give Esau a full chapter of his own. It is the same courtesy the book paid Ishmael (25:12-18): the rejected son is named, counted, and blessed with a future before the story moves on. God keeps his word to Esau too — "kings shall come from you" was said to Jacob (35:11), but here the promise of nationhood the twins were both born under (25:23) comes true on Esau's side, with chiefs and kings of his own. The catalogue is an act of even-handedness.

Esau is Edom, in Seir. The chapter hammers the three names together — "Esau, that is Edom" (v. 1), "Esau dwelt in Seir, Esau is Edom" (v. 8), and again at the close (vv. 19, 43) — folding the man into the nation (Edom, adom, "red," from the red stew and the ruddy skin) and the land (Seir, sa'ir, "hairy"). And the brothers part in peace: "their possessions were too great for them to dwell together, and the land could not bear them" — word for word the reason Abraham and Lot separated (13:6), except that where their herdsmen quarreled, Esau simply gathers everything and goes "away from the face of his brother Jacob," leaving Canaan to the schemer and taking Seir for himself. The reconciliation of chapter 33 holds; the twins who fought in the womb end as neighbors making room. (The wives' names here differ from the lists at 26:34 and 28:9 — a long-noted crux the ancients explained by second names or variant spellings; the KJV keeps the differences plainly, as this translation does.)

Verses 9–19 · the chiefs — and Amalek, hidden in the roll

"Chiefs," not "dukes." The roll of Esau's sons becomes a roll of chiefs — alufim, from eleph, "a thousand / a clan": the head of a clan, a thousand-chief. The KJV famously renders it "duke" (from the Latin dux by way of the Vulgate) — a medieval European coronet comically out of place in Bronze-Age Seir; the ASV and NIV read "chief," the NWT "sheik," and this translation "chief." The word sketches Edom's shape: not one throne but a federation of clan-heads.

A fuse in the family tree. Buried mid-list is one of the Bible's quietest bombshells: "Timna was concubine to Eliphaz, Esau's son, and she bore Amalek" (v. 12). From Esau's grandson descends the nation that will ambush Israel's faint and straggling on the way out of Egypt (Exodus 17) — the one enemy given the oath "Jehovah will have war with Amalek from generation to generation." Agag the Amalekite king costs Saul his crown (1 Samuel 15), and Haman "the Agagite," who plots to wipe out every Jew in Persia (Esther), is read as the last shoot of this root. The chapter that honors Esau also, in a single verse, plants Israel's oldest enemy.

Verses 20–30 · the people who were there first

An honest record of who was displaced. The chapter turns aside to list "the sons of Seir the Horite, the inhabitants of the land" — the original population of Seir, the cave-people (from chor, "cave") whom Esau's descendants pushed out. Deuteronomy says it without flinching: "the Horites lived in Seir formerly, but the sons of Esau dispossessed them and dwelt in their place" (Deuteronomy 2:12). Genesis does not pretend Edom rose from empty ground — it names the people who were there and were displaced, the same honesty the Bible will later turn on Israel's own conquests.

The word no one can translate. One Horite, Anah, is remembered for a desert discovery: "this is the Anah who found the hot springs (yemim) in the wilderness as he pastured the donkeys" (v. 24). But yemim appears exactly once in the whole Bible, and the versions honestly disagree — the KJV guessed "mules" (from the donkey-herding context), the NIV and most moderns read "hot springs" (from an Aramaic cognate), others simply "water." No one knows; it is a clean little window on how a single rare word can leave every translator guessing. This translation renders "hot springs," with the doubt flagged.

Verses 31–43 · kings before there were kings

A verse that looks past its own moment. "These are the kings who reigned in the land of Edom before any king reigned over the sons of Israel" (v. 31). The line presupposes that Israel would have kings — which the promise had already guaranteed ("kings shall come from your loins," 35:11; 17:6) — and it became a famous test-case in the Middle Ages: the commentator Ibn Ezra hinted, carefully, that a sentence like this reads as though written once Israel had kings (a seed of what later became source-criticism), while traditional readers take it as Mosaic foresight, the promised monarchy spoken of as already sure. This translation lays out the reading and does not rule on the date — the Hebrew alone will not settle whether it is prophecy stated as fact or a later scribe's note.

Kings, but not a dynasty. And the list itself makes a quiet point: eight kings, each "died, and X reigned in his place" — but never "his son." Each comes from a different city (Dinhabah, Bozrah, Avith, Pau…); Edom's kingship was seized or elected, not inherited — the opposite of the enduring, hereditary throne God is promising Israel. Edom gets its kings first, and they do not hold. The chapter closes on the chiefs "by their settlements in the land of their possession," and seals the whole account: "This is Esau, the father of Edom" — the brother honored, catalogued, and closed, so the book is free to turn, in the next chapter, wholly to Joseph.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the words exact / distinct: "chief" for alluf (not the KJV's quaint "duke"); "hot springs" for the one-off yemim, with the crux flagged; "generations" for toledot, the book's structural beam; and "Esau is Edom" left standing as the chapter's refrain.

Echoes paid. The peaceful separation echoing Abraham and Lot (13:6, word for word); the Canaanite wives that had "made life bitter" for Isaac and Rebekah (26:35); the promise of a nation (and now kings) to Esau's side of 25:23; and the reconciliation of chapter 33 quietly holding. Echoes planted: Amalek and the war "from generation to generation" (Exodus 17; 1 Samuel 15; Esther); the Horites dispossessed (Deuteronomy 2:12); and Edom as Israel's shadow-brother all through the prophets (Obadiah is one long oracle against it; "the pride of your heart has deceived you").

Next installment in this book: Genesis 37 — the story comes home to Jacob's house and to Joseph: the coat of many colors, the dreams that make his brothers hate him, the pit at Dothan, and the sale into Egypt — the beginning of the long road down that will save the family and enslave it both.
