Translator’s Notes

Verse 1 · the doorway to the whole Psalter, and the descent that begins with a walk

This is the front porch of the entire book of Psalms. Psalm 1 has no title, no author, no tune — it is not itself a prayer or a hymn but a piece of WISDOM POETRY, set deliberately at the head of the collection to tell you how to READ the 149 psalms that follow: they are “the law of Jehovah” to be delighted in and murmured over. The book of prayers opens by teaching you to meditate. (Many ancient authorities joined Psalms 1 and 2 as a single gateway — the two ways and the two kingdoms, the righteous man and the anointed king — and some New Testament manuscripts of Acts 13:33 even quote Psalm 2 as “the FIRST psalm.”)

The Psalter’s very first word is ashrei — “HAPPY.” Not barukh (“blessed,” the word for blessing directed UP to God), but ashrei, a plural-of-intensity exclamation over a PERSON: “Oh, the happinesses of the man who…” It is exactly the word that opens the Beatitudes — the Greek makarios of Matthew 5 (“Happy are the poor in spirit”) is the Septuagint’s ashrei — so the Psalter and the Sermon on the Mount open on the same note. Older English keeps the grander “Blessed” (KJV, ASV), but the Hebrew is warmer and more personal; NWT “Happy is the man,” NIV “Blessed is the one.”

The happy man is drawn first by what he does NOT do — and the negatives descend a staircase. He does not WALK in the counsel of the wicked, nor STAND in the way of sinners, nor SIT in the seat of scoffers: three verbs of settling in (walk → stand → sit), three companies growing worse (wicked → sinners → scoffers), three postures of deepening commitment (a passing stroll → a halt → a permanent seat). It is a small, exact picture of how a life is captured — not by one great fall but by a gradual sitting-down. NWT keeps the three verbs distinct; DRB, from the Vulgate, ends the descent on “the chair of pestilence.”

Verse 2 · delight, and the murmuring of the law day and night

Against the three negatives stands one positive: his delight is in torat YHVH. ⚠ “The LAW” is the traditional rendering (and the one the NWT and ASV keep here), but torah is wider than a legal code — from yarah, “to throw, to point, to teach,” it means DIRECTION, instruction, the whole teaching of God (the Septuagint’s choice of nomos, “law,” narrowed it on the way into Latin and English). What the happy man feels for it is chefetz — not duty but DELIGHT, the pleasure one takes in a thing wanted for its own sake.

And his delight has a sound: he meditates on it — hagah. The verb does not mean silent thought; it means to MURMUR, mutter, growl, half-voice under the breath (the same verb is used of a lion over its prey, and of the wicked who “plot” in Psalm 2:1 — the pointed contrast: the nations mutter rebellion, the happy man mutters Torah). To meditate here is to chew the words aloud, “day and night” — the exact phrase God gives Joshua for the same book: “this book of the law shall not depart from your mouth; you shall meditate on it day and night” (Joshua 1:8). ⚠ It is no accident that the Psalter, which opens on torah, is itself built in FIVE BOOKS (1–41, 42–72, 73–89, 90–106, 107–150), each closed by a doxology — a five-fold answer to the five books of Moses. ASV “on his law doth he meditate day and night”; NWT “he reads in an undertone day and night.”

Verse 3 · the tree planted by the water

The reward of that delight is not a feeling but a TREE. Not a wild tree but one shatul — TRANSPLANTED, deliberately set — “by streams of water” (palgei mayim, the cut irrigation channels of a garden), so that its water never fails whatever the season does. Three things follow: it gives its fruit “IN ITS SEASON” (not on demand, but reliably, in time), its leaf does not wither, and — the summary — “in all that he does, he prospers.” ⚠ The image is almost word-for-word Jeremiah 17:7–8 (“he will be like a tree planted by water … its leaf will stay green … it does not cease to bear fruit”); which poet drew on which is unknown, and both may draw on an older proverb. The rooted, watered, fruit-bearing tree is the Bible’s picture of a life with a hidden source. NIV “whatever they do prospers”; KJV “whatsoever he doeth shall prosper”; ASV the same.

Verses 4–5 · not so the wicked — the chaff, and the judgment they cannot stand in

“Not so the wicked!” (lo khen) — two blunt Hebrew words that snap the poem in half. Where the righteous is a deep-rooted tree, the wicked is motz, CHAFF — the dry, weightless husk that the threshing-fork tosses into the evening wind and that simply blows away. The one is planted and stays; the other is loose and gone. (NWT adds an intensifier the Septuagint and Vulgate also felt, “chaff … which the wind drives away” — the Masoretic Hebrew is spare: “not so.”)

So the two lives end in two different places (v5). The wicked will not stand (qum, rise, hold their footing) “in the judgment,” nor sinners “in the assembly (edah) of the righteous” — the gathered congregation of the faithful. Chaff cannot stand where the winnowing has already sorted it out. The verb picks up v1’s middle step (the sinners in whose “way” he would not stand): they, in the end, are the ones who cannot stand. ASV “the wicked shall not stand in the judgment.”

Verse 6 · the last word is a road, and a knowing

The whole psalm gathers into one contrast of two ROADS (derekh). “Jehovah KNOWS the way of the righteous, but the way of the wicked will perish.” The verb is yada‘, “to know” — but in Hebrew to know is to acknowledge, to attend to, to care for (it is the word for the intimacy of a marriage and of the covenant): God does not merely have information about the righteous road, he WATCHES OVER it, walks it with them. And the other road needs no separate sentence of doom — it simply ’oved, “perishes,” loses itself, comes to nothing. That is the Psalter’s last word of its first poem: one road known and kept, the other road self-erasing.

⚠ Psalm 1 is the gate, and Psalm 2 is its other post. Where Psalm 1 sets out the TWO WAYS of the individual (the wisdom question — how shall I live?), Psalm 2 sets out the TWO KINGDOMS of history (the royal question — the nations that rage against “Jehovah and his ANOINTED”), and the two together are the Psalter’s twin doorway: torah-piety and messianic hope. The “Happy is the man” that opens here is answered by the “Happy are all who take refuge in him” that closes Psalm 2 — the whole gateway bracketed by beatitude. And the first word, ashrei, waits in the Septuagint to become the makarios on the mountain (Matthew 5:3).

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the exact / distinct words: “Happy” for ashrei (v1), the warm personal felicitation that opens both the Psalter and the Beatitudes (kept distinct from barukh, “blessed,” which runs the other way, up to God); the descending staircase “walk … stand … sit” and “wicked … sinners … scoffers” kept in step; “the law of Jehovah” for torat YHVH (v2, following the NWT/ASV) with torah’s wider sense of teaching/direction kept in the note; “meditates” for hagah (v2), the murmured half-voice, not silent thought; “chaff” for motz (v4), the rootless husk against the rooted tree; and “knows” for yada‘ (v6), the caring, covenant knowing.

Echoes paid. Hagah — “meditate day and night” — sending the reader back to Joshua 1:8, the same command over the same book; and forward to Psalm 2:1, where the nations “mutter” with the very same verb. Ashrei reaching the mountain as makarios (Matthew 5:3). And the tree by the water, shared almost verbatim with Jeremiah 17:8.

Echoes planted. The TWO WAYS — the road of the righteous and the road of the wicked — the wisdom framework the book of Proverbs is built on and the Sermon on the Mount ends on (“the narrow gate… the broad road”). The FIVE BOOKS of the Psalter, opened here on torah, each sealed with a doxology. And Psalm 2’s ANOINTED — the mashiach, the king the nations rage against — the messianic thread that the New Testament will hear fulfilled, and that makes this quiet wisdom poem the first step of the road to the cross.

The Psalms are a new book on the site — the hymnbook and prayerbook of Israel, 150 poems in five books. Next in Psalms: chapter 2 — the other post of the gateway: “Why do the nations rage…? He who sits in the heavens laughs… You are my son; today I have begotten you… Happy are all who take refuge in him.”
