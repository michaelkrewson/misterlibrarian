Jerusalem after the fire — an alphabetical dirge over an emptied city: roads with no pilgrims, priests starving mid-errand, and a woman with no one to sit with her.
586 BC and the years just after. The city fell in Nebuchadnezzar's eighteenth/nineteenth year; Nebuzaradan burned the temple, the palace and every large house, and broke down the walls (2 Kings 25:8-10). ⚠ The poems read as eyewitness — the specific detail of who starved and where — and are usually dated within a generation of the fall, before the return under Cyrus in 538. Babylon is never named in the book.

Translator’s Notes

Verses 1–3 · an alphabet, a limp, and a widow

⚠ This chapter is an ACROSTIC, and that is the first thing to know about it. It has exactly twenty-two verses because the Hebrew alphabet has twenty-two letters, and each verse begins with the next one in order — verse 1 with alef, verse 2 with bet, and so on to tav. English cannot reproduce it without rewriting the text, so this translation does not try; instead each verse carries its Hebrew letter in the margin, so the structure is visible even though the sound is gone. Chapters 2, 3 and 4 are acrostics too (chapter 3 three times over, sixty-six verses); chapter 5 has twenty-two verses and abandons the pattern, which is itself a statement.

What the form is for is worth pausing on. It is not decoration. Grief has no natural shape and no natural end, and an alphabet supplies both: a container, and a way to say everything, A to Z, and a guarantee that the poem will stop. This is one of the most controlled pieces of writing in the Bible, about the most uncontrolled thing that can happen to a person.

⚠ The first word is eikhah, and it is the book’s Hebrew name. “How…!” is the opening cry of a funeral dirge. This site met the word two chapters ago, in Isaiah 1:21 — how she has become a whore, the faithful town — sung over Jerusalem while the city was still standing. Here the city has fallen, and the same word opens the book. KJV “How doth the city sit solitary”; ASV the same; NWT “How she sits solitary”; NIV flattens it to “How deserted lies the city.”

The rhythm is a limp. Hebrew laments use a metre called qinah — three beats, then two — so that every line falls short of its own balance. You can hear it in verse 1: eikhah yashvah vadad / ha’ir rabbati am. The line stops before you expect it to, twenty-two times.

The city is a woman throughout — a widow, an abandoned wife, a woman exposed. That is not a decorative personification: bat-Zion, “daughter of Zion,” is standard prophetic usage, and this poem takes it seriously enough to give her a voice halfway through. The last word of verse 1, mas, is the technical term for forced labour, the corvée — the same word used of Israel in Egypt and of the labour gangs Solomon raised. The princess is now on a work detail.

«In the narrow places» — bein ha-metzarim, literally between the straits: cornered where there is no room to turn. KJV “between the straits”, NIV “in the midst of her distress”. In later Judaism the phrase became the name of the three weeks of mourning that end on the ninth of Av, which is the day this book is read.

Verses 4–6 · roads that mourn, and a curse coming true

«The roads to Zion are mourning» — the poem’s method in one line. It is not the pilgrims who mourn; it is the roads, because there are no pilgrims. Absence is described by what is left standing: empty gates, silent festivals, a road with nobody on it.

⚠ Verse 5 is a curse from Deuteronomy, reported as fact. “Her foes have become the head” is the covenant sanction of Deuteronomy 28:44 — he shall be the head and you shall be the tail — and the poet knows it. This is what makes Lamentations something other than a war poem: the disaster is being read through a document, and the document said this would happen. ASV “Her adversaries are become the head”; NWT “Her adversaries have become the head.”

And the agent is named without hesitation: Jehovah has grieved her. The poem never blames Babylon for the fall. Babylon is not mentioned once in the entire book. Everything that has happened is attributed to God, which is the position from which every complaint in the following twenty verses is made — and it is why those complaints are addressed to him.

Verse 6 carries the first of this chapter’s three ketiv/qere notes, visible in the Hebrew column: the consonants write min bat as two words and the reading tradition has mi-bat as one. A spelling matter, not a meaning one — but the scribes recorded it rather than tidying it, which is the habit that makes this text auditable. Verse 11 and verse 18 carry the other two.

Verses 7–11 · nakedness, the sanctuary breached, and a voice breaking in

⚠ The imagery in verses 8–9 is deliberately shaming, and translations have handled it very differently. The city is described as a woman whose nakedness has been seen and whose uncleanness is in her skirts. The word behind “a thing to shake the head at” in verse 8 (nidah) is close in form to the word for menstrual impurity (niddah) that plainly appears in verse 17, and the two are probably being played on. KJV verse 8 “therefore she is removed”, verse 17 “as a menstruous woman”; ASV “an unclean thing”; NIV “has become unclean”; NWT “an object of disgust”. We translate what each verse actually says and note the wordplay rather than levelling the two.

Verse 10 is the specific horror. “Nations entered her sanctuary” — the temple Solomon built, the one place where a boundary was absolute. The clause that follows quotes the law directly: those about whom you commanded that they should not come into your assembly is Deuteronomy 23:3. The poet is not describing looting in general; he is naming a rule that has been broken and pointing out that God made the rule.

⚠ Notice who is speaking. Verses 1–11 are a narrator’s: she, her, seen from outside. But twice the poem breaks — at the end of verse 9 and again at the end of verse 11 — and a first-person voice cuts in: “See, Jehovah, my affliction.” From verse 12 to the end of the chapter, that voice takes over completely. The city interrupts the description of herself, and then never gives it back.

The last word of verse 11 is zolelah — cheapened, held worthless, the word used of a glutton in Deuteronomy 21:20. She is not asking to be pitied; she is asking to be looked at. The imperative in both interruptions is the same: see.

Verses 12–14 · «is it nothing to you» — a famous line, and an honest problem

⚠ Verse 12 opens with two Hebrew words that nobody is sure how to read: lo aleikhem, literally “not to you.” The traditional English — KJV’s “Is it nothing to you, all ye that pass by?”, carried into Handel’s Messiah and into a thousand Good Friday services — supplies a question mark and an “it is nothing” that the Hebrew does not have. Others read it as a wish: may it never come to you. ASV keeps the KJV line; NIV has “Is it nothing to you, all you who pass by?”; NWT “Does it mean nothing to you, all you who pass along the road?”. We keep the familiar reading because it is defensible and because the address to passers-by makes sense of it — but the phrase is genuinely obscure, and no English version is translating a clear Hebrew here.

«Pain like my pain» is makh’ov, the word for a physical wound’s ache. It is the noun in Ecclesiastes’ “whoever adds knowledge adds pain” (1:18), and it is the plural in Isaiah’s “a man of sorrows, and acquainted with grief” (53:3). It is not sadness. It is what a body feels.

Verse 14 has a word nobody can identify. Nisqad occurs only here in the Hebrew Bible; it is usually taken from a root meaning to bind or fasten (so “the yoke … is bound”), while the Septuagint read something else entirely (“watch was kept over my transgressions”). KJV “The yoke of my transgressions is bound by his hand”; NIV “My sins have been bound into a yoke.” We follow the majority reading and record that it is a guess.

⚠ A distinction this translation keeps. From verse 14 the poem starts using Adonai, “the Lord” — a different word from the divine name Jehovah, and this chapter uses both. Most English Bibles print “LORD” for one and “Lord” for the other and hope the reader notices the capitals. We simply keep two different words, because they are two different words.

Verses 15–17 · a festival called against her, and a winepress

⚠ Verse 15 turns two of Israel’s happiest words inside out. A mo’ed is an appointed festival — the same word used in verse 4 for the pilgrim feasts nobody is coming to — and here God “calls a mo’ed against me,” a summons, a festival of destruction. And the winepress, the image of harvest and abundance, is trodden by God with the young men in it. The poet is taking the vocabulary of celebration and using it for the massacre. KJV “he hath called an assembly against me”; NWT “He has called an assembly against me”; ASV “he hath called a solemn assembly against me.”

«My eye, my eye runs down with water» — the doubling is in the Hebrew and every version keeps it. It is the one place in the chapter where the tight alphabetical control audibly slips.

⚠ And here is the refrain, which is the real subject of this chapter. “There is no comforter” occurs five times in twenty-two verses — verses 2, 9, 16, 17 and 21 — and it is the thing the poem returns to instead of returning to hope. Not there is no rescue: there is nobody sitting with her. The word is menachem, from nacham, the verb this site last met in Isaiah 1:24, where God says he will get relief from his adversaries. The answer to this refrain, when it finally comes, is the first line of Isaiah 40: nachamu nachamu ammi — comfort, comfort my people. It is not in this book.

Verse 17 steps back out. For one verse the narrator returns — Zion has spread out her hands; there is no comforter for her — and then the city takes the poem back for the rest of it. It is the only third-person verse in the second half.

Verses 18–19 · the concession

⚠ «Jehovah is righteous» is the hinge of the chapter, and it is a legal admission. Tsaddiq hu Jehovah — the sentence concedes the case. After seventeen verses of describing what has been done to her, the city says the verdict was just: for I rebelled against his mouth. Lamentations does not protest innocence, and that is what separates it from most poetry of catastrophe. It does something harder: it agrees, and goes on grieving anyway.

Verse 19 is the smallest and worst detail in the chapter. The priests and elders — the men who ran the city’s worship and its courts — died in the streets while they were looking for food. Not killed in battle; not executed. Starved, mid-errand. The verse ends with the same phrase used of the ordinary people in verse 11, to bring their life back.

Verse 18 carries the chapter’s third ketiv/qere: the consonants write “peoples” and the reading tradition adds the article, “the peoples.” Both are in the Hebrew column.

Verses 20–22 · the last three letters, and a prayer nobody quotes

«My insides churn» — the verb chomarmaru is built on a root meaning to ferment or boil up. Hebrew locates emotion in the abdomen, and the poem is being physically exact: this is nausea. KJV “my bowels are troubled”; ASV “my heart is turned within me”; NIV “I am in torment within.”

«Outside, the sword has bereaved; in the house, it is like death» — six words in Hebrew, and the whole condition of a besieged city: killed in the streets, starving indoors, no direction that is not the same.

⚠ And the chapter ends by asking God to do it to somebody else. Verses 21–22 are an imprecation: bring on the day you have proclaimed, and let them become like me … deal with them as you have dealt with me. This is not a passage that gets read at funerals, and it should not be softened into one. What the poem wants is not comfort and not rescue — it is for the world to be evened out, for the people who laughed in verse 7 to find out. Every version renders it plainly (KJV “let them be like unto me”; NWT “let them become like me”).

The alphabet runs out here, and so does the poem. Verse 22 begins with tav, the last letter, and the last two words are libbi davvai — my heart is faint. There is no resolution, no turn, no hope in this chapter. That arrives in the middle of chapter 3, at the exact centre of the book, and then this same voice keeps going for two more chapters afterward. The book ends on a question.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the exact / distinct words: “How…” for eikhah, the funeral cry that is also this book’s Hebrew name; “Jehovah” and “the Lord” kept as two different words, because YHWH and Adonai are two different words and this chapter uses both; “comforter” identical all five times, so the refrain reads as a refrain; “forced labourer” for mas, the corvée term, not a general word for servitude; “in the narrow places” for bein ha-metzarim, kept concrete; “pain” for makh’ov, a body’s ache rather than sadness; and the acrostic letters printed in the margin — the one feature of this poem that no English translation can carry, shown rather than faked.

Echoes paid. Eikhah, met two chapters ago in Isaiah 1:21 over a city still standing, now opening the book about the city after it fell. Nacham — in Isaiah 1:24 God says he will get relief; here, five times, there is nobody to give it. Deuteronomy 28:44, the curse that the foe will be the head, reported in verse 5 as something that has happened; and Deuteronomy 23:3, quoted in verse 10 about who may not enter the assembly, by a poet watching them walk in. And makh’ov, the pain of Ecclesiastes 1:18.

Echoes planted. The alphabet, which will run three more times — twice more in order, once tripled — and then be abandoned in chapter 5. «No comforter», the question this book asks and does not answer; its answer is the opening line of Isaiah 40. The concession of verse 18, that the verdict was just, which is what makes chapter 3’s turn possible. And the missing name: Babylon burned this city, and Babylon is not mentioned once in five chapters. Everything here is addressed to God.
