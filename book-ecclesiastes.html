📖 Ecclesiastes

קֹהֶלֶת Qohelet — 'Qohelet' — a participle from qahal, 'to assemble': the one who convenes an assembly, the Congregator. It is used of no one else in the Bible, and it is grammatically FEMININE while taking masculine verbs, which has never been satisfactorily explained; the likeliest account is that it began as a title of office (Hebrew forms offices that way — compare Sophereth and Pochereth in Ezra 2) and hardened into a name. Hebrew tradition names the book after it.
Ἐκκλησιαστής (Ekklēsiastēs) — The Septuagint's rendering of Qohelet, built on ekklēsia, 'assembly, congregation' — the same word this translation renders 'congregation' in the New Testament. Latin took it over unchanged, and English inherited it as the book's title. So the familiar name means, almost exactly, 'the Congregator' — and the English versions' 'the Preacher' (KJV, Geneva, Douay-Rheims) is a later, churchier reading of that same idea.

A king's report on the world, filed from inside it: everything moves, nothing arrives, nobody is remembered — and the honest response is to eat your bread, do your work, and fear God. The Bible's most sceptical book, and it was kept.

Chapter by chapter

Ecclesiastes 1The Bible’s most sceptical book opens with a word almost every English version has translated as a verdict when the Hebrew gives an image: HEVEL, vapor — a breath, mist you can see and cannot hold. “Vanity,” “meaningless,” “futile” all decide something the Hebrew leaves open. Then a poem in which the sun PANTS its way back to its starting line, the wind circles and returns, the rivers run to a sea that never fills, and the generations pass while the earth stays put — four systems in perpetual motion, none of them arriving. “There is nothing new under the sun.” The speaker calls himself Qohelet, which is not a name but an office — ‘the Congregator’ — and says “I WAS king,” a past tense that does not fit Solomon and started a rabbinic legend about a deposed king wandering as a beggar. He ends the chapter with a bill: in much wisdom is much vexation, and whoever adds knowledge adds pain.

About the book

Where it sits
One of the Five Megillot (Festival Scrolls) among the Writings, read at Sukkot. ⚠ Its canonicity was disputed: the Mishnah (Yadayim 3:5) records that the school of Shammai held it does NOT 'defile the hands' — their idiom for sacred status — while the school of Hillel held that it does, and Hillel's ruling stood. The Talmud (Shabbat 30b) preserves the objection plainly: the sages sought to withdraw Ecclesiastes because its words contradict one another, and because it appeared to encourage self-indulgence. It was kept because it begins and ends with words of Torah. Fragments were found at Qumran (4QQoh-a, mid-2nd century BC), so it was being copied as scripture by then.
Genre
WISDOM LITERATURE, but of an unusual and argumentative kind. Where Proverbs teaches the rules, Ecclesiastes tests them against experience and reports where they fail. Its methods are observation ('I saw,' 'I turned and saw'), first-person experiment (chapter 2, where the king buys everything and measures the result), reflection framed as an audit — profit, deficit, portion — and short poems of extraordinary quality (1:4-8; 3:1-8; 12:1-7). It is closer to a philosophical notebook than to a sermon, and its arrangement is deliberately unresolved: the same book says wisdom is better than folly and that the wise die like fools.
Author
Anonymous, and the question is genuinely open. The book presents itself as the work of 'Qohelet, son of David, king in Jerusalem' — traditionally SOLOMON — but it never names him, and ⚠ it says 'I WAS king' (1:12), a past tense that does not fit a man who died on the throne. The royal persona is used hard through chapters 1-2, for an experiment only a king could fund, and then dropped and never resumed; from chapter 3 on the speaker addresses kings from below, warns about corrupt officials, and describes oppression he cannot fix — not the posture of a monarch. An epilogist speaks in the third person at 12:9-14 ('Qohelet sought to find pleasing words'), which is a second hand at minimum.
Date written
Debated, most commonly PERSIAN or early HELLENISTIC, 4th-3rd century BC, on linguistic grounds that are unusually concrete: the Hebrew is late, it carries Aramaic vocabulary (ra'yon, 'thought,' at 1:17 — the ordinary Aramaic word, found in Hebrew only in this book), and two Persian loanwords (pardes, 'park,' 2:5; pitgam, 'decree,' 8:11). The Qumran copy 4QQoh-a, dated palaeographically to about 175-150 BC, sets the latest possible date. A minority still argue for a Solomonic or early monarchic origin and read the late features as dialect. The SETTING the book claims and the century it was written in are two separate questions.
Place
Jerusalem, as the speaker locates himself (1:1, 12, 16) — a court, a building programme, vineyards and pools and slaves in chapter 2. Beyond that the book is strikingly placeless: no temple ritual, no festivals, no Israelite history, no promised land. Its horizon is 'under the sun,' which is to say everywhere and anywhere a person works and dies.
Audience
Readers who already knew the standard wisdom and had noticed it does not always hold — that the diligent are not reliably rewarded, that the wise and the fool share a grave, that oppression happens and 'there was no one to comfort them' (4:1). Ecclesiastes is written for people at the point where piety meets evidence, and it does not resolve the tension by denying either side.

How it's laid out

1:1-11The title, the motto — 'vapor of vapors' — and the great nature poem: generations, sun, wind and rivers, all in motion and arriving nowhere. 'There is nothing new under the sun.'
1:12-2:26THE ROYAL EXPERIMENT — wisdom, pleasure, building, gardens, wealth, music, everything a king can buy, tested and measured. The verdict: all of it vapor, and the wise man dies like the fool, and someone else inherits the work.
3:1-15'For everything there is a season' — the most famous poem in the book, and the sober conclusion that follows it: God has set eternity in the human heart, yet no one can find out what he has done.
3:16-6:12OBSERVATIONS FROM UNDER THE SUN — injustice in the courts, the tears of the oppressed, the futility of hoarding, vows made carelessly, a man who gains everything and cannot enjoy it.
7-8Proverbs collected and then interrogated: the day of death better than the day of birth, the righteous perishing in their righteousness, and the plain admission that the work of God cannot be found out.
9-11The same fate for all; therefore eat your bread with joy, do your work with your might, cast your bread on the waters, and rejoice — because darkness is coming and the days are many.
12:1-8THE CLOSING POEM — 'remember your Creator in the days of your youth,' and then old age and death drawn as a house falling silent, ending where the book began: vapor of vapors.
12:9-14THE EPILOGUE, in another voice: Qohelet taught the people knowledge, the making of many books has no end, and — 'fear God and keep his commandments, for this is the whole of man.'

What it's about

  • HEVEL — vapor. Thirty-eight occurrences, and the whole argument turns on how it is read. Not 'worthless' and not 'meaningless': a breath, something real that cannot be held or kept. The book is about impermanence and un-grasp-ability, not about nothing mattering.
  • 'UNDER THE SUN' — the declared limit of the enquiry, twenty-nine times. Qohelet reports only what can be observed from inside the world. That restriction is why the book sounds sceptical, and it is also what keeps its scepticism honest.
  • DEATH AS THE FLATTENER — the same fate for the wise and the fool, the righteous and the wicked, human and animal (3:19-20). It is the fact Qohelet keeps returning to and refuses to soften, and it is the ground of everything else he says.
  • THE PORTION — over against vapor stands cheleq, your 'portion': bread, wine, work, a companion, the days you actually have. Seven times the book breaks off its argument to say eat, drink, and find good in your labour, 'for this is from the hand of God.' It is not resignation and it is not hedonism; it is the only recommendation the evidence supports.
  • THE LIMIT OF WISDOM — 'in much wisdom is much vexation' (1:18). Knowing more does not make you safer or happier; it makes you see more clearly what you cannot fix (1:15).
  • GOD, UNSEARCHABLE BUT PRESENT — God is named forty times, always as ELOHIM and never by the divine name. He gives, he judges, he has made everything beautiful in its time, and he cannot be found out. Fear him: that is the book's one imperative, and its last word.

The source text

Translated from the pointed Hebrew Masoretic Text (as printed by Mechon-Mamre). ⚠ The Hebrew of Ecclesiastes is unlike any other book's: late in its grammar, Mishnaic in places, carrying Aramaic and Persian vocabulary, and using words found nowhere else in the Bible (chesron, 1:15; inyan, eight times; yitron, ten). Several of its sentences are genuinely hard, and the ancient versions already disagree about them. The Septuagint of Ecclesiastes is an extremely literal, almost word-for-word rendering — often attributed to Aquila or his school — which makes it a useful witness to the consonantal text but a poor guide to meaning. This translation keeps hevel as 'vapor' throughout rather than varying it, so the refrain can be heard as a refrain.

Looking forward

Ecclesiastes contains no prophecy and no messianic figure, and it should not be made to. What it does is clear ground. It states, without consolation, the problem that death makes of every human project, and it declines every cheap answer — which is why Paul's language about creation 'subjected to futility' (Romans 8:20) uses the Greek word the Septuagint uses for hevel, and why 1 Corinthians 15's argument ends where Qohelet's does, at the grave, before it says anything else. Read canonically, Ecclesiastes is the book that makes resurrection a serious question rather than a sentiment; read on its own terms, it is a man telling the truth about the sun and refusing to look past it.

Where the debates are

AUTHORSHIP AND DATE — Solomon, or a later writer in a Solomonic persona; the linguistic evidence is the main battleground. WHETHER THE BOOK IS PESSIMISTIC — whether the recurring 'eat, drink, and find good in your toil' passages are the point of the book or a bitter shrug. THE EPILOGUE (12:9-14) — an integral conclusion by the same hand, or an orthodox corrective added by an editor uneasy with what precedes it. HOW TO TRANSLATE HEVEL, which decides more of a reader's experience than any other single choice in the book. WHETHER QOHELET BELIEVES IN AN AFTERLIFE — 3:21 asks who knows whether the human spirit goes up, and 12:7 says the spirit returns to God who gave it. And the old rabbinic question of the CONTRADICTIONS, which the Talmud names openly and which no harmonisation has ever quite settled.
