The prologue to Proverbs — the purpose of wisdom, a father's first warning, and Wisdom crying aloud in the streets.
Attributed to Solomon, son of David (reigned c. 970–931 BC) — the wisdom gathered at Israel's royal court, though the book collects sayings from several hands across the monarchy. Wisdom literature keeps no calendar of its own: this is timeless counsel filed under the king who made the court famous for it.

Translator's Notes — verse by verse

Each note explains this translation's choice and compares the seven versions on the shelf. Quotations from copyrighted versions (NIV, TLB, NWT) are kept to short phrases for comparison; KJV, Geneva, Douay-Rheims and ASV are public domain.

Verses 1–7 · יִרְאַת יְהוָה, רֵאשִׁית דָּעַת yirat Jehovah, reshit da'at

A new kind of book — and its whole toolkit in seven verses. This is the site's first wisdom book, and it does not tell a story or deliver an oracle: it teaches. The Hebrew is poetry — every line a couplet, the second half echoing, sharpening, or countering the first (this translation sets the two halves on two lines so the parallel shows). The title-word is the book's own name: mishlei, "the proverbs of Solomon" — a mashal is a comparison, a saying that rules by likeness (the root means "to be like," and also "to rule"). Verses 2–6 are one long sentence of purpose, and they lay out the entire vocabulary the rest of the book will use: chokhmah (wisdom, skill for living), musar (instruction — but with an edge of discipline, correction that costs; KJV "instruction," NIV "discipline," and the word carries both), binah (understanding, the power to tell things apart), and the two audiences: the simple (peti — the wide-open, gullible young person) and the already-wise, who are told to keep learning (v5). The prize offered the simple in verse 4 is ormah, "shrewdness" — and here the shelf keeps a secret this project loves: KJV renders it "subtilty," the very word it used for the serpent in (Genesis 3:1), "more subtil than any beast." The serpent's root and Wisdom's are the same (ʿarum / ormah): the craftiness that ruined Eden is exactly what Wisdom gives the naive to survive — shrewdness redeemed.

The motto of the book. Then verse 7, the sentence everything else hangs from: "The fear of Jehovah is the beginning of knowledge" (ASV reads it exactly so — "the fear of Jehovah is the beginning of knowledge" — while KJV prints its small-caps divine name in the same slot). Two things the shelf can't quite carry at once: reshit is "beginning" — and it is the exact word that opens the whole Bible, bereshit, "in the beginning" (Genesis 1:1) — but it also means "the first / chief part," the firstfruits, the best. So the fear of Jehovah is both where knowledge starts and its finest portion. And "fear" (yirah) is not fright but reverent awe — the same posture the angel found in Abraham at the altar, "now I know that you fear God" (22:12). The opposite party is named too: the evilim, the fools who "despise" it — the first of a whole rogues' gallery of the unwise the book will parade.

Verses 8–19 · אִם-יְפַתּוּךָ חַטָּאִים im-yefattukha chatta'im

The first lecture: a father's voice, a mother's law. "Hear, my son" (shema beni) — the address that will open lecture after lecture; wisdom in Proverbs is handed down at home, across a table, parent to child. And it comes from both parents: "your father's instruction and your mother's teaching" — where "teaching" is torah, the very word for the five books of Moses, here given to a mother's home-lesson (KJV "the law of thy mother"): Torah begins on a lap. The reward is jewelry — "a wreath of grace for your head" (KJV "an ornament of grace"): the disciplined life as something you wear.

"If sinners entice you." The verb is patah — and this site has heard it before, in the rawest place: it is the word Jeremiah threw at God, "you enticed me, Jehovah, and I was enticed" (Jeremiah 20:7). The same seduction the prophet felt from heaven, the son is warned against from the street-gang — whose pitch the father then quotes at length, and it is chillingly modern: easy money, belonging ("one purse for us all"), the thrill of the ambush. The trap-image is precise: even a bird won't fly into a net it can see being spread (v17) — yet these men spread it for themselves, "they lie in wait for their own blood" (v18). The moral is stated as physics, not sermon: "such are the paths of everyone greedy for gain — it takes away the life of its owner" (v19). Greed is a boomerang; the loot ends up costing exactly the thing it was traded for.

Verses 20–33 · חָכְמוֹת בַּחוּץ תָּרֹנָּה chokhmot bachuts taronnah

Lady Wisdom takes the street. Now the book's most startling move: Wisdom stands up and preaches. She is personified — a woman (the noun is feminine), and the plural form chokhmot reads as majestic, "Wisdom" at full stature (KJV "Wisdom" for the plural). And she doesn't sit in a school: she "cries aloud in the street," in the squares, "at the head of the noisy streets," "at the openings of the gates" — the market, the courthouse, the town's loudest corners. This is a prophet's posture, not a professor's; she sounds like Jeremiah at the temple gate. Her first words are the prophets' own weary question: "How long?" — aimed at the whole rogues' gallery at once: the simple (petayim) who love their simplicity, the scoffers (letsim — the cynics who mock everything; KJV "scorners"), and the fools (kesilim — the thick, the dull; a different fool from verse 7's evil) who "hate knowledge." Hebrew has a whole thesaurus for foolishness, and this chapter opens the drawer.

The most frightening promise in the book — and the softest. What follows is hard to hear: the same Wisdom who begs in verse 23 ("turn back… I will pour out my spirit to you") warns that a day is coming when the call reverses — "I too will laugh at your calamity" (v26), and "then they will call me, but I will not answer" (v28). It is the exact inversion of every prophet's promise that God answers those who call; refuse wisdom long enough, the poem says, and you unfit yourself to receive it — not because the door is shut in spite, but because "they hated knowledge and did not choose the fear of Jehovah" (v29), and a life reaps "the fruit of its own way" (v31) the way the greedy men of verse 19 did. The Hebrew here even carries a scribe's fingerprint — verse 27's ketiv/qere, the written and read forms of "storm" standing side by side, the manuscript's honesty this site first met in Daniel. But the chapter does not end in the whirlwind. Its last line drops the volume all the way down to a promise anyone can keep: "whoever listens to me will dwell secure, and be at ease, without dread of harm" (v33). The whole book's offer, in one quiet couplet — safety, not from trouble, but from fear.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation sides with the literal shelf: "The fear of Jehovah is the beginning of knowledge" — the KJV's line, all but untouched; "Wisdom cries" for the majestic plural (with KJV); the fools' whole vocabulary kept distinct (peti simple, lets scoffer, kesil and evil, two different fools) rather than flattened to one "fool."

Where it goes its own way: the couplets set on two lines, to show the Hebrew parallelism the prose shelf hides; "shrewdness" for ormah (KJV "subtilty" — the note keeps the KJV's serpent-echo, since that is the whole point); "instruction" for musar with its disciplinary edge flagged (NIV "discipline"); "your mother's teaching" for torah (KJV "law"), to let a mother's Torah be heard as Torah; and "the beginning / chief part" both kept in the note for reshit.

Echoes paid and planted. Paid: ormah, Wisdom's "shrewdness," the serpent's craft (3:1) turned to a survival skill; reshit, "beginning," the Bible's own first word (Genesis 1:1); the fear of God, found in Abraham at the altar (22:12); and patah, "entice," the verb Jeremiah hurled at heaven (Jeremiah 20:7), now the sinners' pitch. Planted: Lady Wisdom, who will rise to her full height in Proverbs 8 (and whom the New Testament will hear behind the voice of Christ); the fools' gallery, filled in chapter by chapter; and "the fear of Jehovah," the refrain the whole book is tuned to.
