Translator’s Notes

Verses 1–3 · a king we can name, a year we can date, and a war the book never mentions

⚠ Ahasuerus is XERXES. The Hebrew Achashverosh is a careful transcription of Old Persian Khshayarsha, which Greek turned into Xerxes — Xerxes I, who reigned 486–465 BC. Every English version keeps the Hebrew form — KJV, ASV and NIV all print “Ahasuerus” — and so do we; the identification belongs in a note rather than smuggled into the text. That places this book precisely: after the temple was finished (516 BC, Ezra 6) and before Ezra and Nehemiah came up under Artaxerxes, who was this king’s son. The three books this site has shipped this week sit in one continuous stretch of Persian history, and Esther falls in the middle of it.

«From India to Cush» — from the Indus to Nubia, the two ends of the known world as an imperial chancery reckoned them. The hundred and twenty-seven provinces are sometimes said to contradict Herodotus, who counts twenty Persian satrapies; they do not. A medinah is a smaller administrative district, and an empire has many more of those than it has satraps.

«In Susa the citadel» — Shushan ha-birah, the same phrase, for the same fortified palace complex, that opens Nehemiah 1. Two Judeans, two generations apart, both inside the same government compound. The apadana and the glazed-brick guardsmen dug up there belong to this king’s father and to this king.

⚠ And now the date, which is the most interesting thing in the verse. “The third year of his reign” is 483 BC — and Herodotus records that in exactly this period Xerxes assembled his nobles, his commanders and the governors of his provinces to plan the invasion of Greece, a campaign that sailed in 480 and ended at Salamis. This chapter describes a hundred and eighty days of the king displaying his wealth to the army of Persia and Media and the princes of the provinces. It is very difficult not to hear a war council in it.

And then note where chapter 2 picks up: Esther becomes queen in the seventh year (2:16), 479 BC — the year after Xerxes came home beaten. The gap between chapter 1 and chapter 2 is the Greek war, and the book never mentions it. It does not have to; its first readers knew where the king had been. The story it wants to tell is what happened in the palace while the empire was busy losing at sea.

Verses 4–9 · a wall of expensive words, and a law about drinking

The chapter opens with two banquets and will end with ten. Mishteh — literally a drinking, from the verb to drink — is the book’s structural unit: everything decisive in Esther happens at a feast, including both of Haman’s. The plot is a chain of banquets, and it starts with the longest and emptiest of them.

⚠ Verse 6 is a deliberate dazzle, and several of its words are genuinely unknown. The Hebrew piles up fabrics and stones with almost no grammar — chur karpas u-tkhelet… bahat va-shesh ve-dar ve-socharet — and at least three of them occur nowhere else in the Bible. Nobody is certain what bahat, dar or socharet are; the guesses run through porphyry, alabaster, mother-of-pearl and various dark or marbled stones. KJV produced “a pavement of red, and blue, and white, and black, marble”; ASV “porphyry, and white marble, and shell, and black marble”; NIV “mosaic pavement of porphyry, marble, mother-of-pearl and other costly stones.” We render them as expensive stone and flag the uncertainty, because the obscurity is doing work: this is a list of things too costly for the reader to picture, which is exactly the impression a Persian court wished to make.

«Vessels differing from vessels.» Kelim mi-kelim shonim — no two the same. Gold vessels at a Persian king’s drinking party, in a book that will never once mention the temple or God, are worth pausing over; the reader who has just come from Ezra 1, where a Persian king counts the temple’s vessels home, and from Daniel 5, where a king drinks from them, will hear something the text does not say.

⚠ «And the drinking was according to the LAW: no one compelling.» Dat is a Persian loanword (data), and it is the key word of this book — it occurs about twenty times, more than in the rest of the Bible together, and by chapter 3 it will be the machinery of a genocide. Here is its first appearance: the law of this empire, on its first outing, is a rule that nobody has to drink more than he wants to. The joke is not accidental, and the book will spend nine chapters darkening it.

«Vashti the queen also made a banquet for the women.» One verse, and it matters: she has her own court, her own guests and her own house. She is not a decoration who has wandered off; she is holding a state function of her own when the summons arrives.

Verses 10–12 · the refusal, and the reason the book withholds

Seven eunuchs, named. The book loves these lists — seven eunuchs here, seven princes four verses later, and ten sons of Haman later still — and the names are good Persian ones. One of them is worth remembering: Harbona reappears at 7:9, at the worst possible moment for Haman, to mention helpfully that there is a gallows already standing in his courtyard.

«When the king’s heart was merry with wine.» The Hebrew idiom is good with wine, and the narrator drops it in without comment on the seventh day of the second banquet. Everything that follows — the summons, the rage, the irrevocable decree — is the business of a drunk man and a room of courtiers who will not tell him so. KJV “when the heart of the king was merry with wine” keeps the idiom; NIV “in high spirits from wine” is politer than the Hebrew.

⚠ «With the royal crown.» The text says exactly that and no more. An ancient and very widespread Jewish reading (Talmud, Megillah 12b) holds that she was ordered to come wearing the crown and nothing else — which would explain a great deal. The Hebrew does not say it, and we do not print it. But note what the text does do: the king has just spent seven verses displaying his possessions, item by item, to an audience, and on the last day of the party he sends for his wife to show the peoples and the princes her beauty. She is the final exhibit. Whatever she was or was not asked to wear, the chapter has already told you what kind of summons it is.

⚠ «But Vashti the queen REFUSED to come» — and the book gives no reason. Not modesty, not principle, not fear, not politics: nothing. The narrator who has itemized the tablecloths declines to explain the one decision the plot turns on. Readers have been filling that silence for two thousand years, and the silence is almost certainly deliberate — the story does not need her motive, it needs her absence, which is the vacancy Esther will be brought in to fill. It is worth saying plainly that the book never criticizes her, and that a queen who says no to Xerxes at the height of his self-display is the first person in this story to defy imperial power and pay for it.

Verses 13–20 · a dinner-party snub becomes an imperial emergency

⚠ This is where the book’s humor becomes unmistakable, and it is at the men’s expense. A king, drunk, is embarrassed in front of his guests. He convenes his legal experts — “the wise men who knew the times,” the seven princes who see the king’s face and sit first in the kingdom, the empire’s entire constitutional apparatus — and asks what the law requires. And Memucan rises and explains that this is not a domestic matter at all: it is a crisis affecting all the princes and all the peoples in all the provinces, and that unless something is done immediately, women everywhere will hear of it by this very day and husbands will be held in contempt across an empire stretching from India to Cush.

The narrator does not editorialize by so much as a syllable. He simply reports the speech, and the speech is ridiculous. Every version renders it straight — KJV “there shall arise too much contempt and wrath,” ASV the same — which is correct: the comedy is in the content, and a translator who winks at it spoils it. Esther is, among other things, a comedy about pompous, frightened officialdom — and a decree commanding “that every man should be master in his own house” is transparently the act of men who have just discovered that one of them is not. The reader is meant to laugh, and then, three chapters later, to stop laughing when the same apparatus is turned on an entire people just as fast and just as thoughtlessly.

⚠ «Let it be written among the laws of Persia and Media so that it cannot pass away.» The irrevocable decree — a law that once issued cannot be cancelled even by the king who issued it. It is the mechanism of the entire plot: in chapter 8 Haman’s edict against the Jews cannot be revoked, only counteracted by a second edict, which is why the book ends the way it does. The same convention drives Daniel 6, where Darius cannot save Daniel from his own signature (“the law of the Medes and Persians, which does not pass away”).

It is worth being honest that this feature is not attested in Persian sources. No Persian document describes such a rule, and a monarch genuinely unable to reverse his own decisions would be a strange sort of absolute ruler. It is generally taken as a literary convention rather than constitutional history — a storyteller’s device that both Esther and Daniel use to trap a king inside his own machinery. That does not make the books less interesting; it makes them a particular kind of writing about empire.

⚠ Verse 16 carries a ketiv/qere: the name is written Momukan and read Memukan.

Verses 21–22 · the imperial post, and a God who is never mentioned

«To every province in its own script and to every people in its own language.» The Persian royal road and its relay couriers were among the genuine wonders of the ancient world — Herodotus’s line about neither snow nor rain nor heat nor darkness staying them from their appointed rounds is about these riders, and it ended up carved on a New York post office. The book takes the system entirely seriously and uses it three times. And the first thing we see it carry, to a hundred and twenty-seven provinces in every script in the empire, is an announcement that men should be in charge at home. The machinery is magnificent; the message is a drunk man’s hangover.

The last clause is odd — “and speak according to the language of his own people” — and translators have never been comfortable with it. KJV “and that it should be published according to the language of every people” makes it a note about the decree’s distribution; ASV and NIV take it as part of the decree itself, which is what the Hebrew most naturally says. On that reading a household is to speak the husband’s language — a second small tyranny bolted onto the first, and a detail that will matter in a book about a woman who conceals her people.

⚠ And here, at the end of the first chapter, is the thing Esther is most famous for: GOD IS NOT MENTIONED. Not in this chapter, and not in the nine that follow — no prayer, no sacrifice, no covenant, no law of Moses, no Jerusalem, not one occurrence of the divine name. It is the only book in the Hebrew Bible of which that is true (the Greek version was uncomfortable enough to add six substantial passages full of prayers and dreams, which is why Catholic and Orthodox Bibles have a longer Esther than Protestant ones). The absence is total and it is deliberate, and readers have taken it two ways ever since: as a book in which providence is invisible but everywhere, working through coincidences, insomnia and bad timing; or as a book that honestly describes what it is like to be a small people in a large empire, where nobody speaks from heaven and you have to act anyway. The library prints the book and does not decide. But it is worth beginning it with the absence noticed rather than explained away.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the exact / distinct words: “Ahasuerus” as the Hebrew has it, with Xerxes identified in the note rather than substituted in the text; “banquet” for mishteh, literally a drinking, kept the same for all of the book’s feasts; “law” for dat, the Persian loanword, held identical from this drinking rule to the decrees that follow; “vessels differing from vessels” for the odd doubled phrase; the unknown stones of verse 6 left as expensive stone, with the uncertainty flagged rather than resolved; and “refused” for tema’en, with no motive supplied, because the Hebrew supplies none.

Echoes paid. Susa the citadel, the same palace complex as Nehemiah 1:1; the kelim, gold drinking vessels at a Persian feast in a book that never mentions the temple whose vessels Ezra 1 counted home; and the irrevocable law of the Medes and Persians, the same convention that traps Darius in Daniel 6.

Echoes planted. The vacancy Vashti leaves, which is the whole reason there is a story. Harbona, one of seven named eunuchs, who will speak one sentence at exactly the right moment in chapter 7. The irrevocable decree, which is how Haman’s edict cannot be cancelled in chapter 8. Dat, the law, introduced here as a courtesy about wine and ending as an instrument of extermination. And the silence about God, which begins in verse 1 and is never once broken.
