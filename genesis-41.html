Genesis 41 — The Mister Translation

1
וַיְהִי, מִקֵּץ שְׁנָתַיִם יָמִים; וּפַרְעֹה חֹלֵם, וְהִנֵּה עֹמֵד עַל-הַיְאֹר.
And so it was, at the end of two full years, that Pharaoh was dreaming — and look, he was standing by the Nile.note
Y así fue que, al cabo de dos años cumplidos, el faraón estaba soñando — y mira, estaba de pie junto al Nilo.
2
וְהִנֵּה מִן-הַיְאֹר, עֹלֹת שֶׁבַע פָּרוֹת, יְפוֹת מַרְאֶה, וּבְרִיאֹת בָּשָׂר; וַתִּרְעֶינָה, בָּאָחוּ.
And look — out of the Nile came up seven cows, lovely to look at and fat of flesh, and they grazed in the reed-grass.note
Y mira — del Nilo subían siete vacas, de bello aspecto y gordas de carne, y pacían en el carrizal.
3
וְהִנֵּה שֶׁבַע פָּרוֹת אֲחֵרוֹת, עֹלוֹת אַחֲרֵיהֶן מִן-הַיְאֹר, רָעוֹת מַרְאֶה, וְדַקּוֹת בָּשָׂר; וַתַּעֲמֹדְנָה אֵצֶל הַפָּרוֹת, עַל-שְׂפַת הַיְאֹר.
And look — seven other cows came up after them out of the Nile, bad to look at and thin of flesh, and they stood beside the cows on the bank of the Nile.note
Y mira — otras siete vacas subían tras ellas del Nilo, de mal aspecto y flacas de carne, y se pusieron junto a las vacas a la orilla del Nilo.
4
וַתֹּאכַלְנָה הַפָּרוֹת, רָעוֹת הַמַּרְאֶה וְדַקֹּת הַבָּשָׂר, אֵת שֶׁבַע הַפָּרוֹת, יְפֹת הַמַּרְאֶה וְהַבְּרִיאֹת; וַיִּיקַץ, פַּרְעֹה.
And the cows that were bad to look at and thin of flesh ate up the seven cows that were lovely to look at and fat. And Pharaoh woke.note
Y las vacas de mal aspecto y flacas de carne devoraron a las siete vacas de bello aspecto y gordas. Y el faraón despertó.
5
וַיִּישָׁן, וַיַּחֲלֹם שֵׁנִית; וְהִנֵּה שֶׁבַע שִׁבֳּלִים, עֹלוֹת בְּקָנֶה אֶחָד--בְּרִיאוֹת וְטֹבוֹת.
And he slept and dreamed a second time — and look, seven ears of grain were coming up on one stalk, fat and good.note
Y se durmió y soñó por segunda vez — y mira, siete espigas subían en una sola caña, gruesas y buenas.
6
וְהִנֵּה שֶׁבַע שִׁבֳּלִים, דַּקּוֹת וּשְׁדוּפֹת קָדִים--צֹמְחוֹת, אַחֲרֵיהֶן.
And look — seven ears, thin and scorched by the east wind, were sprouting after them.note
Y mira — siete espigas delgadas y abrasadas por el viento del este brotaban tras ellas.
7
וַתִּבְלַעְנָה, הַשִּׁבֳּלִים הַדַּקּוֹת, אֵת שֶׁבַע הַשִּׁבֳּלִים, הַבְּרִיאוֹת וְהַמְּלֵאוֹת; וַיִּיקַץ פַּרְעֹה, וְהִנֵּה חֲלוֹם.
And the thin ears swallowed the seven fat and full ears. And Pharaoh woke — and look, it was a dream.note
Y las espigas delgadas se tragaron las siete espigas gruesas y llenas. Y el faraón despertó — y mira, era un sueño.
8
וַיְהִי בַבֹּקֶר, וַתִּפָּעֶם רוּחוֹ, וַיִּשְׁלַח וַיִּקְרָא אֶת-כָּל-חַרְטֻמֵּי מִצְרַיִם, וְאֶת-כָּל-חֲכָמֶיהָ; וַיְסַפֵּר פַּרְעֹה לָהֶם אֶת-חֲלֹמוֹ, וְאֵין-פּוֹתֵר אוֹתָם לְפַרְעֹה.
And so it was in the morning that his spirit was disturbed; and he sent and called all the diviner-priests of Egypt and all its wise men, and Pharaoh recounted his dream to them — and there was no one to interpret them for Pharaoh.note
Y así fue que por la mañana su espíritu se turbó; y envió a llamar a todos los sacerdotes-adivinos de Egipto y a todos sus sabios, y el faraón les contó su sueño — y no hubo quien se los interpretara al faraón.

9
וַיְדַבֵּר שַׂר הַמַּשְׁקִים, אֶת-פַּרְעֹה לֵאמֹר: אֶת-חֲטָאַי, אֲנִי מַזְכִּיר הַיּוֹם.
And the chief of the cupbearers spoke to Pharaoh, saying, "I bring my faults to remembrance today.note
Y el jefe de los coperos habló al faraón, diciendo: «Traigo hoy a la memoria mis faltas.
cross-refs⤷ 40:23
10
פַּרְעֹה, קָצַף עַל-עֲבָדָיו; וַיִּתֵּן אֹתִי בְּמִשְׁמַר, בֵּית שַׂר הַטַּבָּחִים--אֹתִי, וְאֵת שַׂר הָאֹפִים.
Pharaoh was furious with his servants, and he put me in custody in the house of the chief of the executioners — me and the chief of the bakers.note
El faraón se enfureció contra sus siervos, y me puso bajo custodia en la casa del jefe de los verdugos — a mí y al jefe de los panaderos.
11
וַנַּחַלְמָה חֲלוֹם בְּלַיְלָה אֶחָד, אֲנִי וָהוּא: אִישׁ כְּפִתְרוֹן חֲלֹמוֹ, חָלָמְנוּ.
And we dreamed a dream in one night, he and I; each of us dreamed according to the interpretation of his dream.note
Y soñamos un sueño en una misma noche, él y yo; cada uno soñó conforme a la interpretación de su sueño.
12
וְשָׁם אִתָּנוּ נַעַר עִבְרִי, עֶבֶד לְשַׂר הַטַּבָּחִים, וַנְּסַפֶּר-לוֹ, וַיִּפְתָּר-לָנוּ אֶת-חֲלֹמֹתֵינוּ: אִישׁ כַּחֲלֹמוֹ, פָּתָר.
And there with us was a Hebrew lad, a slave of the chief of the executioners; and we recounted them to him, and he interpreted our dreams for us — to each man according to his dream he interpreted.note
Y allí con nosotros había un muchacho hebreo, esclavo del jefe de los verdugos; y se los contamos, y él nos interpretó nuestros sueños — a cada uno conforme a su sueño interpretó.
13
וַיְהִי כַּאֲשֶׁר פָּתַר-לָנוּ, כֵּן הָיָה: אֹתִי הֵשִׁיב עַל-כַּנִּי, וְאֹתוֹ תָלָה.
And so it was: as he interpreted for us, so it happened — me he restored to my post, and him he hanged."note
Y así fue: como nos interpretó, así sucedió — a mí me restituyó a mi puesto, y a él lo colgó».
14
וַיִּשְׁלַח פַּרְעֹה וַיִּקְרָא אֶת-יוֹסֵף, וַיְרִיצֻהוּ מִן-הַבּוֹר; וַיְגַלַּח וַיְחַלֵּף שִׂמְלֹתָיו, וַיָּבֹא אֶל-פַּרְעֹה.
And Pharaoh sent and called Joseph, and they hurried him out of the pit; and he shaved and changed his garments, and came in to Pharaoh.note
Y el faraón envió a llamar a José, y lo sacaron a toda prisa del pozo; y él se afeitó y cambió sus vestiduras, y entró ante el faraón.
cross-refs⤷ 37:24
15
וַיֹּאמֶר פַּרְעֹה, אֶל-יוֹסֵף, חֲלוֹם חָלַמְתִּי, וּפֹתֵר אֵין אֹתוֹ; וַאֲנִי, שָׁמַעְתִּי עָלֶיךָ לֵאמֹר, תִּשְׁמַע חֲלוֹם, לִפְתֹּר אֹתוֹ.
And Pharaoh said to Joseph, "I have dreamed a dream, and there is no one to interpret it; and I have heard it said of you that you hear a dream, to interpret it."note
Y el faraón dijo a José: «He soñado un sueño, y no hay quien lo interprete; y yo he oído decir de ti que oyes un sueño para interpretarlo».
16
וַיַּעַן יוֹסֵף אֶת-פַּרְעֹה לֵאמֹר, בִּלְעָדָי: אֱלֹהִים, יַעֲנֶה אֶת-שְׁלוֹם פַּרְעֹה.
And Joseph answered Pharaoh, saying, "Not I — God will answer for Pharaoh's peace."note
Y José respondió al faraón, diciendo: «No yo — Dios responderá por la paz del faraón».
cross-refs⤷ 40:8⤷ 37:4
17
וַיְדַבֵּר פַּרְעֹה, אֶל-יוֹסֵף: בַּחֲלֹמִי, הִנְנִי עֹמֵד עַל-שְׂפַת הַיְאֹר.
And Pharaoh spoke to Joseph: "In my dream — there I was, standing on the bank of the Nile.note
Y el faraón habló a José: «En mi sueño — allí estaba yo, de pie a la orilla del Nilo.
18
וְהִנֵּה מִן-הַיְאֹר, עֹלֹת שֶׁבַע פָּרוֹת, בְּרִיאוֹת בָּשָׂר, וִיפֹת תֹּאַר; וַתִּרְעֶינָה, בָּאָחוּ.
And look — out of the Nile came up seven cows, fat of flesh and beautiful in form, and they grazed in the reed-grass.note
Y mira — del Nilo subían siete vacas, gordas de carne y hermosas de forma, y pacían en el carrizal.
19
וְהִנֵּה שֶׁבַע-פָּרוֹת אֲחֵרוֹת, עֹלוֹת אַחֲרֵיהֶן, דַּלּוֹת וְרָעוֹת תֹּאַר מְאֹד, וְרַקּוֹת בָּשָׂר: לֹא-רָאִיתִי כָהֵנָּה בְּכָל-אֶרֶץ מִצְרַיִם, לָרֹעַ.
And look — seven other cows came up after them, poor and very bad in form and lean of flesh; I have never seen their like in all the land of Egypt for badness.note
Y mira — otras siete vacas subían tras ellas, pobres y malísimas de forma y enjutas de carne; nunca vi otras semejantes en toda la tierra de Egipto de tan malas.
20
וַתֹּאכַלְנָה, הַפָּרוֹת, הָרַקּוֹת, וְהָרָעוֹת--אֵת שֶׁבַע הַפָּרוֹת הָרִאשֹׁנוֹת, הַבְּרִיאֹת.
And the lean and bad cows ate up the first seven fat cows.note
Y las vacas enjutas y malas devoraron a las siete primeras vacas gordas.
21
וַתָּבֹאנָה אֶל-קִרְבֶּנָה, וְלֹא נוֹדַע כִּי-בָאוּ אֶל-קִרְבֶּנָה, וּמַרְאֵיהֶן רַע, כַּאֲשֶׁר בַּתְּחִלָּה; וָאִיקָץ.
And they went inside them, and it could not be known that they had gone inside them — their appearance was as bad as at the start. And I woke.note
Y entraron en sus entrañas, y no se notaba que hubieran entrado en sus entrañas — su aspecto era tan malo como al principio. Y desperté.
22
וָאֵרֶא, בַּחֲלֹמִי; וְהִנֵּה שֶׁבַע שִׁבֳּלִים, עֹלֹת בְּקָנֶה אֶחָד--מְלֵאֹת וְטֹבוֹת.
And I saw in my dream — and look, seven ears were coming up on one stalk, full and good.note
Y vi en mi sueño — y mira, siete espigas subían en una sola caña, llenas y buenas.
23
וְהִנֵּה שֶׁבַע שִׁבֳּלִים, צְנֻמוֹת דַּקּוֹת שְׁדֻפוֹת קָדִים--צֹמְחוֹת, אַחֲרֵיהֶם.
And look — seven ears, shrivelled, thin, scorched by the east wind, were sprouting after them.note
Y mira — siete espigas marchitas, delgadas, abrasadas por el viento del este, brotaban tras ellas.
24
וַתִּבְלַעְןָ הַשִּׁבֳּלִים הַדַּקֹּת, אֵת שֶׁבַע הַשִּׁבֳּלִים הַטֹּבוֹת; וָאֹמַר, אֶל-הַחַרְטֻמִּים, וְאֵין מַגִּיד, לִי.
And the thin ears swallowed the seven good ears. And I told it to the diviner-priests, and there was no one to declare it to me."note
Y las espigas delgadas se tragaron las siete espigas buenas. Y se lo dije a los sacerdotes-adivinos, y no hubo quien me lo declarara».
25
וַיֹּאמֶר יוֹסֵף אֶל-פַּרְעֹה, חֲלוֹם פַּרְעֹה אֶחָד הוּא: אֵת אֲשֶׁר הָאֱלֹהִים עֹשֶׂה, הִגִּיד לְפַרְעֹה.
And Joseph said to Pharaoh, "Pharaoh's dream is one. What God is about to do he has declared to Pharaoh.note
Y José dijo al faraón: «El sueño del faraón es uno solo. Lo que Dios va a hacer, se lo ha declarado al faraón.
26
שֶׁבַע פָּרֹת הַטֹּבֹת, שֶׁבַע שָׁנִים הֵנָּה, וְשֶׁבַע הַשִּׁבֳּלִים הַטֹּבֹת, שֶׁבַע שָׁנִים הֵנָּה: חֲלוֹם, אֶחָד הוּא.
The seven good cows are seven years, and the seven good ears are seven years — the dream is one.note
Las siete vacas buenas son siete años, y las siete espigas buenas son siete años — el sueño es uno solo.
27
וְשֶׁבַע הַפָּרוֹת הָרַקּוֹת וְהָרָעֹת הָעֹלֹת אַחֲרֵיהֶן, שֶׁבַע שָׁנִים הֵנָּה, וְשֶׁבַע הַשִּׁבֳּלִים הָרֵקוֹת, שְׁדֻפוֹת הַקָּדִים--יִהְיוּ, שֶׁבַע שְׁנֵי רָעָב.
And the seven lean and bad cows that came up after them are seven years, and the seven empty ears scorched by the east wind — they will be seven years of famine.note
Y las siete vacas enjutas y malas que subían tras ellas son siete años, y las siete espigas vacías abrasadas por el viento del este — serán siete años de hambre.
28
הוּא הַדָּבָר, אֲשֶׁר דִּבַּרְתִּי אֶל-פַּרְעֹה: אֲשֶׁר הָאֱלֹהִים עֹשֶׂה, הֶרְאָה אֶת-פַּרְעֹה.
This is the thing I have spoken to Pharaoh: what God is about to do he has shown Pharaoh.note
Esto es lo que he hablado al faraón: lo que Dios va a hacer, se lo ha mostrado al faraón.
29
הִנֵּה שֶׁבַע שָׁנִים, בָּאוֹת--שָׂבָע גָּדוֹל, בְּכָל-אֶרֶץ מִצְרָיִם.
Look — seven years are coming of great plenty in all the land of Egypt.note
Mira — vienen siete años de gran abundancia en toda la tierra de Egipto.
30
וְקָמוּ שֶׁבַע שְׁנֵי רָעָב, אַחֲרֵיהֶן, וְנִשְׁכַּח כָּל-הַשָּׂבָע, בְּאֶרֶץ מִצְרָיִם; וְכִלָּה הָרָעָב, אֶת-הָאָרֶץ.
And seven years of famine will rise after them, and all the plenty will be forgotten in the land of Egypt, and the famine will finish off the land.note
Y tras ellos se levantarán siete años de hambre, y toda la abundancia será olvidada en la tierra de Egipto, y el hambre acabará con la tierra.
31
וְלֹא-יִוָּדַע הַשָּׂבָע בָּאָרֶץ, מִפְּנֵי הָרָעָב הַהוּא אַחֲרֵי-כֵן: כִּי-כָבֵד הוּא, מְאֹד.
And the plenty will not be known in the land because of that famine afterward, for it will be very heavy.note
Y no se conocerá la abundancia en la tierra a causa de aquel hambre posterior, porque será muy pesada.
32
וְעַל הִשָּׁנוֹת הַחֲלוֹם אֶל-פַּרְעֹה, פַּעֲמָיִם--כִּי-נָכוֹן הַדָּבָר מֵעִם הָאֱלֹהִים, וּמְמַהֵר הָאֱלֹהִים לַעֲשֹׂתוֹ.
And as for the dream being doubled to Pharaoh twice — it is because the thing is established from God, and God is hurrying to do it.note
Y en cuanto a haberse repetido el sueño al faraón dos veces — es porque el asunto está establecido de parte de Dios, y Dios se apresura a hacerlo.
cross-refs⤷ 37:5
33
וְעַתָּה יֵרֶא פַרְעֹה, אִישׁ נָבוֹן וְחָכָם; וִישִׁיתֵהוּ, עַל-אֶרֶץ מִצְרָיִם.
And now let Pharaoh look out a man of discernment and wisdom, and set him over the land of Egypt.note
Y ahora busque el faraón un hombre entendido y sabio, y póngalo sobre la tierra de Egipto.
34
יַעֲשֶׂה פַרְעֹה, וְיַפְקֵד פְּקִדִים עַל-הָאָרֶץ; וְחִמֵּשׁ אֶת-אֶרֶץ מִצְרַיִם, בְּשֶׁבַע שְׁנֵי הַשָּׂבָע.
Let Pharaoh act, and appoint overseers over the land, and take a fifth of the land of Egypt during the seven years of plenty.note
Actúe el faraón, y nombre supervisores sobre la tierra, y tome la quinta parte de la tierra de Egipto durante los siete años de abundancia.
cross-refs⤷ 47:14
35
וְיִקְבְּצוּ, אֶת-כָּל-אֹכֶל הַשָּׁנִים הַטֹּבוֹת, הַבָּאֹת, הָאֵלֶּה; וְיִצְבְּרוּ-בָר תַּחַת יַד-פַּרְעֹה, אֹכֶל בֶּעָרִים--וְשָׁמָרוּ.
And let them gather all the food of these good years that are coming, and pile up grain under Pharaoh's hand — food in the cities — and let them keep it.note

Translator's Notes

Verses 1–7 · two years later, a king dreams

"At the end of two full years." The previous chapter ended with a man forgetting; this one opens by telling you how long the forgetting lasted. Genesis does not describe those two years and does not need to — the reader has been holding the cupbearer's promise since 40:23, and the delay is the point. Nothing happens because someone remembers a favour. Something happens because a king has a bad night.

Cows out of the Nile. The imagery is precisely, verifiably Egyptian. The ye'or — a Hebrew word borrowed from Egyptian itrw, used almost exclusively of the Nile — is the source of everything in that country: its annual flood decides whether there is food, and a low Nile means famine. Cattle standing in the shallows and grazing the achu (reed-grass, another Egyptian loanword) is an ordinary Delta scene. Seven emaciated cows walking up out of the river that feeds Egypt is a nightmare about the river failing, dressed in the country's own furniture. The famine is announced in Egyptian imagery two chapters before anyone says the word.

"Scorched by the east wind." The qadim is the khamsin — the desert wind off the Sahara that arrives in spring and can burn a standing crop in hours. Again the dream is speaking local weather. And note the pairing that runs the whole chapter: the bad devours the good and is no better for it (v. 21, "it could not be known that they had gone inside them") — which is a fair description of what famine does to a granary.

Verses 8–13 · the experts fail, and the debt is remembered

The professionals. Pharaoh summons the chartummim — not "magicians" in the conjuring sense, but the lector-priests of the Egyptian temple establishment, the literate specialists who kept the ritual books and, among much else, read dreams. This is the trade Joseph sidestepped in 40:8, and it is a real profession with real manuals (the Dream Book of Papyrus Chester Beatty III survives). They fail. That is not a slur on Egyptian learning; the narrator is making a narrower claim — that this particular dream is not in the reference books, because it did not come from where dreams usually come from. The word will turn up again in another empire's court, where Babylon's experts fail in exactly the same way in front of exactly the same kind of Hebrew (Daniel 1:20; 2:2). The KJV reads "magicians," the NIV the same, the NWT "magic-practicing priests" — which is clumsy English but closer to the institution.

"I bring my faults to remembrance today." And there it is. The verb is zakhar in its causative form — the cupbearer causes to be remembered — and it is the word the last chapter ended on when he did not remember Joseph and forgot him (40:23). Two years late, prompted entirely by his own exposure in a room where the king is upset and nobody has an answer, he remembers. Read his speech carefully and notice how he tells it: "a Hebrew lad, a slave of the chief of the executioners." Both words are true and both are diminishing, and he never once uses Joseph's name. The man who saved his life is introduced to the throne as an anonymous foreign servant.

Verses 14–24 · out of the pit, and "not I"

"They hurried him out of the pit." Bor — the third and last time this word is used of Joseph, and the only time he comes up out of one. His brothers threw him into a bor at Dothan; he called his Egyptian cell a bor when he asked the cupbearer for help; now they run him out of the bor to stand in front of a king. Every English version that reads "dungeon" in these last two verses (the KJV, ASV and NIV all do) breaks a three-link chain that the Hebrew forges deliberately. This translation reads "pit" all three times.

He shaved. Two words of costume detail, and they are exactly right. Egyptians were a clean-shaven people — priests shaved the whole body, and courtiers wore false beards only as formal regalia — while Semites wore beards; Egyptian tomb painting is unambiguous about the difference. A man cannot be brought before Pharaoh looking like a foreigner out of a cell, so he is shaved and re-dressed. It is also the third time in this story that Joseph's clothes are changed by someone else's decision: the coat stripped by his brothers, the garment seized by his master's wife, and now prison rags exchanged for something fit for a throne room.

"Not I." Pharaoh says he has heard that Joseph hears a dream to interpret it, and Joseph's first word to the most powerful man on earth is biladai — "apart from me," "not I." It is the same thing he said to two prisoners in 40:8, said now where it costs something: the one moment when overstating his gift would have served him, and he refuses the credit before he has been offered anything. The KJV renders it "It is not in me: God shall give Pharaoh an answer of peace," the ASV nearly identically; the NWT "Not I! God will speak concerning Pharaoh's welfare." And that last word is shalom — the word the brothers could not speak to him (37:4) and the errand his father sent him on (37:14). Joseph promises a king the thing his own family withheld.

Pharaoh's retelling is not identical. Compare verses 17-24 with 1-7 and small things have moved: the lean cows are now "poor and very bad in form… I have never seen their like in all the land of Egypt for badness," and there is a whole new detail — they ate the fat cows and you could not tell (v. 21). Hebrew narrative repeats with variation on purpose; a dreamer retelling a nightmare adds what frightened him most. The version Joseph actually interprets is the version with the horror in it.

Verses 25–32 · one dream, and why it came twice

"Pharaoh's dream is one." The first thing Joseph does is reduce two dreams to a single message — cows and grain are the same seven years said twice — and the second thing he does is assign the whole business to God, twice, before he says a word about famine ("what God is about to do he has declared to Pharaoh… has shown Pharaoh"). Only then does he give the reading. The order matters: he is not selling a service.

And now the sentence this whole book has been waiting to say. "As for the dream being doubled to Pharaoh twice — it is because the thing is established from God, and God is hurrying to do it." That is the interpretive key to every doubled dream in Genesis, supplied at last by the man whose own two dreams — sheaves and stars — were the doubled pair his brothers hated him for. He is explaining, to a foreign king, the rule that governs his own life; and by this rule the boy's dreams were never adolescent boasting but a settled decree, and his brothers will bow. He does not appear to notice. Two chapters from now they will walk in and do it.

Seven-year famines are not a literary invention. An Egyptian text known as the Famine Stele, carved on Sehel Island near Aswan, tells of a seven-year famine under the Third Dynasty king Djoser and a low Nile that would not rise. ⚠️ The inscription itself is Ptolemaic — roughly two thousand years later than the reign it describes — so it is evidence for the seven-year famine as an established Egyptian tradition, not independent confirmation of this chapter. Egypt's food supply hung on the flood, records of Nile levels were kept for millennia, and successive lean years were a standing national fear. The dream is speaking to a real anxiety.

Verses 33–36 · the interpreter writes a policy

Nobody asked him for this. Pharaoh requested an interpretation; Joseph supplies one and then keeps talking — appoint overseers, take a fifth in the good years, store it in the cities under royal control, hold it as a reserve. It is a complete grain policy, offered unbidden by a prisoner who has been in the room for about four minutes. And it is the reason he gets the job: verse 37 says "the thing was good in the eyes of Pharaoh," and "the thing" is the plan, not the prophecy.

"A man of discernment and wisdom." Navon ve-chakham — the pairing of binah, the discernment that tells things apart, with chokhmah, the practical master-skill. It is the vocabulary of the wisdom books, and Pharaoh will hand it straight back in verse 39. ⚠️ Interpreters differ on whether Joseph is angling: he describes an office and does not name himself, which is either admirable restraint or the oldest move in the book. Genesis, characteristically, does not say.

A fifth. A 20% levy in the fat years — high, but not confiscatory, and Egypt is later described as living under a permanent one-fifth tax that dates from this administration (47:26). ⚠️ Worth reading on to the end of that story before calling this a straightforward rescue: by chapter 47 the same policy has taken the Egyptians' money, then their livestock, then their land, then their persons, and Genesis reports it flatly, without praise or apology. The plan that saves lives also builds a state monopoly, and both halves of that are in the text.

Verses 37–44 · slave to vizier in an afternoon

"A man in whom is the spirit of God." Said by an Egyptian king, about a Hebrew convict, in front of his court — and it is the same diagnosis Potiphar made silently in 39:3 ("his master saw that Jehovah was with him") and the prison keeper made after him. Three foreign employers in a row reach the same conclusion about the same man. Pharaoh names Elohim, the general word for God, not the divine name; the narrator lets the pagan king's theology stand as he said it.

The regalia is documented Egyptian practice. Every item in verses 42-43 is attested: the transfer of the royal signet ring as delegation of authority to seal documents in the king's name; fine linen (shesh — the Hebrew word is itself Egyptian) as the dress of rank; the gold collar, the shebyu-necklace, a specific decoration shown in tomb paintings being hung on officials at their investiture; and the second chariot, riding in the vehicle immediately behind the king's. The chapter knows what an Egyptian promotion looked like. And there is a quiet rhyme in the ring: the last man in this book to hand over a seal was Judah, surrendering his identity to a stranger by a roadside; here a king gives his away to make a slave into himself.

"Abrekh!" — and nobody knows what it means. The word is a complete mystery: a Hebrew guess from barakh "to kneel" gives the KJV's and ASV's "Bow the knee"; an Egyptian derivation (ib-r-k, "attention!" or a title like "chief steward") gives the NIV's "Make way!"; an Akkadian loanword abarakku, a high household officer, is a third serious proposal. The NWT simply transliterates — "Abrekh!" — and this translation does the same, because a herald's shout in a foreign court that no one has successfully translated in two thousand years should look like what it is. It is one of the small honest pleasures of the Hebrew Bible that a word can survive perfectly intact and completely opaque.

Verses 45–49 · an Egyptian name, an Egyptian wife, thirteen years

Zaphenath-paneah. Pharaoh renames him, as conquerors and courts do — the same move Babylon makes on four Hebrew youths in Daniel 1, and the inverse of God renaming Abram. The meaning is disputed: the most widely accepted Egyptian reconstruction is something like ḏd-pꜣ-nṯr-iw.f-ꜥnḫ, "the god speaks and he lives"; older Jewish tradition read it through Hebrew as "revealer of secrets." No one is certain. What is certain is that the name is genuinely Egyptian in shape, as are Asenath, Potiphera and On — this chapter's proper nouns behave like real Egyptian, whatever century they come from.

The marriage Genesis does not fuss about. Joseph is given the daughter of a priest of the sun-cult at On — Heliopolis — which makes the mother of two Israelite tribes an Egyptian priest's daughter. Abraham sent a servant a thousand miles to avoid a Canaanite bride for Isaac; Judah's Canaanite marriage in 38:2 was reported without comment and went badly. Here the narrator says nothing at all, and it goes fine. Later readers were less comfortable — a Hellenistic novella, Joseph and Aseneth, exists largely to convert her first.

Thirty years old. The one hard number in the chapter, and it does arithmetic on the whole story: he was seventeen when his brothers sold him. Thirteen years — as a slave, as a steward, as a convict, as a forgotten man — compressed into the white space between two verses. Genesis never once tells us what he felt during them.

Verses 50–57 · two names, and the whole world at the door

Manasseh: "God has made me forget." The name puns on nashani, and the sentence he attaches to it is one of the strangest in Genesis: "God has made me forget all my toil, and all my father's house." He is not only naming a son; he is announcing that the ache has stopped — including the ache of home. Given what the reader knows is coming up the road with money in its hands, the line is nearly unbearable. And it will be answered: at the end, Joseph will weep over the very house he claims to have forgotten.

Ephraim: "God has made me fruitful in the land of my affliction." Hiphrani, punning on the name, with oni — affliction, misery — for the country where he is now the second most powerful man alive. Both names credit God twice, and both are honest in a way that a purely triumphant man's names would not be: the second son's name concedes, in a single word, that Egypt is still where he was hurt. KJV "the land of my affliction," and no version has improved on it.

"Go to Joseph." Pharaoh's instruction to a starving nation (v. 55) is the sentence the whole chapter has been building toward, and it makes the trap: everyone who is hungry, anywhere, must come and stand in front of this man. The chapter closes with "all the earth came to Egypt to buy grain from Joseph," which is a statement about the scale of the famine and, at the same time, the machinery clicking into place. Somewhere in Canaan there is a hungry old man with eleven sons left. Two of the dreams that started all this involved bowing.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the words exact / distinct: "pit" for bor a third time (v. 14), completing the chain from 37:24 and 40:15 that every major version breaks by reading "dungeon"; "Abrekh!" left untranslated because two thousand years of guessing have not settled it (with the NWT, against the KJV's committed "Bow the knee"); "diviner-priests" for chartummim rather than the conjuring-word "magicians"; "Not I" for the blunt biladai; "peace" keeping shalom visible in Joseph's promise to Pharaoh; and "the land of my affliction" left as Joseph said it.

Echoes paid. Zakhar — "I bring my faults to remembrance today" (v. 9), two years after the cupbearer forgot; the pit, entered at Dothan and finally exited here; "the doubled dream" (v. 32) supplying at last the rule for reading Joseph's own two dreams; "Do not interpretations belong to God?" repeated in front of a king as "Not I"; the diviner-priests who cannot read a dream, exactly as Babylon's experts later cannot; shalom, unspeakable by his brothers, promised to Pharaoh; and the signet — pledged away by Judah, handed over by a king.

Echoes planted. "Go to Joseph" and "all the earth came to Egypt" — the brothers are one verse of the next chapter away, and they will bow (42:6), which is the dream of 37:7 coming due; Manasseh's "God has made me forget… all my father's house," waiting to be undone by the sight of Benjamin; the one-fifth policy, which by 47:26 will have bought Egypt itself; Jacob crossing his hands over these two boys to put the younger first (48:14); and Joseph's final verdict on the whole thirteen years, still nine chapters off — "you meant evil against me, but God meant it for good" (50:20).
