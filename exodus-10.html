Plagues eight and nine: locusts brought in on an east wind and swept into the Sea of Reeds by a west wind, and three days of darkness that can be felt. Pharaoh's own officials break first — 'do you not yet know that Egypt is destroyed?' — and the bargaining ends with Moses refusing to leave a hoof behind.
c. 1446 BC on the early-date chronology this project foregrounds. The locusts finish the late wheat and spelt the hail spared, so this falls weeks after chapter 9 and shortly before the Passover in the month of the ear.

Translator’s Notes

Verses 1–2 · why any of this is happening

⚠ For the first time the plagues are given a PURPOSE that is not Pharaoh at all. Everything so far has been aimed at a king who said he did not know Jehovah. Here the reason shifts to a different audience entirely: “so that you may RECOUNT in the ears of your son and your son’s son.” The signs are being set down for grandchildren who were not there. The plagues are, among other things, a story being made on purpose — and the verb is sapar, the same word as “that my name may be DECLARED in all the earth” four verses earlier in the last chapter (9:16). What God is producing in Egypt is a telling.

This verse is the root of the Passover seder, where the youngest child asks and the family answers by rehearsing exactly this — and of Deuteronomy’s repeated “when your son asks you in time to come… you shall say” (6:20-21). The instruction to remember is older than the deliverance being remembered.

«How I dealt harshly with Egypt» is a strong and slightly shocking verb (hit’allalti) — it carries the sense of dealing ruthlessly with someone, and can edge toward making sport of. Versions handle it warily: KJV smooths it to “what things I have wrought in Egypt”; the ESV and NIV read “how I dealt harshly with”; the NWT “how severely I have dealt.” The harder edge is in the Hebrew, and it is God’s own word for what he has been doing.

Note also who is hardened now: “his heart, AND the heart of his servants.” The court is included — which makes what happens five verses later more remarkable, not less.

Verses 3–11 · the officials break before the king does

«How long will you refuse to HUMBLE yourself before me?» The verb is anah — and it is the word the story began with. Egypt set taskmasters over Israel “to AFFLICT them” (1:11), and God had told Abraham centuries earlier that his offspring would be AFFLICTED four hundred years (Genesis 15:13). The same root now asks whether Pharaoh will let himself be brought low. The man who has spent the book afflicting is being invited to be humbled, in his own vocabulary.

⚠ And then the court cracks. “How long shall this man be a SNARE to us? Send the men away… Do you not YET know that Egypt is destroyed?” This is the first open dissent in the palace, and it is worth sitting with: the officials — whose hearts verse 1 says were hardened too — can now see plainly what their king cannot. Mokesh is a hunter’s word, the trigger-stick of a bird trap, and they use it of Moses; but the trap they are actually caught in is the man on the throne. Their verb for Egypt is past tense: it is already lost. KJV “a snare unto us”; the NIV “knowest thou not yet” becomes “Do you not yet realize?”

The bargain, and the sarcasm. Recalled to the throne, Moses states the terms without moving an inch: young and old, sons and daughters, flocks and herds — “for it is a FESTIVAL of Jehovah for us.” Pharaoh’s reply is a sneer with a threat folded into it: “May Jehovah be with you the same way I send you off with your little ones!” — that is, not at all. The second half is deliberately ambiguous in Hebrew: ra’ah neged peneikhem is either “trouble is ahead of you” or “you have evil in mind.” KJV “look to it; for evil is before you”; the ESV “Look, you have some evil purpose in mind.”

⚠ The ladder continues to keep a hostage. “Not so! Go, THE MEN.” Worship inside the land, then not far, and now: the fighting-age men may go, and the wives and children stay — the surest guarantee that the men come back. Every concession is a leash, and each one is shorter than it looks.

Verses 12–20 · an east wind, and a west wind

The eighth plague is delivered by weather, and Exodus is careful to say so twice: an EAST wind blows all day and all night to bring the locusts in, and a very strong SEA wind — a west wind off the Mediterranean — to take them out. Nothing here is outside the range of what Egypt’s own weather can do. What is outside the range is the timing, announced the day before and reversed on request.

The devastation is total and stated in the negative: “nothing green was left, in tree or in plant of the field.” The hail had taken the flax and barley; the locusts take the wheat and spelt that the hail spared (9:32) and the fruit trees besides. Between the two plagues, one year of Egyptian agriculture is gone.

⚠ And they “covered the eye of the land… and the land was DARKENED” (v15). The ninth plague is already casting its shadow in the eighth: a swarm thick enough to blot out the sun is the rehearsal for a darkness that needs no swarm at all.

⚠ Where the locusts are dumped is not incidental. The wind drives them into the Sea of Reeds — the yam SUPH, its first appearance in the book. That water has been quietly present since chapter 2, where the same word named the REEDS among which a Hebrew mother hid her baby; it is about to swallow an army. Here it swallows the locusts first. KJV and most versions print “the Red sea”, following the Greek Old Testament; the Hebrew says “Sea of Reeds,” and the NWT keeps the traditional rendering with a footnote to that effect.

Pharaoh’s second confession is faster and smaller than his first. “I have sinned” — but the request is “lift up my sin just THIS ONCE,” and what he wants removed is not his guilt but “this death.” He confesses in order to get the plague off, and the relief hardens him again, exactly as it did with the frogs.

Verses 21–29 · darkness that can be felt, and the last audience

⚠ The ninth plague strikes the top of Egypt’s religion. RA, the sun, was the chief god of the pantheon — and “Son of Ra” was part of Pharaoh’s own titulary. The king is a solar figure by office. Blotting out the sun for three days is therefore not one more calamity in a list; it is an argument aimed directly at the throne, and it comes with no warning, no demand, and no negotiation beforehand.

«And let the darkness be FELT.» The Hebrew (veyamesh choshech) is strange and physical — the root has to do with groping and touching, so the phrase is either darkness thick enough to handle or a darkness in which men grope. Either way it is not merely the absence of light. KJV “darkness which may be felt”; the ESV “a darkness to be felt”; the NIV “darkness that can be felt.” Then verse 22 doubles the word for good measure: a darkness of thick gloom.

What it actually does to people is the quietest horror in the cycle: “They did not see one another, and no one rose from his place, three days.” Not deaths, not sores — isolation and paralysis. Egypt is stopped where it sits. “But for all the sons of Israel there was LIGHT in their dwellings”: the distinction that began with the swarms is now literally visible, and it is the last one before the night of the firstborn.

⚠ The final offer, and the answer that ends the negotiation. Pharaoh gives up the children at last — but keeps the livestock, which is the same hostage in a different form: a people without herds must come back to eat. Moses does not haggle. He raises the demand instead: Pharaoh must himself SUPPLY sacrifices, and “NOT A HOOF shall be left behind.” And then a line worth pausing on — “we ourselves do not know with what we are to serve Jehovah until we come there.” The worship has not been legislated yet; Sinai is still ahead. They must take everything precisely because they cannot yet know what will be asked of them. Obedience is being required in advance of the instructions.

⚠ «On the day you see my face you shall die.» The interview ends in a death threat, and Moses agrees with it: “Rightly you have spoken.” ⚠ Readers have long noticed that Pharaoh does summon them again at 12:31, on the night the firstborn die. Two readings, both old: that Moses’ speech in 11:4-8 is still part of THIS audience — it ends with him leaving Pharaoh “in hot anger,” which reads like the exit from this room — so the two men genuinely never meet again; or that the later summons is carried by messengers rather than face to face. The text does not adjudicate, and neither does this library.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the exact / distinct words: “recount” for sapar, the same verb as “that my name may be DECLARED” (9:16); “dealt harshly” for the hard hit’allalti rather than the smoothed “wrought”; “humble yourself” for anah, the affliction-word from 1:11 turned on Pharaoh; “snare” for the hunter’s mokesh; “Sea of Reeds” for yam suph, where most versions print “Red Sea”; “let the darkness be felt” kept as strange as the Hebrew; and “not a hoof” left literal.

Echoes paid. The reeds of chapter 2, in which a mother hid a baby, now named as the sea that swallows the locusts; anah, the affliction Abraham was told of (Genesis 15:13) and Egypt imposed (1:11); the hail, whose survivors — the late wheat and spelt of 9:32 — the locusts finish; and the bargaining ladder, which reaches its last rung here.

Echoes planted. The telling to a son and a son’s son, which becomes the Passover instruction and the shape of Deuteronomy’s catechism; the Sea of Reeds, which will take an army; the light in Israel’s dwellings while Egypt sits blind, the last distinction before the blood on the doorposts; and “we do not know with what we are to serve,” a worship still waiting on a mountain.

Next in this book: Exodus 11 — the last plague announced, the silver and gold asked of the neighbors, and a threat so total that Moses leaves the palace in hot anger: “about midnight I will go out into the midst of Egypt.”
