Jacob at the well of Haran, and Laban's morning trick — Leah for Rachel; two wives, fourteen years' service, and Leah's first four sons.
AM ≈ 2185–2199 · c. 1760–1746 BC — the first years of Jacob's twenty in Haran. He serves seven years for Rachel, is tricked into Leah, serves seven more; the twelve tribes begin to be born (Reuben, Simeon, Levi, Judah to Leah). The deceiver's own schooling in being deceived.

Translator's Notes — verse by verse

The deceiver deceived — a wedding-night switch, and the unloved wife God chooses. Quotations from copyrighted versions (NIV, TLB, NWT) are kept to short phrases for comparison; KJV, Geneva, Douay-Rheims and ASV are public domain.

Verses 1–14 · עַצְמִי וּבְשָׂרִי atsmi u-vsari — "my bone and my flesh"

The third meeting at a well. Genesis loves this scene: a traveler comes to a well in a strange land and finds there his future wife. Abraham's servant met Rebekah drawing water for Isaac (ch. 24); Moses will meet Zipporah at a Midianite well (Exodus 2); and here Jacob meets Rachel. His entrance is pure emotion: he single-handedly rolls away the great stone the local shepherds needed a crowd to move, waters her flock, kisses her, and "lifted up his voice and wept" — the fugitive, alone and far from home, undone at finding his mother's family. Laban runs out to greet his nephew (last time it was the gold that made him run, 24:30) and welcomes him with the old kinship-cry, "you are my bone and my flesh" — the very words Adam first spoke over Eve, "bone of my bones and flesh of my flesh" (2:23). A warm welcome; but this is the same Laban, and the warmth has terms.

Verses 15–20 · וְעֵינֵי לֵאָה רַכּוֹת ve-einei Leah rakkot

Two sisters, and a love worth fourteen years. Laban asks what wages Jacob wants — the negotiation that will run, and cheat, for twenty years. The two daughters are set side by side: Leah, the elder, whose eyes were rakkot, and Rachel, the younger, "beautiful in form and lovely to look at." The word for Leah's eyes is genuinely uncertain: the KJV's "tender eyed" can be read as a flaw — weak, dull, watery eyes — set against Rachel's full beauty (the reading the contrast supports, and this translation's), or kindly, as "soft, lovely eyes," her one charm. The Hebrew won't say; the sister who is not chosen is left a little in shadow from her first line. And then one of scripture's tenderest sentences about love: Jacob serves seven years for Rachel, "and they seemed to him but a few days, because of his love for her."

Verses 21–30 · וְלָמָּה רִמִּיתָנִי ve-lammah rimmitani — "why have you deceived me?"

The deceiver deceived. This is the great irony of Jacob's life, and Genesis lets it land without a word of comment. Jacob, who once put on his elder brother's clothes to fool a father who could not see, is now fooled in the dark by an elder daughter brought veiled to his tent — and "in the morning, behold, it was Leah!" His outraged question — "why have you deceived me?" (rimmitani; KJV "beguiled") — is the very root of the word Isaac used of him: "your brother came with deceit" (27:35). And Laban's defense is a blade turned exactly on the wound: "it is not done in our place to give the younger before the firstborn" — a quiet lecture on the rights of the elder to the man who sold his brother's birthright and stole his blessing (27:36). The measure Jacob gave is measured back to him. He serves another seven years for Rachel, loves her more than Leah — and the rivalry that will fill the next chapter is already seeded.

Verses 31–35 · שְׂנוּאָה senuah — the unloved

God on the side of the unloved. The chapter's quietest line is its deepest: "Jehovah saw that Leah was unloved (senuah; KJV "hated," in the Hebrew idiom for the less-loved of two), and he opened her womb." The world's verdict and God's run opposite: Rachel is adored and barren; Leah is unloved and fruitful. And each son's name is a small heartbreak — a woman naming her babies after her longing to be loved. Reuben ("see, a son"): "Jehovah has looked on my affliction — now my husband will love me." Simeon ("hearing"): "Jehovah heard that I am unloved." Levi ("joined"): "now my husband will be joined to me." And then something turns. Judah ("praise"): "this time I will praise Jehovah" — no longer reaching for her husband's love, just thanking God. And it is these two least-expected sons of the least-loved wife who carry everything: from Levi comes the priesthood, and from Judah come David, the kings, and the Messiah — the line the word "Jew" itself is named from. The wife nobody chose is the mother God chose.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the words exact / distinct: "weak" for the contested rakkot of Leah's eyes (with the ambiguity — soft/lovely or weak/dull — flagged); "deceived" for rimmitani (the KJV's "beguiled"), kept the same root as the mirmah Jacob himself trafficked in; and "unloved" for senuah (literally "hated," but the Hebrew idiom for the less-loved), so God's siding with her reads as the mercy it is.

Echoes paid. The well-betrothal type-scene, third time; "bone and flesh," Adam's cry (2:23); "why have you deceived me," turning 27:35 back on Jacob; "the younger before the firstborn," the rebuke to 27:36; and God opening the barren womb, as for Rebekah.
