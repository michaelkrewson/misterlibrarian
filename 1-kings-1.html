The last days of David — an old king who cannot get warm, two sons claiming the throne on the same afternoon, and Solomon anointed at the Gihon spring.
c. 970 BC. David reigns forty years (seven in Hebron, thirty-three in Jerusalem, 2 Samuel 5:4-5), so his death falls near 970 and Solomon's temple is begun 'in the four hundred and eightieth year after the exodus' and his own fourth year (1 Kings 6:1) — the verse on which the whole biblical chronology of the monarchy is pinned, and one of the two anchors of the exodus date.

1 Kings 1 — The Mister Translation

1
וְהַמֶּלֶךְ דָּוִד זָקֵן, בָּא בַּיָּמִים; וַיְכַסֻּהוּ, בַּבְּגָדִים, וְלֹא יִחַם, לוֹ.
Now King David was old, come into days; and they covered him with clothes, but he was not warm.note
Y el rey David era viejo, entrado en días; y lo cubrían con ropas, pero no se calentaba.
2
וַיֹּאמְרוּ לוֹ עֲבָדָיו, יְבַקְשׁוּ לַאדֹנִי הַמֶּלֶךְ נַעֲרָה בְתוּלָה, וְעָמְדָה לִפְנֵי הַמֶּלֶךְ, וּתְהִי-לוֹ סֹכֶנֶת; וְשָׁכְבָה בְחֵיקֶךָ, וְחַם לַאדֹנִי הַמֶּלֶךְ.
And his servants said to him, “Let them seek out for my lord the king a young woman, a virgin, and let her stand before the king and be his attendant; and let her lie in your bosom, and my lord the king will be warm.”note
Y le dijeron sus siervos: «Busquen para mi señor el rey una joven virgen, y que esté delante del rey y sea su cuidadora; y que se acueste en tu seno, y se calentará mi señor el rey».
3
וַיְבַקְשׁוּ נַעֲרָה יָפָה, בְּכֹל גְּבוּל יִשְׂרָאֵל; וַיִּמְצְאוּ, אֶת-אֲבִישַׁג הַשּׁוּנַמִּית, וַיָּבִאוּ אֹתָהּ, לַמֶּלֶךְ.
So they sought a beautiful young woman throughout all the territory of Israel, and they found Abishag the Shunammite, and brought her to the king.note
Y buscaron una joven hermosa por todo el territorio de Israel, y hallaron a Abisag la sunamita, y la trajeron al rey.
4
וְהַנַּעֲרָה, יָפָה עַד-מְאֹד; וַתְּהִי לַמֶּלֶךְ סֹכֶנֶת וַתְּשָׁרְתֵהוּ, וְהַמֶּלֶךְ לֹא יְדָעָהּ.
And the young woman was very beautiful; and she became the king’s attendant and served him, but the king did not know her.note
Y la joven era hermosísima; y fue cuidadora del rey y le servía, pero el rey no la conoció.
5
וַאֲדֹנִיָּה בֶן-חַגִּית מִתְנַשֵּׂא לֵאמֹר, אֲנִי אֶמְלֹךְ; וַיַּעַשׂ לוֹ, רֶכֶב וּפָרָשִׁים, וַחֲמִשִּׁים אִישׁ, רָצִים לְפָנָיו.
Now Adonijah son of Haggith was exalting himself, saying, “I will be king.” And he made himself a chariot and horsemen, and fifty men running before him.note
Y Adonías hijo de Haguit se ensalzaba a sí mismo, diciendo: «Yo reinaré». E hizo para sí carro y jinetes, y cincuenta hombres que corrieran delante de él.

And his father had never pained him all his days by saying, “Why have you done so?” And he too was very good-looking; and she had borne him after Absalom.note
Y su padre nunca lo había apenado en todos sus días diciéndole: «¿Por qué has hecho así?». Y él también era de muy buen parecer; y ella lo había dado a luz después de Absalón.

7

And his words were with Joab son of Zeruiah and with Abiathar the priest, and they gave their help, following Adonijah.note
Y sus tratos eran con Joab hijo de Sarvia y con Abiatar el sacerdote, y ellos le prestaban ayuda, siguiendo a Adonías.
8
וְצָדוֹק הַכֹּהֵן וּבְנָיָהוּ בֶן-יְהוֹיָדָע וְנָתָן הַנָּבִיא, וְשִׁמְעִי וְרֵעִי, וְהַגִּבּוֹרִים, אֲשֶׁר לְדָוִד--לֹא הָיוּ, עִם-אֲדֹנִיָּהוּ.
But Zadok the priest and Benaiah son of Jehoiada and Nathan the prophet and Shimei and Rei and the mighty men who belonged to David were not with Adonijah.note
Pero Sadoc el sacerdote, y Benaía hijo de Joiada, y Natán el profeta, y Simei, y Rei, y los valientes que tenía David, no estaban con Adonías.
9
וַיִּזְבַּח אֲדֹנִיָּהוּ, צֹאן וּבָקָר וּמְרִיא, עִם אֶבֶן הַזֹּחֶלֶת, אֲשֶׁר-אֵצֶל עֵין רֹגֵל; וַיִּקְרָא, אֶת-כָּל-אֶחָיו בְּנֵי הַמֶּלֶךְ, וּלְכָל-אַנְשֵׁי יְהוּדָה, עַבְדֵי הַמֶּלֶךְ.
And Adonijah sacrificed sheep and oxen and fatlings by the Stone of Zoheleth, which is beside En Rogel; and he invited all his brothers, the king’s sons, and all the men of Judah, the king’s servants.note
Y Adonías sacrificó ovejas y bueyes y animales cebados junto a la peña de Zohélet, que está al lado de En-rogel; e invitó a todos sus hermanos, los hijos del rey, y a todos los hombres de Judá, siervos del rey.
10
וְאֶת-נָתָן הַנָּבִיא וּבְנָיָהוּ וְאֶת-הַגִּבּוֹרִים, וְאֶת-שְׁלֹמֹה אָחִיו--לֹא קָרָא.
But Nathan the prophet and Benaiah and the mighty men and Solomon his brother he did not invite.note
Pero a Natán el profeta, y a Benaía, y a los valientes, y a Salomón su hermano, no los invitó.
11
וַיֹּאמֶר נָתָן, אֶל-בַּת-שֶׁבַע אֵם-שְׁלֹמֹה לֵאמֹר, הֲלוֹא שָׁמַעַתְּ, כִּי מָלַךְ אֲדֹנִיָּהוּ בֶן-חַגִּית; וַאֲדֹנֵינוּ דָוִד, לֹא יָדָע.
And Nathan said to Bathsheba the mother of Solomon, “Have you not heard that Adonijah son of Haggith has become king, and our lord David does not know it?note
Y dijo Natán a Betsabé, madre de Salomón: «¿No has oído que Adonías hijo de Haguit se ha hecho rey, y nuestro señor David no lo sabe?
12
וְעַתָּה, לְכִי אִיעָצֵךְ נָא עֵצָה: וּמַלְּטִי, אֶת-נַפְשֵׁךְ, וְאֶת-נֶפֶשׁ בְּנֵךְ, שְׁלֹמֹה.
Now then, come, let me give you counsel, please, that you may save your own life and the life of your son Solomon.note
Ven ahora, permíteme darte un consejo, para que salves tu propia vida y la vida de tu hijo Salomón.
13
לְכִי וּבֹאִי אֶל-הַמֶּלֶךְ דָּוִד, וְאָמַרְתְּ אֵלָיו הֲלֹא-אַתָּה אֲדֹנִי הַמֶּלֶךְ נִשְׁבַּעְתָּ לַאֲמָתְךָ לֵאמֹר, כִּי-שְׁלֹמֹה בְנֵךְ יִמְלֹךְ אַחֲרַי, וְהוּא יֵשֵׁב עַל-כִּסְאִי; וּמַדּוּעַ, מָלַךְ אֲדֹנִיָּהוּ.
Go and come in to King David and say to him, ‘Did not you, my lord the king, swear to your servant, saying, “Solomon your son shall reign after me, and he shall sit on my throne”? Why then has Adonijah become king?’note
Ve y entra al rey David, y dile: “¿No juraste tú, mi señor el rey, a tu sierva, diciendo: ‘Salomón tu hijo reinará después de mí, y él se sentará en mi trono’? ¿Por qué, pues, se ha hecho rey Adonías?”.

14
הִנֵּה, עוֹדָךְ מְדַבֶּרֶת שָׁם--עִם-הַמֶּלֶךְ; וַאֲנִי אָבוֹא אַחֲרַיִךְ, וּמִלֵּאתִי אֶת-דְּבָרָיִךְ.
And look — while you are still speaking there with the king, I myself will come in after you and fill out your words.”note
Y mira: mientras tú todavía estés allí hablando con el rey, yo entraré detrás de ti y completaré tus palabras».
15
וַתָּבֹא בַת-שֶׁבַע אֶל-הַמֶּלֶךְ הַחַדְרָה, וְהַמֶּלֶךְ זָקֵן מְאֹד; וַאֲבִישַׁג, הַשּׁוּנַמִּית, מְשָׁרַת, אֶת-הַמֶּלֶךְ.
So Bathsheba came in to the king, into the inner chamber; and the king was very old, and Abishag the Shunammite was serving the king.note
Y entró Betsabé al rey, a la habitación interior; y el rey era muy viejo, y Abisag la sunamita servía al rey.
16
וַתִּקֹּד בַּת-שֶׁבַע, וַתִּשְׁתַּחוּ לַמֶּלֶךְ; וַיֹּאמֶר הַמֶּלֶךְ, מַה-לָּךְ.
And Bathsheba bowed low and did obeisance to the king. And the king said, “What do you want?”note
Y se inclinó Betsabé y se postró ante el rey. Y dijo el rey: «¿Qué quieres?».
17
וַתֹּאמֶר לוֹ, אֲדֹנִי אַתָּה נִשְׁבַּעְתָּ בַּיהוָה אֱלֹהֶיךָ לַאֲמָתֶךָ, כִּי-שְׁלֹמֹה בְנֵךְ, יִמְלֹךְ אַחֲרָי; וְהוּא, יֵשֵׁב עַל-כִּסְאִי.
And she said to him, “My lord, you yourself swore by Jehovah your God to your servant, ‘Solomon your son shall reign after me, and he shall sit on my throne.’note
Y ella le dijo: «Señor mío, tú mismo juraste por Jehová tu Dios a tu sierva: ‘Salomón tu hijo reinará después de mí, y él se sentará en mi trono’.
18
וְעַתָּה, הִנֵּה אֲדֹנִיָּה מָלָךְ; וְעַתָּה אֲדֹנִי הַמֶּלֶךְ, לֹא יָדָעְתָּ.
And now, look — Adonijah has become king; and now, my lord the king, you do not know it.note
Y ahora, mira: Adonías se ha hecho rey; y ahora, señor mío el rey, tú no lo sabes.
19

And he has sacrificed oxen and fatlings and sheep in abundance, and has invited all the sons of the king and Abiathar the priest and Joab the commander of the army; but Solomon your servant he has not invited.note
Y ha sacrificado bueyes y animales cebados y ovejas en abundancia, y ha invitado a todos los hijos del rey y a Abiatar el sacerdote y a Joab el jefe del ejército; pero a Salomón tu siervo no lo ha invitado.
20
וְאַתָּה אֲדֹנִי הַמֶּלֶךְ, עֵינֵי כָל-יִשְׂרָאֵל עָלֶיךָ: לְהַגִּיד לָהֶם--מִי יֵשֵׁב עַל-כִּסֵּא אֲדֹנִי-הַמֶּלֶךְ, אַחֲרָיו.
And you, my lord the king — the eyes of all Israel are on you, to tell them who shall sit on the throne of my lord the king after him.note
Y tú, señor mío el rey: los ojos de todo Israel están sobre ti, para que les declares quién se ha de sentar en el trono de mi señor el rey después de él.
21
וְהָיָה, כִּשְׁכַב אֲדֹנִי-הַמֶּלֶךְ עִם-אֲבֹתָיו; וְהָיִיתִי, אֲנִי וּבְנִי שְׁלֹמֹה--חַטָּאִים.
Otherwise it will be that, when my lord the king lies down with his fathers, I and my son Solomon will be counted offenders.”note
De otro modo sucederá que, cuando mi señor el rey duerma con sus padres, yo y mi hijo Salomón seremos tenidos por culpables».
22
וְהִנֵּה עוֹדֶנָּה מְדַבֶּרֶת, עִם-הַמֶּלֶךְ; וְנָתָן הַנָּבִיא, בָּא.
And look — while she was still speaking with the king, Nathan the prophet came in.note
Y mira: mientras ella aún hablaba con el rey, entró Natán el profeta.
23
וַיַּגִּידוּ לַמֶּלֶךְ לֵאמֹר, הִנֵּה נָתָן הַנָּבִיא; וַיָּבֹא לִפְנֵי הַמֶּלֶךְ, וַיִּשְׁתַּחוּ לַמֶּלֶךְ עַל-אַפָּיו אָרְצָה.
And they told the king, “Here is Nathan the prophet.” And he came in before the king and did obeisance to the king with his face to the ground.note
Y avisaron al rey: «Aquí está Natán el profeta». Y él entró delante del rey y se postró ante el rey con el rostro en tierra.
24
וַיֹּאמֶר, נָתָן, אֲדֹנִי הַמֶּלֶךְ, אַתָּה אָמַרְתָּ אֲדֹנִיָּהוּ יִמְלֹךְ אַחֲרָי; וְהוּא, יֵשֵׁב עַל-כִּסְאִי.
And Nathan said, “My lord the king, have you said, ‘Adonijah shall reign after me, and he shall sit on my throne’?note
Y dijo Natán: «Señor mío el rey, ¿has dicho tú: “Adonías reinará después de mí, y él se sentará en mi trono”?
25
כִּי יָרַד הַיּוֹם, וַיִּזְבַּח שׁוֹר וּמְרִיא-וְצֹאן לָרֹב, וַיִּקְרָא לְכָל-בְּנֵי הַמֶּלֶךְ וּלְשָׂרֵי הַצָּבָא וּלְאֶבְיָתָר הַכֹּהֵן, וְהִנָּם אֹכְלִים וְשֹׁתִים לְפָנָיו; וַיֹּאמְרוּ, יְחִי הַמֶּלֶךְ אֲדֹנִיָּהוּ.
For he has gone down today and has sacrificed oxen and fatlings and sheep in abundance, and has invited all the king’s sons and the commanders of the army and Abiathar the priest; and look — they are eating and drinking before him, and they have said, ‘Long live King Adonijah!’note
Porque hoy ha bajado y ha sacrificado bueyes y animales cebados y ovejas en abundancia, y ha invitado a todos los hijos del rey y a los jefes del ejército y a Abiatar el sacerdote; y mira: están comiendo y bebiendo delante de él, y han dicho: “¡Viva el rey Adonías!”.
26
וְלִי אֲנִי-עַבְדֶּךָ וּלְצָדֹק הַכֹּהֵן וְלִבְנָיָהוּ בֶן-יְהוֹיָדָע, וְלִשְׁלֹמֹה עַבְדְּךָ--לֹא קָרָא.
But me — me your servant — and Zadok the priest and Benaiah son of Jehoiada and Solomon your servant he has not invited.note
Pero a mí, a mí tu siervo, y a Sadoc el sacerdote, y a Benaía hijo de Joiada, y a Salomón tu siervo, no los ha invitado.
27
אִם, מֵאֵת אֲדֹנִי הַמֶּלֶךְ, נִהְיָה, הַדָּבָר הַזֶּה; וְלֹא הוֹדַעְתָּ, אֶת-עבדיך עַבְדְּךָ, מִי יֵשֵׁב עַל-כִּסֵּא אֲדֹנִי-הַמֶּלֶךְ, אַחֲרָיו. {ס}
If this thing has been done by my lord the king, then you have not made known to your servant who should sit on the throne of my lord the king after him.”note
Si esto ha sido hecho de parte de mi señor el rey, entonces no has dado a conocer a tu siervo quién había de sentarse en el trono de mi señor el rey después de él».
28
וַיַּעַן הַמֶּלֶךְ דָּוִד וַיֹּאמֶר, קִרְאוּ-לִי לְבַת-שָׁבַע; וַתָּבֹא לִפְנֵי הַמֶּלֶךְ, וַתַּעֲמֹד לִפְנֵי הַמֶּלֶךְ.
And King David answered and said, “Call Bathsheba to me.” And she came in before the king and stood before the king.note
Y respondió el rey David y dijo: «Llámenme a Betsabé». Y ella entró delante del rey y se puso delante del rey.
29
וַיִּשָּׁבַע הַמֶּלֶךְ, וַיֹּאמַר: חַי-יְהוָה, אֲשֶׁר-פָּדָה אֶת-נַפְשִׁי מִכָּל-צָרָה.
And the king swore and said, “As Jehovah lives, who has redeemed my soul out of every distress:note

Translator’s Notes

Verses 1–4 · a king who cannot get warm

The book of Kings opens on a body that will not work. No summary of the reign, no theology, no fanfare — just an old man under a pile of blankets. It is one of the coldest opening sentences in the Bible, and everything in the chapter grows out of it: a king who cannot warm himself is a king who cannot rule, and the whole court knows it before the reader has finished the verse.

«Old, come into days.» The Hebrew idiom is ba ba-yamim, “come into the days” — used of Abraham and of Joshua, always at the point where a life is over except for the arrangements. KJV keeps it (“stricken in years”); NIV flattens to “very old.”

«Let her be his attendant.» Sokhenet is a rare word from a root meaning to be of use, of benefit — a female steward or carer. KJV “let her cherish him,” ASV “let her cherish him,” NIV “to take care of him.” The proposal is medical in form and dynastic in substance.

⚠ «But the king did not know her.» Six words, and the narrator has no reason to include them except that they matter. In the ancient Near East a king’s virility was not a private matter but a public credential, and the court has just run a test and published the result. The reader is being told, without a word of editorial, that David is finished — which is why the very next sentence is somebody announcing that he intends to be king. And note it now: this same young woman, and specifically the question of who has a claim on her, is the pretext on which Adonijah is executed in the next chapter (2:13–25). Nothing in these four verses is idle.

Verses 5–10 · a coup you have read before — and a father who never once asked why

⚠ The narrator makes his sharpest point by repeating a sentence and saying nothing. “He made himself a chariot and horsemen, and fifty men running before him” is, almost word for word, what Absalom did when he set out to take the throne (2 Samuel 15:1). Then verse 6 adds, as though it were a genealogical footnote, “and she had borne him after Absalom.” The book does not say Adonijah was imitating his brother, and it does not need to. The reader who knows how Absalom’s attempt ended has been handed the outcome in advance.

«Was exalting himself» — mitnasse, the reflexive of nasa, to lift. He lifts himself. Every other king in this chapter is lifted by someone else — onto a mule, onto a throne, by a priest with a horn of oil — and this one does his own lifting. KJV “exalted himself”; NIV “put himself forward”.

⚠ «His father had never pained him.» The verb atsav is to hurt or grieve, and the sentence is one of the quietest indictments in the Old Testament: David never once said to this son, “Why have you done that?” KJV “had not displeased him at any time”; ASV the same. It is the identical failure the book of Samuel laid against Eli — whose sons “made themselves contemptible, and he did not restrain them” (1 Samuel 3:13) — and the identical result. And the little clause that follows, “he too was very good-looking,” has been said in this family before, of Absalom and of David himself. In these books beauty in a young man is never a compliment; it is a warning.

Two lists, and they are the kingdom’s fault lines. With Adonijah: Joab, the general who has run David’s wars and killed for him, and Abiathar, the priest who escaped the massacre at Nob and has carried the ark for David since the wilderness years — the old comrades, the men of the long campaign. Against him: Zadok the priest, Benaiah who commands the Cherethites and Pelethites (the foreign professional bodyguard), and Nathan the prophet — the Jerusalem establishment, the men of the new order. This is not a family quarrel; it is the old guard against the administration, and by the end of chapter 2 every man on the first list is dead or exiled.

The party at En Rogel is a coronation. Sacrificing sheep and oxen, with the king’s sons and the men of Judah invited, at a named landmark outside the city: this is a feast with a purpose, and the guest list is the point. The chapter says four times that Solomon was not invited (vv 10, 19, 26 — and by implication v 25). In a succession, an omission from a guest list is a sentence.

Verses 11–21 · the oath nobody has ever heard of

⚠ This is the great withheld verdict of the chapter, and possibly of the whole succession narrative. Nathan tells Bathsheba to go and remind David of an oath: “Did not you, my lord the king, swear to your servant… Solomon your son shall reign after me?” And the reader has never heard of it. The books of Samuel record God’s promise of a dynasty to David (2 Samuel 7), but no oath naming Solomon — not to Bathsheba, not to anyone. Two readings, both entirely serious:

• The oath was real and simply not recorded — the narrative is selective everywhere else, and a private promise to a wife is exactly the sort of thing it would not have reported. Bathsheba’s composure and David’s instant acceptance both fit.
• There was no oath, and two people who stand to be killed if Adonijah is crowned have just invented one, in front of a man too old to be certain what he said and when. Nothing in the text rules this out; everything about how the scene is staged — the coaching, the timing, the second witness arriving on cue — is the mechanics of persuading someone.

The narrator declines to tell us which. That is not an oversight. This author has just spent twenty chapters of Samuel declining to editorialize about David, and he is not going to start on the last day of the reign. Whether the kingdom passes to Solomon by David’s word or by a good piece of stagecraft is a question the Bible sets up with great care and then leaves in the reader’s hands. It is worth adding that the book’s later verdict on Solomon is not simple either.

«And I will fill out your words.» Milleti et devarayikh — literally “I will fill your words,” i.e. complete, corroborate, top up. KJV “confirm thy words”; ASV the same. It is the language of a prepared witness.

And Bathsheba improves the script. Nathan told her to say David swore; she says he swore by Jehovah your God (v17). Nathan gave her no ending; she supplies one that no father could hear calmly — “I and my son Solomon will be counted offenders” (chatta’im, guilty parties, the losing claimant’s family after a successful coup). She is not a passive instrument in this scene; she is the better lawyer of the two.

⚠ A curiosity of the Hebrew text at verse 19: the Masoretic scribes place a paragraph mark in the middle of the verse — a rare feature (a pisqah inside a sentence) that occurs only a handful of times in the Bible, and which nobody has satisfactorily explained. We print it where they put it. Some read it as marking a pause in her delivery.

Verses 22–31 · how a prophet says a thing without saying it

Read Nathan’s speech for what it never claims. He arrives exactly on cue, prostrates himself, and then — remarkably — never once asserts that the oath happened. He asks a question instead: “Have you said Adonijah shall reign?” He lets Bathsheba’s claim stand behind him untouched, reports only facts anyone could verify (the sacrifice, the guest list, the shouting), and closes with a courtier’s reproach so deferential it is almost a compliment: if my lord has done this, he has forgotten to tell his servant. Every word is deniable. Whatever one concludes about verse 13, this is a masterclass in speaking to power, and the writer clearly enjoyed composing it.

«But me — me your servant — he has not invited.» The Hebrew stumbles over itself with pronouns here (ve-li ani avdekha), and the effect is of a man laying his own exclusion on the table first and Solomon’s last. ⚠ The verse ends with a ketiv/qere: the consonants as written read “your servants,” the reading tradition gives the singular. The written form would sweep the whole loyal faction into the complaint.

«As Jehovah lives, who has redeemed my soul out of every distress.» David’s oath-formula is the one line in the chapter that sounds like the psalms attributed to him — padah, the ransoming word, and a lifetime of narrow escapes behind it. And note precisely what he does: he swears a new oath in the grammatical form of confirming an old one (“as I swore to you… so I will do this very day”). Hebrew permits both readings, and the chapter has already been careful not to let us settle it.

«May my lord King David live forever!» Said by the woman who has just arranged the succession past him. The court formula is not insincere — it is simply what one says — and the writer puts it in her mouth at the exact moment it has become impossible.

«Long live the king» is shouted three times in this chapter, for three different men: for Adonijah at his party (v25), for David in this bedroom (v31), and for Solomon at the spring (vv34, 39). The words are the same every time; only the man changes, and only one of the acclamations turns out to be the real one.

Verses 32–40 · a mule, a spring, and a horn of oil

Every element of David’s instruction is a public signal, and the geography is the sharpest of them. Adonijah is feasting at En Rogel, a spring at the bottom of the valley, outside the city, out of sight. David sends Solomon down to Gihon — the spring that supplies Jerusalem with its water, in the Kidron valley immediately below the city wall, where the noise carries up into the streets and where the whole population can walk out and see it. One claimant holds a private dinner; the other is put on display at the city’s water source, and the crowd walks him back up. The chapter never explains this. It just gives you the two place names.

«The mule that is mine.» Riding the reigning king’s personal mount is a claim, and letting someone ride it is a transfer — which is why David specifies it twice and the messenger repeats it in his report (v44). It is worth remembering when a later king rides into the same city on a borrowed animal, deliberately (Zechariah 9:9; Matthew 21:5).

«Let Zadok the priest and Nathan the prophet anoint him.» Both offices, together. Anointing (mashach) is what makes a king a mashiach, an anointed one — the word that travels through the Greek christos into English as Messiah. Samuel anointed Saul and then David in relative privacy; this is the first coronation of a son in the line, and the ceremony that will be the pattern.

«The horn of oil from the tent.» Not the tabernacle at Gibeon but the tent David pitched in Jerusalem for the ark (2 Samuel 6:17). Zadok fetches the oil from where the ark actually is.

«So that the earth was split with their sound.» Vattibbaqa ha-arets — the verb is the one used of splitting wood, or of the sea being split at the exodus. KJV “so that the earth rent with the sound of them” keeps the violence; NIV “the ground shook” is an earthquake, which is not what the word says. The joy is loud enough to break something.

Verses 41–53 · “on the contrary” — and a man holding on to the altar

The scene is built on sound. Adonijah’s party hears the noise before it hears the news; Joab — a soldier, who knows what a ram’s horn in a city means — is the one who asks the question. And the messenger tells them the whole story a second time, in detail the reader already has, because the writer wants us in the room while it lands on them.

⚠ The best single word in the chapter. Adonijah greets Jonathan as “a worthy man” who “brings good news,” and Jonathan begins his reply with aval — a flat adversative: on the contrary. ASV “Nay but” is exactly right. KJV prints “Verily,” which in modern English reads as agreement and is the one place the older version genuinely misleads. One word, and a coronation banquet becomes a room full of men who need to be somewhere else.

«Trembled, and rose, and each went his own way.» No speech, no last stand. The guests who were going to be a government thirty seconds ago simply leave, and the writer names none of them going.

«He took hold of the horns of the altar.» The projections at the four corners of the altar; grasping them was a recognized claim of sanctuary. ⚠ And the law is not on Adonijah’s side as clearly as he hopes: Exodus 21:14 says that a man who kills deliberately is to be taken from my altar to die. The altar protected the man with a case, not everyone who could reach it — and in chapter 2 Joab will try the same thing and be killed holding on.

⚠ Read Solomon’s answer carefully, because it is not a pardon. “If he proves a worthy man — a son of valor — not one hair of his shall fall; but if evil is found in him, he shall die.” It is a conditional discharge with the condition unspecified and the judge deciding when it is broken. The phrase “son of valor” is the same word Adonijah used nine verses earlier to flatter Jonathan (“a worthy man”), and the chapter closes with the new king using it as a threat. “Go to your house” is a dismissal, not an amnesty: house arrest by courtesy. Chapter 2 collects on all of it.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the exact / distinct words: “come into days” for the idiom of a life that is over except for the arrangements; “attendant” for the rare sokhenet; “was exalting himself” for mitnasse, the reflexive of the lifting-verb, against a rival who is lifted by everyone else; “pained him” for atsav, the word for the rebuke David never gave; “fill out your words” for the coaching of a witness; “counted offenders” for chatta’im, what a losing claimant’s family becomes; “on the contrary” for aval; “the earth was split” for the exodus-strength verb; and “a worthy man” for ish/ben chayil, kept identical so the flattery in v42 and the threat in v52 are visibly the same phrase.

Echoes paid. Absalom’s chariot and fifty runners (2 Samuel 15:1), repeated without comment; Eli’s unrestrained sons (1 Samuel 3:13), the same fatherly failure with the same result; the anointing of Saul and David by Samuel, now done for a son and in public; and the altar-sanctuary law of Exodus 21:14, which does not cover as much as Adonijah needs it to.

Echoes planted. Abishag, whom the reader has been carefully introduced to and who becomes, in chapter 2, the pretext for a death sentence. The conditional Solomon attaches to Adonijah’s life, and the men on Adonijah’s guest list — Joab and Abiathar — every one of whom chapter 2 will deal with. The mule, and a king who will one day enter this city on a borrowed colt on purpose. And the throne itself, named ten times in this chapter and promised to David’s house forever (2 Samuel 7:16) — a promise this book will spend twenty-two chapters watching go wrong.
