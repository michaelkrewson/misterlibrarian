Translator's Notes

Verses 1–13 · the trap, built to the original specification

What the test actually is. Strip the theatre away and look at the engineering. Joseph arranges for the favoured youngest son of Rachel — the only one their father cannot bear to lose — to be caught in a capital offence, alone, with the other ten free to go home unharmed. That is not a random cruelty; it is the situation of chapter 37 rebuilt to specification, with the roles recast. Once, given the chance to abandon Rachel's son and walk away with their own skins intact, these men took it and sat down to eat. The whole point of the cup is to offer them that chance again.

The silver, put back a second time. Notice verse 1: he returns their money again. Nothing turns on it in the plot — the cup is what gets found — but it means every man is carrying unexplained silver at the moment the guard catches up. Joseph is not leaving them a plausible defence.

The divining cup. Nachesh yenachesh — the infinitive doubled for emphasis: he indeed divines by it. The practice is real and Egyptian: lecanomancy, reading the patterns made by oil dropped into water in a bowl, is attested across the ancient Near East. ⚠️ It is also a practice the Torah will flatly prohibit (Leviticus 19:26; Deuteronomy 18:10), which leaves the honest reader with a question the text does not answer: is Joseph describing what he does, or maintaining an Egyptian persona that includes a magician's reputation? He is a man who has told a foreign king that "not I — God will answer," so the second is at least as likely as the first. Genesis reports the claim and never comments on it. The KJV reads "whereby indeed he divineth," the ASV the same, the NIV "uses for divination"; none of them soften it, and neither does this.

They swear to their own innocence, and set the penalty. "Let him die; and we also will be slaves" — an oath of the confident. It is the second time this family has volunteered a death sentence for a crime it did not understand: Jacob said the same about Laban's stolen household gods (31:32) without knowing Rachel had them under her. The steward, who knows exactly where the cup is, immediately negotiates the terms down — nobody dies, only the guilty man is enslaved, the rest go free. He is not being merciful. He is setting the trap's release catch so the other nine can leave.

"He began with the eldest and finished with the youngest." One clause, and it is pure cruelty of pacing — a search that runs down the whole line in birth order while ten men watch, each one's relief lasting exactly as long as it takes to open the next bag. The steward knows. The reader knows. It takes as long as it takes.

And then the sentence that answers everything. "And they tore their garments" — the gesture their father made over the bloodied coat (37:34) and Reuben made at the empty pit (37:29) — and then every one of them loads his donkey and goes back to the city. They were free. The steward had explicitly released them. Not one of them takes it. That, and not the speech that follows, is the moment the test is passed; Judah's words are the explanation of a decision the whole group has already made with its feet.

Verses 14–17 · a confession about the wrong crime

"God has found out the guilt of your servants." Read Judah's answer carefully, because he does not defend Benjamin and he does not deny the theft — he cannot, and he does not try. What he says is stranger: God has found out the guilt of your servants — avon, the weightiest of the words for iniquity, the crookedness a person carries. They are innocent of the cup and Judah knows it. He is confessing something else, and the reader knows what, and the man in front of him knows what. It is the same reading of events the brothers made in 42:21: a ledger settling at last. They are wrong about the mechanism again — this is not heaven catching them, it is their brother — and right about the debt. The KJV's "God hath found out the iniquity of thy servants" is exact; the NIV's "God has uncovered your servants' guilt" is the same. Judah then offers all eleven into slavery together.

And Joseph refuses the group offer. "Far be it from me" — chalilah, the same protest Abraham used when he argued with God over Sodom ("far be it from you to do such a thing, to kill the righteous with the wicked," 18:25) and the word the brothers themselves used three verses earlier. Joseph is not being merciful either. A collective sentence would ruin the experiment. He insists on precisely the outcome that gives the ten what they once wanted: "the man in whose hand the cup was found — he will be my slave; and you, go up in peace to your father." Rachel's son enslaved; the rest of you, walk away clean. He even says shalom — go up in peace. It is the cruellest offer in Genesis, and it is made by a man who is testing whether it will be taken.

Verses 18–29 · the longest speech in Genesis

Seventeen verses, and it is almost all quotation. Judah's speech is the longest single utterance in the book, and its technique is remarkable: he barely argues. He recounts. He simply retells the conversation — you asked, we said, you said, we said, our father said — and lets the accumulated weight of an old man's words do the work. There is no appeal to justice (he has no case), no protest of innocence (he has already conceded), no offer of money. It is a speech made entirely out of somebody else's grief.

"You are like Pharaoh himself." The opening is exact court protocol from a man begging not to be cut off mid-sentence — and, as the reader alone knows, an understatement rather than flattery.

What Judah says to Joseph's face, without knowing. "His brother is dead, and he alone is left of his mother, and his father loves him." Every clause is a small detonation. He tells the man that the man is dead; he calls Benjamin the only surviving son of his mother, which quietly concedes the thing the whole family could never say out loud — that Jacob had one wife who counted and two sons who counted; and he states as bare fact the favouritism that started all of this: his father loves him. Judah is describing, without a flicker of self-pity or excuse, the exact structure that made him sell the man he is talking to. And notice what has changed: he is no longer complaining about it. He accepts it as the shape of his father's heart, and he has come to protect the beneficiary of it.

"My wife bore me two." Judah reports Jacob's words verbatim, and they are outrageous — the old man, in front of the sons of Leah, Bilhah and Zilpah, says "my wife" as though he had only ever had one, and counts his children as two. Judah repeats it to a stranger without editorialising. He has stopped keeping score.

"Surely he is torn to pieces." Tarof toraf — the exact words Jacob cried over the bloodied tunic in 37:33, delivered now, by one of the men who staged that scene, to the man it was staged about. And the reason the old man believes it is that Judah helped make him believe it. The room contains one person who does not know this is a lie, and it is not Judah, and it is not Joseph.

"To Sheol." Twice in five verses (vv. 29, 31), quoting his father: the grey head brought down in calamity, in sorrow, to Sheol. It is the third and fourth time this book has used the word, and every time it is the same man's grief — 37:35, 42:38, and now twice in Judah's mouth. The KJV reads "grave" in all four places; the ASV and NWT read "Sheol," and keeping it lets the reader hear one unbroken lament running twenty-two years and four chapters.

Verses 30–34 · the substitution

"His soul is bound up with the boy's soul." Nafsho qeshurah be-nafsho — nefesh twice, tied together with a verb used of literal knotting. Judah is telling a stranger that his father and his youngest brother are one life in two bodies, and therefore that a sentence on the boy is a sentence on the old man. He is arguing, with complete accuracy, about the very love that displaced him.

The surety comes due. "For your servant became surety for the boy" — the word from 43:9, and this is the moment it stops being a form of words. A guarantor pays when the thing is lost. The thing is lost. He pays.

"Let your servant remain instead of the boy." Tachat ha-na'ar — in his place, in exchange for him. The man who once said "what profit is there if we kill our brother? — come, let us sell him" (37:26-27) now offers to become the slave so that Rachel's son can go home. He proposes, precisely and voluntarily, to take the fate he once sold his brother into. Genesis does not tell you this is the point. It just puts the two sentences in the same book and lets them face each other.

And the last line is not about himself. "For how can I go up to my father, and the boy not with me? — lest I see the evil that would find my father." He does not end by pleading his own case, or his family's, or even Benjamin's. He ends on the thing he cannot bear to watch: an old man's face when he is told, a second time, that a son of Rachel is gone. It is the exact grief Judah once inflicted and then lived beside for twenty-two years, and he will do anything not to be in the room for it again. This is where the test ends. The first words of the next chapter are "Joseph could no longer hold himself in."

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the words exact / distinct: "indeed practises divination" for the doubled nachesh yenachesh, unsoftened, with the tension against Deuteronomy 18:10 left in the note rather than smoothed away; "the guilt" for avon, the heaviest of the iniquity words, because Judah is confessing something other than the cup; "Far be it" for chalilah in both mouths (vv. 7, 17), matching Abraham at Sodom; "Sheol" twice more, matching 37:35 and 42:38 where the KJV reads "grave" throughout; "his soul is bound up with the boy's soul" keeping nefesh twice; and "instead of the boy" for tachat, the language of substitution.

Echoes paid. Chapter 37 rebuilt to specification — Rachel's favoured son condemned alone, the others free to walk away — and this time nobody walks; the torn garments of Reuben and Jacob, now torn by all ten; arav, Judah's surety from 43:9, called in; tarof toraf, "surely he is torn to pieces," quoted from 37:33 back to the man it was invented about; chalilah from Abraham's argument at Sodom; and Judah's own "what profit is there" answered by an offer to be sold in his brother's place.

Echoes planted. "Joseph could no longer hold himself in" — the restraint counted at 43:31 failing in the next verse of the next chapter (45:1); "I am Joseph — is my father still alive?"; and the verdict that reframes the whole thirteen years, six chapters off: "you meant evil against me, but God meant it for good" (50:20). Judah's substitution also becomes his blessing: the brother who offered to take a slave's place is the one who receives the sceptre (49:8-10).

Next installment in this book: Genesis 45 — Joseph clears the room and breaks, and the Egyptians hear him weeping from the next building. "I am Joseph. Is my father still alive?" — and his brothers cannot answer him, because they are terrified. Then the sentence that has been twenty-two years arriving: "it was not you who sent me here, but God."
