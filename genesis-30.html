The war of the wives — the maidservants' sons, the mandrake bargain, and eight more children (Dan through Joseph); then Jacob out-shrewds Laban with the speckled flocks and grows rich.
AM ≈ 2192–2205 · c. 1753–1740 BC — the middle and later years of Jacob's twenty in Haran. Most of the twelve tribes are born here (Dan, Naphtali, Gad, Asher, Issachar, Zebulun, and Joseph, plus Dinah); with Joseph's birth Jacob turns for home, and in his last six years builds the flocks that make him wealthy enough to leave.

Translator's Notes — verse by verse

The war of the wives, and the tribes are born — a chapter of desperate rivalry beneath which God quietly builds a nation. A birth-list, so the notes group by episode. Quotations from copyrighted versions (NIV, TLB, NWT) are kept to short phrases for comparison; KJV, Geneva, Douay-Rheims and ASV are public domain.

Verses 1–13 · the war of the wives

"Give me children, or else I die." The chapter opens on Rachel's anguish — the beloved wife, barren, watching her unloved sister fill the tent with sons. Her cry to Jacob is raw, and his answer is sharp but true: "Am I in God's place, who has withheld from you the fruit of the womb?" Children are God's to give, not a husband's — the theology the whole chapter will bear out, against every scheme the sisters try. So begins a fertility contest fought by proxy: each wife hands her maidservant to Jacob to "bear on my knees" (a formal adoption gesture) — Rachel gives Bilhah, Leah gives Zilpah — exactly as Sarai once gave Hagar (16:2), and with the same word: "I too may be built up (ibbaneh) through her" — banah, "to build," the root of both ben ("son") and the hope of being built into a house.

Four more sons, four more name-sermons. Every birth is a claim in the sisters' quarrel, and each name is its Hebrew pun: Dan — "God has judged me" (dananni, from din); Naphtali — "with wrestlings of God I have wrestled (niphtalti) with my sister, and I have prevailed" (KJV "with great wrestlings"; the word niphtal, "to twist, wrestle," quietly foreshadows the night Jacob himself will wrestle God and be renamed Israel, 32:24–28); Gad — "Fortune has come!" (here the scroll writes one word, begad, and reads two, ba gad, a ketiv/qere — gad was also the name of a Canaanite luck-deity, though Leah means only "good fortune"); and Asher — "Happy am I! for the daughters will call me happy" (be-oshri, from osher, "happiness" — the very root of the ashrei, "Happy," that opens the Psalms and that Jesus takes up in the Beatitudes).

Verses 14–24 · דּוּדָאִים dudaim — the mandrakes, and God remembers Rachel

The love-apples. Little Reuben brings his mother mandrakes (dudaim; KJV "mandrakes") — a plant whose forked root and heady scent made it the ancient world's love-charm and fertility-drug, its very name echoing dod, "beloved." The scene drips with irony: Rachel, who has Jacob's love, has no children; Leah, who has children, has lost his nights — so they strike a bargain, and Rachel trades a night with her own husband for a handful of fertility-plants. And then the point lands like a verdict: it is Leah, not Rachel, who conceives from that night; the mandrakes do nothing. Leah bears a fifth son, Issachar — "God has given my hire" (sakhar, punning on the mandrakes she "hired" Jacob with); a sixth, Zebulun — "God has endowed me a good endowment; now my husband will dwell with me" (a double play on zeved and zaval); and a daughter, Dinah.

"And God remembered Rachel." Only after all the striving fails does the answer come — and it comes as sheer gift: "And God remembered (zakhar) Rachel… and opened her womb" (v. 22) — the same verb, and the same turn, as when "God remembered Noah" and the flood began to recede (8:1), and the same mercy that had opened Leah's womb (29:31). Her son's name carries a double meaning folded into one word: Joseph (Yosef) — "God has taken away (asaf) my reproach," and, looking ahead, "may Jehovah add (yosef) to me another son" — a prayer answered, at last, with Benjamin (35:18). The son the whole last quarter of Genesis will follow is born of the wife who waited longest.

Verses 25–43 · the speckled flocks — the deceiver out-deceived

The wages of a shrewd shepherd. With Joseph born, Jacob wants to go home, but Laban — who admits, "I have divined that Jehovah has blessed me for your sake" — will not let his lucky son-in-law leave. That admission is the Abrahamic promise at work in a pagan household: "in you all the families of the earth will be blessed" (12:3), the blessing spilling onto everyone near the chosen line (as it later will onto Potiphar and Pharaoh through Joseph). Jacob names his price: not a wage, but every off-colored animal — the speckled, spotted, streaked, and dark (KJV "ringstraked, speckled, and spotted") — the rare and undesirable ones in a flock of white sheep and dark goats. Laban agrees, then promptly cheats, culling every such animal that day and hustling it three days off with his sons.

The peeled rods. So Jacob answers guile with guile. He peels white streaks in fresh rods of poplar, almond, and plane, and sets them at the watering-troughs where the flocks breed, "in front of the flocks" — trusting the old belief that what a mother looks upon in the moment of conception marks her young — and the flocks bear streaked and speckled offspring in droves. Whatever the biology (the next chapter has Jacob tell it differently — a dream in which the angel of God, not the rods, made the flocks conceive, 31:10–12), the plot is unmistakable: the man who put on his brother's skin to steal a blessing, and was paid back a veiled bride by this same Laban, now out-maneuvers the master deceiver himself. He even breeds selectively, laying the rods only for the stronger animals, so the vigorous stock becomes his and the feeble stays Laban's. "And the man increased exceedingly" (me'od me'od, the "very, very" of the promise to Abraham, 17:2) — rich in flocks and servants and camels, and ready, now, to go home.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the words exact / distinct: the eight name-puns rendered so the Hebrew shows through — Dan/judged, Naphtali/wrestled, Gad/fortune, Asher/happy, Issachar/hire, Zebulun/endowed-and-dwell, and Joseph's double asaf/yasaf ("taken away" / "add"); "maidservant" kept for the surrogate-mothers' status (amah/shifchah); the flock-colors held distinct — speckled, spotted, streaked, and dark; and "increased exceedingly" for me'od me'od, the promise-word.

Echoes paid. "Built up through her" — Sarai's word over Hagar (16:2); "God remembered Rachel," as he remembered Noah (8:1) and opened Leah's womb (29:31); Laban blessed "for your sake," the 12:3 blessing on a pagan house; and "increased exceedingly" (17:2). Echoes planted: Naphtali's wrestling, waiting for the Jabbok (ch. 32); Joseph's "add another son," waiting for Benjamin (ch. 35); the mandrake-lender Leah's Issachar and the whole roll of twelve, waiting to be numbered as an army (Numbers 1); and Laban's cheated wages, waiting for the reckoning of chapter 31.

Next installment in this book: Genesis 31 — Jacob's flight from Haran, Rachel and the stolen household gods, Laban's pursuit, and the treaty-heap at Mizpah: "May Jehovah watch between you and me when we are out of each other's sight."
