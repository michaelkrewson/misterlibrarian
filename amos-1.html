Five foreign nations sentenced in turn — Damascus, Gaza, Tyre, Edom, Ammon — by a Judean sheep-breeder standing in the northern kingdom at the height of its prosperity.
≈760 BC, and this is one of the most precisely dated books in the Bible: Uzziah is on the throne in Judah, Jeroboam II in Israel, and Amos adds a second fix nobody else uses — 'two years before THE earthquake', with the definite article, because his readers could count from it. Zechariah 14:5 was still measuring by that earthquake two and a half centuries later, and mid-eighth-century destruction layers at Hazor, Lachish, Gezer and in the City of David are commonly identified with it. Amos is, on most reckonings, the EARLIEST of the writing prophets — about thirty years before Samaria falls.

Translator’s Notes

Verse 1 · the words of a sheep-breeder, and a book dated by an earthquake

«The WORDS of Amos» is not how a prophetic book normally begins. Hosea, Joel, Micah, Zephaniah and Jonah all open the same way: the word of Jehovah that came to… Only Amos and Jeremiah start instead with the words of a man. And then the sentence does something stranger still — those words are the ones “which he SAW” (chazah, the verb of visions). Words seen, not heard. The book keeps both halves of that all the way through: it is a man talking, and it is a man reporting what was shown him.

⚠ Noqedim is not the ordinary word for shepherd, and the choice matters. Noqed occurs exactly twice in the Hebrew Bible: here, and at 2 Kings 3:4, where it describes Mesha king of Moab — the same Mesha who left the Moabite Stone — who paid Israel a tribute of a hundred thousand lambs and the wool of a hundred thousand rams. The Arabic cognate naqad names a small, ugly, fine-fleeced breed of sheep. So the word is at home in the mouth of a man who managed flocks as a business, and the familiar picture of Amos as a barefoot peasant is a picture the vocabulary does not require. The shelf is split, and one version is noticeably sharper than the rest: KJV “among the herdmen of Tekoa”; ASV “among the herdsmen of Tekoa”; DRB “among the herdsmen of Thecua”; NIV “one of the shepherds of Tekoa”; NWT alone keeps the technical sense — “sheep raisers” — and on the merits it is right, so we follow it with Mister “sheep-breeders.”

⚠ Do not over-read it either. At 7:14, hauled up in front of the priest of Bethel, Amos says he is no prophet and no prophet’s son but a herdsman (boqer, a cattleman — a different word again) and a dresser of sycamore figs. Piercing sycamore fruit to make it ripen is hired work. Both cases are made from real evidence, and the honest position is that the rare word points one way and the day-work points the other. We render the rare word rarely and leave the argument standing.

Tekoa is a Judean town about ten miles south of Jerusalem, on the lip of the wilderness that falls away to the Dead Sea — the home also of the “wise woman of Tekoa” whom Joab coached into changing David’s mind (2 Samuel 14). ⚠ Note what that makes Amos: a southerner whose entire message is aimed north, at a kingdom that was not his, in a sanctuary that was not his, and he will be told to go home for it (7:12).

⚠ And the date is fixed twice — by two kings, and by a natural disaster. Uzziah in Judah and Jeroboam II in Israel both had long reigns of unusual wealth, which places this around 760 BC and makes Amos, on most reckonings, the earliest of the writing prophets. Then: “two years before THE earthquake” — with the definite article, because his readers knew which one. Nobody else in the Bible dates a book that way. It was still being used as a marker two and a half centuries later, when Zechariah told his readers they would flee “as you fled from the earthquake in the days of Uzziah king of Judah” (Zechariah 14:5). Archaeologists have found mid-eighth-century collapse and destruction layers at Hazor, Lachish, Gezer, Deir Alla, Tell Judeideh and En Haseva, and in 2021 the excavation in the City of David reported smashed vessels and toppled walls of the same date in Jerusalem itself; sediment cores from the Dead Sea fault register a very large seismic event in the same window. The identification of all this with Amos’s earthquake is standard, and it is an identification rather than a proof — which is the honest way to hold it.

Verse 2 · the roar, and a line Joel will use word for word

The first thing God does in this book is make a noise no shepherd could mistake. Yish’ag is the roar of a lion — specifically the roar over prey, the sound after the kill rather than the hunting growl. A man who has spent his life on the edge of the Judean wilderness has heard it, and he gives it to God in the first sentence of his own book. It becomes his signature: “A lion has roared — who will not fear? The Lord Jehovah has spoken — who will not prophesy?” (3:8).

⚠ «From ZION … from JERUSALEM» is a claim, not a piece of scenery. This is a Judean prophet’s opening line to the northern kingdom, and it locates the voice in the southern temple — not at Bethel, not at Dan, which is where the north actually worshipped, and where Amos is eventually thrown out (7:10–13). Everything that follows is being spoken over a border.

The two halves of the verse measure the whole country. “The pastures of the shepherds” are the dry grazing land of the south, the first ground to burn off; Carmel is the wet green headland on the northern coast, the last place in the land to dry. From the first thing to go to the last thing to go — end to end, nothing left. ASV “the pastures of the shepherds shall mourn, and the top of Carmel shall wither”; KJV has “the habitations of the shepherds”; NIV “the pastures of the shepherds dry up”; NWT “the pasture grounds of the shepherds will mourn.” We keep the present tense: Hebrew prophecy announces in a tense English does not have, and the older versions’ “shall” pushes into the future what the Hebrew simply declares.

⚠ This verse turns up again, almost word for word, in another prophet. Joel 4:16 (English 3:16) reads “Jehovah roars from Zion, and from Jerusalem he gives his voice” — identical — and Joel’s first chapter has the pastures mourning too. One prophet is quoting the other, and since nobody can date Joel, nobody can say which. It is one of the exhibits in the five-hundred-year argument over when Joel wrote, and the library sets it out on both books’ pages without voting.

Verses 3–5 · a formula that will be used eight times, a word that means REVOLT, and a pronoun with nothing to refer to

The shape of the next two chapters is set here and never varies: Thus says Jehovah: for three revolts of X, and for four, I will not turn it back — because… then the crime, then fire. It runs eight times: Damascus, Gaza, Tyre, Edom and Ammon in this chapter; Moab, then Judah, then Israel in the next. Watch the list narrow. The first three are outsiders. Then Edom, who is Esau; then Ammon and Moab, who are Lot’s two sons; then Judah, the brother kingdom; and then, in the eighth oracle and the same words, the audience.

«For three … and for four» is a known Hebrew form, and it is not arithmetic. The graded numerical saying — x, and x+1 — runs all through Proverbs 30 (“three things are too wonderful for me, four I do not understand”) and Job 5:19 and 33:29. It means: the count is full, and then some. Which is why, for most of these nations, only one crime is then named. It is not a list; it is a verdict.

⚠ Pesha is not the general word for sin, and translating it “transgressions” loses the courtroom. Its plain sense is a vassal’s REVOLT against his overlord — the word used when “Moab rebelled against Israel” (2 Kings 1:1) and when “Israel rebelled against the house of David” (1 Kings 12:19). Amos is convening a court in which foreign nations stand charged as rebels, which quietly presumes a sovereign whose authority they had never acknowledged. KJV and ASV have “transgressions”; NIV “sins”; DRB, following the Vulgate, “crimes”; NWT alone keeps the political sense with “revolts,” and here that is simply the more exact word, so Mister follows it.

⚠ «I will not turn IT back» — and the Hebrew never says what “it” is. The verb carries a bare masculine suffix with no antecedent in the sentence, so every translator has to supply one, and they supply different things. KJV “I will not turn away the punishment thereof” — and prints the added words in italics, which is honest of it; ASV the same; NIV smooths it to “I will not relent”; DRB, from the Vulgate’s non convertam eum, has the startling “I will not convert it”; NWT keeps the shape without filling the gap — “I shall not turn it back.” We do the same. The candidates for “it” are the decree, the anger, the word just spoken, or the people themselves; the Hebrew leaves the door open and so do we.

The crime is named with a farm tool. A threshing sledge is a heavy board studded underneath with stone or iron teeth, dragged over cut grain by an ox to shred the straw and free the seed — and it is applied here to a region full of people. Whether Amos means a literal atrocity or a figure for a campaign that ground Gilead into the dirt, the historical referent is not in doubt: 2 Kings 10:32–33 records Hazael taking all of Gilead from Israel. NIV “sledges having iron teeth”; ASV “threshing instruments of iron”; DRB, oddly and memorably, “iron wains” — wagons, from the Vulgate’s plaustris ferreis.

⚠ The two names in verse 4 are men we can read about outside the Bible. Hazael seized the throne of Damascus by smothering his master (2 Kings 8:15); Assyrian records of Shalmaneser III call him “son of a nobody,” their standard phrase for a usurper, and the Tel Dan victory stele is generally attributed to him. Ben-hadad is the dynastic name of his son and successor (2 Kings 13:3). Amos does not curse the country — he names the palace and the heir.

Two places, and a deliberate distortion. The Valley of Aven means “valley of trouble / wickedness,” and the usual candidate is the Beqa valley with its great sun-temple at Baalbek — so the name may be a sneer at a shrine, the way Hosea calls Bethel Beth-aven, “house of trouble.” Beth-eden is not a joke about paradise: it is Bit-Adini, a real Aramean state on the middle Euphrates that the Assyrians swallowed in the 850s. KJV “the plain of Aven … the house of Eden”; ASV “the valley of Aven … the house of Eden.” And armenot, which we render citadels, is Amos’s own word for what he is against: the fortified keep of a ruling class. It appears in every oracle here and keeps coming back (3:9–11; 6:8). KJV/ASV “palaces”; NIV “fortresses”; NWT “dwelling towers.”

⚠ «To KIR» is the sharpest line in the oracle, and you need chapter 9 to feel it. At 9:7 Amos asks: did I not bring Israel up from Egypt — and the Philistines from Caphtor, and Aram from KIR? Kir is where the Arameans came from. The sentence passed on Damascus is therefore: go back to the beginning; the exodus I gave you is cancelled. And it happened. In 732 BC Tiglath-pileser III took Damascus and deported its people — and 2 Kings 16:9 says he carried them to Kir, using this very name, about thirty years after this was spoken.

Verses 6–8 · a whole population sold, and the Philistine city that is missing

«A whole population» (galut shelemah) is the point of the charge. Not prisoners taken in a battle, which everybody did, but a clean sweep: a raid that emptied a place of its people and moved them on for sale. And the buyer is named — Edom, which reappears two verses later as Tyre’s customer as well. ASV “they carried away captive the whole people, to deliver them up to Edom”; NIV “she took captive whole communities and sold them to Edom”; NWT “their taking into exile a complete body of exiles.”

⚠ Stop and notice what Amos does NOT charge these nations with. Not idolatry. Not failing to worship Jehovah. Not neglecting a law they had never been given. Every single crime in this chapter is an atrocity against human beings — deportation, trafficking, treaty-breaking, endless vengeance, the killing of the unborn — and the whole rhetorical structure assumes that the nations knew these were wrong without anyone having to hand them a covenant. When the list finally reaches Judah in the next chapter, the charge changes shape entirely: they rejected the instruction of Jehovah (2:4). Different relationships, different standards; but the floor is the same for everyone.

⚠ Four of the five Philistine cities are named — and GATH is not. Gaza, Ashdod, Ashkelon, Ekron are all here; the fifth member of the classic pentapolis is skipped. The usual explanation is that Gath had already been destroyed: 2 Kings 12:17 has Hazael capture it, and the long excavation of Tell es-Safi has uncovered a massive late-ninth-century destruction together with a siege trench traced for nearly two and a half kilometres around the site — one of the largest ancient siege works known. ⚠ It is a good explanation and not a certainty: Amos names Gath at 6:2, so the place still existed. What it may no longer have been is a power.

The oracle closes on a title. Not “says Jehovah” as the others do, but “says the Lord Jehovah” — Adonai YHVH, which is Amos’s characteristic name for God and turns up around twenty times in nine chapters, far more densely than in any other prophet. ASV, alone on the shelf, prints it exactly: “saith the Lord Jehovah.”

Verses 9–10 · a covenant of brothers

Tyre is charged with the same crime as Gaza, and then with something extra. The trafficking clause is repeated almost verbatim — a whole population handed over to Edom — and then: and did not remember a covenant of brothers. The aggravation is not the deed but the betrayal wrapped around it.

⚠ «Brothers» here is treaty language, not biology. In the diplomatic vocabulary of the age, kings who signed a treaty as equals addressed each other as brother. The obvious candidate is the alliance of Tyre with Israel: “there was peace between Hiram and Solomon, and the two of them cut a covenant” (1 Kings 5:12), and Hiram writes to Solomon as “my brother” (1 Kings 9:13). Other readings are defensible — a treaty between Tyre and the people it sold, or simply the general bond of neighbours — and the library gives them without choosing. KJV and ASV “the brotherly covenant”; NIV “a treaty of brotherhood”; NWT “the covenant of brothers.”

⚠ A structural oddity worth recording. The Tyre and Edom oracles are visibly shorter than the others: no closing “says Jehovah,” and none of the expansion that cuts off the enthroned one and the sceptre-holder. Some scholars read the short oracles — Tyre, Edom, and Judah in the next chapter — as later additions to an original set; others read the variation as deliberate pacing, so that the formula does not become a drone. We print what the Masoretic Text prints and record the argument rather than settling it.

Verses 11–12 · his BROTHER — and the mercy that is a womb

Edom is Esau, and Israel is Jacob, and the charge is that one twin hunted the other with a sword. Everybody hearing this had read Genesis. The word his brother does the work of a whole family history in two syllables — and the rest of the verse describes not an outburst but a policy of remembering.

⚠ Shichet rachamav — literally «he RUINED his compassions» — and both words are loaded. Shachat, “to ruin,” is the verb that tolls three times over the world before the flood: the earth was RUINED … all flesh had RUINED its way … I am about to RUIN them (Genesis 6:11–13). And rachamim, “compassion,” is built on rechem, the womb — the same root this translation met four verses into Hosea, where a newborn daughter is named LO-RUCHAMAH, Not-Pitied. Put the two together and the accusation is not that Edom felt no pity. It is that he had the organ of mercy and destroyed it.

⚠ And here the shelf splits — with one version changing its mind in living memory. KJV and ASV “did cast off all pity”; DRB “and had violated his mercy”; NWT is closest to the Hebrew with “he ruined his own mercies.” NIV 1984 read “stifling all compassion” — but the NIV of 2011 now reads “and slaughtered the women of the land,” taking rachamav in the concrete sense of wombs, and therefore of women. It is an old proposal, it is defensible, and it is a very large change to make quietly. We keep the sense the Masoretic pointing gives and flag the other.

The last two clauses are predator vocabulary. His anger TORE — taraf is what a lion does to a carcass, the same animal register as the roar in verse 2, now handed to a man’s temper. And his fury he KEPT forever — shamar, the verb for guarding a treasure or keeping a commandment. Edom kept his rage the way other people keep an oath.

Teman and Bozrah are a district and the northern capital of Edom. Bozrah is Buseirah in southern Jordan, excavated in the 1970s and found to be exactly what the oracle assumes: a fortified administrative centre. And Teman is a name this translation has met before — Esau’s grandson, the son of Eliphaz (Genesis 36:11), whose clan gave the district its name. Job’s first and most confident friend is a Temanite.

Verses 13–15 · the worst of the five, and a king who may be a god

The atrocity charged against Ammon is the one the ancient world used to mark the far edge of war. It is charged elsewhere against Hazael — Elisha weeps as he foresees it (2 Kings 8:12) — and, pointedly, against Menahem king of Israel at Tiphsah (2 Kings 15:16). Amos’s northern audience is not being told about a crime only foreigners commit.

⚠ What makes this the worst of the five is the little clause at the end: «in order to enlarge their own border.» Not rage. Not reprisal. Not the blindness of a battle. Policy — a land-acquisition programme executed by killing a generation before it could be born. Amos has arranged five oracles so that this one lands last, and it is the only one that states a motive.

The formula finally breaks its own shape. Four times we have had I will SEND fire (shilachti); Ammon gets I will KINDLE (hitzati), and then the sentence accelerates into noise — a war cry (teru’ah, the same word as the ram’s-horn blast of the festivals and of the walls of Jericho) on the day of battle, a tempest on the day of the whirlwind. Nowhere else in the eight oracles does the verdict pick up speed like this. KJV “with shouting in the day of battle, with a tempest in the day of the whirlwind”; ASV the same; NIV “amid war cries on the day of battle, amid violent winds on a stormy day.”

Rabbah is Rabbath-Ammon, under the modern city of Amman — the wall outside which Uriah the Hittite was arranged to die (2 Samuel 11:14–17), and the city David eventually took (2 Samuel 12:26–31).

⚠ And the last word of the chapter may be a king or may be a god. Malkam is “their king” — or it is MILCOM, the national god of Ammon, whose name is spelled with exactly these consonants (1 Kings 11:5, 33). The same ambiguity is live at Jeremiah 49:1–3, where the versions split. A god going into exile is not a strange idea but a normal one: when a city fell, the conquerors carried the statue away, and that is the picture an ancient reader would see. KJV, ASV and NWT all read “their king”; NIV reads “her king” and footnotes the alternative Molek. We keep the king and flag the god.

And there the chapter stops — five nations condemned, and a northern audience that has been nodding along the entire way down the list. Damascus had it coming. Gaza had it coming. Tyre, Edom, Ammon: all of them. Chapter 2 adds Moab, and then Judah, and then, in the eighth oracle and in exactly the same words, for three revolts of ISRAEL, and for four…

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the exact / distinct words: “sheep-breeders” for noqedim, a rare word used elsewhere only of a king who paid tribute in a hundred thousand lambs — rendered rarely so the argument about Amos’s social standing stays visible; “revolts” for pish’ei, following the NWT against the familiar “transgressions,” because pesha is the vassal’s rebellion and this chapter is a courtroom; “I will not turn it back” with the pronoun left dangling, as the Hebrew leaves it, instead of supplying “the punishment”; “citadels” for armenot, the fortified keep of a ruling class and Amos’s standing target; “ruined his own compassion” for shichet rachamav, keeping both the flood-verb shachat = ruin and the womb-root of rachamim; “tore” for taraf, the lion’s verb given to a temper; and “the Lord Jehovah” for Adonai YHVH, Amos’s own title for God.

Echoes paid. Shachat, the “ruin” that tolls three times over the pre-flood world in Genesis 6, turned on a man’s own capacity for pity. Rachamim, the womb-mercy that named Hosea’s daughter LO-RUCHAMAH, destroyed here by her brother-nation. Esau and Jacob, still quarrelling a thousand years after Genesis 27. Gilead, the treaty-highlands of Genesis 31, now threshed by one neighbour and disembowelled by another. And Joel’s mourning pastures and roaring Jehovah — the same two lines, in two books nobody can put in order.

Echoes planted. The eighth oracle — the formula runs five times here, three more in chapter 2, and the last one is aimed at the people listening. The lion, roaring in verse 2 and explained at 3:8: “a lion has roared; who will not fear?” Kir, the exile of verse 5, revealed at 9:7 to be the place Aram originally came from — the same verse in which God claims to have run other nations’ exoduses too. The citadels, which Amos will come back to until the ruling class inside them is the whole subject (3:9–11; 6:8). “I am no prophet, nor a prophet’s son” (7:14), the sentence that reopens everything verse 1 said about sheep. And the day of Jehovah — because Amos is the first man on record to turn it around and point it at his own audience: “Why do you want the day of Jehovah? It is darkness and not light” (5:18).
