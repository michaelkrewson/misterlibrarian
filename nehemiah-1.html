Susa, in the Persian winter palace — a Judean court official hears that Jerusalem still lies open and disgraced, and spends four months praying about it.
445 BC — Kislev of the twentieth year of Artaxerxes I. ⚠ A wrinkle: this is Kislev (Nov/Dec) of the twentieth year and 2:1 is Nisan (Mar/Apr) of the SAME twentieth year, though Nisan begins the Babylonian year — so Nehemiah is counting regnal years from an autumn new year or from the king's accession. Roughly a century after Cyrus's decree, and some ninety years after the second temple was finished.

Translator’s Notes

Verses 1–3 · a man’s own words, and news that is a century and a half old

⚠ «The words of Nehemiah son of Hacaliah.» No other book in the Hebrew Bible opens like this and then keeps going in the FIRST PERSON. A prophetic book begins “the word of Jehovah that came to…”; a history begins “and it happened in the days of…”. This begins with a man’s name and his own account, and it reads that way for most of its length — which is why the long first-person stretches are usually called the Nehemiah Memoir. He is not a prophet and not a king. He is a senior civil servant writing up what he did, and the closest formal parallels are not in the Bible at all but in Near Eastern royal and official inscriptions, where a man records his works and asks his god to remember them — which is exactly what Nehemiah does, four times over, at intervals through the book (“remember me, my God, for good”).

«In Susa the citadel.» Shushan ha-birah — the fortified palace complex, not the town below it. Susa was the Persian winter capital, and the same acropolis is the setting of Esther. KJV and ASV transliterate, “Shushan the palace”; NIV renders the sense, “the citadel of Susa.” Both are right; the Hebrew word birah means a fortified complex, not a residence. It has been excavated since the 1880s: the apadana, the glazed-brick reliefs of the royal guard now in the Louvre, the lot. When this book says a Judean was there, it is describing a real building.

⚠ A calendar wrinkle worth noticing. This is Kislev (Nov/Dec) of “the twentieth year”; at 2:1 Nehemiah goes to the king in Nisan (Mar/Apr) of the same twentieth year. But Nisan begins the Babylonian year, so Nisan should come before Kislev, not four months after it. Either Nehemiah is counting regnal years from an autumn (Tishri) new year, or from the king’s accession date rather than the calendar. The text does not explain, and the discrepancy is one of the small pieces of evidence people use in the long argument about the chronology of this period.

«The escaped remnant.» Two remnant-words in one breath: peletah, those who slipped away to safety, and ha-nish’arim asher nish’aru, “those left who were left.” The first is Joseph’s word at Genesis 45:7, where God sent him ahead to preserve a peletah for his family. KJV keeps the doubling audible — “the remnant that are left of the captivity” — where NIV tidies it to “those who survived the exile.” Six centuries later it is the ordinary administrative term for the people still in Judah.

⚠ And now the question this chapter always raises: why is a ruined wall NEWS? Jerusalem’s walls were burned by Nebuchadnezzar in 586 BC. Nehemiah hears this report in 445. Nobody needs a delegation from Judah to tell them about something that happened a hundred and forty years earlier. Two readings:

• It is fresh news of a recent disaster. Ezra 4:23 records that an attempt to rebuild the walls was stopped by force in the reign of Artaxerxes — officials went to Jerusalem “and made them cease by force and power.” On this reading Hanani is reporting the wreckage of that attempt, and the chapter follows directly on it. This is the usual explanation and it fits the urgency.
• It is the cumulative state of things, heard in personal detail for the first time. Nehemiah has never been there; a man raised in Susa may know a city is ruined and still be undone by hearing it described by someone who has stood in it.

Every version prints the report flatly and none of them explains it — ASV “the wall of Jerusalem also is broken down, and the gates thereof are burned with fire.” The text does not say. What it does say is the word cherpah — reproach, public disgrace — and that is the word the book turns on: at 2:17 Nehemiah will tell the people “let us build the wall of Jerusalem, that we may no longer be a reproach.” The problem he sets out to solve is not primarily military. It is shame.

Verses 4–5 · what a Persian official calls God

«I sat down and wept and mourned for days.» Four verbs, and none of them is acted. KJV “I sat down and wept, and mourned certain days, and fasted, and prayed”; the TLB compresses the four months into a sentence and loses the length of it, which is the whole point of the verse. The book that is about to become the most practical in the Bible — work rotas, wall sections, building materials, night patrols — opens with a man sitting on the floor crying for several days, and then fasting and praying for four months before he says a word to anyone who could help. That order is deliberate and the book never comments on it.

⚠ «Before the God of the heavens.» Elohei ha-shamayim — the identical title Cyrus’s decree uses at Ezra 1:2. It is the standard way of naming the supreme God in Persian-period official documents, and here it is not a foreign emperor using it but a Judean who works at the Persian court, praying privately. He thinks in the vocabulary of his employer’s administration, and the book is entirely unembarrassed about that: Nehemiah is a man of two worlds and does not pretend otherwise.

⚠ And the prayer begins by quoting. “The great and awesome God, who keeps the covenant and the kindness (chesed) for those who love him and keep his commandments” is Deuteronomy 7:9, almost word for word. That is the method of the whole prayer, and the next note follows it through.

Verses 6–10 · a prayer made almost entirely of other people’s sentences

⚠ Nehemiah does not compose a prayer. He prays Scripture back to God. Set the pieces beside their sources:

• v5, “the great and awesome God, keeping covenant and kindness with those who love him and keep his commandments” — Deuteronomy 7:9.
• vv8–9, “if you act unfaithfully I will scatter you among the peoples… but if you return to me… though your outcasts were at the end of the heavens, from there I will gather them” — Deuteronomy 30:1–5, compressed.
• v10, “your servants and your people, whom you redeemed by your great power and by your strong hand” — Deuteronomy 9:29.

And notice which passage he chooses. Deuteronomy 30 is the one that promises exactly the thing he wants: that scattering is not the end of the sentence, and that a return is written into the terms. “Remember, please, the word that you commanded Moses” is not a devotional flourish — it is a man holding God to a document. This is what prayer looks like in a world where the Torah has become the community’s constitution, and it is the same instinct as the scribe in the next book over who “set his heart to study the law of Jehovah, and to do it, and to teach.”

«We have sinned… both I and my father’s house.» Read that from where he is standing. Nehemiah was born in exile, holds a comfortable and prestigious post, and had no part whatever in the events that destroyed Jerusalem a century and a half earlier. He confesses them in the first person plural, and then narrows to the first person singular, and then to his own family. It is the standard shape of communal confession in this period (Ezra 9 and Daniel 9 do the same), and it assumes something modern readers often do not: that a person can be genuinely implicated in what their people did before they were born.

«We have acted very corruptly.» Chavol chavalnu — the verb doubled with its own infinitive, Hebrew’s way of leaning hard on a word. KJV “we have dealt very corruptly”; ASV the same. The root is the one used of ruining or wrecking a thing. NIV “we have acted very wickedly” is a moral judgement; the Hebrew is closer to we have wrecked things.

«The place I have chosen to make my name dwell there.» The Deuteronomic formula again, and the same careful theology met two books ago at 2 Chronicles 1:18: a place can house the NAME without housing God. ⚠ Verse 9 also carries a ketiv/qere — the written and read forms of “and I will bring them” differ by a letter; nothing turns on it.

Verse 11 · “this man” — and five words held to the very end

⚠ «Grant him mercy before THIS MAN.» The man in question is the king of Persia, ruler of everything from the Indus to the Aegean, the most powerful human being alive. In his own prayer Nehemiah calls him ha-ish ha-zeh — this man. It is not contempt; it is proportion. He has just spent six verses addressing “the great and awesome God”, and in that company the emperor is a man who will need to be asked for something.

⚠ And then the last five words of the chapter. Va-ani hayiti mashqeh la-melekh — “Now I was cupbearer to the king.” The single most important fact in the chapter, dropped at the end like a footnote, and the whole prayer is retroactively transformed by it. “This man” is someone Nehemiah sees daily. “Give success to your servant today” is a request about a conversation he is in a position to have.

A king’s cupbearer was not a waiter. He tasted the wine, which made him one of the few people permitted to be close to the king with something in his hand; he was therefore chosen for absolute trustworthiness, and in Persian and Assyrian courts the office carried real political weight. KJV here reads “the king’s cupbearer,” correctly — the same version that turned Pharaoh’s identical office into “butler” in Genesis 40, which is why English readers have underestimated Joseph’s prison companion for four centuries. The reader has met one before in this translation, in another empire: Pharaoh’s mashqeh, imprisoned beside Joseph and restored to hand the king his cup. Two cupbearers, a thousand years apart, and in both stories the office turns out to be the hinge.

Structurally it is a beautiful piece of writing. Withhold the one fact that makes the prayer make sense; let the reader hear a man in Susa weeping over a ruined wall four months’ journey away and apparently able to do nothing about it; and then, in the last clause, reveal that he pours the emperor’s wine.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the exact / distinct words: “The words of Nehemiah” as the superscription it is, not smoothed into a narrative opening; “the escaped remnant” for peletah, Joseph’s word; “reproach” for cherpah, the book’s theme word, kept the same here and at 2:17; “the God of the heavens” for elohei ha-shamayim, identical to Cyrus’s decree in Ezra 1; “acted very corruptly” for the doubled chavol chavalnu; “this man” left exactly as flat as the Hebrew has it; and “cupbearer” for mashqeh, as in Genesis 40.

Echoes paid. Peletah, the survivor-word from Genesis 45:7; elohei ha-shamayim, the Persian-court title of Ezra 1:2, now in the private prayer of a Judean who works there; the “place to make my name dwell,” the Deuteronomic formula last seen at 2 Chronicles 1:18; the ruined wall of Ezra 4:23, on the likeliest reading of why this is news; and Pharaoh’s mashqeh from Genesis 40 — the same office, the other empire, the same hinge.

Echoes planted. The wall, which will be built in fifty-two days against organized opposition. Hanani, who reappears at 7:2 put in charge of Jerusalem, because he was “a faithful man and feared God more than many.” Artaxerxes, the man Nehemiah has to ask. And the phrase “remember me, my God,” which is how this book will end — a memoir addressed, in the end, to its subject’s employer’s superior.

Nehemiah is a new book on the site. Next in sequence: Nehemiah 2 — four months later, the king notices his cupbearer’s face and asks why it is sad, which the text says frightened Nehemiah very much; he prays between the question and the answer, and asks for a leave of absence, timber, and letters; then the night ride around the broken walls with nobody told what he intended, and the first appearance of Sanballat and Tobiah, who were “greatly displeased that someone had come to seek the welfare of the sons of Israel.”
