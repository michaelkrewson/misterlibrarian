Translator's Notes

Verses 1–8 · two officials, one night — and who owns an interpretation

Butlers and bakers, and how big these jobs were. The KJV's "butler" has shrunk in four hundred years into a comic figure in a tailcoat; the Hebrew mashqeh is simply "one who gives drink," and the office was enormous. A king's cupbearer tasted what the king swallowed, which means he was chosen for loyalty above almost anyone at court and stood closer to the royal ear than most ministers; centuries later Nehemiah is cupbearer to the king of Persia and can get imperial policy changed over a single sad face (Nehemiah 1-2). Both men here are sarisim — the same court-official word used of Potiphar — and their offence is left completely unstated. Genesis does not care what they did. It cares that they dreamed.

The wheel turns quietly. Notice who owns the prison: they are put "in the house of the chief of the executioners" — Potiphar's house, the same title from 37:36 and 39:1. Joseph is still, technically, inside his master's establishment; and it is Potiphar himself who appoints Joseph to attend them. Whatever the man believed about his wife's accusation, he is still handing Joseph responsibility. And the verb in verse 4 is the same one used when he first put Joseph over his house (39:4). Slave, steward, prisoner, steward again.

"Why are your faces bad today?" A small line that does a lot of work. The literal Hebrew — ra'im, "bad," the plain word for evil — is blunt, and every version softens it ("sad," KJV and ASV; "dejected," NIV; "gloomy," NWT). This translation keeps "bad" and lets the strangeness stand. But the point is that he asked. A slave in a foreign jail, framed and forgotten, notices that two other men are having a worse morning than usual and stops to ask why. The entire remainder of his life turns on that question.

"Do not interpretations belong to God?" The chapter's thesis, and it is aimed at a very specific audience. Egypt had a serious, professional dream-interpretation industry — an actual reference manual survives, the Dream Book of Papyrus Chester Beatty III, which lists dreams in columns with standard readings ("if a man sees himself looking into a deep well: bad; it means being put in prison"). The two officials complain that "there is no one to interpret" — meaning no specialist, because they are in jail and cut off from the professionals. Joseph's reply sidesteps the whole profession: pitronim are God's, not a technician's. It is also, quietly, the sharpest thing he could have said about himself. This is the boy whose own dreams got him thrown in a pit and who has not mentioned a dream since. He does not claim the gift; he assigns it upward and then asks to hear.

Verses 9–15 · the vine, and the favour he asks for

A dream in fast-forward. The vine buds, blossoms and ripens in a single sentence — budding, blossom, clusters — which is agriculturally impossible and exactly how dreams work. The cupbearer's hands are doing his old job throughout: he takes, he presses, he places the cup on Pharaoh's palm. The dream is a man rehearsing his own restoration, and the detail that gives Joseph the reading is the number three.

"Pharaoh will lift up your head." Here is the phrase the whole chapter is built on — nasa rosh, "to lift the head." It is a real court idiom meaning to single a man out of the crowd for notice: to review him, to reckon him, to raise him up. Applied to the cupbearer it means pardon and promotion. Hold on to it; it comes back twice, and it does not mean the same thing either time. The KJV and ASV both keep it literally ("shall Pharaoh lift up thine head"), which is the right call and preserves the pun; the NIV resolves it to "will lift up your head and restore you," and the TLB paraphrases the idiom away entirely — losing the machinery of verse 19.

The one thing he asks for. Joseph interprets for free and then makes a request, and the word he uses for it is chesed — "do kindness with me." It is the covenant word, the loyal, faithful sort of kindness — and the last time it appeared, two verses from the end of the previous chapter, it was God extending it to Joseph in this same jail (39:21). Joseph now asks a man for what God has been giving him. The chapter's final verse is the answer.

"I was stolen — stolen away." Gunnov gunnavti, an infinitive absolute doubled for emphasis, and a startling choice of verb. Joseph does not say "my brothers sold me." He says he was stolen — ganav, the theft word, which when the object is a human being names a capital crime in the law that is coming ("whoever steals a man… shall surely be put to death," Exodus 21:16). He states the crime precisely and omits the criminals entirely. Whether that is discretion, family loyalty, or simply what it honestly felt like from inside a caravan, Genesis does not say — but it is the only time Joseph ever refers to the sale before he reveals himself, and he does it without naming a single brother.

"They should put me in the pit." And here the versions bury something. The word is bor — the identical Hebrew noun used of the dry cistern his brothers threw him into at Dothan (37:24, "the pit was empty; there was no water in it"). Joseph is describing an Egyptian jail with the same word he would use for that hole in the ground, which is either the plainest possible statement of how his life looks from the inside, or the sharpest. But the KJV, ASV and NIV all read "dungeon" here while reading "pit" back in chapter 37 — so the English reader cannot hear that Joseph has been in this hole before. This translation renders both "pit."

Verses 16–19 · the same words, inside out

He only speaks because the first answer was good. "And the chief of the bakers saw that he had interpreted well" — the narrator tells us the man's motive before he opens his mouth. He is not seeking truth; he heard a favourable verdict and would like one too. It is a very ordinary human thing and Genesis records it without comment, one verse before the worst news in the chapter.

Three baskets of something. Salei chori — the second word is uncertain. Traditionally it is derived from a root for "white," giving the KJV's "three white baskets" (and, following the same instinct, "white bread"); others connect it to holes, giving wicker or openwork baskets; the NIV settles on "three baskets of bread." Nobody is certain. This translation reads "white bread" with the doubt noted here — the detail is not load-bearing, and the number is.

Now watch the idiom turn. Joseph gives the baker the identical opening — "in three days more Pharaoh will lift up your head" — and then adds two words: from off you. The court phrase for being singled out for honour becomes, with a preposition, a sentence of decapitation, and the second half of the verse confirms it: hanged on a tree, the birds eating his flesh. It is one of the cruellest and most brilliant turns in the Hebrew Bible, and it only works because the two interpretations are worded the same way up to that point. The birds the baker saw eating from the basket on his head are the same birds, doing the same thing, in the reading.

Hanged on a tree. Ancient practice here is almost certainly impalement or the public display of a corpse after execution rather than hanging in the modern sense. The phrase acquires a long afterlife: Deuteronomy legislates that a man "hanged on a tree" must be taken down before nightfall, "for a hanged man is a curse of God" (21:22-23) — a text Paul later quotes in an argument about the crucifixion (Galatians 3:13). Genesis itself is doing none of that. It is reporting an Egyptian execution, three days out, correctly predicted.

Verses 20–22 · one phrase, two men, two fates

The birthday. The first birthday celebration mentioned in the Bible belongs to a pharaoh, and it is the occasion for a prisoner amnesty and an execution on the same afternoon — which is roughly how royal birthdays worked. (The only other birthday party the Bible records is Herod's, and a man loses his head at that one too: Matthew 14:6-10. The pattern was not lost on later readers.) The KJV says plainly "Pharaoh's birthday," as do all the versions; the Hebrew is a slightly odd construction, literally "the day of giving birth to Pharaoh."

And then the sentence lands on both of them at once. Verse 20 is the payoff, and in Hebrew it is a single verb governing two objects: Pharaoh lifted up the head of the chief cupbearer and the head of the chief baker. One phrase. Two men. One is lifted back to his cup; the other is lifted off his shoulders. The narrator does not explain the wordplay or apologize for it — he simply uses the idiom in the way that includes both fates, and lets the next two verses sort out which man got which. Every English version that renders the idiom literally (the KJV and ASV do) keeps this; every version that smooths it into "summoned" or "sent for" loses the entire architecture of the chapter.

"Just as Joseph had interpreted to them." The narrator's only editorial comment, and it is about accuracy. Both readings were right, including the one nobody wanted. Joseph told a man he would die in three days, to his face, having been asked; there is no record of him softening it.

Verse 23 · the verb that fails

"Did not remember… and forgot him." The chapter ends on a doubled negative that is not redundant but insistent: he did not remember, and he forgot. And zakhar is not a neutral word in this book. It is the covenant verb — "God remembered Noah" and the waters went down (8:1); "God remembered Abraham" and Lot was pulled out (19:29); "God remembered Rachel" and Joseph himself was born (30:22). In Hebrew it never means merely "recalled"; it means acted on behalf of. Every time God has remembered someone in Genesis, a rescue followed. Here the verb is put in a man's hands, with a favour attached, and it fails. That is the chapter's real ending: not cruelty, just ordinary human forgetfulness by a man who got his job back and had a lot on.

Two years. Genesis does not say how long the silence lasts until the very next verse of the next chapter — "at the end of two full years" (41:1). The reader is not told that at the time. Verse 23 simply closes, and the effect is deliberate: the man who can read other men's futures is left with no reading of his own. It is the third time Joseph has been in a hole and the third time someone has walked away holding something of his — a coat, a garment, and now a promise. When the cupbearer finally does speak, the first words out of his mouth are "I remember my faults today" (41:9), and by then Joseph is thirty years old.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the words exact / distinct: "lift up your head" kept literally in all three places (vv. 13, 19, 20), because the chapter's whole architecture is one court idiom used for a pardon, an execution, and both at once; "pit" for bor in v. 15 — the same English as 37:24, where the versions switch to "dungeon" and hide the fact that Joseph has been in this hole before; "cupbearer" for mashqeh rather than the KJV's now-comic "butler"; "stolen — stolen away" for the doubled gunnov gunnavti; "your faces are bad" left blunt; and "kindness" for chesed, continuous with 39:21.

Echoes paid. Chesed — extended by God to Joseph in the same jail one chapter ago, now requested from a man, and refused by forgetfulness; bor, the pit of 37:24, reused by Joseph for his cell; dreams, absent since his brothers threw him in that pit for having them (37:5-11), returning as someone else's and as the thing that will lift him out; the land of the Hebrews, naming Canaan by the outsider-word last heard of Abram; and the chief of the executioners still, quietly, putting Joseph in charge of things.

Echoes planted. A cupbearer who will remember two years too late — "I remember my faults today" (41:9); Pharaoh's own pair of dreams, and an Egyptian court that again has no interpreter (41:8); "it is not in me — God will answer" (41:16), Joseph saying verse 8 again in front of a king; and zakhar, the remembering that always precedes a rescue in this book, waiting to be done properly.

Next installment in this book: Genesis 41 — two years later Pharaoh dreams of seven fat cows and seven gaunt ones, and no one in Egypt can read it; the cupbearer finally remembers; and a thirty-year-old Hebrew prisoner is shaved, dressed, and brought from the pit to the throne room to say "it is not in me."
