A country prophet from the Shephelah watches the storm-God come down and melt the mountains, hears the sin named as the two capital cities themselves, and mourns his own home region town by town as the Assyrian invasion rolls through it.
c. 730–700 BC — Micah's heading dates him by three kings of Judah (Jotham, Ahaz, Hezekiah), making him the exact contemporary of Isaiah in Jerusalem. The chapter's horizon spans the two great blows of the age: Samaria's fall to Assyria in 722 (vv6-7) and Sennacherib's invasion of the Judean Shephelah in 701 (the town lament, vv10-16, and 'the wound has reached the gate of Jerusalem,' v9). Jeremiah 26:18 fixes his preaching 'in the days of Hezekiah,' the only prophet the Hebrew Bible quotes by name.

Translator’s Notes

Verse 1 · the country prophet who out-lived his own prophecy — and once saved another prophet’s life

⚠ Micah is the fourth eighth-century prophet, and the pair to Isaiah. His heading dates him by three kings of Judah — Jotham, Ahaz, Hezekiah, roughly 740–700 BC — which makes him the exact contemporary of Isaiah in the same city, and a slightly later contemporary of Amos and Hosea in the north. But where Isaiah moves in the royal court, Micah is a countryman: he is “of Moresheth,” a farming village in the Shephelah about twenty-five miles south-west of Jerusalem, and he speaks for the rural poor being ground up by the capital’s land-grabbing elite (2:1–2). Isaiah and Micah are the same message from opposite ends of society — and they even share a poem, the swords-into-plowshares vision that stands in both books (Micah 4:1–3; Isaiah 2:1–4).

The name is a question. Mikhah is a shortened form of Mikha-yahu, “Who is like Jehovah?” — and the book will end (7:18) by answering it: “Who is a God like you, pardoning iniquity?” The whole book is bracketed by its author’s own name.

⚠ And Micah has a distinction no other prophet has: he is quoted, BY NAME, by a later prophet — and the quotation once saved a life. A century after Micah, when Jeremiah was on trial for his life for predicting the temple’s destruction, the elders rose to defend him by citing precedent: “Micah of Moresheth prophesied in the days of Hezekiah … ‘Zion will be plowed as a field’ … Did Hezekiah put him to death? Did he not fear Jehovah … and did not Jehovah relent?” (Jeremiah 26:18–19, quoting Micah 3:12). It is the ONLY place in the Hebrew Bible where one writing prophet is named and quoted by another — and it tells us something the book does not: that Micah’s preaching actually moved a king to repent, and that people remembered it for a hundred years.

⚠ The vision is “concerning Samaria AND Jerusalem” — both capitals at once. Micah indicts north and south together, and he will identify each kingdom’s sin with its capital city by name (v5). He is a southern prophet, but he opens by watching the northern capital fall and warning that the same wound is travelling south.

Verses 2–5 · God comes down, the mountains melt — and the crime turns out to be the two capitals

The book opens as a COURTROOM, not a sermon. “Hear, all you peoples! Listen, earth and all that fills it!” — heaven and earth summoned as witnesses, exactly as in Isaiah 1:2 and Deuteronomy’s covenant lawsuit — and “let the Lord Jehovah be a witness against you.” The God who is about to be described in cosmic terms first takes the stand as a prosecuting witness.

⚠ Then one of the great THEOPHANIES of the Bible: God comes DOWN, and the earth cannot bear it. “Jehovah is coming out of his place; he comes down and treads upon the high places of the earth. The mountains melt under him … like wax before the fire, like waters poured down a slope.” This is the storm-God of Sinai (Exodus 19), of Deborah’s song (“the mountains quaked before Jehovah,” Judges 5:5), of Psalm 18 and Habakkuk 3 — the Lord descending in fire so that solid rock runs like candle-wax and valleys split like a hillside sluiced with water. KJV “the mountains shall be molten under him … as wax before the fire”; ASV “the mountains shall be melted under him, and the valleys shall be cleft”; NWT “the mountains will melt under him, and the valleys will split apart, like wax before the fire.” ⚠ There is an irony built into the verb: God treads the high places (bamot) — and bamot is also the word for the illicit hilltop shrines. He tramples the very high places the next verses will name as the crime.

⚠ And then the astonishing identification: the SIN is the two capital CITIES. “All this is for the revolt of Jacob … What IS the revolt of Jacob — is it not Samaria? And what are the high places of Judah — are they not Jerusalem?” ⚠ Micah does not say the sin is in the cities; he says the sin is the cities. The seats of power — the palace, the temple establishment, the whole apparatus of the capital — are themselves the transgression. Pesha, the same word Amos used for the nations’ rebellion, is here turned on Israel’s own two thrones; and “the high places of Judah” being “Jerusalem” is a shocking equation — the holy city named in the same breath as a pagan shrine. NWT renders the parallel questions crisply — “What is the revolt of Jacob? Is it not Samaria? And what are the high places of Judah? Are they not Jerusalem?” — and NIV softens “high places” to the interpretive “What is Judah’s high place? Is it not Jerusalem?”, spelling out the shrine sense the Hebrew leaves as a bare noun.

Verses 6–7 · a city poured down its own hill, and the wages of a harlot

⚠ The sentence on Samaria is weirdly specific — and the archaeology fits it. “I will make Samaria a heap in the field … I will POUR her stones down into the valley and lay bare her foundations.” Samaria was built on an isolated hill, its great terraces and palace walls rising in courses of dressed stone; to “pour the stones into the valley” is exactly what happens when a hilltop city is demolished and its masonry tumbles down the slopes — which is what the excavated ruins of Omri’s and Ahab’s Samaria look like. The city becomes farmland: “a place for planting vineyards.” Assyria took Samaria in 722 BC. KJV “I will make Samaria as an heap of the field … and I will pour down the stones thereof into the valley”; ASV the same; NWT “a heap of ruins in the field, a place for planting a vineyard.”

⚠ «A harlot’s wage» is idolatry described as prostitution — and there is a bitter economics in it. The carved images are smashed, and “all her harlot’s wages (etnan) burned.” Etnan is the specific word for a prostitute’s FEE (Deuteronomy 23:18 forbids bringing it into the temple). Two things run together: the prophets routinely call idolatry “whoring” after other gods; and the wealth that gilded Samaria’s idols had been gathered from cult-prostitution offerings and tribute. So Micah closes the circle with grim symmetry: “from a harlot’s wage she gathered them, and to a harlot’s wage they will return” — the plundered gold of Samaria’s idols will end up in the temples (and the brothels) of Assyria, where it started. NIV “since she gathered her gifts from the wages of prostitutes, as the wages of prostitutes they will again be used.”

Verses 8–9 · the prophet strips and howls — and a famous KJV zoo that isn’t there

Micah turns the funeral onto himself. “For this I will lament and wail; I will go stripped and naked” — the prophet performs the grief, walking barefoot and bared like a prisoner of war or a mourner, a piece of living street-theatre (Isaiah does the same, going naked three years, Isaiah 20). The point is that the disaster is worth a body thrown into mourning.

⚠ And here is one of the shelf’s most charming mistranslations. Micah says he will wail “like the tannim” and mourn “like the benot ya’anah.” Tannim are jackals — the desert dogs whose long, rising, human-sounding howl is the classic sound of a ruin at night — and benot ya’anah (“daughters of greed / of the wilderness”) are ostriches, whose hollow booming moan carries for miles. ASV gets both right: “a wailing like the jackals, and a lamentation like the ostriches.” But KJV, guessing at two rare animal names, produced “a wailing like the dragons, and mourning as the owls” — dragons and owls, from jackals and ostriches. The right animals are the right image: the noises a wrecked and empty place actually makes after dark.

⚠ Verse 9 is the hinge of the chapter: the wound moves SOUTH. “Her wound is incurable; it has come even to Judah, it has reached the gate of my people, even to Jerusalem.” The blow that fell on Samaria in 722 does not stop at the border; the same Assyrian tide will roll down and lap at Jerusalem’s own gate — which is exactly what Sennacherib did in 701, overrunning the whole Shephelah and shutting Hezekiah up “like a bird in a cage.” And the towns that tide is about to swallow are Micah’s own neighbours, which is why the next seven verses are so personal.

Verses 10–15 · the town-by-town lament — the most untranslatable passage in the prophets

⚠ Here Micah does something no translation can carry: he mourns his home region town by town, and turns each town’s NAME into a pun on its FATE. These are real villages of the Shephelah — Micah’s own countryside — and the passage traces, roughly, the path of Sennacherib’s invasion through them. To an ancient ear a name was not just a label but a little prophecy of a thing’s character; Micah plays that belief for all its grief is worth. We keep the town names and gloss each pun, because levelling them to plain English would erase the entire point of the passage. The chain:

«In GATH, tell it not» — b’Gat al-tagidu, Gat chiming with tagidu, “tell”; and it is a deliberate quotation of David’s lament over Saul, “tell it not in Gath” (2 Samuel 1:20) — don’t let the enemy gloat. «In Weeping, weep not» (the Hebrew of this clause is damaged). «In BETH-LE-APHRAH — House of Dust — roll in the DUST» (aphrah / aphar): a town whose very name is “dust” is told to wallow in dust, the mourner’s rite.

«Pass on, inhabitant of SHAPHIR — Fair-town — in nakedness and shame»: the “beautiful” town goes stripped. «The inhabitant of ZAANAN — Going-out — does not come out» (Tsa’anan / yatsa’ah): the town whose name means “march out” cannot march. BETH-EZEL (“house beside / support”) loses its standing-place — the neighbour you leaned on is gone. «MAROTH — Bitterness — waits for GOOD, but EVIL came down from Jehovah» (Marot / mar): Bitter-town hopes for sweet and gets bitter.

«Harness the chariot to the steeds, inhabitant of LACHISH!» — Lakhish punning on la-rekhesh, “to the steeds”: the great fortress city is told to hitch up and flee. And it gets the sharpest line: “she was the beginning of sin for Daughter Zion” — Lachish, the second city of Judah, is charged with importing the north’s idolatry and chariot-trust into the south. ⚠ Lachish is where the archaeology is loudest: Sennacherib carved his siege of it across a whole room of his palace at Nineveh (the Lachish reliefs, now in the British Museum), the only biblical battle we can look at through the conqueror’s own eyes.

«You will give parting gifts to MORESHETH-GATH» — and this one is Micah’s OWN home town. Moresheth chimes with me’orasah, “betrothed,” and shilluchim, “parting gifts,” is a bride’s dowry-send-off: Micah’s village is handed over to the enemy like a girl given away in marriage — the prophet mourns his own home by name. «The houses of ACHZIB — Deceit-town — will be a deception (akzab) to the kings of Israel»: Akhziv puns on akzab, a “deceptive brook” that dries up in summer just when you need it — a town that will fail its kings like a dry streambed.

«I will bring the DISPOSSESSOR upon you, inhabitant of MARESHAH» — Mareshah punning on yoresh, “heir / one who takes possession”: Possession-town is repossessed. And the chain ends: “to ADULLAM the glory of Israel will come” — Adullam is the cave where the fugitive David hid from Saul (1 Samuel 22:1). The “glory of Israel” — its nobility, its leadership — will end up cowering in a cave like an outlaw. The country’s greatness runs backward, all the way to David-on-the-run.

Verse 16 · shave your head, mother — your children are gone

The chapter closes by addressing the land as a mother whose children have been carried off. “Make yourself bald and shave your head over the children of your delight” — shaving the head was a mourning rite (however much Deuteronomy 14:1 disapproved of it), and the “children of your delight” are the population of the Shephelah being marched into exile by the Assyrians. Sennacherib’s own annals boast of deporting 200,150 people from Judah’s towns in 701 — the human freight behind this verse.

⚠ «Make your baldness wide as the vulture’s» — the nesher is the great griffon-vulture of the crags, and the point is precise: the griffon-vulture has a bare, down-covered head that looks shaved. Mourn until your whole scalp is as bald as that carrion bird’s — “for they have gone from you into exile.” KJV and ASV both render nesher “eagle” here (“enlarge thy baldness as the eagle”), but the eagle is not bald — the image only works for the bare-headed griffon-vulture, which is what the word must mean in this verse; NIV keeps “eagle” too, footnoting “or vulture.” The same soaring bird that could not save Edom (Obadiah 4) here supplies the image of a mother-city’s shaved head of grief. ⚠ The chapter ends on the word galu, “gone into exile” — the fate the rest of the book will spend six chapters answering, until the very last line: “Who is a God like you?”

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the exact / distinct words: “revolt” for pesha (v5), the same rebellion-word Amos aimed at the nations, here turned on Israel’s own two capitals; the town names kept and each pun glossed in verses 10–15 — Dust-town rolling in dust, Fair-town stripped bare, Going-out that cannot go out, Bitter-town waiting for sweet, Deceit-town proving a deception — because flattening them to plain English erases the whole passage; “jackals” and “ostriches” for tannim and benot ya’anah (v8), the real ruin-animals, against the KJV’s “dragons and owls”; “a harlot’s wage” for etnan (v7), the specific prostitute’s fee, kept literal so the grim economics shows; and “the vulture” for nesher (v16), the bare-headed griffon whose shaved look is the image of grief.

Echoes paid. The covenant lawsuit that opened Isaiah 1 — heaven and earth summoned as witnesses — opens Micah too. Pesha, the “revolt” of the nations in Amos 1, turned on Jerusalem and Samaria. “Tell it not in Gath,” David’s lament over Saul (2 Samuel 1:20), quoted into a lament over Judah. Adullam, David’s outlaw cave (1 Samuel 22), now the refuge of a ruined nobility. And the griffon-vulture of Obadiah 4, its bare head made an emblem of mourning.

Echoes planted. “Who is like Jehovah?” — the meaning of Micah’s own name, which the book will answer in its last verse (7:18, “Who is a God like you, pardoning iniquity?”). Zion plowed as a field (3:12) — the line a century of memory kept, quoted by name to save Jeremiah’s life (Jeremiah 26:18). Swords into plowshares (4:3), the peace-vision Micah shares with Isaiah 2. Bethlehem (5:2), the little town from which a ruler will come — the verse the wise men are given in Matthew 2. And “what does Jehovah require of you but to do justice, to love mercy, and to walk humbly with your God” (6:8), the sentence for which the whole book is remembered.
