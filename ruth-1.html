Translator’s Notes

Verses 1–5 · a story told against the Judges

«In the days when the judges judged.» The opening line pins the book to the era of the Judges — and Ruth is its deliberate counter-story. Where Judges spirals down into idolatry, civil war, and a Levite’s concubine cut into twelve pieces, its refrain “everyone did what was right in his own eyes,” Ruth tells one small, quiet story of chesed (loyal love) among ordinary people in that same dark time. It is the book that says the covenant survived not in the judges’ battles but in a widow, a foreigner, and a field. The Hebrew is literally “in the days of the judging of the judges,” a play on the root the English can only approximate. KJV and ASV “when the judges ruled.”

A famine in the House of Bread. The town’s name is Beth-lechem, “House of Bread” — and it has no bread. A man of the House of Bread leaves the land of promise to sojourn in Moab, the country east of the Dead Sea whose people descend from Lot’s daughters (Genesis 19:37) and who once hired Balaam to curse Israel (Numbers 22). The narrator states the move without a verdict; later readers hear a failure of faith in it (the leading family abandoning its starving town), but the text keeps its own counsel. It simply notes that the House of Bread emptied, and a family went hungry into old enemy ground.

⚠ Names as omens. Hebrew narrative loves a loaded name. Elimelech is “My God is King” (a heavy name for a man who dies in exile); Naomi is “my pleasant one, sweetness”; and the two sons — Mahlon and Chilion — carry roots that sound like “sickness / weakness” (chalah) and “wasting, coming to an end” (kalah). The etymologies are uncertain, likely folk-etymological, but the storyteller lets them ring: the sweet woman and her two failing sons. Within five verses all three men are dead and “the woman was left” — the verb of a bare survivor, sounded twice (vv3, 5), stripping her of husband and then of children until only she remains.

Verses 6–9 · the road home, and a prayer of chesed

«Jehovah had visited his people.» The turn comes on a rumour: paqad, the visitation verb — the same word used when Jehovah “visited” Sarah and gave her Isaac (Genesis 21:1), and would “visit” his people in Egypt (Exodus 3:16). He has visited to give bread; the House of Bread is filling again, and Naomi turns for home.

«May Jehovah deal kindly with you.» Here is the book’s key word, and it falls first from Naomi’s mouth over her two Gentile daughters-in-law: chesed — loyal, covenant love, kindness that keeps faith past all obligation. It runs the length of the book (1:8; 2:20; 3:10), Jehovah’s chesed and human chesed answering each other. Naomi has nothing left to give these women, so she gives them a blessing and lets them go, praying that the God of Israel will show them the loyalty they showed her dead sons. KJV and ESV “deal kindly”; the NWT “loyal love.”

«That you may find rest.» Menuchah — rest, a settled security; verse 9 spells out what she means, “in the house of her husband.” It is the one thing Naomi cannot give, and the very thing the whole book will circle back to place in Ruth’s hands at the end.

Verses 10–13 · Naomi’s argument, and the hand of the LORD

The levirate math. Naomi’s reply is built on the custom of levirate marriage — a dead man’s brother takes his widow to raise up an heir in his name (later codified in Deuteronomy 25:5–10, and the engine of the whole book’s ending). She presses it to the point of absurdity: even if she married tonight and bore sons, would these young women wait years for infants to grow up? She has no more sons to give, so no claim binds them to her. It is a self-emptying argument — a mother-in-law reasoning her own daughters-in-law free of every duty.

«The hand of Jehovah has gone out against me.» Naomi names God as the author of her grief without flinching — a note she will sound again at Mara. This is not the language of doubt but of an Israelite who assumes Jehovah’s hand in everything, the sweet and the bitter alike. KJV “the hand of the LORD is gone out against me.”

Verses 14–18 · Orpah’s kiss, Ruth’s clinging — and the great oath

Two verbs, two destinies. Orpah kisses her mother-in-law and turns back; Ruth clings to her. The clinging verb is dabaq — the very word of Genesis 2:24, where a man “clings” to his wife and the two become one flesh. Ruth’s loyalty to Naomi is written in marriage language: she cleaves to her as a spouse cleaves. ⚠ Orpah is not blamed — Naomi urged her to go, and she went home to her people and her gods, the reasonable choice. The book sets the reasonable choice beside the extravagant one and lets you feel the whole distance between them.

«Your people my people, your God my God.» The most famous words in the book, and one of the great confessions in the Bible — spoken by a Moabite. Ruth binds herself to Naomi’s road, lodging, people, God, death, and grave, and seals it with a self-imprecation using the covenant name: “May Jehovah do so to me, and more also” — the oath-formula of an Israelite, sworn by Jehovah by a woman of Moab. ⚠ This cuts hard against Deuteronomy 23:3, “no Moabite shall enter the assembly of Jehovah, even to the tenth generation.” The book’s whole quiet argument is here: covenant belonging is won by chesed and faith, not by blood — and this Moabite convert becomes the great-grandmother of David (4:17) and, in Matthew’s genealogy, an ancestress of Jesus (Matthew 1:5). Many read Ruth as written or kept as a gentle protest against the expulsion of foreign wives under Ezra and Nehemiah; the dating is debated, the argument unmistakable. KJV “whither thou goest, I will go… thy people shall be my people, and thy God my God.”

«She stopped speaking to her.» Naomi’s silence is defeat and consent at once. There is nothing to say to a loyalty that will not be argued down.

Verses 19–22 · Mara — full, and empty

«Is this Naomi?» The whole town is stirred (a strong verb, the ground fairly humming); the women barely know the one who left. And Naomi answers with a pun on her own name: do not call me Naomi (“sweet”) — call me Mara (“bitter”), for Shaddai, the Almighty, has dealt bitterly (hemar) with me. In her grief she reaches past the covenant name to the old patriarchal name of God, El Shaddai (Genesis 17:1), the God of Abraham’s hard promises. KJV and the NWT “the Almighty.”

«I went out full, and Jehovah has brought me back empty.» Her theology is honest and, the narrator gently shows, not quite true. She calls herself empty — but Ruth is standing right beside her, and the very next verse names her again: “and Ruth the Moabite, her daughter-in-law, with her.” Naomi cannot yet see that the emptiness she mourns is holding the redemption of the whole book.

«At the beginning of the barley harvest.» The last line turns the key. The famine that opened the book is over; the fields around the House of Bread stand white for harvest; and into a barley field, next chapter, will walk a man named Boaz. The emptying is finished; the filling is about to begin.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the exact / distinct words: “in the days when the judges judged” keeping the root-play that ties the book to Judges; “the woman was left” for the survivor’s verb sounded twice (vv3, 5); “deal kindly” / “loyal love” for chesed, the book’s theme word, kept the same wherever it recurs; “rest” for menuchah, the settled security of a home; “clung to her” for dabaq, the Genesis-2:24 marriage verb; “Mara” kept untranslated so the hemar pun on “bitter” can land; and “the Almighty” for Shaddai, the old patriarchal name Naomi reaches for.

Echoes paid. The famine that empties the House of Bread; paqad — Jehovah “visited” his people to give bread, the verb of Sarah (Genesis 21:1) and the Exodus (Exodus 3:16); Moab traced back to Lot’s daughters (Genesis 19:37) and Balaam (Numbers 22); the levirate custom (Deuteronomy 25:5–10) argued to absurdity by Naomi and fulfilled by Boaz four chapters on; and Deuteronomy 23:3’s ban on Moabites set against a Moabite who out-loves Israel.

Echoes planted. Chesed, sounded first here and again at 2:20 and 3:10; rest (menuchah), the thing Naomi cannot give and Boaz will; the barley harvest, the field Ruth walks into next chapter; the emptiness Naomi mourns while the redemption of the book stands beside her; and Ruth the Moabite herself, on the road to becoming the great-grandmother of David (4:17) and an ancestress of Jesus (Matthew 1:5).

Next in this book: Ruth 2 — Ruth goes out to glean and “happens” upon the field of Boaz, a man of standing and a kinsman of Elimelech; his kindness to a foreign widow, the first stirring of chesed answered, and Naomi’s first flicker of hope: “he is one of our redeemers.”
