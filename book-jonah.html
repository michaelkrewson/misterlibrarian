📖 Jonah

יוֹנָה Yonah — 'Dove' — and the prophet is 'Jonah SON OF AMITTAI,' amittai being built on emet, 'truth, faithfulness.' So the runaway's full name reads, with a straight face, 'Dove, son of Faithfulness.' ⚠ The dove is also the bird Noah sent out over the flood-waters and the emblem of Israel in the Song and the Psalms — apt for a book that is all sea, and about the one Israelite who would not fly where he was sent.
Ἰωνᾶς (Iōnas) — The Septuagint keeps Jonah sixth among the Twelve. ⚠ The name comes into the Gospels as 'the sign of Jonah': Jesus tells his generation that no sign will be given it 'except the sign of Jonah the prophet' — three days and nights in the fish, read as his own burial and rising, and the repentance of Nineveh held up as a rebuke (Matthew 12:39-41; 16:4; Luke 11:29-32). The Vulgate and Douay call the book JONAS.

The odd one out of the Twelve: not a book of oracles but a STORY, and the only prophetic book whose prophet disobeys. Told to go east and warn Nineveh, the great enemy capital, Jonah runs west to the ends of the sea — and is out-prayed by pagan sailors, swallowed by a great fish, out-repented by a whole heathen city, and left at the end furious that God was merciful. A four-chapter argument, in narrative, against the idea that God's compassion stops at Israel's border.

Chapter by chapter

Jonah 1The odd one out of the Twelve: not a book of oracles but a STORY, and the only prophetic book whose prophet DISOBEYS. Told to go east and cry against Nineveh — the great enemy capital, the future destroyer of Israel — Jonah runs west to the ends of the sea, boarding a ship at Joppa “away from before Jehovah.” God hurls a storm; the pagan sailors pray, cast lots, and beg Jonah's God for mercy while Jonah sleeps in the hold, and when they finally throw him overboard (rowing hard first, to try to save him — they are more merciful than the prophet), they end up fearing Jehovah and offering sacrifice. The chapter is charted by one word — DOWN: down to Joppa, down into the ship, down into the hold — and built on one irony: “I fear Jehovah, who made the sea and the dry land,” says the man fleeing across the sea. ⚠ And the famous fish is NOT here — it is Hebrew 2:1, which English Bibles renumber 1:17; this site follows the Masoretic count, and it is a “great fish,” not a whale.

About the book

Where it sits
FIFTH of THE TWELVE in the Hebrew order (after Obadiah, before Micah), which the Hebrew canon counts as a single book. Four short chapters. ⚠ A versification offset to watch: the famous 'great fish' verse is Hebrew 2:1, which English Bibles renumber as 1:17 (so English chapter 1 has 17 verses, Hebrew chapter 1 has 16, and the fish opens chapter 2). This site follows the Masoretic numbering throughout. In the synagogue Jonah is read in its entirety on the afternoon of Yom Kippur, the Day of Atonement — the book about second chances and God's readiness to relent, read on the day that turns on exactly those.
Genre
PROSE NARRATIVE — the great exception among the Twelve, which are otherwise books of poetic oracle. Jonah contains exactly one line of preaching (five Hebrew words, 3:4) and is otherwise pure story: flight, storm, fish, city, plant. ⚠ Its genre is the book's largest debate. It reads to many as a deliberate SATIRE or PARABLE — everything is oversized and slightly absurd (a three-days'-walk city, a king decreeing that even the cattle fast in sackcloth, a prophet furious at success), the prophet is the anti-hero, and the point is aimed at the reader, not at Nineveh. The traditional reading takes it as HISTORY (the events really happened). The library gives both and does not vote — but notes that whichever it is, the book is TEACHING, and its lesson is the same.
Author
Unknown, and almost certainly NOT the prophet it is about. The book speaks of Jonah in the third person as a figure of the past, and the historical Jonah son of Amittai (2 Kings 14:25) is an 8th-century northern prophet, while the book's Hebrew, its outlook, and its portrait of Nineveh as a distant legend point most scholars to a POST-EXILIC author (5th-4th c.) writing a pointed tale under the old prophet's name. The traditional view assigns it to Jonah himself in the 8th century. Either way the author is a master of compact, ironic storytelling — every verb chosen, every repetition deliberate.
Date written
⚠ TWO dates that do not conflict. THE PROPHET is 8th-century: 2 Kings 14:25 sets Jonah son of Amittai under Jeroboam II (c. 780 BC), a contemporary of Amos and Hosea. THE BOOK is most often dated centuries later, after the exile — its concern is not Assyria (long gone by then) but a narrow, us-only nationalism the returned community needed to unlearn, and Nineveh (which fell in 612 BC) is treated as a distant, almost fabulous 'great city.' A minority holds to 8th-century composition. So: an old northern prophet, and a later book that borrows his name to argue with his kind of religion.
Place
The SEA and the road east. Jonah is called to NINEVEH, the Assyrian capital on the Tigris (opposite modern Mosul); he flees toward TARSHISH, the far western seaport (probably Tartessos in Spain), going down to JOPPA (Jaffa) to take ship. His home, per 2 Kings, was GATH-HEPHER in Galilee, near the later Nazareth. So the book stretches from one edge of the known world to the other, and its real setting is the gap between where God sends the prophet and where the prophet will go.
Audience
Israel — and specifically the part of Israel that assumed God's mercy was its private possession. The book is written for readers who would cheer Nineveh's destruction, and it works by making the reader take Jonah's side and then discover, in the last chapter, that Jonah's side is indefensible. Its closing question — 'and should I not have pity on Nineveh, that great city?' — is left hanging, addressed as much to the reader as to the prophet.

How it's laid out

1THE FLIGHT AND THE STORM — Jonah runs from his commission by sea; God hurls a storm; pagan sailors pray, cast lots, and at last (reluctantly, mercifully) throw him overboard, and the sea calms. They worship the God Jonah was fleeing. (The great fish 'appoints' at the chapter's very end, Hebrew 2:1.)
2THE PRAYER FROM THE FISH — a psalm of thanksgiving from the belly of the deep: 'I went down to the roots of the mountains… and you brought up my life from the pit.' The fish vomits him onto dry land. The descent finally reverses.
3NINEVEH REPENTS — recommissioned, Jonah walks in and preaches one line, 'forty days more and Nineveh is overthrown,' and the entire city, king and cattle included, turns in sackcloth. 'And God relented of the evil he had said he would do to them.'
4THE ARGUMENT ON THE HILL — Jonah, furious that God was merciful, asks to die. God appoints a plant, a worm and a wind to teach him, and the book ends not with the prophet's answer but with God's unanswered question: 'should I not have pity on that great city, with more than 120,000 people who do not know their right hand from their left — and also much cattle?'

What it's about

  • GOD'S MERCY REACHES THE ENEMY — the scandal the whole book is built on. Nineveh is Assyria, Israel's future destroyer, and God spares it; the pagan sailors of chapter 1 are converted before a word is preached to them. The book insists that divine compassion is not Israel's private property, and that this is precisely what its prophet cannot bear.
  • THE RELUCTANT, RESENTFUL PROPHET — Jonah is the anti-hero of every other prophetic book. Where Isaiah says 'here am I, send me' and Jeremiah is overpowered into speaking, Jonah runs; where other prophets grieve that no one listens, Jonah rages that everyone did. He is a mirror held up to a religion that would rather see its enemies destroyed than saved.
  • 'WHO KNOWS?' — the slim hope of mercy runs as a refrain: the pagan captain's 'perhaps the god will give us a thought' (1:6) and the king of Nineveh's 'who knows? God may turn and relent' (3:9). The book's God is exactly the God they hope for — 'gracious and compassionate, slow to anger, abounding in loyal love, and relenting of evil' (4:2) — which Jonah quotes as a COMPLAINT.
  • DOWN, AND BACK UP — the flight is charted by the verb 'go down': down to Joppa, down into the ship, down into the hold, down into the sea, down to the roots of the mountains. Only the fish reverses it, bringing his life 'up from the pit.' Descent is what fleeing God looks like; resurrection-language is what returning looks like.
  • THE UNANSWERED QUESTION — uniquely, the book ends on a question from God and gives no reply. The reader is left holding it: if God pities a city of the ignorant (and their animals), what does that make of the reader's own wish to see the wicked destroyed? The silence is the point.

The source text

Translated from the pointed Hebrew Masoretic Text (as printed by Mechon-Mamre), following the Hebrew chapter division (so the great fish is 2:1, not 1:17). Jonah's Hebrew is clear, brisk and full of deliberate repetition — 'great,' 'go down,' 'hurl,' 'evil,' 'fear,' 'appoint' — which this translation preserves rather than smoothing into variety, because the repetition is the craft. The one genuinely hard word is BOLES in the historical Amos (not here); Jonah's difficulties are literary, not lexical. The psalm of chapter 2 is older, denser poetry set inside the prose.

Looking forward

Jonah is the Old Testament book Jesus points to most pointedly about himself. ⚠ 'The SIGN OF JONAH' is his answer to those demanding a miracle: 'as Jonah was three days and three nights in the belly of the great fish, so will the Son of Man be three days and three nights in the heart of the earth' (Matthew 12:40) — the fish read as a figure of burial and resurrection. And he twins it with a rebuke: 'the men of Nineveh will rise at the judgment with this generation and condemn it, for they repented at the preaching of Jonah, and look — something greater than Jonah is here' (Matthew 12:41; Luke 11:32). Beyond the sign, the whole book is a Gospel in miniature: mercy extended to outsiders, a reluctant messenger, and a God 'slow to anger and abounding in loyal love' — the very character the New Testament says was made flesh. The rooftop-at-Joppa vision that finally sends Peter to the Gentiles (Acts 10) begins from Jonah's own port, and is the book's argument carried into the church.

Where the debates are

THE GENRE — history, parable, satire, or didactic novella (the largest question, and increasingly answered 'satire/parable' by scholars, though the traditional 'history' reading persists and Jesus references Nineveh's repentance as real). THE DATE — 8th-century (with the prophet) versus post-exilic (the majority for the book). THE FISH — a real event, a symbol of exile-and-return, or a folktale motif; and its odd gender shift (masculine dag in 2:1, feminine dagah in 2:2). WHETHER JONAH'S 'throw me overboard' (1:12) is repentance, self-sacrifice or a death-wish. And the great open ending: does Jonah ever answer God's final question, and does the reader?
