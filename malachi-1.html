Translator’s Notes

Verse 1 · a burden, a hand — and a prophet who may not have a name

«A burden.» The book opens on one word, massa, and the shelf cannot agree what it means. It is built on nasa, “to lift” — so either a load lifted onto the shoulders (a burden, something heavy to carry and heavy to hear) or a voice lifted up (an utterance, a pronouncement). Both senses are live in the prophets, and the versions divide along exactly that seam: KJV and ASV “the burden of the word of Jehovah”; NWT “a pronouncement”; NIV “a prophecy.” We keep Mister “a burden” because the root is audible again later in the chapter, where the same verb asks whether anyone will lift up your face (vv 8, 9) — the burden and the lifted face are one word apart.

«By the hand of» (be-yad) is left literal, as ASV and KJV keep it; NIV smooths to “through.” It is the ordinary Hebrew way of saying an intermediary carried the message — which is exactly the question the next word raises.

⚠ Who is Malachi? The name Mal’akhi means simply “my messenger” — the ordinary word mal’akh with a first-person suffix — and it occurs nowhere else in the Bible as a person’s name. That has left an old and genuinely open question, and the readings have real pedigrees:

• A personal name. The plain sense of the Hebrew line, the way every English version prints it, and the ancient Jewish view recorded in the Talmud alongside the alternatives (Megillah 15a). Names of this shape are unremarkable in Hebrew.
• A title, not a name. The Greek Septuagint does not treat it as a name at all: it reads “by the hand of his messenger.” On this reading the book is anonymous and takes its title from 3:1, where God says “behold, I send my messenger (mal’akhi) to prepare the way before me” — the very phrase, and the verse that gives this prophet his afterlife (Mark 1:2).
• Ezra. The Aramaic Targum adds to this verse: “whose name is called Ezra the scribe” — an old identification (Jerome knew it) resting on nothing in the text.

The library prints the name and does not cast a vote. If it is a title, the last prophetic book in the Christian Old Testament is signed by a man who kept his own name out of it.

The shape of the whole book starts here: the DISPUTATION. Six times Malachi runs the same move — God makes a statement, the people answer back with a question, and God answers the question. It begins in the very next verse (“I have loved you” / “in what have you loved us?”) and fires twice more in this chapter (vv 6, 7). Nobody else in the Bible argues like this. It is the sound of a people who have stopped denying God and started cross-examining him.

Verses 2–5 · “Jacob I loved, and Esau I hated”

⚠ The hardest sentence in the book, and it arrives second. Paul quotes it at Romans 9:13 and it has been a battlefield ever since. Two readings, both with long pedigrees, and the library does not vote between them:

• Love and hate as covenant CHOOSING and NOT-choosing. In legal and covenant Hebrew the pair often means preference and rejection rather than emotion: the law of the man with a loved wife and a hated wife (Deuteronomy 21:15–17) is about which son inherits, not about feelings; Jesus’ “if anyone does not hate his own father and mother” (Luke 14:26) uses the same idiom. On this reading Malachi is saying what Genesis said: the younger was chosen, the elder was not.
• Love and hate at full strength. The next clause is not about a preference; it is about razed mountains. Whatever the idiom can mean elsewhere, here it is spelled out as a nation left in rubble — and Paul, quoting the verse, is arguing precisely about God’s freedom to choose and to pass over.

Note what the argument is actually FOR. This is not a lecture on election; it is the answer to “in what have you loved us?” The proof of love offered to a shabby, disappointed, post-exile Judah is that they are still here — and their brother-nation is not.

«Was not Esau Jacob’s brother?» The whole force is in brother. Edom is not some foreign empire; Edom is the twin. Genesis spent a long chapter honoring Esau’s line and settling him peacefully in the red highlands of Seir (Genesis 36), and the two nations have been rubbing against each other ever since. TLB paraphrases the point out of existence (“I have loved you very deeply”); the Hebrew keeps the family word.

«The utterance of Jehovah» (ne’um) is deliberately kept distinct from “says Jehovah” (amar), which runs through the rest of the chapter. They are two different Hebrew words and most versions flatten both into a single “says” formula; ne’um is the solemn, formulaic one — an oracle-marker — and it is used exactly once here, on the sentence about Jacob and Esau.

Edom’s ruin was recent news. The taunt in v4 (“we will return and rebuild”) fits what happened to Edom in the Persian period: pressed out of its highlands by Arab movement from the desert — the people the Nabataeans of Petra would later become — and resettling west into southern Judah, the region Greek writers afterward call Idumea. Malachi’s hearers could look south and see it.

«The territory of wickedness» — gevul, “border, territory,” and it is the hinge into v5, which uses the same word: Edom is the border of wickedness; Jehovah is great beyond the border of Israel. KJV reads the second one as “from the border of Israel” (magnified starting there and spreading), ASV and NIV as “beyond” (great outside it, too). We follow the second, because the chapter goes on to say it twice more: great is the word this chapter is built on (vv 5, 11 twice, 14), and every time it is aimed outward, at the nations.

Verses 6–9 · where is my honor? — and try that on the governor

Honor is a matter of WEIGHT. “A son honors a father” — the verb is kaved, whose plain meaning is heavy; to honor someone is to treat them as having weight, and the noun built on it, kavod, “glory,” is heaviness itself. Readers of this translation have met the root already, doing the opposite job, in Pharaoh’s heavy heart (Exodus 8:11, 28). Against it stands the chapter’s other verb, bazah, “despise” — to treat a thing as of no account. The priests are not accused of atheism. They are accused of finding God slight.

«Where is my fear?» (mora’i) — not terror but the awe owed to a master, and worth marking now, because it is the word the chapter’s last line answers: “my name is feared among the nations” (v14). The honor and the fear withheld at the altar are being paid somewhere else.

«Defiled bread.» The Hebrew is lechem me’go’al. KJV and ASV keep “polluted bread”; NIV generalizes to “defiled food.” We keep bread, because the metaphor governing this whole section is a MEAL: God’s altar is his table (vv 7, 12), the offerings are his bread and his food, and the complaint is that the host is being served leavings. ⚠ A small trap for the English reader: this verb ga’al (“defile”) is spelled exactly like the ga’al that means “redeem”. They are two unrelated roots that happen to look alike — the redeemer-word of Ruth and Boaz has nothing to do with the pollution here.

The animals are named from the law itself. Blind, lame, sick — these are not random defects but the disqualifying list of Leviticus 22:17–25 and Deuteronomy 15:21, which forbid exactly these animals by name. The priests are not being held to an invented standard; they are being read their own rulebook. And the sin is not poverty. Leviticus makes generous provision for the poor (a pair of birds instead of a lamb); what is condemned here is a man who has a sound animal and offers the sick one.

⚠ «Present it, please, to your governor» — the flattest, most devastating line in the chapter, and a historical fingerprint. The word is pechah, a Persian-era administrative title (the office Nehemiah holds, Nehemiah 5:14). Judah has no king; it has a provincial governor answering to Susa. So the challenge is: take that blind animal, walk it up to the Persian administrator as a gift, and see how you are received. “Will he lift up your face?” — the Hebrew idiom for accepting a person, receiving them with favor. KJV keeps it literal (“or accept thy person” in the margin tradition); NIV renders the sense, “would he accept you?” We keep the idiom visible, because the same lifted face returns in the next verse and because the idiom is the ancestor of the Bible’s standard phrase for partiality — which is precisely what Malachi will accuse the priests of two chapters on (2:9).

Verse 9 is sarcasm, and should read as sarcasm. “And now, entreat the face of God, that he may be gracious to us” — it is the priest’s own liturgical language, the blessing-and-intercession business of the office, quoted back at him with the receipts attached: from your own hand has this come. Then the same question again, and the answer is obviously no.

Verses 10–12 · shut the doors — and the pure offering in every place

«Would that someone would shut the doors.» God says he would rather the temple were closed than kept open like this. It is one of the most startling sentences in the prophets, and it belongs to a line that runs through all of them — Isaiah’s “who asked this of you, this trampling of my courts?”, Amos’s “I hate, I despise your festivals” — the insistence that a worship God does not want is not a small thing done imperfectly but a thing he wishes stopped.

⚠ And the shelf splits on WHY, over one word: chinnam, which means both “in vain, for nothing gained” and “for free, without pay.” KJV takes the second and attaches it to the priests’ wages — “neither do ye kindle fire on mine altar for nought,” i.e. you will not lift a finger unpaid. ASV and NIV take the first — the fires are useless. Geneva goes with the wages reading too. Both are real Hebrew and both fit a chapter about a priesthood going through the motions; we print “in vain” and flag the other.

«An offering I will not accept from your hand.» Minchah — the gift-offering, the word used of Cain’s and Abel’s offerings (Genesis 4:3–5) and of the present Jacob sent ahead to Esau (Genesis 32:14) — and ratsah, “accept, be pleased with,” the technical verb of the sacrificial system (Leviticus 1:4: “it shall be accepted for him”). The machinery of acceptance is being turned off at the switch.

⚠ Verse 11 is one of the most disputed verses in the Old Testament — and the dispute is grammatical before it is theological. The Hebrew clause has no verb: “from the rising of the sun to its setting, great my name among the nations.” Hebrew leaves the tense to context, and the context is arguable, so the readings are:

• Present. Right now, everywhere, God’s name is honored better outside Israel than inside it — the reading that makes the sharpest sense of the argument (it is the ground of v10’s refusal), and lands as the most severe thing in the chapter: the nations do it properly and you do not.
• Future / prophetic. A day is coming when the nations will worship the true God. This is the traditional Christian reading, and from very early on the “pure offering” offered “in every place” was read as the Eucharist — explicitly in the Didache (c. AD 100), then in Justin Martyr’s Dialogue with Trypho and in Irenaeus, and much later at the Council of Trent. It is fair to say no verse of Malachi has had a longer afterlife in Christian liturgy.
• The diaspora reading. The scattered Jews of the Persian empire, worshiping faithfully in every place they have been driven to — a whole world of sincere offerings, set against the shabby ones at the one legitimate altar.

The library prints the verse and does not choose. What every reading has in common is the direction of travel: a book that opens by proving God’s love to Israel is, ten verses later, pointing outward.

A quiet detail in v12: the table is called “the table of the Lord” (Adonai) where v7 had “the table of Jehovah.” The Hebrew genuinely changes words; we change with it.

Verses 13–14 · “what a weariness” — and a great King

«Look — what a weariness!» Mattela’ah occurs nowhere else in the Bible; it is built on la’ah, “to be weary,” and it is the sound of a man looking at his job. KJV “Behold, what a weariness is it!” — still the best rendering on the shelf. The complaint is not that God is too demanding. It is that he is boring.

⚠ «And you sniff at it» — a place where the scribes changed the text, and told us so. Jewish tradition preserves a list of tiqqunei soferim, “corrections of the scribes” — a handful of passages where the transmitted reading is said to be a reverent softening of an original that was too blunt about God. This verse is on the list: the received text has the priests snorting at it (the offering); the tradition says the original had them snorting at ME. We print the received Masoretic text and report the tradition — the same policy this translation followed at Genesis 18:22. Either way the gesture is the same: a puff of breath through the nose, the universal human sign for not worth my time.

«What was seized by violence.» Gazul is the participle of robbery — a stolen animal, laid on the altar of the God who forbade the stealing. ASV “that which was taken by violence”; NIV flattens to “injured animals,” following a possible but weaker sense and losing the crime.

⚠ The curse falls on a specific man, and the specificity is the whole point. Not the poor man with nothing. The cheat (nokhel, a swindler — the root of the plotting the brothers do against Joseph at Genesis 37:18) who has a sound male in his flock, and vows it, and then brings something else to the altar. He could have kept his mouth shut; instead he made a promise in public and paid it in damaged goods. The sin here is not scarcity, it is calculation.

«What is ruined.» The word is moshchat, from shachat — the ruin-verb of the flood, where the earth ruined itself and God completed the ruin (Genesis 6:11–13). In a sacrificial setting it means simply blemished, damaged, and every version renders it that way (KJV “a corrupt thing”); we keep the older, harder word audible, because this translation has rendered that root “ruin” since Genesis 6.

And the chapter closes by answering its own question. Verse 6 asked: if I am a father, where is my honor? If I am a master, where is my fear? Verse 14 answers, and the answer is not from Jerusalem: “I am a great King… and my name is feared among the nations.” The word for “feared” (nora) is the noun of v6’s “my fear” (mora) — the same root, given back. Malachi is not saying the nations are righteous. He is saying that the God the priests find tedious is being taken seriously by strangers, and that a great King — in a province with a governor and no king at all — is not going to be paid in sick animals.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the exact / distinct words: “a burden” for massa, the lifted load, so the same root can lift a face in vv 8 and 9; “the utterance of Jehovah” for ne’um, kept distinct from “says Jehovah” (amar) which most versions flatten into it; “honor” for kaved, the weight-word, against “despise” (bazah), the slightness-word; “bread” and “table” kept literal so the meal-metaphor stands; “your governor” for the Persian pechah; “in vain” for chinnam with the “for free” reading flagged; and “what is ruined” for moshchat, holding on to the flood’s own ruin-verb.

Echoes paid. Esau and Jacob, and the brother-nation of Genesis 25 and 36, called in as evidence; kaved, met last as Pharaoh’s heavy heart (Exodus 8), doing its other job as honor; shachat, the flood’s ruin (Genesis 6:11–13), turning up on an altar; the disqualifying-blemish law of Leviticus 22 and Deuteronomy 15 quoted back at the men whose job it was to enforce it; and a second tiqqun soferim reported, after Genesis 18:22.

Echoes planted. Mal’akhi, “my messenger” — the word that is either this prophet’s name or the book’s title, and that returns at 3:1 as the forerunner God will send, the verse Mark chose to open his Gospel with (Mark 1:2). The lifted face, which becomes the accusation of partiality at 2:9. The covenant with Levi, implied by every charge in this chapter and stated outright in the next. And the nations, introduced here as an embarrassment to Judah and left running: from the rising of the sun to its setting.

Malachi is a new book on the site — and the last of the prophets. Next in sequence: Malachi 2 — the sentence on the priests (“I will spread dung on your faces”), the covenant with Levi and what a true priest was supposed to be, and then the turn on the people themselves: marriage, faithlessness, and the line every argument about divorce has quoted since — “he hates divorce.”
