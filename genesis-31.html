Jacob flees Haran with his family and flocks; Rachel steals the household gods; Laban pursues and is warned off in a dream, and the two schemers part at the treaty-heap of Galeed and Mizpah.
AM ≈ 2205 · c. 1740 BC — the very end of Jacob's twenty years with Laban (he is about 97). Warned by God to go home, he flees across the Euphrates toward Gilead; Laban overtakes him after a seven-day chase, they trade accusations and make a boundary-covenant, and Laban goes home for good. Jacob is now poised to re-enter Canaan and face the brother he cheated.

Translator's Notes — verse by verse

Jacob flees Haran with his family and flocks, Rachel steals her father's gods, and the twenty-year contest of two schemers ends at a heap of stones. Quotations from copyrighted versions (NIV, TLB, NWT) are kept to short phrases for comparison; KJV, Geneva, Douay-Rheims and ASV are public domain.

Verses 1–16 · "Return… and I will be with you"

The call home. The mood in Haran has soured — Laban's sons grumble that Jacob has swallowed their father's wealth, and Laban's face has turned — and it is exactly then that Jehovah speaks: "Return to the land of your fathers and to your kindred, and I will be with you" (v. 3). It is the promise of Bethel coming due: the God who said "I am with you and will keep you wherever you go" (28:15) now says the keeping is over — go home. Jacob calls Rachel and Leah out to the field, away from the house, and lays out his case: twenty years of hard service, a father-in-law who "changed my wages ten times," and a God who quietly turned every trick to Jacob's good.

The dream that reframes the rods. Jacob tells the sisters something the reader did not see in chapter 30: the speckled flocks were not really the work of his peeled sticks at all. "The angel of God" showed him in a dream that God was making the flocks bear streaked and speckled — "I have seen all that Laban is doing to you" — and named himself: "I am the God of Bethel, where you anointed a pillar and vowed a vow" (v. 13). The clever shepherd's trick was God's providence all along; the vow Jacob made as a frightened fugitive is now being called in. And the wives, for once, agree without a quarrel — with a startling verdict on their own father: "he has sold us, and utterly devoured our money" (v. 15). In this house, even the daughters feel like foreigners.

Verses 17–35 · גָּנַב ganav — a family of thieves, and the stolen gods

Everyone in this chapter steals. The word ganav, "to steal," rings through the flight like a bell. Rachel steals (ganav) her father's teraphim (v. 19; KJV "images"); Jacob "steals the heart" of Laban (v. 20) — ganav lev, the Hebrew idiom for hoodwinking someone (the same phrase Absalom will use to steal Israel's heart). Then Laban arrives and hurls the word back three times: "you have stolen my heart" (v. 26), "you stole from me" (v. 27), "why did you steal my gods?" (v. 30). The supplanter's household is a household of thieves — and God has to warn Laban in a dream not to lay a finger on them.

Why did Rachel take the gods? The teraphim were small household idols, and why Rachel wanted them the text leaves open — the interpretations run from the practical (in the customs of that world, holding the household gods could back a claim on the inheritance her father had denied her, v. 14) to the superstitious (portable luck, or divination) to the pious (rabbinic tradition: she took them to wean her father off idolatry). Whatever her reason, the scene is played for comedy at Laban's expense: he ransacks every tent while Rachel sits calmly on the idols, tucked in the camel's saddle, pleading "the way of women is upon me" — no man will ask a menstruating woman to stand. The gods of Laban end up sat upon. But there is a shadow over it, too: Jacob, not knowing, has sworn that whoever has the gods "shall not live" (v. 32) — an oath that hangs, unspoken, over the wife who will die young on the road home (35:16–19).

Verses 36–42 · פַּחַד יִצְחָק pachad Yitzchak — the Fear of Isaac

Twenty years, spoken at last. Now Jacob's own anger breaks, and out pours the one honest speech of his life so far — not a trick, just the truth of two decades of brutal shepherding: ewes that never miscarried, predators' kills he swallowed the cost of, "the heat by day and the frost by night, and my sleep fled from my eyes." He has been, for once, entirely faithful. And he names the God who carried him with a title found only here and a few verses on: the Fear of Isaac (pachad Yitzchak) — "the Awe-inspiring One whom Isaac reveres," the God of his father set beside "the God of Abraham." And the verb that seals it is Leah's: "God has seen my affliction" (oni, v. 42) — the very word she spoke over Reuben's birth (29:32); the God who sees the unloved sees the cheated servant too.

Verses 43–54 · יְגַר שָׂהֲדוּתָא · גַּלְעֵד — the heap of witness, in two tongues

The first Aramaic in the Bible. Two men who will never trust each other build a boundary and back away from it. They pile stones into a gal, a "heap," and name it — and here Genesis records a small, telling thing: Laban the Aramean names it in Aramaic, Jegar-sahadutha, and Jacob in Hebrew, Galeed — the two phrases mean exactly the same thing, "heap of witness," but in two languages. It is the earliest Aramaic in Scripture, and the moment the family's two branches are shown speaking two tongues; the name sticks to the whole region as Gilead.

The Mizpah "blessing" that is really a fence. The pillar is also called Mizpah, "the Watchpost," because Laban says, "May Jehovah watch (yitsef) between me and you, when we are hidden from each other" (v. 49; ASV "Jehovah watch between me and thee"). Cross-stitched onto keepsakes as a tender farewell, the line is nothing of the sort in context: it is two rivals invoking God to police a truce neither will keep on trust — "watch him, Lord, because I can't." Laban adds a clause protecting his daughters, and the two swear — but note by whom. Laban calls on "the God of Abraham and the God of Nahor," blurring the true God into his own family's gods; Jacob swears only, and precisely, "by the Fear of his father Isaac." A sacrifice, a shared meal on the mountain, a night's sleep — and in the morning Laban will kiss his grandchildren, bless them, and go home for good, out of the story. Twenty years in Haran are over; ahead lies the brother Jacob cheated, and a night by the Jabbok.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the words exact / distinct: the ganav ("steal") thread rendered "steal" throughout — Rachel steals the gods, Jacob "steals the heart" (deceives), Laban flings the word back — so the family of thieves is audible; "teraphim" kept as the specific term (KJV "images"); "the Fear of Isaac" for the divine epithet pachad Yitzchak; "affliction" for oni, Leah's word; and the bilingual heap kept in both tongues (Jegar-sahadutha / Galeed).

Echoes paid. "I will be with you" and "the God of Bethel, where you vowed" (28:15, 28:20) — the vow called in; "God has seen my affliction" (oni, 29:32); the speckled flocks of ch. 30 revealed as God's doing; and "the God of Nahor" (11:29), the family line Laban swears by. Echoes planted: Jacob's unwitting death-oath over the teraphim, waiting for Rachel's grave at Bethlehem (ch. 35); the crossing back toward Canaan, and the brother — and the wrestling angel — waiting at the Jabbok (ch. 32).

Next installment in this book: Genesis 32 — messengers to Esau, Jacob's terror and his prayer, the gift sent ahead, and the night by the Jabbok where a man wrestles him until dawn and renames him Israel, "for you have striven with God and with men, and have prevailed."
