Translator’s Notes

Verses 1–6 · a reign that begins where the other account ended — and a high place explained

⚠ VERSIFICATION: this chapter has EIGHTEEN verses in the Hebrew and seventeen in English Bibles. What the Masoretic text prints as 1:18 — “and Solomon said that he would build a house” — English versions print as 2:1, and the offset then runs through the whole of chapter 2 (Hebrew 2:1–17 = English 2:2–18) before the two systems fall back into step at chapter 3. We follow the Masoretic numbering, as this translation does everywhere, and flag it here because a reader landing mid-book will otherwise think a verse has gone missing. The same offset was noted at Exodus 7.

⚠ And now read verse 1 against the book it is quietly answering. “Solomon strengthened himself over his kingdom” is where Chronicles begins his reign. 1 Kings spends two long chapters getting to the same point: Adonijah’s coup, the oath nobody has ever heard of, Bathsheba and Nathan working the dying king, the anointing at Gihon — and then a deathbed list of names, Adonijah executed over a request for Abishag, Abiathar exiled, Joab killed holding the horns of the altar, Shimei kept inside the walls until he isn’t. That account ends: “and the kingdom was established in the hand of Solomon.” Chronicles opens on that sentence and does not tell you what it cost.

It is worth being accurate about why. This is not concealment — the readers had Samuel and Kings, and the Chronicler plainly expects them to. It is a different subject. Chronicles is about the throne and the temple, not about the succession politics of a palace, and it starts the reign at the point where the man is in a position to build.

⚠ The high place at Gibeon, and a problem solved. In 1 Kings 3 Solomon goes to sacrifice at Gibeon, “the great high place,” and the narrator is visibly uncomfortable about it — high places are what this book will condemn every later king for, and Kings can only explain that no house had yet been built. Chronicles supplies an explanation Kings never gives: the tent of meeting that Moses made in the wilderness was at Gibeon (v3), and Bezalel’s bronze altar — the altar of Exodus 38, by the craftsman God filled with his spirit — was standing in front of it (v5). Solomon is not at a local shrine of dubious standing. He is at the Mosaic sanctuary, at the legitimate altar, and what he does there is regular worship.

Whether that is older information Kings did not bother to record, or the Chronicler’s own reconstruction defending a beloved king, is a real question and the library does not vote on it. What is not in doubt is the effect: everything in Chronicles is being connected to Moses and to the proper cult, and the great crime of Kings is being pre-emptively answered.

«However» (v4) is aval — the flat adversative that appeared in the mouth of Jonathan at 1 Kings 1:43. Here it does careful bookkeeping: the tent is at Gibeon, but the ark is in Jerusalem, in a tent David pitched for it. The two halves of Israel’s worship are in two different towns, and the whole point of the chapter’s last verse is that Solomon intends to put them back together.

Verses 7–10 · what Solomon actually asked for — and it is not what you remember

«In that night God APPEARED to Solomon.» 1 Kings 3:5 says God appeared “in a dream by night.” Chronicles drops the dream. It is a small change and a characteristic one: the same firming-up instinct that moved the tent of meeting into view a few verses earlier.

⚠ And the request itself is different in the two books, which is the most interesting thing in the chapter. In 1 Kings 3:9 Solomon asks for a lev shomea — “a listening heart to judge your people, to discern between good and evil.” It is one of the loveliest phrases in the Old Testament, and it is a request for moral discernment. In Chronicles he asks for chokhmah u-madda — “wisdom and knowledge” — and gives the reason as “that I may go out and come in before this people,” an idiom for leading, for public administration. The listening heart has become executive competence.

Neither book is misquoting; they are making different points about the same night. Kings is interested in the man who will shortly decide which woman is the mother; Chronicles is interested in the man who will organize twenty-four courses of priests and build a house. And the note is not that one is nobler — it is that an ancient writer felt free to render a remembered prayer in the vocabulary his own argument needed, and left both versions standing in the same Bible.

⚠ Madda is a late word. It occurs only a handful of times in the Hebrew Bible — here, in Ecclesiastes, in Daniel — all of them books whose Hebrew belongs to the Persian period or after, and it is one of the small pieces of evidence for dating Chronicles late. (It has had a long afterlife: in modern Hebrew madda is the ordinary word for science.)

«Great kindness» (v8) is chesed, the covenant-loyalty word — and Solomon uses it of what God did for David, not for himself. His whole prayer is framed as a request that a promise made to his father be kept: “let your word to David my father be confirmed.” He asks for competence on the grounds that he has inherited an obligation.

Verses 11–13 · the things he did not ask for

God’s answer works by listing the alternatives — riches, wealth, honor, the life of your enemies, long life — and it is a fair summary of what ancient kings actually prayed for. Chronicles includes one item that 1 Kings 3:11 does not put quite so bluntly: “the life of those who hate you.” Coming from the book that has just declined to narrate the executions of chapter 2 of Kings, it is an unexpectedly sharp sentence, and the Chronicler makes no connection at all. He simply lists it among the things Solomon did not ask for.

«Such as none of the kings before you has had, and none after you shall have.» Written for readers who could count the kings after Solomon on their fingers and knew exactly how the line had ended, this is not flattery. It is a boundary marker: there was one reign like this, it is behind us, and the wealth is not the part that is coming back.

⚠ Verse 13 is textually awkward in the Hebrew — literally “and Solomon came to the high place that is in Gibeon, Jerusalem, from before the tent of meeting.” Both places are named with no preposition to sort them out. KJV supplies a journey (“came from his journey to the high place… to Jerusalem”); ASV and NIV read it as we do, coming from Gibeon to Jerusalem, which the sense plainly requires. It is a small reminder that even a straightforward narrative verse can need a decision.

Verses 14–17 · horses out of Egypt, reported without comment

⚠ There is a law about this, and the chapter does not mention it. Deuteronomy 17:16 lays down what a king of Israel may not do: “he shall not multiply horses for himself, nor cause the people to return to EGYPT in order to multiply horses.” Four verses after God grants Solomon wisdom, the chapter records that he accumulated fourteen hundred chariots and twelve thousand horsemen, and that the source of his horses was Egypt. The two named prohibitions, in one paragraph.

The Chronicler offers no verdict — and the placement is his own decision. 1 Kings carries almost the same passage, but keeps it until chapter 10, in the long description of Solomon’s magnificence that comes immediately before chapter 11, where the wives and the foreign gods arrive and the sentence falls. Kings uses the horses as part of a slow decline. Chronicles moves them to the first page of the reign and sets them beside the gift of wisdom without a word of connection. Whether that is a compliment (the promised riches, arriving on schedule) or something drier is left entirely to the reader, and the library does not decide it. But the law is real, the words are the law’s own two words, and any reader in the Persian period knew Deuteronomy.

«Silver and gold as common as stones.» A frank hyperbole, repeated later in the book, and the sycamore-figs of the lowland are the cheap wood of the coastal plain against the imported cedar of Lebanon. The image is of luxury goods gone ordinary.

⚠ «From Kue» — and one of the KJV’s most famous stumbles. The Hebrew miqve can be read as a common noun (a collection, a drove) or as a place-name, Kue — Cilicia, in south-eastern Anatolia, which was in fact the great horse-breeding region of the ancient Near East and is attested as such in Assyrian records. Modern versions read Kue. KJV, working before that was known, produced “and linen yarn” — “the king’s merchants received the linen yarn at a price” — which has puzzled English readers for four centuries. ASV has “from Kue”; NIV the same. It is a good example of a place where later knowledge genuinely improved a translation.

Verse 18 · two houses

The chapter ends on a decision, and on a pair. “A house for the name of Jehovah, and a house for his kingdom” — the temple and the palace, announced in one breath. Kings builds both too, and takes longer over Solomon’s own house than most readers notice (seven years for the temple, thirteen for the palace, 1 Kings 6:38–7:1). Chronicles names both here and will then give the palace almost no attention at all, because it is not what this book is for.

«For the NAME of Jehovah.» Not for Jehovah — the careful formula, inherited from Deuteronomy, that a building can house the Name without containing God, and which Solomon will spell out at the dedication: “the heavens and the heaven of heavens cannot contain you; how much less this house that I have built.”

⚠ And this is the verse the numbering falls out over. In English Bibles it is 2:1, the opening of the building chapter; in the Hebrew it is the last verse of chapter 1, the close of the accession. Both are defensible readings of where the seam lies — and it is worth noticing that the two traditions disagree about whether the resolve to build is the end of becoming king or the beginning of building.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the exact / distinct words: the Masoretic verse numbering, so this chapter has eighteen verses and the offset is flagged rather than silently corrected; “wisdom and knowledge” for chokhmah u-madda, not assimilated to 1 Kings’ “listening heart”; “however” for aval, the same adversative as 1 Kings 1:43; “great kindness” for chesed, and note that Solomon uses it of God’s dealings with his father; “from Kue” rather than the KJV’s “linen yarn”; and “for the NAME of Jehovah,” the Deuteronomic formula, kept exact.

Echoes paid. 1 Kings 1–2, whose entire content is the gap between that book’s “the kingdom was established in the hand of Solomon” and this book’s opening sentence; 1 Kings 3, whose Gibeon high place is here explained and whose “listening heart” is here reworded; Bezalel’s altar from Exodus 38, connecting Solomon’s worship directly to Moses; and aval, last heard from a messenger ruining a coronation banquet.

Echoes planted. The two houses of the last verse — one of which this book will describe at enormous length and the other of which it will barely mention. Deuteronomy 17:16, left sitting unremarked beside the horses of Egypt. The ark, still in a tent in Jerusalem while the altar is at Gibeon, and waiting for chapter 5.
