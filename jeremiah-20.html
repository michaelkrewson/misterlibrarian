Translator's Notes — verse by verse

Each note explains this translation's choice and compares the seven versions on the shelf. Quotations from copyrighted versions (NIV, TLB, NWT) are kept to short phrases for comparison; KJV, Geneva, Douay-Rheims and ASV are public domain.

Verses 1–3 · מָגוֹר מִסָּבִיב Magor-Missaviv

You are entering the sixth book of this site mid-story. Jeremiah of Anathoth — a priest's son drafted as a boy ("before I formed you in the womb, I knew you… and I said, Look — I do not know how to speak, for I am only a youth," chapter 1, not yet on these pages) — has by now spent years saying what no one in Jerusalem wants said. "These things" that Pashhur heard are chapter 19, just off this page: a clay flask smashed at the Potsherd Gate, and a sermon preached over the valley of Ben-Hinnom — the very valley whose Greek name this site met three chapters ago as Gehenna (Matthew 5:22). The sermon in Gehenna's valley earned the prophet the first recorded violence of his life: Pashhur son of Immer, paqid nagid — chief overseer in the house of Jehovah, the head of temple order — struck him and locked him overnight in the mahpekhet: "the twister," a frame that bent the prisoner's body (the word is built on haphakh, the overturning-verb — hold that root; the chapter will close with it).

The renaming. A night in the stocks produced not a retraction but a name. "Not Pashhur has Jehovah called your name — but Magor-Missaviv": Terror-All-Around (KJV runs it together as one grim word, "Magormissabib"; Douay translates it away — "fear on every side"). Prophets rename people as verdicts; this one is a forecast, and it is Jeremiah's own signature phrase of dread (6:25; 46:5; 49:29; Psalm 31 borrows it) — verse 10 will show the street throwing it back in his face.

Verses 4–6 · בְּיַד מֶלֶךְ-בָּבֶל beyad melekh-Bavel

Babylon, named at last. Until this oracle, the doom in Jeremiah's preaching comes from an unnamed "north." Here, for the first time in the book's oracles, the destination is said out loud — Babylon, five times in three verses, as if the word itself had been waiting in the stocks overnight. The naming dates the scene: after Carchemish (605 BC — the year this site's Daniel 1 opens), when the identity of the "north" stopped being a riddle. "All Judah I will give into the hand of the king of Babylon" — prophesied here, and recorded as done on Daniel's very first page. Pashhur's own sentence is measured to his office: exile, death, and burial in Babylon, "you, and all who love you, to whom you have prophesied falsehood" — the chief of temple order was also, it turns out, a comfortable false prophet; the man who policed the true one.

A librarian's aside on sealed clay. From the ash of the very destruction Jeremiah foretold, excavations in the City of David (2005, 2008) produced two clay seal-impressions found a few meters apart: "Gedaliah son of Pashhur" and "Jehucal son of Shelemiah" — two officials who appear side by side, as Jeremiah's enemies, in Jeremiah 38:1. Not this chapter's Pashhur (ours is the son of Immer) — but the prophet's enemies list, countersigned in fired clay.

Verses 7–9 · פִּתִּיתַנִי יְהוָה וָאֶפָּת pittitani … va'eppat

The boldest prayer in the prophets. The verb is patah, and its range is the whole scandal: to persuade; to entice (the lying spirit is sent to "entice" Ahab to his death, 1 Kings 22 — this verb); to seduce (the seduction law of Exodus 22:16 — this verb); to deceive. The shelf has spent four centuries deciding how much to let the verse say: KJV "O LORD, thou hast deceived me" — quoted here with its small-caps name set aside — NIV "you deceived me"; NWT "You have fooled me, O Jehovah"; TLB flatly "you deceived me"; the 1917 JPS, whose English runs beside the Hebrew in this site's own source edition, "enticed" — the choice this translation makes, leaving the darker readings in this note at full strength rather than in the verse's mouth. "You overpowered me, and you prevailed" — mark that last verb (yakhol, to be able, to prevail): the chapter is about to decline it through every party.

The fire in the bones. The resignation is tendered in verse 9 — "I will not remember him, and I will not speak anymore in his name" — and rejected by the prophet's own anatomy: "like a burning fire, shut up in my bones" (the KJV's line here — "as a burning fire shut up in my bones" — is nearly perfect and nearly ours); "I wore myself out holding it in — and I could not" (lo ukhal): the prevail-verb again, negated. God can; the prophet cannot. That asymmetry is the entire confession.

Verses 10–13 · כֹּל אֱנוֹשׁ שְׁלֹמִי kol enosh shelomi

His own oracle, thrown back — and the verb declined. The whispering crowd has adopted his nickname-verdict as a taunt: "Terror all around!" — Magor-Missaviv, now street mockery. "Every man of my peace" — the Hebrew idiom for the trusted circle, the table-friends (Psalm 41:9 runs on the same idiom, "the man of my peace, who ate my bread" — a verse with an upper-room future) — are now "watchers of my stumble" (a small shelf-wink: the letters of his "stumble," tsal'i, are the letters this project met in Eden as "side"). And their hope is verbatim: "perhaps he will be enticed, and we shall prevail over him" — the exact two verbs of verse 7. What the prophet said God did to him is precisely what his enemies dream of doing; the difference is the outcome. Verse 11 closes the ledger: "my pursuers… will not prevail… they have not succeeded" — and that last verb (lo hiskilu) is the very root whose participle is Daniel's maskilim, "the wise": his enemies are, by the letter, the un-wise. Four occurrences of one verb of power: God prevailed (v7); the prophet could not (v9); the enemies hope to (v10); they will not (v11).

The assayer, the kidneys, and the pivot. "Jehovah is with me like a dread warrior" (gibbor arits — gibbor, the mighty-man word from Genesis 6's strange page, now God's epithet); "who tests the righteous" — bochen, the assayer's verb, the refining furnace of Daniel 11:35 compressed to a participle; "who sees kidneys and heart" — the kidneys as Hebrew's seat of the hidden self (KJV, wonderfully, "the reins" — Latin renes, kidneys; four centuries of English readers picturing bridles). Revelation quotes this oath back across the canon: Thyatira's Christ — "I am the one who searches kidneys and hearts." And then, with no warning, verse 13: "Sing to Jehovah! Praise Jehovah!" — the lament-psalm's pivot (complaint → confidence → praise) compressed into a single line. Hold the praise carefully; the floor is about to give way.

Verses 14–18 · אָרוּר הַיּוֹם arur hayyom

The birth-curse — kept raw. From "Sing to Jehovah" to "Cursed be the day I was born," with no seam. The book's editors did not smooth the sequence, and this translation will not either: this is the rawest run of verses in the prophets, and its arrangement — praise, then the pit — is the honest shape of a real interior life. Job 3 is the twin ("Let the day perish wherein I was born"); which poet echoes which, scholars split, and this note reports the split. Notice where the curse does not land: not on God, not on himself — on the day, and on the poor messenger who brought the good news ("A son is born to you — a male!"), who is wished the fate of "the cities that Jehovah overthrew" — haphakh, Sodom's own verb from this site's Genesis 19 (ASV "the cities which Jehovah overthrew"), closing the bracket the chapter opened: the man twisted in the mahpekhet ends by wishing the overturning on the world. "And did not relent" — nacham, the comfort-and-regret root this site has tracked since Noah's name.

The womb, twice. "Why is it that I came out of the womb — to see toil and sorrow?" — asked, devastatingly, by the one man in scripture who was told why: "before I formed you in the womb, I knew you" (1:5). The call and the curse are aimed at the same womb. The chapter ends with the question unanswered — no seam, no comfort; the next verse of the book (21:1) is simply the next assignment. The resolution is the one verse 9 already gave: the fire in the bones wins by default, and he keeps speaking. Not an answer — a vocation.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation sides with the literal shelf: "a burning fire shut up in my bones" — the KJV's line, nearly untouchable, nearly kept whole; Magor-Missaviv left standing as the name it is (with KJV, against Douay's "fear on every side").

Where it goes its own way: "You enticed me… and I was enticed" (KJV/NIV "deceived," NWT "fooled" — the darker readings carried in the note at full strength); "kidneys and heart" kept literal (KJV "the reins"); "every man of my peace" kept as the idiom is (KJV "my familiars"); "all who love you" for Pashhur's circle (KJV "thy friends"); "the twister" explained for the stocks.

Echoes paid and planted. Paid: haphakh, Sodom's overthrow-verb (19:25) — bracketing the chapter from the "twister" stocks to the birth-curse; nacham, the relent-root, from Noah's page; gibbor (6:4) turned into God's own epithet; the valley of Ben-Hinnom — the sermon that caused it all, preached in the valley Greek wears as Gehenna (Matthew 5:22); "kidneys and heart," paid forward across the whole canon to Thyatira (Revelation 2:23); and "into the hand of the king of Babylon" — prophesied here, receipted on Daniel's first page (Daniel 1:2). Planted: "every man of my peace," waiting for Psalm 41:9 and an upper room; Babylon, now on this site's map with its own encyclopedia entry — and waiting, much further on, for Revelation to make the name a cipher; and Jeremiah's other confessions (chapters 11–18), of which this chapter is the last and rawest.
