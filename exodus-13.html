Translator’s Notes

Verses 1–10 · the firstborn claimed, and a son told

⚠ The claim follows directly from the night before. Every firstborn in Egypt died; Israel’s did not — therefore Israel’s firstborn are not simply their parents’. “Consecrate to me every firstborn… IT IS MINE.” The logic is a purchase: what was spared belongs to the one who spared it. That claim will later be transferred wholesale onto the tribe of Levi, taken “instead of every firstborn” (Numbers 3:12-13), which is how a priesthood ends up being founded on this night.

“From the house of slaves.” The phrase (beit avadim) becomes one of the Bible’s standing names for Egypt, and it is worth hearing what it does: it names the place not by its power — the greatest kingdom on earth — but by what it did to them. KJV “the house of bondage.”

The month is named: ABIB, the month of the ripening ear — the same word the hail chapter used of the barley it destroyed (9:31). The agricultural detail dropped there quietly set this calendar.

⚠ «And you shall TELL YOUR SON on that day.» This is the third time in four chapters. At 10:2 the plagues existed so a father could recount them to a son and a grandson; at 12:26 the rite was built around the child’s question; here the telling is commanded outright, and it will come a fourth time at verse 14. Four tellings in four chapters — the drumbeat is deliberate. A people is being made, and the mechanism is a conversation across generations.

⚠ «A sign on your hand and a memorial between your eyes.» Read literally, this and verse 16 are the basis of TEFILLIN — the small boxes of scripture bound to arm and forehead in Jewish practice to this day (the “phylacteries” of Matthew 23:5). Read figuratively, it is an idiom for keeping something before you in what you do and what you think, like Proverbs’ “bind them on your fingers, write them on the tablet of your heart” (7:3). Both readings are ancient and the text supports both; note that the purpose clause here is verbal — “so that the law of Jehovah may be IN YOUR MOUTH.” KJV “for a memorial between thine eyes”; the NIV “a reminder on your forehead.”

Verses 11–16 · a donkey, a lamb, and a broken neck

“You shall SET APART to Jehovah everything that opens the womb.” The verb is ha’avarta, literally to cause to PASS OVER — the same root as the passing over of the destroying night (12:12). What was passed over is now passed across, into God’s possession.

⚠ Then a strange and revealing law. A firstborn ox or sheep is sacrificed; but the DONKEY is unclean and cannot be. So it must be REDEEMED — bought back by substituting a lamb — and if its owner will not pay that price, its neck is broken. The animal does not simply revert to ordinary use. It belongs to God, and it is either bought back or it dies; the one thing not on offer is keeping it for nothing. ⚠ The donkey is singled out because it was the ordinary Israelite’s working animal — the pack-beast, the plow-beast, the most valuable thing a poor family owned. The law puts its most expensive unclean possession under the same claim as everything else. KJV “thou shalt break his neck”; the ESV the same.

And then the sentence the whole law exists to produce: “every firstborn of MAN among your sons you shall redeem.” Human firstborn are never sacrificed — they are bought back, always. In a world that practiced child sacrifice, the law claims Israel’s sons absolutely and then, in the same breath, forbids the obvious way of collecting.

⚠ «And when your son asks you in time to come, ‘What is this?’» The fourth telling — and note its trigger: not a festival date but a child noticing something odd. A boy sees a lamb swapped for a donkey and asks why, and the answer he is given is not about livestock. It is the exodus. The rituals are built to be strange enough to prompt the question. (Jewish tradition reads the four tellings — here and 12:26; 13:8; Deuteronomy 6:20 — as the four sons of the Passover seder: the wise, the wicked, the simple, and the one who does not know how to ask.)

“Frontlets” (totafot) is a rare word of uncertain origin — possibly a band or ornament worn on the head. KJV “frontlets between thine eyes”; the NIV “a symbol on your forehead”; the NWT “a band between your eyes.”

Verses 17–22 · the long way round, a coffin, and a pillar

⚠ The first thing God does with a free people is take them the LONG way. The direct road to Canaan was the coastal highway — Egypt’s military road, garrisoned and fortified — and it is rejected for a stated reason: “LEST THE PEOPLE CHANGE THEIR MINDS WHEN THEY SEE WAR, AND RETURN TO EGYPT.” The route is chosen around their fragility. They have been slaves for four hundred years; they are not ready, and God is said to know it and to route accordingly rather than to demand they be otherwise. It is one of the most humane sentences in the book. KJV “lest peradventure the people repent”; the ESV “lest the people change their minds.”

⚠ «The land of the PHILISTINES» is an anachronism, and a well-known one: the Philistines as history knows them are an Aegean people who settled that coast around 1200 BC, centuries after any date proposed for the exodus. It is the same editorial updating of a place-name this library has already flagged at “Dan” (Genesis 14:14), “Ur of the Chaldeans,” and “Raamses” (Exodus 1:11) — the reader’s name for a region, not the name it bore at the time.

“And the sons of Israel went up in BATTLE ORDER.” Chamushim is uncertain and the versions scatter: armed, equipped for battle, in ranks — or, from the resemblance to the word for FIVE, in five divisions or by fifties. KJV “harnessed”; the ESV “equipped for battle”; the NWT “in battle formation.” Whatever the detail, it cuts against the picture of a fleeing rabble — and it sits pointedly next to the previous verse, where they are judged unready for war.

⚠ «And Moses took the BONES OF JOSEPH with him.» In the middle of a national evacuation, one man carries a coffin. Joseph had made his brothers swear it four hundred years earlier — “God will surely VISIT you, and you shall carry up my bones from here” (Genesis 50:25) — and the doubled Hebrew verb he used, paqod yifqod, is the same one Moses was told to repeat at the burning bush (3:16). Joseph died certain of a rescue he would not see, and left instructions for his own participation in it. The oath is finally discharged in Joshua 24:32, where the bones are buried at Shechem, in ground Jacob had bought — so the arc runs from Genesis to Joshua, and this verse is its hinge.

⚠ And then the pillar. Not a map and not a route given in advance, but a presence that moves and is followed — a pillar of cloud by day and of fire by night, so that they could travel at either hour. Verse 22 states it as a negative, which is stronger: it did NOT depart. This is the beginning of a presence that will fill the tabernacle at the end of the book (40:34-38), where the same cloud settles and Israel moves only when it lifts.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the exact / distinct words: “whatever opens the womb” kept literal for peter rechem; “set apart” for ha’avarta, the causing-to-PASS-OVER that echoes 12:12; “redeem” for padah, the buying-back verb, held distinct from ga’al; “frontlets” for the rare totafot, with both the literal and figurative readings given; “in battle order” for the uncertain chamushim, alternatives noted; and “the house of slaves” rather than the softer “house of bondage.”

Echoes paid. The firstborn spared on Passover night, now claimed outright (12:29; 4:22); the month of Abib, set by the ripening barley of 9:31; the telling of a son, commanded at 10:2 and built into the rite at 12:26, here twice more; and Joseph’s bones, carried out on the oath of Genesis 50:25 with the same doubled verb, “God will surely VISIT you,” that Moses was given at the bush (3:16).

Echoes planted. The firstborn claim, later transferred onto the tribe of Levi (Numbers 3:12-13) — a priesthood founded on this night; the pillar, which will stand between Israel and Egypt at the sea (14:19-20), descend at the tent of meeting (33:9-11), and finally fill the tabernacle so that Israel moves only when it lifts (40:34-38); and Joseph’s coffin, not buried until Joshua 24:32, at Shechem.

Next in this book: Exodus 14 — the sea. Pharaoh changes his mind and comes after them with six hundred chariots; Israel, trapped against the water, discovers it would rather have been a slave than dead — and is told to be still and watch.
