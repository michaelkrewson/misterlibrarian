Translator's Notes

Verses 1–4 · the elder, to a friend

The same signature, and now a name to go with it. "The elder" opens this letter exactly as it opened 2 John — no personal name, authority assumed rather than asserted — but here the recipient is a single, ordinary, nameable man: Gaius. ⚠️ It is one of the commonest names in the Roman world (three or four other people called Gaius appear in the New Testament, and there is no way to prove any of them is this one), which is part of the point: 3 John is the most private document in the Bible, a note from one church leader to one Christian host about a local quarrel, and it survived. See the book's introduction page for the author question, which is the same open question as 2 John's.

⚠️ "That you may prosper and be in health." Verse 2 is the single most abused verse in these letters, and the honest thing is to say what it is. Euodousthai — literally "to have a good road," to go well — was a standard health-wish in ancient letters, the Greek equivalent of "I hope this finds you well." It is a greeting, not a doctrine. A whole strand of modern preaching has read it as a promise that God wills material prosperity for every believer; the sentence is a personal courtesy at the head of a letter to one friend, and the parallel it draws — as your soul prospers — is a compliment to Gaius's spiritual state, not a formula for anyone's bank balance. KJV "that thou mayest prosper and be in health"; the NWT "that you may be prospering and having good health."

Truth, and walking in it. The word that saturated 2 John is here too — alētheia four times in four verses — and again the test is not assent to it but walking in it (v. 3, v. 4). ⚠️ These two letters are held together by vocabulary this tight on purpose: they are almost certainly a matched pair, sent by the same hand into the same situation, one warning a community to shut a door and the other thanking a man for keeping one open.

"My children." The elder calls Gaius one of his tekna, as he called the lady's community her children in 2 John. Whether Gaius was literally converted by him or is "his child" in the looser sense of belonging to his circle is not said; the affection is the same either way, and "I have no greater joy than this" is as warm a sentence as the New Testament's letters contain.

Verses 5–8 · the exact thing 2 John forbade — commanded

Read this against 2 John 10, deliberately. There the instruction was: a teacher who brings a false Christ gets no house and no welcome. Here the instruction is: receive the travelling brothers, feed them, and send them on their way. ⚠️ The two are not in tension; they are the same principle applied to opposite people. The test was never hospitality-in-the-abstract but what the traveller carries — a corrupt teaching (2 John) or "the Name" (here). Gaius is praised for exactly the act the lady was told to refuse, because the men at his door are the opposite men. This is the clearest place in the New Testament to see that its hospitality rules are about the message, not about the mood.

"And these, strangers." Kai touto xenous — a tight, emphatic phrase: he helps the brothers, and these ones he did not even know. The praise is specifically for hospitality to people who could not repay it and were not his own. Xenos is the guest-stranger, the word behind philoxenia, "love of strangers," which is simply the Greek for hospitality — a whole virtue named for treating the unknown traveller well.

"Send them on their way worthily of God." Propempsas — a semi-technical word in the early church for outfitting a traveller for the next leg: food, money, letters, an escort. ⚠️ It is the material engine of the whole early Christian mission, and it is what verse 8 makes into a theology: to support such people is to "become fellow workers with the truth" — synergoi tē alētheia. KJV "fellowhelpers to the truth"; the ESV "fellow workers for the truth"; the NIV "work together for the truth". The one who never leaves home but funds the one who does is not a spectator to the mission; he is a partner in it. It is the exact positive of 2 John's warning, where funding the wrong teacher made you a partner in his work.

"For the sake of the Name, taking nothing from the outsiders." They travelled as missionaries and refused support from non-believers — so that the message could never be mistaken for a paid enterprise. "The Name" stands alone, without "of Jesus" or "of the Lord" attached, the way a family says the name; and ethnikoi, "the outsiders" (literally the ethnic/gentile ones), here means simply those outside the community, whoever they are. The principle is that the church funds its own mission, precisely so that its preachers owe nothing to anyone they are preaching to.

Verses 9–10 · Diotrephes, who loves to be first

The one genuinely unpleasant portrait in John's letters, and it is of a churchman. Ho philoprōteuōn — "the one who loves to be first," a word coined for the occasion (it appears nowhere else in Greek literature before this) and a perfect one. KJV "who loveth to have the preeminence among them"; the NIV "who loves to be first". Diotrephes is not a heretic; nothing is said about his doctrine. His fault is entirely about place: he wants the top seat, and everything he does follows from that. ⚠️ It is worth sitting with how ordinary and how modern this is. The danger 2 John names is false teaching from outside; the danger 3 John names is ambition inside — and the second may be the harder of the two, because it wears the right clothes.

The escalation is precise. Read verse 10 as the ladder it is: he (1) does not receive us — he rejects the elder's own authority; (2) prates against us with wicked words — phluarōn, to talk nonsense, to babble accusations; (3) will not receive the brothers himself; (4) prevents those who want to; and (5) throws them out of the assembly — ekballei ek tēs ekklēsias, the first excommunication in the New Testament (KJV "casteth them out of the church"; NWT "tries to throw them out"), and it is being done by the wrong man, to the right people, for the wrong reason. A leader is expelling members for the crime of showing hospitality.

"If I come, I will bring to mind the works he does." ⚠️ Notice the restraint. The elder, whose authority is being flouted, does not command Gaius to depose Diotrephes, does not pronounce a curse, does not even write to the church again (his first letter, verse 9, was ignored). He says only that he will address it in person. It is the same instinct as the close of 2 John — I would rather not do this with ink — and it is striking in a man who plainly has the standing to do more. He intends to handle a personal defiance face to face, not by letter.

Verses 11–12 · imitate the good — and Demetrius

The one line of general teaching in the whole letter. "Do not imitate the bad but the good." Everything else is particular — Gaius, the brothers, Diotrephes, Demetrius — and then verse 11 lifts, for a sentence, into the plain Johannine test: the one who does good is from God; the one who does bad has not seen God. ⚠️ It is placed exactly between the bad example (Diotrephes) and the good one (Demetrius), and it tells Gaius which of his neighbours to copy without naming either in the sentence. "Has not seen God" is the same standard as 1 John — conduct reveals origin; you can read where a person comes from by what they do. KJV "he that doeth good is of God"; the ASV the same.

"Testified to by all, and by the truth itself." ⚠️ Demetrius appears from nowhere and vanishes; almost certainly he is the carrier of this very letter, a travelling brother arriving at Gaius's door who needs vouching for — which is precisely the situation the whole letter is about. So the elder vouches, in a rising series: by all who know him, by the truth itself (his life matches the message), and by us — "and you know that our testimony is true." He is handing Gaius exactly the recommendation 2 John's false teachers could never earn.

Verses 13–15 · ink, a reed, and friends by name

Almost the same ending as 2 John, with the tools spelled out differently. 2 John said "paper and ink" (chartēs and melan); here it is "ink and reed" — melan kai kalamos, the black and the reed pen, the sharpened kalamos that actually made the marks. Between the two letters you get the whole kit: the sheet, the ink, and the pen. And the same longing closes both: stoma pros stoma, mouth to mouth — kept literal here as there, against the usual "face to face."

⚠️ "Peace to you. Greet the friends by name." The ending is quietly extraordinary. Hoi philoi — "the friends" — is how this little circle refers to itself: not "the saints," not "the brothers," but the friends, the word Jesus used at John 15:15 ("I have called you friends"). And "greet them by name" — kat' onoma — is the smallest, most human instruction in the New Testament's correspondence: not a blanket greeting to the congregation but Gaius going round, one at a time, saying each person's name. For a letter that has just described a man who threw people out of the assembly, it is the perfect last line: name them, one by one, and leave nobody out.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the words exact / distinct: "prosper and be in health" left as the ordinary ancient health-wish it is, with the note refusing the prosperity-gospel reading; "and these, strangers" for the emphatic kai touto xenous; "send them on their way" for propempsas, the missionary outfitting-word; "fellow workers with the truth" for synergoi tē alētheia; "who loves to be first" for the coined philoprōteuōn; "throws them out of the assembly" kept plain; "mouth to mouth" for stoma pros stoma, not "face to face"; and "the friends… by name" left as literal as the Greek.

Echoes paid. 2 John's refusal of hospitality answered by its exact positive — the same principle protecting a different traveller; "mouth to mouth" and the reluctance to write, from the companion letter; and the Johannine test of 1 John — origin shown by conduct — dropped in for one verse.

Echoes planted. Propempō and the whole economy of the travelling mission that the letters of Paul assume; the friends, a congregation naming itself by Jesus' own word for them; and the uncomfortable permanence of Diotrephes — a man remembered in scripture for exactly one thing, that he loved to be first.

With 3 John the pair is complete: two single-sheet letters from the same elder, one telling a church to close a door against a false teacher, the other thanking a man for holding one open to true ones — and, between them, a churchman who shut the door on the wrong people because he loved the head of the table.
