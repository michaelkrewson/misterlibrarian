Written in the Persian province of Yehud, generations after the return — a genealogist walking from Adam to Edom to establish who his readers are.
⚠ Two clocks. The CONTENT spans from creation to the Edomite kings, recycling Genesis; the COMPOSITION belongs to the post-exilic period (commonly 4th century BC), which is why the chapter is filed here rather than at creation. Chronicles is a book about the deep past written for a small community with a temple, a governor and no king.

Translator’s Notes

Verses 1–4 · a book that begins with one word and no verb

«Adam.» That is the whole of verse 1’s first clause, and there is no sentence anywhere in the first four verses — nine names, three to a line, and then the world. It is the most extreme compression in the Bible. Genesis 5 takes thirty-two verses to walk from Adam to Noah, with every man’s age at fatherhood, every man’s total years, and the drumbeat “and he died” eight times over. The Chronicler gives you nine words and moves on. Even Enoch, who in Genesis walks with God and is not, because God took him, is here simply the name between Jared and Methuselah.

This is a deliberate method, and it is worth stating at the outset, because it governs everything that follows. Chronicles is not an alternative history for readers who do not know the story; it is written for readers who do. It assumes Genesis lying open on the table and lists only what its own argument needs. Nothing here is abbreviated from ignorance; it is abbreviated from confidence.

⚠ And the shape of the list is the message. Watch what happens at every fork: the line that is not carried forward is given first, in full, and then dropped — Japheth and Ham before Shem; Ishmael and Keturah’s sons before Isaac; Esau at length before Israel. Nobody is erased. Everyone is honored and then set down, so that at the end of nine generations and four continents there is exactly one name still standing. A reader in the small, poor, kingless province this book was written for is being told, without a word of argument, what line he is at the end of. And notice where the line starts: not with Abraham, and not with Israel. With Adam. The claim being made is as large as it could possibly be.

KJV, ASV and NIV all print these four verses as bare lists, correctly; the TLB supplies connecting words (“The descendants of Adam were…”) and in doing so quietly repairs the very thing that makes the opening remarkable.

Verses 5–23 · the whole known world, and one man who gets a verb

The Table of Nations, recycled. Verses 5–23 are Genesis 10 with the narrative sanded off — seventy-odd peoples of the ancient world arranged as a family: Japheth to the north and west (Gomer, Javan — the Greeks — Tubal, Meshech), Ham to the south (Cush, Egypt, Put, Canaan), Shem to the east (Elam, Asshur, Aram). It remains one of the most remarkable documents in the ancient world simply for what it takes for granted: that every people on the map is one family, and that the differences between them are genealogical rather than natural.

⚠ And in forty-two verses of names, exactly one man is given a verb. “And Cush fathered Nimrod; he was the first to become a mighty man on the earth” (v10). The Chronicler cuts Genesis 5’s ages, Genesis 10’s geography, Genesis 11’s tower — and keeps this. He also keeps two other fragments of comment: that the Philistines came out of the Casluhim (v12), which matters because Israel has spent four books fighting them; and Peleg’s name (v19), “for in his days the earth was divided” — niflegah, the pun this translation kept when it first appeared at Genesis 10:25, and the only etymology in the chapter. Three survivals out of a whole book of narrative. What a compiler keeps is a decision.

Small differences from Genesis, faithfully reproduced. Genesis 10:3 has Riphath where verse 6 has Diphath; Genesis 10:4 has Dodanim where verse 7 has Rodanim. In the Hebrew script d (ד) and r (ר) differ by the length of one stroke, and they are the single most commonly confused pair of letters in the Bible. We print what stands in each book rather than harmonizing them, and note the mechanism — it will matter a great deal by the end of this chapter. ⚠ Verse 11 also carries a ketiv/qere, the written and read forms of “Ludim” differing by a letter.

Verses 24–34 · “Abram — he is Abraham”

⚠ Four words for the greatest stretch of Genesis. Verse 27 reads, in full: Abram — he is Abraham. Between the two halves of that sentence lie the call at Haran, the journey to Canaan, the covenant of the pieces, Hagar and Ishmael, the covenant in the flesh, the three visitors at Mamre, the destruction of Sodom, the birth of Isaac, and the binding on Moriah. Genesis spends a chapter (17) on the name change alone, and God explains it. Chronicles states it as a bare apposition, the way one notes that a man is also known by another name.

The three verses before it are pure spine: Shem to Terah, nine names, no ages, no Ur, no Babel, no father who set out for Canaan and stopped halfway. The reader who does not already know that story will not learn it here. The reader who does is being shown its skeleton.

⚠ Keturah is called a CONCUBINE here (pilegesh, v32). Genesis 25:1 calls her Abraham’s wife (ishah). The difference is real and every version preserves it, because it is in the text of both books; what it means is arguable. It may be a later clarification of her status relative to Sarah, or a demotion in the interest of Isaac’s line, or simply the other of two words that could always be used of her. We print each book as it stands and do not reconcile them.

⚠ And the sons of Isaac are «Esau and ISRAEL.» Not Jacob. The name Jacob does not appear in this chapter at all — the Chronicler goes straight to the name given at the Jabbok and never mentions the one it replaced. Genesis takes thirty-two chapters, a stolen blessing, twenty years of exile and a night of wrestling to get from Jacob to Israel. Chronicles gets there by declining to say the first word. It is the single most eloquent omission in a chapter built out of omissions.

Verses 35–42 · Esau, at length — and the people who were there first

Esau gets nineteen verses; Israel, for now, gets nothing. The pattern of the chapter holds to the end: the brother who is not the line is honored first and in detail, and the line itself waits. Everything here is drawn from Genesis 36, and it includes the Horites — “the sons of Seir” (v38) — the people who lived in the red hills before Esau’s descendants displaced them, listed with the same care as everyone else.

⚠ A compression worth catching. Verse 36 lists Timna among the sons of Eliphaz, immediately before Amalek. Genesis 36:12 says something different and more specific: Timna was a concubine of Eliphaz, and she bore him Amalek. Timna is a woman, and in the very next verse of this chapter she reappears correctly as Lotan’s sister (v39). Compression has flattened a relationship into a list. It is the kind of thing that happens when a genealogy is copied for its shape rather than its story, and it is worth seeing once, plainly, rather than being smoothed over — NIV supplies “by Timna” to repair it; KJV and ASV print the flat list, as the Hebrew does, and so do we.

And there is Amalek again (v36), buried in a list as he was in Genesis 36 — Israel’s oldest and bitterest enemy, entered without comment as a great-grandson of Isaac. The reader is expected to feel it, and the Chronicler does not help.

Verses 43–54 · a king-list for people who have no king — and the copying made visible

«Before any king reigned over the sons of Israel.» A genealogy of the covenant line ends with twelve verses about foreign monarchs, and the note attached to them is that Edom had kings first. It was pointed in Genesis; it is much sharper here, because Chronicles was written for a community that had a temple and a governor and no king at all, and would not have one again. The book’s whole burden is the throne of David, and it opens by recording, without comment, that the brother-nation was organized as a monarchy while Israel was still a family. Eight kings, none of them a son of the last — the list is not a dynasty, and everyone reading it knew what a dynasty was.

⚠ And here you can watch a text being copied. These twelve verses are Genesis 36:31–43, and the two lists differ in a small, systematic and extremely instructive way:

• Genesis Hadar → Chronicles Hadad (v50) — ר / ד, one stroke.
• Genesis Pau → Chronicles Pai (v50) — ו / י, a long stroke and a short one.
• Genesis Hemam → Chronicles Homam; Genesis Alvan → Alian; Genesis Shepho → Shephi; Genesis Zepho → Zephi — the same two pairs again.

Daleth and resh, waw and yod: the two most easily confused pairs in the Hebrew alphabet, and they account for nearly every difference between these two lists. This is not a doctrinal problem and it is not a scandal — it is the visible fingerprint of hand-copying, in a place where we happen to possess the same list twice and can hold them side by side. It is one of the most concrete demonstrations in the whole Bible of how texts were transmitted, and it is a good deal more reassuring than it first sounds: after a thousand years of copying, the difference between two versions of a twelve-verse name-list is a handful of strokes.

«Chief» renders alluf, as it did in Genesis 36 — a clan-chief, not the KJV’s “duke,” which was accurate Jacobean English for a leader and is now a Wodehouse character. Note that Genesis 36:15 has “the chief of Teman” and this chapter has “chief Timna” — the Hebrew itself shifts between reading these as places and as persons, and we follow each book’s own construction.

⚠ A rarity in the Hebrew text, and the third on this site in three chapters: the Masoretes place a paragraph mark inside verse 51, between the death of the last king and the list of chiefs. The same feature appeared at 1 Kings 1:19 and 2 Kings 1:17. Nobody has satisfactorily explained it; we print it where they put it.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the exact / distinct words: the bare lists left bare, with no connecting words supplied, because the compression is the point; “fathered” for yalad, as in Genesis 5 and 10; “chief” for alluf, as in Genesis 36; “divided” for niflegah so Peleg’s name still puns; “concubine” for pilegesh where Genesis says wife, each book printed as it stands; and every spelling that differs from Genesis left standing rather than harmonized.

Echoes paid. Genesis 5’s ten generations, reduced to nine words; Genesis 10’s Table of Nations, with Nimrod’s lone verb and the Philistines and Peleg’s pun kept and everything else cut; Genesis 17’s renaming, reduced to “Abram — he is Abraham”; Genesis 25 and 36 for Ishmael, Keturah, Esau and the Horites; and the Edomite king-list of Genesis 36:31–43, reproduced closely enough that the copying itself is visible.

Echoes planted. Israel, the last name standing at the end of the funnel and the only one the book will follow — his twelve sons open chapter 2, and the genealogies run nine chapters before a single event is narrated. Judah and David, toward whom the whole apparatus is pointed. Amalek, dropped in a list and left to detonate later. And Adam — the deliberate first word of a book written for a people who might reasonably have wondered whether their story had ended.
