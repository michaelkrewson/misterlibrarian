Translator’s Notes

Verses 1–5 · Who is Jehovah?

The first confrontation. “Thus says Jehovah” — the messenger-formula of the prophets, spoken here to a king. And Pharaoh’s answer is the question the entire book exists to answer: “WHO IS JEHOVAH, that I should obey his voice? I do not know Jehovah.” ⚠ It is not merely defiance; from an Egyptian throne it is a statement of fact — this god is not on his lists. Everything that follows is aimed at it: the plagues come again and again “so that you may KNOW that I am Jehovah” (7:5, 17; 8:22; 14:4). The book is the answer to Pharaoh’s sneer. KJV “Who is the LORD, that I should obey his voice?”

⚠ The modest request. What Moses asks for is a three days’ journey to hold a feast and sacrifice — not, on its face, a permanent departure. Readers have long asked whether this is less than the whole truth. The likeliest reading is that it is a genuine first demand, a test of Pharaoh’s willingness in a small thing, which he refuses outright — so that the total demand (“let my people go,” with no return) follows his own refusal. The text offers no comment, and the note will not manufacture one.

Pharaoh’s counter is pure administration: “why do you take the people away from their work? Get to your burdens!” He hears a request for worship and files it as a labor problem. It is the whole conflict in miniature: to Pharaoh these are units of production; to Jehovah they are his firstborn son (4:22).

Verses 6–14 · bricks without straw

The retaliation is precise and cruel. Straw (teben) was chopped and mixed into Nile mud to bind the bricks — the practice is well attested in Egyptian brickmaking, as are the daily brick quotas recorded by Egyptian scribes. Pharaoh does not lower the quota; he removes the supply and keeps the number. The people must now scatter across Egypt gathering stubble and still deliver as before.

Two ranks stand between Pharaoh and the people: the NOGSIM, Egyptian taskmasters, and the SHOTRIM, Israelite foremen appointed over their own kinsmen. And it is the Israelite foremen who are beaten when the quota fails — the cruelty of every such system, that it makes the oppressed enforce their own oppression and then punishes them when it breaks.

«They are idle» (nirpim). Pharaoh’s theory of worship: a people with time to think about God is a people with too little work. It is the oldest slander against the sabbath, and he will repeat it, doubled, in verse 17.

Verses 15–19 · the fault is your own people’s

The foremen appeal over the taskmasters’ heads to Pharaoh himself — and get his word thrown back at them doubled: “Idle, you are idle!” The appeal to the top, in a system like this, only confirms that the cruelty comes from the top.

⚠ Their protest ends in a genuinely difficult phrase (v16): chatat ammekha — either “the fault is your own people’s” (your Egyptians are to blame) or “you sin against your own people” (a far bolder accusation, aimed at Pharaoh himself). The Hebrew genuinely supports both, and the versions divide: KJV “the fault is in thine own people”; the ESV “the fault is in your own people.” This translation keeps the ambiguity rather than choosing a side for the reader.

And so the foremen “saw that they were in trouble.” The first casualties of the deliverance are the men who tried to work within the system.

Verses 20–23 · you have not delivered your people at all

The chapter ends in a double collapse. The foremen, coming out from Pharaoh, meet Moses and Aaron waiting and turn on them: “May Jehovah look upon you and judge — you have made us a stench in Pharaoh’s eyes and put a sword in his hand to kill us.” The people God sent Moses to save now count him their disaster.

And Moses turns on God, with a bluntness Scripture does not censor: “My Lord, why have you brought evil on this people? Why did you ever send me?… and you have not delivered your people at all.” KJV “neither hast thou delivered thy people at all.”

⚠ This is the honest shape of the story, and it is worth saying plainly: the FIRST result of God’s word of deliverance is that things get WORSE. The oppression intensifies, the people despair, and the deliverer accuses God to his face. The Bible neither hides this nor rebukes Moses for it — chapter 6 opens with the answer, “NOW you shall see what I will do to Pharaoh.” The dawn is real; but it comes after the darkest hour, and Exodus makes the reader sit in that hour for a whole chapter before it breaks. (Jeremiah will one day accuse God in the same register, 20:7.)

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the exact / distinct words: “Let my people go” for shalach, the exodus refrain, kept identical to “let my SON go” of 4:23; “I do not know Jehovah” for yada, the verb the plagues exist to reverse; “straw” and “quota” kept concrete, as the Egyptian brick-records have them; “taskmasters” (nogsim) held distinct from “foremen” (shotrim), the Egyptians over the Israelites over their own; “idle” for nirpim; and the ambiguity of “the fault is your own people’s” (v16) left standing.

Echoes paid. “Let my people go” answering “Israel is my firstborn son… let my son go” (4:22-23); Pharaoh’s “I do not know Jehovah” set against the Name revealed at the bush (3:14-15); and the “burdens” (sivlot) that were the whole condition Jehovah said he had come down to see (3:7).

Echoes planted. The question “Who is Jehovah?”, which the ten plagues will answer one by one (“that you may know that I am Jehovah”); the hardening already promised at 4:21, now visible in Pharaoh’s flat refusal; and Moses’ complaint, which chapter 6 answers with the great “I am Jehovah” speech and the promise, “NOW you shall see what I will do to Pharaoh.”

Next in this book: Exodus 6 — God’s answer to Moses’ accusation: “I am Jehovah… I appeared to Abraham, to Isaac, and to Jacob as God Almighty, but by my name Jehovah I did not make myself known to them” — the seven “I will” promises of redemption, spoken to a people so crushed by their labor that they cannot hear them.
