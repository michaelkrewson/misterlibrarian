The word that restarted the temple: a discouraged remnant, busy roofing its own houses while God's house lies a ruin, is asked to 'set your heart on your ways' — and, uniquely among the prophets, OBEYS, breaking ground twenty-three days later. The chapter's engine is not a threat but a presence: 'I am with you, declares Jehovah.'
The first day of the sixth month in the second year of Darius the king — 29 August 520 BC, dated to the day. Eighteen years after the first exiles came home under Cyrus (538) and laid a foundation that then sat untouched for some sixteen years; Haggai and his contemporary Zechariah rouse the rebuilding, and the second temple is finished in 516 (Ezra 6:15). The calendar now runs by a Persian emperor, for there is no king in Jerusalem.

Translator’s Notes

Verse 1 · a prophecy dated by a Persian king — the first voice of the second temple

The clock has been reset. Every earlier prophet dated himself by an Israelite or Judahite reign — “in the year King Uzziah died” (Isaiah 6:1), “two years before the earthquake” (Amos 1:1). Haggai dates himself by Darius the king — a foreign emperor in Susa. There is no king in Jerusalem; there has not been one for seventy years. The kingdom of David is a Persian province called Yehud, and even its calendar now hangs from a Gentile throne. This one phrase is the whole condition of the post-exilic age in five words.

Dated to the DAY — the most precisely-timed of the prophets. “The second year of Darius, the sixth month, day one” is 29 August 520 BC (the new moon of Elul). Haggai’s whole book is four such dated oracles inside a single autumn, Elul to Kislev; nothing in it is undatable. That precision is not incidental — a book about getting a long-postponed job actually STARTED keeps a calendar.

The two men it is addressed to are the prince and the priest. Zerubbabel son of Shealtiel is the Persian-appointed governor (Hebrew pechah, a loan-word for the imperial office) — but he is also of the house of David, the grandson of the exiled king Jehoiachin, the last real Davidic hope in the Old Testament. Joshua son of Jehozadak is the first high priest of the rebuilt temple, grandson of the Seraiah whom Nebuchadnezzar executed at the fall of Jerusalem (2 Kings 25:18–21). Between them they are the two offices that will carry Israel through the age with no king — and Zechariah, Haggai’s exact contemporary, will make them his “two olive trees, the two sons of oil” (Zechariah 4). ASV keeps the office literal, “Zerubbabel … governor of Judah”; NWT the same; KJV too. The book of Ezra names this exact moment from the outside: “the prophets, Haggai and Zechariah … prophesied … and Zerubbabel … began to build the house of God” (Ezra 5:1–2).

Verses 2–4 · “the time has not come” — and the roof over their own heads that gives them the lie

The excuse is a single doubled word: ‘et’, “time.” “Not the TIME — the time for the house of Jehovah to be built.” It is the oldest evasion there is: not no, just not yet — the economy is bad, the neighbours are hostile (the work had in fact been stalled some sixteen years, Ezra 4), the moment is never quite right. Haggai answers the excuse with its own word, turned back as a question: “Is it a TIME for YOU …?” (v4). If it is not the time to roof God’s house, how is it the time to roof your own?

“Paneled houses” renders batim sefunim — safun is wood-paneling, the cedar ceiling that was the mark of a finished, luxurious house (Solomon “paneled the house with cedar,” 1 Kings 6:9; Jeremiah’s doom on the king who must have his “paneled” rooms in cedar, Jeremiah 22:14). The contrast is exact and damning: their houses are sefunim, finished and ceiled; God’s house is charev, “a ruin.” It is the inversion of David’s old unease — “I dwell in a house of cedar, but the ark of God dwells in a tent” (2 Samuel 7:2). David could not bear the gap; this generation has stopped noticing it. NIV “paneled houses”; ASV “ceiled houses”; KJV the same “cieled”; NWT “paneled houses,” with the ruined house set against it.

Verses 5–7 · “set your heart on your ways” — and the wages that fall through the purse

The refrain of the chapter is simu levavchem ‘al darkeichem — literally “set your heart upon your ways” — a call not to work harder but to notice, to reckon up how the strategy of “me first, God later” has actually been paying out. It frames the whole complaint (vv5, 7) and returns twice more in chapter 2. NWT “Set your heart upon your ways”; ASV the same; NIV paraphrases the idiom, “Give careful thought to your ways.”

The futility curse (v6) is kept in its five hammer-blows. Sown much / brought in little; eat / never full; drink / never your fill; dress / no one warm; and the last, sharpest image: “the wage-earner earns wages into a bag full of holes” (tzeror naqub, a pierced purse) — money that is gone the moment it is earned. This is deliberate covenant language: the anti-blessing of Deuteronomy 28:38–39 (“you will sow much and gather little”) and Leviticus 26:26 (“you will eat and not be satisfied”). TLB makes the picture plain — “your wages disappear as though you were putting them in pockets filled with holes”; KJV keeps it literal, “a bag with holes”; Geneva the same. The point is not scarcity but LEAK: the effort is real, only nothing STAYS — because the one thing it was all built around was left a ruin.

Verse 8 · go, fetch ordinary wood, and build — “that I may be glorified”

The command is startlingly plain: go up the mountain, bring wood, build. Not cedar shipped from Lebanon (that was for the foundation, Ezra 3:7), but ordinary timber from the nearby hills — this second temple will be a humbler thing than Solomon’s, and Haggai knows it (2:3 will ask the old men who remembered the first, “is it not, in your eyes, as nothing?”). Yet God’s response to the plain house is ve’ertzeh bo ve’ekavdah: “I will take PLEASURE in it and be GLORIFIED.” The verb is kavod, the weight-of-glory that filled Solomon’s temple in a cloud (1 Kings 8:11) and then abandoned it in Ezekiel’s vision (Ezekiel 10–11). Haggai’s promise, two verses into the future (2:7–9), is that the kavod comes back: “the latter glory of this house will be greater than the former.”

⚠ A ketiv/qere sits on the last word. The written text (ketiv) is ’ekaved, “I will be honored”; the read tradition (qere) is ’ekavdah, a cohortative “let me be glorified” — the Masoretes print both, and so do we, unpointed then pointed, the difference a shade of emphasis, not of sense. ASV “I will … be glorified”; NWT the same.

Verses 9–11 · the wordplay at the heart of the book: a ruined house, a ruined land

⚠ The literary key of the whole chapter is a pun on three consonants, ח‑ר‑ב (ch-r-b). God’s house lies charev — “a ruin, a waste” (vv4, 9). So, says v11, “I called a chorev — a DROUGHT, a drying-up — upon the land.” Same root: the desolation they left on God’s house, God has returned, letter for letter, on their fields. Because you left my house charev, I have made your land chorev. English cannot keep the single word, but the measure-for-measure is the point, and the note keeps it visible.

“I blew it away” (nafachti bo, v9): the little they did gather, God puffed out of their hands like chaff. The withheld dew and the summoned drought (vv10–11) fall on a list that reads like the creation account run dry — “the grain, the new wine, the fresh oil” (the classic triad dagan / tirosh / yitzhar), “whatever the ground brings forth … humankind … beast … all the labor of your hands.” Where Zephaniah had God sweep the same categories off the ground, Haggai has him quietly dry them out. KJV “I did blow upon it”; ASV the same; NIV “I blew it away.” NWT keeps the drought concrete, “a drying up … upon the land and upon the mountains.”

Verses 12–14 · the one prophet whose audience OBEYS — and the four words that move them

This is the rarest thing in the prophets: they listen. Isaiah preached to people who would not hear; Jeremiah was thrown in a cistern; Jonah’s only converts were pagan sailors. Haggai says a few dozen words and “Zerubbabel … and Joshua … and all the remnant of the people OBEYED the voice of Jehovah their God … and the people feared before Jehovah” (v12). Twenty-three days later they are on the site (v15). There is no other prophetic book where the message simply WORKS.

What moves them is not a threat but a presence. Haggai — here given the unique title mal’ach YHVH, “the messenger of Jehovah, in the message of Jehovah” (the only prophet in the Hebrew Bible called God’s messenger; it is the very word that becomes the NAME Malachi, “my messenger”) — delivers one line: ’ani ’itchem, “I AM WITH YOU, declares Jehovah” (v13). It is the covenant word spoken to Moses at the bush (Exodus 3:12), to Joshua (Joshua 1:5), to Gideon, to Jeremiah — and it is the engine of the whole book. Not “work harder,” but “I am with you.” ASV “I am with you, saith Jehovah”; NWT “‘I am with you,’ is the utterance of Jehovah”; NIV “I am with you.”

And then it is God who does the building. “Jehovah stirred up the spirit (va-ya’ar … ’et ruach) of Zerubbabel … and of Joshua … and of all the remnant” (v14). It is the same verb (‘ur, to rouse) used of the very thing that let them come home in the first place: “Jehovah stirred up the spirit of Cyrus king of Persia” (Ezra 1:1; 2 Chronicles 36:22). The book’s theology in one sentence: God stirs the will, and then the willing hands build. (A small textual grace-note: v1 spells the father Shealtiel, vv12 and 14 the shorter Shaltiel — the same name, the fuller and the contracted form side by side.)

Verse 15 · the day the shovels moved — twenty-three days from word to work

The chapter ends on a date, not a doxology. “On day twenty-four of the sixth month” — 21 September 520 BC. The word came on day one (v1); the work began on day twenty-four. Twenty-three days from the sermon to the first timber: for a project that had sat untouched for the better part of two decades, the speed IS the miracle. A book obsessed with dates closes its first movement by telling you exactly which day the excuse died.

⚠ A versification seam runs here. The Masoretic Text keeps v15 as the CLOSE of chapter 1 — the day they started. Some English printings pull the phrase forward to open chapter 2 (so that Haggai’s next dated oracle, on the twenty-first of the seventh month, is given a running start). We follow the Hebrew: chapter 1 has fifteen verses and ends on the day the building resumed. The second temple these hands began would stand, finished, in 516 BC (Ezra 6:15) — and stand five centuries, until Herod remade it and Rome burned it.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the exact / distinct words: “paneled houses” for batim sefunim (v4), set against “a ruin” for charev, so the roof-over-my-own-head rebuke lands; “set your heart on your ways” for simu levavchem (vv5, 7), the refrain that asks them to reckon, not to hustle; “a bag full of holes” for tzeror naqub (v6), wages that leak the moment they’re earned; “governor” for the Persian office pechah; “the messenger of Jehovah” for mal’ach YHVH (v13), the title that is elsewhere a NAME; and “I am with you” for ’ani ’itchem, kept bare because its bareness is its power.

The wordplay paid. ח‑ר‑ב — the house left a ruin (charev, vv4, 9) answered by the land struck with drought (chorev, v11): the desolation they left on God’s house returned, letter for letter, on their fields. And the creation-list of v11 (grain, wine, oil, ground, human, beast) read against Zephaniah’s de-creation of the same categories — there God sweeps them off the ground, here he dries them up.

Echoes planted. “I am with you” (v13) — the covenant thread from the burning bush (Exodus 3:12) forward, and Immanuel’s far horizon. The two olive trees — Zerubbabel the governor and Joshua the priest, whom the contemporary Zechariah (ch 3–4, 6) will crown and call “the Branch,” and whom Haggai’s own last oracle (2:23) will make the signet ring — reversing the un-signeting of Zerubbabel’s grandfather Jehoiachin at Jeremiah 22:24. And the glory of the latter house (2:7–9), the returning kavod — the promise that the plain little temple these hands began would, in the end, hold a greater glory than Solomon’s.

Haggai is a new book on the site. Next in Haggai: chapter 2 — “who is left among you who saw this house in its former glory? … is it not, in your eyes, as nothing?”; “yet once more, in a little while, I will shake the heavens and the earth … and I will fill this house with glory”; and the closing word to Zerubbabel: “I will make you like a signet ring, for I have chosen you.”
