The dark chapter at Shechem — Dinah violated by the prince, the brothers' answer 'with deceit,' and the massacre of the whole town by Simeon and Levi, that Jacob rebukes only for the danger it brings on him.
AM ≈ 2206 · c. 1739 BC — soon after Jacob settles near Shechem. The prince Shechem violates Dinah; her brothers trick the men of the town into circumcision, and on the third day Simeon and Levi put them all to the sword and plunder the city, carrying off its women and children. The atrocity forces the family off the land and drives the flight to Bethel in the next chapter — and it is not forgiven: Jacob curses the two brothers' swords on his deathbed (49:5-7).

Translator's Notes — verse by verse

The dark chapter at Shechem: the violation of Dinah, the brothers' deceit, and a massacre the text refuses to either bless or excuse. It is told plainly and unsparingly, and these notes try to do the same — naming the crime a crime, and the revenge a revenge. Quotations from copyrighted versions (NIV, TLB, NWT) are kept to short phrases for comparison; KJV, Geneva, Douay-Rheims and ASV are public domain.

Verses 1–4 · וַיְעַנֶּהָ vay'anneha — "and he violated her"

The verb the text will not soften. Dinah "goes out" to see the women of the land, and the sentence turns brutal in three quick verbs: Shechem "saw her, and took her, and lay with her, and violated her." That last word is anah (the intensive innah) — to force, degrade, break down; the same verb the law uses for the rape of an unbetrothed woman (Deuteronomy 22:29) and that names what Amnon does to Tamar (2 Samuel 13). The shelf hears its weight unevenly: the NWT and NIV render it "violated," the Douay "by force," while the KJV and ASV settle for "humbled" — a genteel word for a crime, and one this translation declines. There is also a grammar of force in the plain Hebrew: it says he "lay her" (vayishkav otah, direct object), not the ordinary "lay with her" — the accusative itself reads like something done to her, not with her.

And then the whiplash. The very next verse says "his soul clung to Dinah… and he loved the girl, and spoke to her heart" — dibber al-lev, the tender idiom of comfort and courtship (Boaz will "speak to the heart" of Ruth). The text sets the violation and the "love" side by side without a word of comment, and the horror of that juxtaposition is the point: the man who has just wrecked her now wants to keep her, and sends his father to "get me this girl" as though she were a purchase. The narrator names her four times — Dinah, the girl, the girl again — and never once lets Shechem's tenderness stand in for her voice. She does not speak in this chapter. That silence is itself the wound.

Verses 5–12 · נְבָלָה nevalah — "an outrage in Israel"

Jacob's first silence. Word reaches the father — "Jacob heard that he had defiled (timme) his daughter" — and Jacob does nothing: "he held his peace (hecherish) until they came." It is the first of two silences that frame the chapter, and it will matter that the man renamed Israel, who wrestled God and prevailed, cannot find a word for his own daughter's ruin. The sons, out in the field, are the ones who feel it — "the men were grieved, and they burned with anger," for Shechem had done a nevalah in Israel.

The Bible's word for the unspeakable. Nevalah is not ordinary wrong; it is the fixed, heavy term for an atrocity that tears the fabric of a people — the same word for the gang-rape of the Levite's concubine that starts a civil war (Judges 19–20) and for what Amnon does to Tamar ("no such nevalah is done in Israel," 2 Samuel 13:12). The KJV flattens it to "folly," and the ASV follows — far too mild; the NIV "disgraceful thing" and the NWT "disgraceful folly" reach a little higher, but the force is closer to outrage, a thing that "should not be done." And note the phrase "in Israel": Jacob was renamed only two chapters ago, and here the new name has its first use as a people — a moral boundary — and it lands, of all places, on a crime. The nation is barely named before it must reckon with an atrocity inside its own tents.

Two offers. Hamor comes proposing a merger — intermarry, dwell, trade, "take holdings" — the language of assimilation that would quietly dissolve Abraham's set-apart line into the Canaanite towns. Shechem himself is more frantic and more honest: "let me find favor… pile on me a very great bride-price (mohar) and gift — only give me the girl." He will pay any price; the one thing neither he nor his father says is sorry.

Verses 13–24 · בְּמִרְמָה b'mirmah — "with deceit," the family trade

The word that runs in the blood. "The sons of Jacob answered… with deceit (b'mirmah)." Of all the words the narrator could have chosen, this is the one that indicts the whole family, because it is Jacob's own. His father Isaac said it of him: "your brother came with deceit (mirmah) and took your blessing" (27:35). Laban paid it back on him — the wrong sister in the dark — and Jacob's cry was the same word: "why have you deceived me?" (29:25). Now the deceiver's sons deceive in their turn, and one day they will deceive Jacob himself with a bloodied coat (ch. 37). The trait breeds true. The KJV renders it "deceitfully" (elsewhere "subtilty"), the NWT "with deception," the ASV "with guile" — all agreed on the ugly thing: this was a lie from the first word.

The covenant sign, made a weapon. And notice what they weaponize. Their "condition" is circumcision — the sign God gave Abraham as the everlasting mark of the covenant (ch. 17). The brothers dress a massacre in the language of faith ("we cannot give our sister to a man who is uncircumcised… a disgrace to us," cherpah), knowing full well that the third day after the surgery every man in town will be helpless. The holiest sign in their inheritance is turned into a surgeon's ambush. Whatever one thinks of the revenge, this detail the text does not let us admire.

Sold at the gate. Hamor and Shechem carry the deal to their townsmen, and here the mask slips: they do not sell it with love but with greed — "their livestock and their property and all their beasts — will they not be ours?" (v. 23). Each side is working the other; the beautiful phrase "become one people" is, on both lips, a swindle. And a quiet, bitter echo hangs over it: Jacob had just come "in peace" (shalem, 33:18) to this very city and bought his field "from the sons of Hamor" (33:19); the townsmen now call his people "peaceable" (shlemim, v. 21) — the same root — the last word anyone will be able to use of what happens next.

Verses 25–29 · the third day — Simeon and Levi

Two men, a whole town. "On the third day, when they were in pain," two of Jacob's sons — Simeon and Levi, "Dinah's brothers" — take each his sword and fall on the city "unopposed" and kill every male. The Hebrew betach carries the chill: it means "in security," and the versions split over whose — the KJV "came upon the city boldly" (the brothers' confidence), the ASV and NWT "unawares / while it felt secure" (the town's false safety). Both are true at once: a city that thought itself at peace, cut down by men who knew it could not fight back. They kill Hamor and Shechem "by the edge of the sword" (literally "the mouth of the sword," l'fi-charev), take Dinah out of the house, and go.

The narrator says nothing — and that is the verdict withheld. Then the rest of the sons come to plunder: flocks, herds, donkeys, wealth, and "all their little ones and their wives… they captured" — the women and children of the murdered men carried off, the "one people" now spoils. The report is flat, itemized, without a single adjective of horror or of praise. The scale is the argument the text lets stand on its own: one man committed the crime, and a whole city of men is slaughtered and its families enslaved for it. The narrator does not tell us what to feel. He does not have to.

Verses 30–31 · the rebuke that never names the wrong, and the question no one answers

Jacob's second silence. The father finally speaks — and every word is about himself. "You have brought trouble (achartem) on me, making me a stench to the inhabitants of the land… I am few in number; they will gather against me, and I will be destroyed, I and my house." Not one syllable for Dinah, for the dead, for right or wrong — only the danger to Jacob. The KJV "ye have troubled me to make me to stink," the NIV "made me obnoxious," the NWT "made me a stench" — the picture is a man worried about his reputation and his skin. His silence at the start was a father's paralysis; his silence about the morality, here at the end, is harder to forgive.

The last word is a question. "But they said, 'Should he treat our sister like a prostitute?'" — and the chapter simply stops. No answer from Jacob, none from the narrator. Both sides stand indicted and neither is acquitted: the rape was real and monstrous; the revenge was mass murder of the innocent and the enslavement of their families. The text holds the two in an unresolved grip — and leaves the reader in it too. This has always divided its readers: some hear the brothers as the only ones who acted, avengers of a sister nobody else defended; others hear cold, disproportionate slaughter cloaked in covenant language. The translation's job is to let both readings stand where the Hebrew leaves them — and to note that the Bible itself does not, in the end, stay neutral. The verdict only comes a lifetime later, at Jacob's deathbed, and it comes down against the brothers: "Simeon and Levi are brothers; weapons of violence are their swords… cursed be their anger, for it is fierce" (49:5-7). For that day's work the two tribes are scattered in Israel — Levi given no land of his own, Simeon absorbed into Judah. The question Dinah's brothers throw down here is answered, at last, by their father's dying mouth.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the words exact / unsoftened: "violated" for anah (not the KJV/ASV "humbled"); "an outrage in Israel" for nevalah (not the KJV's thin "folly"); "with deceit" for b'mirmah; "bride-price" for mohar; "at peace / peaceable" for shlemim (holding the echo of shalem); the covenant-sign named plainly; and Jacob's rebuke left as self-interested as the Hebrew makes it.

Echoes paid. Mirmah — the family's signature — passed from Jacob's theft of the blessing (27:35) through Laban's trick (29:25) to the sons here; Shechem, the town Jacob had just entered "in peace" and where he bought a field from Hamor's sons (33:18-19), destroyed by his own; shalem → shlemim, the "peace" of the arrival turned bitter at the gate; the covenant circumcision (ch. 17) made a weapon; and the new name Israel (ch. 32) getting its first collective use as the measure of an atrocity. Echoes planted: Jacob's silence and the brothers' swords, waiting for the deathbed curse of Simeon and Levi (49:5-7); Levi the avenger, whose descendants will become the priesthood; and the blood at Shechem that sends the family fleeing to Bethel next — where "the terror of God" falls on the cities around so that none pursue (35:5).
