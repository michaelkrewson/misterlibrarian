📖 Lamentations

אֵיכָה Eikhah — 'How…!' — the book's first word, and the opening cry of a Hebrew funeral dirge. It is the same word Isaiah sings over Jerusalem while the city is still standing (1:21) and David sings over Saul and Jonathan ('How the mighty have fallen'). ⚠ The Talmud also calls the book Qinot, 'Dirges,' after its metre.
Θρῆνοι (Thrēnoi) — 'Dirges' — the Septuagint's title, which Latin took as Threni and then as Lamentationes, giving the English name. ⚠ The Greek adds a superscription the Hebrew does not have: 'And it came to pass, after Israel was taken captive and Jerusalem laid waste, that Jeremiah sat weeping and lamented this lamentation over Jerusalem, and said…' — which is where the traditional attribution to Jeremiah comes from, and it is a translator's note, not part of the Hebrew book.

Five poems over a burned city, four of them alphabetical — the most formally controlled writing in the Bible about the least controllable thing that happens to people. It concedes that the verdict was just and goes on grieving anyway, and it never once names Babylon.

Chapter by chapter

Lamentations 1Twenty-two verses, because the Hebrew alphabet has twenty-two letters and each verse begins with the next one — the most tightly controlled writing in the Bible, about the least controllable thing that happens to people. Jerusalem has burned, and the city is a widow sitting alone: the roads to Zion mourn because nobody is walking on them, priests and elders die in the streets while out looking for food, and five separate times the poem says the same thing — SHE HAS NO COMFORTER. Not that there is no rescue; that there is nobody sitting with her. Halfway through, the city interrupts the description of herself and takes the poem over, and she concedes the verdict was just. Babylon burned this city, and Babylon is not named once in five chapters — every clause has God as its subject, which is exactly why the complaints can be addressed to him.

About the book

Where it sits
One of the Five Megillot (Festival Scrolls) among the Writings, read on the NINTH OF AV, the fast that mourns the destruction of both temples. ⚠ In the Septuagint and in Christian Bibles it is moved out of the Writings and placed after Jeremiah, on the strength of the Greek superscription — which is why English Bibles print it between Jeremiah and Ezekiel rather than between Ruth and Ecclesiastes. Fragments were found at Qumran (3QLam, 4QLam, 5QLam-a and -b), and 4QLam preserves some readings that differ from the Masoretic text.
Genre
DIRGE — the funeral lament (qinah), applied to a city instead of a person, in a limping metre of three beats then two so that every line falls short of its own balance. ⚠ Four of the five poems are ACROSTICS: chapters 1, 2 and 4 have twenty-two verses, one for each letter of the alphabet; chapter 3 has sixty-six, three per letter; chapter 5 has twenty-two verses and no acrostic at all, which is itself a statement. Woven through are the forms of communal complaint — the appeal to be looked at, the concession of guilt, the imprecation against the onlookers — and, at the exact centre of the middle poem, four verses of hope that the rest of the book does not build on.
Author
Anonymous in the Hebrew. The traditional ascription to JEREMIAH is old and universal — it stands in the Greek superscription, in the Targum, in the Talmud (Bava Batra 15a) and in Christian tradition — and 2 Chronicles 35:25 records that Jeremiah composed laments, though for Josiah, not for the city. ⚠ Against it: the Hebrew book makes no such claim; the poems are acrostics, a form Jeremiah never uses; and several of their positions are hard to square with his book — Lamentations 4:17 looks to Egypt for help, which Jeremiah denounced, and 4:20 mourns Zedekiah as 'the breath of our nostrils,' which is warmer than anything Jeremiah says of that king. Most scholarship now reads the five poems as the work of one or more eyewitnesses, possibly not the same hand throughout.
Date written
Very close to the events: 586 BC and the years immediately after, before the return under Cyrus in 538. The detail is the argument — who starved, where they died, what people ate, the fact that the priests and elders went out looking for food and did not come back. Chapter 5 assumes the ruin is still standing and the exile still running.
Place
JERUSALEM, entirely — its gates, its roads, its sanctuary, its streets. There is no view from Babylon in this book. It is written from inside the wreckage, by someone who stayed.
Audience
The people left in the land, and the people taken from it: a community that had lost its city, its temple, its king and its explanation at the same time. The poems give them words for it — literally, since they are still the words used on the ninth of Av, read aloud in the dark.

How it's laid out

1ACROSTIC — the city as a widow, sitting alone, with no comforter. A narrator describes her; halfway through she interrupts and takes the poem over, and she concedes that the verdict was just.
2ACROSTIC — the fiercest chapter: God himself as the enemy, bending his bow against his own people, and mothers with dying children in the streets. ⚠ Here the letters ayin and pe are reversed.
3TRIPLE ACROSTIC, sixty-six verses — an individual voice: 'I am the man who has seen affliction.' At its exact centre, verses 22-24: his mercies are new every morning, great is your faithfulness. Then the poem returns to the dark and keeps going.
4ACROSTIC — the siege remembered in detail: gold gone dim, children's tongues stuck to the roofs of their mouths, and the shocking line that those killed by the sword were better off than those who starved.
5NO ACROSTIC — a plain communal prayer, the shortest poem, ending on the book's last two verses: 'restore us to yourself… unless you have utterly rejected us.'

What it's about

  • GRIEF GIVEN A SHAPE — the alphabet is the book's central formal fact. Grief has no natural container and no natural end; an acrostic supplies both, and says 'everything, from A to Z,' and guarantees the poem will stop.
  • NO COMFORTER — the refrain of chapter 1, five times in twenty-two verses. Not that there is no rescue: that there is nobody sitting with her. The answer, when the Bible finally gives one, is the first line of Isaiah 40 — and it is not in this book.
  • GOD AS THE AGENT — Babylon burned Jerusalem, and Babylon is never named. Every clause of destruction has God as its subject: he grieved her, he sent fire, he trod the winepress. That is not evasion; it is the reason the complaints can be addressed to him at all.
  • THE VERDICT CONCEDED — 'Jehovah is righteous, for I rebelled against his mouth' (1:18). Lamentations does not protest innocence, which is what separates it from most poetry of catastrophe. It agrees, and grieves anyway.
  • HOPE, PLACED AND NOT RESOLVED — the famous verses ('his mercies are new every morning') sit at the mathematical centre of the book and are followed by two and a half more chapters of unrelieved lament. The structure says something the sentences do not: hope is real, central, and does not end the grief.
  • AN UNANSWERED ENDING — the last verse is a conditional that hangs: 'unless you have utterly rejected us.' Synagogue practice repeats verse 21 after verse 22 so the reading does not end there, which is an old and honest admission of how the book actually closes.

The source text

Translated from the pointed Hebrew Masoretic Text (as printed by Mechon-Mamre). Chapter 1 alone carries three ketiv/qere notes (vv 6, 11, 18), all preserved in the Hebrew column, and a word that occurs nowhere else in the Bible (nisqad, v14) whose meaning is a reasoned guess in every version including this one. ⚠ The acrostic makes the text unusually well protected — a missing or reordered verse is immediately visible — and it also makes the ayin/pe reversal in chapters 2-4 a genuine puzzle rather than a scribal slip; the same order appears on an abecedary found at Kuntillet Ajrud. The Septuagint's added superscription naming Jeremiah is not translated here, since it is not in the Hebrew.

Looking forward

The New Testament never quotes Lamentations. What it inherits is the posture: a city wept over rather than argued with, which is what Luke 19:41 describes Jesus doing on the descent from the Mount of Olives, and again in the words about a house left desolate. Christian reading has long heard 1:12 — 'is there any pain like my pain?' — as a Good Friday text, and Handel set it that way; that is a use of the verse, not its meaning, and the library says so plainly. The deeper connection is structural: this is the book that establishes that faith can say the worst thing out loud, in God's own presence, without either denying the justice of it or pretending it is bearable.

Where the debates are

AUTHORSHIP — Jeremiah, as the Greek superscription and every ancient tradition say, or an anonymous eyewitness whose form and politics do not match his. WHETHER 3:22-24 IS THE BOOK'S CENTRE OR ITS EXCEPTION — a turn the rest builds on, or a moment the rest overwhelms. THE AYIN/PE REVERSAL in chapters 2-4, evidence of an older alphabetical order or of separate composition. HOW MUCH OF CHAPTER 2 CAN BE SAID — whether 'the Lord has become like an enemy' is hyperbole or the book's actual claim. And the ending, which is either a prayer or a fear, and which the synagogue's reading practice quietly declines to leave as it stands.
