Translator's Notes — verse by verse

Each note explains this translation's choice and compares the seven versions on the shelf. The Greek shown is the SBL Greek New Testament (ed. Michael W. Holmes, Society of Biblical Literature, 2010), a modern critical text in the Alexandrian tradition; quotations from the copyrighted English versions (NIV, TLB, NWT) are kept to short phrases for comparison, and the KJV, Geneva, Douay-Rheims, and ASV are public domain.

Verses 1–7 · Ephesus · τὴν ἀγάπην σου τὴν πρώτην ἀφῆκες tēn agapēn sou tēn prōtēn aphēkes

The anatomy of a letter. All seven letters share one form, worth learning on the first: an address ("to the angel of the congregation in…"); a title of the speaker, drawn from chapter 1's vision (here: the stars and lampstands of 1:13-16 — each city gets the facet of the vision it needs); "I know…" — praise first, wherever praise is possible; the complaint ("but I have this against you"); a call — "the one who has an ear, let him hear"; and a promise "to the one who overcomes" (ho nikōn, the victor), every one of which is paid out in the book's last two chapters. A word about the word: "congregation" here renders ekklēsia — an assembly of people, with no building and no institution in it (Acts 19 uses the same word for the Ephesus riot). It was Tyndale's rendering, and King James's translation rules expressly forbade it — "the word Church not to be translated Congregation"; the dictionary entry tells the whole story. Ephesus — the province's great harbor city, home of the temple of Artemis and, by tradition, of John himself — gets the fullest praise in the set: toil, endurance, doctrinal vigilance (they "tested those who call themselves apostles… and found them false"). And then the sentence that has stung readers for nineteen centuries: "you have left your first love" — orthodoxy intact, the fire gone; and the threat is not persecution but removal: "I will remove your lampstand." (The Nicolaitans, hated here and taught at Pergamum: honestly unknown — Irenaeus guessed followers of Nicolaus of Acts 6:5; the letters themselves pair them with "Balaam's teaching," idol-food and immorality, which is likely all we can say.)

The tree, again. The first overcomer-promise reaches all the way back to this project's first pages: "to eat from the tree of life, which is in the paradise of God." Paradeisos — a Persian loan-word for a walled garden — is the very word the Greek Bible chose for the garden of Eden; and the tree of life is the one whose way home has been under guard since "the cherubim… and a flaming sword… to guard the way to the tree of life" (Genesis 3:24). The Bible's last book begins its promises by unbarring its first book's gate.

Verses 8–11 · Smyrna · γίνου πιστὸς ἄχρι θανάτου ginou pistos achri thanatou

The letter with no complaint. Smyrna — the beautiful harbor city whose ringed acropolis ancient writers praised as "the crown of Smyrna" — receives, with Philadelphia, one of only two letters containing no rebuke at all. Its speaker-title is the one a persecuted congregation needs: "the first and the last, who was dead and came to life" (1:17-18's own words). "I know your… poverty — but you are rich": the city was wealthy; the congregation was poor, likely because confessing Christ cost trade and standing. "Affliction ten days" — a bounded number: short, counted, survivable; the point is the limit, not the calendar. And the charge that follows became one of the most famous in martyrdom's history: "Be faithful until death, and I will give you the crown of life" — the stephanos, the victor's laurel, not a monarch's diadem. Two generations later Smyrna's own bishop, Polycarp — who tradition says knew John — was burned in this city's stadium at 86, refusing to recant ("eighty-six years I have served him, and he has done me no wrong"): the letter, kept to the letter.

A hard verse, handled honestly. "The slander of those who say they are Jews, and are not, but are a synagogue of Satan." Two things are true and must both be said. First: in its own century this is an intra-Jewish polemic — John is Jewish, much of Smyrna's congregation was Jewish, and the charge is that the accusers (likely denouncing Christians to the Roman authorities, as Polycarp's martyrdom-account describes) have forfeited the name they claim; "synagogue" here is simply "assembly," the ordinary word. Second: torn from that setting, the phrase has been used for centuries to license Christian hatred of Jews as such — a use the text does not support and this project names as the abuse it is, exactly as it did with the "curse of Ham" (Genesis 9). The second death of the promise (v11) is defined by the book itself much later — the lake of fire (20:14) — and the promise is an exact mirror: those faithful until death are untouchable by the death that matters.

Verses 12–17 · Pergamum · ὅπου ὁ θρόνος τοῦ Σατανᾶ hopou ho thronos tou Satana

"Where Satan's throne is." Pergamum, the old royal capital of Asia, terraced up its mountain like a crown — and something up there is being called Satan's throne (KJV "Satan's seat" undersells the Greek: thronos is a throne). The candidates, each with a pedigree: the Great Altar of Zeus, whose sculpted frieze of gods battling serpent-legged giants dominated the acropolis (the altar itself was excavated and carried off to Berlin — it stands today in the Pergamon Museum, one of the strangest afterlives any biblical landmark has had); the temple of Rome and Augustus — Pergamum was the first city of Asia licensed for the imperial cult, and emperor-worship is Revelation's great enemy; or the healing shrine of Asclepius with its serpent emblem. Perhaps all of them at once: the whole skyline. Here Antipas was killed — "my witness, my faithful one" (martys, the word already crossing into "martyr," and the exact title of Jesus himself in 1:5) — the book's only named Christian casualty.

Balaam's program, and the white stone. The complaint reaches back to Numbers: Balaam, who could not curse Israel, taught Balak to seduce it instead — "idol-sacrifices and sexual immorality" — the Bible's shorthand for assimilation by dinner-party (in these trade cities, the guild feasts in the gods' honor were where business got done; the pressure was economic, and the "teaching" said go along). The promises answer the menu: instead of idol-meat, hidden manna; and "a white stone, and on the stone a new name written." The psēphos (NWT reads "white pebble," which is exactly what the word means) had many uses — a juror's pebble of acquittal, an admission-token to a feast, an amulet bearing a secret name — and the letter may intend the whole cluster: verdict, invitation, identity. As for the new name: readers of this site have watched God rename people since "your name shall be Abraham" (Genesis 17:5); the last book makes the renaming personal, private, and permanent.

Verses 18–23 · Thyatira · ὁ ἐραυνῶν νεφροὺς καὶ καρδίας ho eraunōn nephrous kai kardias

The longest letter goes to the smallest city. Thyatira — no acropolis, no famous temple, a workshop town of trade guilds on the inland road (its one other New Testament appearance is a businesswoman: Lydia, "a seller of purple from Thyatira," Acts 16:14) — receives the longest of the seven letters, from "the Son of God" (the title's only occurrence in all Revelation), with the furnace-eyes and bronze feet of the vision. The praise is real and rising — "your last works are more than the first," the exact inverse of Ephesus. The complaint is a prophetess the letter code-names Jezebel — as Pergamum's problem was code-named Balaam: Old Testament villains as ciphers for local teachers — whose program is the same guild-feast accommodation ("eat idol-sacrifices"), taught from inside the congregation, with time already given to repent and refused. (A textual note the KJV tradition preserves: some manuscripts read "your wife Jezebel" — one Greek pronoun, and the town's whole drama becomes the angel's own household; the older text reads simply "the woman.")

"The one who searches kidneys and hearts." Kept literal, and worth its strangeness: Hebrew anatomy located the inmost self in the kidneys (kelayot; KJV "reins," from the Latin) — and the sentence is Jehovah's own self-description at Jeremiah 17:10, "I, Jehovah, search the heart and test the kidneys… to give each according to his ways," here spoken by the Son of God in the first person. The same claim closed John 2 a chapter shelf away: "he knew what was in the human." The sickbed sentence is grim, and its idiom is Hebrew to the bone — "I will kill with death" (apoktenō en thanatō), which in the Greek Bible's usage likely means by pestilence; this translation keeps the doubled strangeness and files the reading here.

Verses 24–29 · Thyatira, continued · ποιμανεῖ αὐτοὺς ἐν ῥάβδῳ σιδηρᾷ poimanei autous en rhabdō sidēra

"No other burden." To the rest of Thyatira — those who never learned what the sect grandly called "the deep things of Satan" (the letter quotes their slogan back with visible irony: "as they say") — the demand is almost startlingly light: "I lay on you no other burden; only hold fast what you have, until I come." No new program, no heroics: endurance as the whole assignment.

The iron rod, and the letter of the LXX. The overcomer's promise quotes Psalm 2 — "authority over the nations… he will shepherd them with an iron rod" — and inside that verb sits one of the tidiest text-critical stories on this site. The Hebrew of Psalm 2:9 reads "you will break them" (tero'em); the Greek translators read the same consonants from a different root and wrote "you will shepherd them" (poimaneis) — and Revelation quotes the Greek, then keeps the Hebrew's shattering in the very next clause ("as clay pots are shattered"): both readings of one ancient word, side by side in a single sentence. KJV and Douay flatten to "rule"; ASV margins the truth ("as a shepherd"); this translation prints "shepherd" because that is the word John wrote. Last, the strangest gift in the seven letters: "I will give him the morning star." The book decodes it only on its final page — "I am the root and the offspring of David, the bright morning star" (22:16). The gift is the giver.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation sides with the literal shelf: "Satan's throne" (thronos — against KJV's "seat"), "white stone" with NWT's "pebble" saluted as exactly right, "slaves" for douloi again, and "shepherd them with an iron rod" (the verb John actually wrote, against the shelf's "rule").

Where it goes its own way: "searches kidneys and hearts" kept anatomically literal (KJV's "reins" honored in the note), "I will kill with death" kept as the Hebraism it is (with the pestilence reading filed), and the synagogue-of-Satan sentence translated plainly and then handled in full — first-century meaning and later abuse both named.

Echoes paid and planted. Paid: the tree of life, under guard since Eden's east gate (3:24), promised open in the paradise of God; the new name on the white stone from the God who renamed Abram (17:5); "the first and the last, who was dead" — Smyrna addressed by the Patmos vision's own title (1:17); and the searcher of hearts, who a chapter-shelf away "knew what was in the human" (John 2:25). Planted: the second death, defined at 20:14; the hidden manna (Exodus's jar, waiting for that book); the morning star, decoded at 22:16; and three letters still to deliver — Sardis, Philadelphia, Laodicea, next in this book.

Next installment in this book: Revelation 3 — the letters conclude: Sardis ("you have a name that you live, and you are dead"), Philadelphia's open door, and lukewarm Laodicea.
