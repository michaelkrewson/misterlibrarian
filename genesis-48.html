Translator's Notes

Verses 1–7 · a legal act, and an interruption about a grave

What verse 5 actually does. This is not sentiment; it is adoption, and it is the most consequential legal sentence in the last quarter of Genesis. "Ephraim and Manasseh, like Reuben and Simeon, shall be mine" — named against the first and second sons, which is the point. Two grandsons, born in Egypt to an Egyptian mother, are made Jacob's own sons, and therefore heads of tribes in their own right. Everything downstream follows from this: there is no tribe of Joseph in the land, there are the tribes of Ephraim and Manasseh; Joseph receives, through them, the double portion that belongs to a firstborn — forfeited by Reuben at 35:22 and reassigned here, as 1 Chronicles 5:1-2 later spells out. Verse 6 closes the loop: any later sons stay Joseph's and inherit inside their brothers' allotments. It is drafted like a will because it is one.

He starts by quoting God. Before he adopts anyone he recites the promise made to him at Luz — Bethel — naming El Shaddai, "an assembly of peoples," and the land as an everlasting holding. The word is achuzzat olam, the same root as the "holdings" the family had just started taking in Goshen (47:27). He is quietly correcting the address. The permanent property is somewhere else.

⚠️ And then, with no transition, Rachel. "And as for me — when I came from Paddan, Rachel died on me…" It reads like an old man's mind slipping its track, and in a sense it is; but the association is not random. He has just taken Rachel's grandsons as his own sons, which is the nearest thing available to giving Rachel the children she died without having. And metah alai — "died on me" — is a construction of loss laid against the speaker, not the neutral "died." He is also, three verses after asking to be carried out of Egypt, remembering the one person he did not manage to bury at Machpelah, "when there was still some distance to go." Sixty years on he still measures the distance. Genesis gives no comment and no connective; it simply lets the sentence arrive where grief actually puts it.

Verses 8–16 · a blind man asks who is in the room

"Who are these?" ⚠️ The chapter has been careful to tell you, one verse later, that Israel's eyes were heavy with age and he could not see. If that arrangement feels familiar it should: the last time this book put a dying, blind father in a room to bless two brothers of unequal birth order, the father was Isaac, the question was "who are you?", and the man supplying the answer was Jacob, lying (27:18-24). Genesis has staged the scene again, with Jacob now in Isaac's chair. The difference is the whole point of the chapter, and it lands in verse 19.

"I had not expected to see your face." Lo pillalti — from a root about judging or reckoning; not "I hoped and it happened" but closer to I had not reckoned on it. He had written it off. And then the excess: not only the son but the son's children. Twice in three chapters the old man is handed more than he asked for.

"From between his knees." The boys are placed at their grandfather's knees and taken out from them, which is a recognised gesture of acknowledgement in the ancient world — a child received on or between the knees is a child claimed. It is the physical enactment of the legal sentence in verse 5.

"The angel who has redeemed me from all evil." ⚠️ This is the first appearance of GA'AL in the Bible, and it arrives in a family blessing. The go'el is not a religious abstraction: he is the nearest kinsman with the standing and the obligation to buy back what a relative has lost — land, freedom, a life. Jacob applies it to the messenger who saw him through everything from Esau to the famine, and puts him in apposition with "the God who has shepherded me." Whether "the angel" is being identified with God or distinguished from him is grammatically open, and readers have gone both ways for two thousand years; this translation keeps the apposition as loose as the Hebrew keeps it. The word goes on to carry Ruth's Boaz, the "kinsman-redeemer" laws of Leviticus 25, and a great deal of Isaiah. KJV "the Angel which redeemed me from all evil"; ASV the same; NWT "the angel who has been recovering me from all calamity."

"Let them teem." Ve-yidgu — a verb built on dag, "fish," and used nowhere else in the Bible of people. Multiply like fish: shoals of them, uncountable. The KJV's "let them grow into a multitude" is smooth and drops the image entirely; this keeps the swarming.

Verses 17–20 · the crossed hands, and "I know, my son, I know"

Why the right hand matters at all. The blessing is not merely words; it is words delivered through a hand, and which hand lands where is the content. The right hand carries the greater portion. Joseph, who is a competent administrator and has thought about the logistics, positions the boys so that the firstborn will meet his father's right hand automatically — Manasseh to Israel's right, Ephraim to his left. He has set the room up so that nothing can go wrong. Then his father crosses his arms.

The verb is the sentence. Sikkel et yadav. Every translation renders it "he crossed his hands," and that is certainly what happens — but the verb is sakal, whose whole family of meanings is about insight, prudence, acting with understanding. It is not the ordinary word for crossing anything. Hebrew has chosen, at the exact moment the hands go over each other, a word that means to do a thing advisedly. So the crossing and the knowing are the same act, in one word, and the narrator has told you the answer before Joseph asks the question. The KJV caught this and has never been bettered on it: "guiding his hands wittingly." ASV "guiding his hands wittingly"; the ESV "crossing his hands"; the NIV "crossing his arms" — accurate, and the deliberateness evaporates. This translation keeps both halves: he crossed his hands knowingly.

What Joseph does next is the most human thing in the chapter. "It was evil in his eyes" — the same phrase used of genuine wrong elsewhere — and he physically takes hold of his father's wrist to move it. He is not being pious about custom; he is watching his firstborn get demoted and reaching in to stop it. He is also, without knowing it, playing Esau's part in a scene his father wrote.

"I know, my son, I know." Yada'ti veni yada'ti — three words, doubled, refusing the correction without raising the voice. ⚠️ This is the hinge of the whole chapter, and it is a moral event, not a genealogical one. Four times now this book has put the younger ahead of the elder: Isaac over Ishmael, Jacob over Esau, Judah and Joseph over Reuben, and now Ephraim over Manasseh. The first three arrived through expulsion, a bought birthright, a stolen blessing, a rape, an incest. This one happens in daylight, with the father's eyes shut, announced in advance, over the loud objection of the most powerful man in Egypt — and nobody is deceived. The old man who put on his brother's clothes and lied to a blind father is now the blind father, and he refuses to be lied to or to lie. The pattern that has run through Genesis by fraud is finally performed openly, by the one man in the book who knows exactly what it costs.

Was he right? Historically, yes, and conspicuously. Ephraim became the dominant northern tribe — so dominant that the prophets simply call the whole northern kingdom "Ephraim." Manasseh was large and split by the Jordan, but it never led. Melo ha-goyim, "the fullness of the nations," is an odd and expansive phrase for a tribal allotment; KJV "a multitude of nations."

The blessing that outlived everyone in the room. Verse 20 is not a prediction but a formula: this is what Israelites will say when they bless a child. And it is still said — over sons, on Friday nights, in Jewish homes, in these words. ⚠️ It is worth noticing what it means that the standing blessing for a Jewish boy names these two: brothers born in Egypt to a foreign mother, raised entirely outside the land, who were adopted in and who — uniquely among the brothers in this book — never fought each other. Nothing in the text says that is why. It is simply the pair the tradition kept.

Verses 21–22 · "one shoulder above your brothers"

"God will be with you" — plural. The Hebrew switches from addressing Joseph alone to addressing all of them: God will be with you (pl.) and bring you (pl.) back. The dying man is not promising his favourite son a private future; he is handing on, in the last words he speaks to him, the same promise he was given at Beersheba (46:4) and then made Joseph swear an oath about (47:29-31). Egypt is an address, not a destination.

⚠️ "One shoulder above your brothers." Shekhem achad, and it is genuinely ambiguous in three directions at once. Shekhem means a shoulder, and by extension a ridge or slope of land — so "one portion" or "one mountain-slope." It is also, unavoidably, the name of the city Shechem, which sits in exactly the territory Ephraim and Manasseh will inherit and where Joseph's bones are eventually buried (Joshua 24:32). Translations split: KJV "one portion above thy brethren"; ASV the same with a margin note; the ESV "one mountain slope"; the NIV makes the pun explicit with "the ridge of land," footnoting Shechem. Keeping "one shoulder" holds all three open, which is where the Hebrew sits.

⚠️ "With my sword and with my bow" is a real problem, and it should be said out loud. Jacob never took anything from the Amorites by force. The only violence at Shechem in this book is chapter 34 — Simeon and Levi's massacre — which Jacob condemned at the time and will curse from his deathbed one chapter from now (49:5-7). And the only ground he is recorded as acquiring there, he bought (33:19). The explanations offered are: that he is claiming his sons' conquest as the family's by right if not by approval; that a lost tradition of a battle stands behind it; that "sword and bow" is figurative for what God secured for him; or that he means the purchase held only because the family could defend it. Genesis supplies none of them, and this translation supplies none either. It is one of the places where the book declines to reconcile itself.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the words exact / distinct: "he crossed his hands knowingly" for sikkel, holding both the gesture and the deliberateness the verb's own root carries (the KJV's "wittingly" is the model); "redeemed" for ga'al, its first occurrence in the Bible, left as the kinsman-word it is; "let them teem" for ve-yidgu, keeping the fish; "died on me" for metah alai; "it was evil in his eyes" for Joseph's reaction, unsoftened; and "one shoulder" for shekhem achad, holding portion, slope and city together.

Echoes paid. Isaac's blindness restaged with Jacob in the chair — and this time nobody is deceived; the birthright Reuben forfeited reassigned as a double portion; Rachel's grave on the road to Ephrath remembered sixty years on, in the middle of a legal document; "I will surely bring you up again" handed on in the plural; and Joseph's own dreams of precedence answered by a father who overrules his choice of heirs to his face.

Echoes planted. Ephraim, whose name the prophets will use for the entire northern kingdom; the two tribes that replace Joseph on every later map; the go'el, who becomes Boaz, and the redemption laws of Leviticus 25, and much of Isaiah; Shechem, where Joseph's bones are finally buried (Joshua 24:32); and the blessing of verse 20, still spoken over children in these words.

Next installment in this book: Genesis 49 — Jacob calls all twelve sons to his bed and tells them "what will happen to you in the latter days." It is the longest poem in Genesis, and it is not gentle: Reuben is stripped of his rank in three lines, Simeon and Levi are cursed by name for what they did at Shechem, and Judah is handed the sceptre.
