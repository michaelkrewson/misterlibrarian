Deuteronomy 1 — The Mister Translation

1
אֵלֶּה הַדְּבָרִים, אֲשֶׁר דִּבֶּר מֹשֶׁה אֶל-כָּל-יִשְׂרָאֵל, בְּעֵבֶר, הַיַּרְדֵּן: בַּמִּדְבָּר בָּעֲרָבָה מוֹל סוּף בֵּין-פָּארָן וּבֵין-תֹּפֶל, וְלָבָן וַחֲצֵרֹת--וְדִי זָהָב.
These are the words that Moses spoke to all Israel across the Jordan — in the wilderness, in the Arabah, opposite Suph, between Paran and Tophel and Laban and Hazeroth and Di-zahab.note
Estas son las palabras que Moisés habló a todo Israel al otro lado del Jordán — en el desierto, en el Arabá, frente a Suf, entre Parán y Tofel y Labán y Hazerot y Di-zahab.
2
אַחַד עָשָׂר יוֹם מֵחֹרֵב, דֶּרֶךְ הַר-שֵׂעִיר, עַד, קָדֵשׁ בַּרְנֵעַ.
Eleven days from Horeb by the way of Mount Seir to Kadesh-barnea.note
Once días desde Horeb, por el camino del monte Seír, hasta Cades-barnea.
cross-refs⤷ 1:3
3
וַיְהִי בְּאַרְבָּעִים שָׁנָה, בְּעַשְׁתֵּי-עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ בְּאֶחָד לַחֹדֶשׁ; דִּבֶּר מֹשֶׁה, אֶל-בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל, כְּכֹל אֲשֶׁר צִוָּה יְהוָה אֹתוֹ, אֲלֵהֶם.
And it happened in the fortieth year, in the eleventh month, on the first of the month, that Moses spoke to the sons of Israel according to all that Jehovah had commanded him for them,note
Y sucedió en el año cuarenta, en el mes undécimo, el primero del mes, que Moisés habló a los hijos de Israel conforme a todo lo que Jehová le había mandado para ellos,
cross-refs⤷ 1:2
4
אַחֲרֵי הַכֹּתוֹ, אֵת סִיחֹן מֶלֶךְ הָאֱמֹרִי, אֲשֶׁר יוֹשֵׁב, בְּחֶשְׁבּוֹן--וְאֵת, עוֹג מֶלֶךְ הַבָּשָׁן, אֲשֶׁר-יוֹשֵׁב בְּעַשְׁתָּרֹת, בְּאֶדְרֶעִי.
after he had struck down Sihon king of the Amorites, who dwelt in Heshbon, and Og king of Bashan, who dwelt in Ashtaroth, at Edrei.note
después de haber derrotado a Sijón rey de los amorreos, que habitaba en Hesbón, y a Og rey de Basán, que habitaba en Astarot, en Edrei.
5
בְּעֵבֶר הַיַּרְדֵּן, בְּאֶרֶץ מוֹאָב, הוֹאִיל מֹשֶׁה, בֵּאֵר אֶת-הַתּוֹרָה הַזֹּאת לֵאמֹר.
Across the Jordan, in the land of Moab, Moses undertook to make this instruction plain, saying:note
Al otro lado del Jordán, en la tierra de Moab, Moisés se dispuso a hacer clara esta instrucción, diciendo:
6
יְהוָה אֱלֹהֵינוּ דִּבֶּר אֵלֵינוּ, בְּחֹרֵב לֵאמֹר: רַב-לָכֶם שֶׁבֶת, בָּהָר הַזֶּה.
"Jehovah our God spoke to us at Horeb, saying: You have stayed long enough at this mountain.note
«Jehová nuestro Dios nos habló en Horeb, diciendo: Bastante han estado en este monte.
7
פְּנוּ וּסְעוּ לָכֶם, וּבֹאוּ הַר הָאֱמֹרִי וְאֶל-כָּל-שְׁכֵנָיו, בָּעֲרָבָה בָהָר וּבַשְּׁפֵלָה וּבַנֶּגֶב, וּבְחוֹף הַיָּם--אֶרֶץ הַכְּנַעֲנִי וְהַלְּבָנוֹן, עַד-הַנָּהָר הַגָּדֹל נְהַר-פְּרָת.
Turn and set out, and go to the hill country of the Amorites and to all its neighbours — in the Arabah, in the hill country and in the lowland and in the Negev and by the seashore — the land of the Canaanite, and the Lebanon, as far as the great river, the river Euphrates.note
Vuélvanse y pónganse en marcha, y vayan a la región montañosa de los amorreos y a todos sus vecinos — en el Arabá, en la montaña y en la Sefela y en el Négueb y en la costa del mar — la tierra del cananeo, y el Líbano, hasta el gran río, el río Éufrates.
8
רְאֵה נָתַתִּי לִפְנֵיכֶם, אֶת-הָאָרֶץ; בֹּאוּ, וּרְשׁוּ אֶת-הָאָרֶץ, אֲשֶׁר נִשְׁבַּע יְהוָה לַאֲבֹתֵיכֶם לְאַבְרָהָם לְיִצְחָק וּלְיַעֲקֹב לָתֵת לָהֶם, וּלְזַרְעָם אַחֲרֵיהֶם.
See — I have set the land before you. Go in and take possession of the land that Jehovah swore to your fathers, to Abraham, to Isaac, and to Jacob, to give to them and to their offspring after them.note
Miren — he puesto la tierra delante de ustedes. Entren y tomen posesión de la tierra que Jehová juró a sus padres, a Abraham, a Isaac y a Jacob, darles a ellos y a su descendencia después de ellos.
cross-refs⤷ Gen 50:24
9
וָאֹמַר אֲלֵכֶם, בָּעֵת הַהִוא לֵאמֹר: לֹא-אוּכַל לְבַדִּי, שְׂאֵת אֶתְכֶם.
And I said to you at that time: I am not able to carry you by myself.note
Y les dije en aquel tiempo: No puedo yo solo cargarlos.
cross-refs⤷ 1:31
10
יְהוָה אֱלֹהֵיכֶם, הִרְבָּה אֶתְכֶם; וְהִנְּכֶם הַיּוֹם, כְּכוֹכְבֵי הַשָּׁמַיִם לָרֹב.
Jehovah your God has multiplied you, and look — today you are as many as the stars of the heavens.note
Jehová su Dios los ha multiplicado, y miren — hoy son tantos como las estrellas de los cielos.
11
יְהוָה אֱלֹהֵי אֲבוֹתֵכֶם, יֹסֵף עֲלֵיכֶם כָּכֶם--אֶלֶף פְּעָמִים; וִיבָרֵךְ אֶתְכֶם, כַּאֲשֶׁר דִּבֶּר לָכֶם.
May Jehovah, the God of your fathers, add to you a thousand times as many as you are, and bless you as he has spoken to you!note
¡Que Jehová, el Dios de sus padres, les añada mil veces más de los que son, y los bendiga como les ha hablado!
12
אֵיכָה אֶשָּׂא, לְבַדִּי, טָרְחֲכֶם וּמַשַּׂאֲכֶם, וְרִיבְכֶם.
How can I carry by myself your bother and your burden and your quarrelling?note
¿Cómo cargaré yo solo su molestia y su carga y sus pleitos?
13
הָבוּ לָכֶם אֲנָשִׁים חֲכָמִים וּנְבֹנִים, וִידֻעִים--לְשִׁבְטֵיכֶם; וַאֲשִׂימֵם, בְּרָאשֵׁיכֶם.
Provide for yourselves men who are wise and discerning and known, by your tribes, and I will set them as your heads.note
Provéanse de hombres sabios y entendidos y conocidos, según sus tribus, y yo los pondré como cabezas de ustedes.
14
וַתַּעֲנוּ, אֹתִי; וַתֹּאמְרוּ, טוֹב-הַדָּבָר אֲשֶׁר-דִּבַּרְתָּ לַעֲשׂוֹת.
And you answered me and said, "The thing you have spoken is good to do."note
Y ustedes me respondieron y dijeron: «Bueno es hacer lo que has dicho».
15
וָאֶקַּח אֶת-רָאשֵׁי שִׁבְטֵיכֶם, אֲנָשִׁים חֲכָמִים וִידֻעִים, וָאֶתֵּן אוֹתָם רָאשִׁים, עֲלֵיכֶם: שָׂרֵי אֲלָפִים וְשָׂרֵי מֵאוֹת, וְשָׂרֵי חֲמִשִּׁים וְשָׂרֵי עֲשָׂרֹת, וְשֹׁטְרִים, לְשִׁבְטֵיכֶם.
So I took the heads of your tribes, men wise and known, and gave them as heads over you — commanders of thousands and commanders of hundreds and commanders of fifties and commanders of tens, and officers for your tribes.note
Así que tomé las cabezas de sus tribus, hombres sabios y conocidos, y los puse por cabezas sobre ustedes: jefes de mil y jefes de cien y jefes de cincuenta y jefes de diez, y oficiales para sus tribus.
16
וָאֲצַוֶּה, אֶת-שֹׁפְטֵיכֶם, בָּעֵת הַהִוא, לֵאמֹר: שָׁמֹעַ בֵּין-אֲחֵיכֶם וּשְׁפַטְתֶּם צֶדֶק, בֵּין-אִישׁ וּבֵין-אָחִיו וּבֵין גֵּרוֹ.
And I commanded your judges at that time, saying: Hear the cases between your brothers, and judge righteously between a man and his brother and the stranger who is with him.note
Y mandé a sus jueces en aquel tiempo, diciendo: Oigan las causas entre sus hermanos, y juzguen con justicia entre un hombre y su hermano y el forastero que está con él.
17
לֹא-תַכִּירוּ פָנִים בַּמִּשְׁפָּט, כַּקָּטֹן כַּגָּדֹל תִּשְׁמָעוּן--לֹא תָגוּרוּ מִפְּנֵי-אִישׁ, כִּי הַמִּשְׁפָּט לֵאלֹהִים הוּא; וְהַדָּבָר אֲשֶׁר יִקְשֶׁה מִכֶּם, תַּקְרִבוּן אֵלַי וּשְׁמַעְתִּיו.
You shall not recognise faces in judgement; you shall hear the small as the great; you shall not shrink before any man, for the judgement is God's. And the case that is too hard for you, bring to me, and I will hear it.note
No reconocerán rostros en el juicio; oirán al pequeño como al grande; no se encogerán ante nadie, porque el juicio es de Dios. Y la causa que sea demasiado difícil para ustedes, tráiganmela a mí, y yo la oiré.
18
וָאֲצַוֶּה אֶתְכֶם, בָּעֵת הַהִוא, אֵת כָּל-הַדְּבָרִים, אֲשֶׁר תַּעֲשׂוּן.
And I commanded you at that time all the things that you should do.note
Y les mandé en aquel tiempo todas las cosas que debían hacer.
19
וַנִּסַּע מֵחֹרֵב, וַנֵּלֶךְ אֵת כָּל-הַמִּדְבָּר הַגָּדוֹל וְהַנּוֹרָא הַהוּא אֲשֶׁר רְאִיתֶם דֶּרֶךְ הַר הָאֱמֹרִי, כַּאֲשֶׁר צִוָּה יְהוָה אֱלֹהֵינוּ, אֹתָנוּ; וַנָּבֹא, עַד קָדֵשׁ בַּרְנֵעַ.
And we set out from Horeb and went through all that great and fearful wilderness that you saw, by the way of the hill country of the Amorites, as Jehovah our God had commanded us; and we came to Kadesh-barnea.note
Y partimos de Horeb y anduvimos por todo aquel desierto grande y temible que vieron, por el camino de la región montañosa de los amorreos, como Jehová nuestro Dios nos había mandado; y llegamos hasta Cades-barnea.
20
וָאֹמַר, אֲלֵכֶם: בָּאתֶם עַד-הַר הָאֱמֹרִי, אֲשֶׁר-יְהוָה אֱלֹהֵינוּ נֹתֵן לָנוּ.
And I said to you: You have come to the hill country of the Amorites, which Jehovah our God is giving to us.note
Y les dije: Han llegado a la región montañosa de los amorreos, que Jehová nuestro Dios nos está dando.
21
רְאֵה נָתַן יְהוָה אֱלֹהֶיךָ, לְפָנֶיךָ--אֶת-הָאָרֶץ: עֲלֵה רֵשׁ, כַּאֲשֶׁר דִּבֶּר יְהוָה אֱלֹהֵי אֲבֹתֶיךָ לָךְ--אַל-תִּירָא, וְאַל-תֵּחָת.
See — Jehovah your God has set the land before you. Go up, take possession, as Jehovah the God of your fathers has spoken to you. Do not be afraid and do not be dismayed.note
Mira — Jehová tu Dios ha puesto la tierra delante de ti. Sube, toma posesión, como te ha hablado Jehová el Dios de tus padres. No temas ni te desanimes.
22
וַתִּקְרְבוּן אֵלַי, כֻּלְּכֶם, וַתֹּאמְרוּ נִשְׁלְחָה אֲנָשִׁים לְפָנֵינוּ, וְיַחְפְּרוּ-לָנוּ אֶת-הָאָרֶץ; וְיָשִׁבוּ אֹתָנוּ, דָּבָר--אֶת-הַדֶּרֶךְ אֲשֶׁר נַעֲלֶה-בָּהּ, וְאֵת הֶעָרִים אֲשֶׁר נָבֹא אֲלֵיהֶן.
And all of you came near to me and said, "Let us send men ahead of us, and let them dig out the land for us and bring us back word about the way by which we should go up and the cities we shall come to."note
Y todos ustedes se acercaron a mí y dijeron: «Enviemos hombres delante de nosotros, y que nos exploren la tierra y nos traigan noticia del camino por el cual hemos de subir y de las ciudades a las que llegaremos».
23
וַיִּיטַב בְּעֵינַי, הַדָּבָר; וָאֶקַּח מִכֶּם שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר אֲנָשִׁים, אִישׁ אֶחָד לַשָּׁבֶט.
And the thing was good in my eyes, and I took twelve men from you, one man for each tribe.note
Y el asunto fue bueno a mis ojos, y tomé de ustedes doce hombres, un hombre por cada tribu.
24
וַיִּפְנוּ וַיַּעֲלוּ הָהָרָה, וַיָּבֹאוּ עַד-נַחַל אֶשְׁכֹּל; וַיְרַגְּלוּ, אֹתָהּ.
And they turned and went up into the hill country and came to the Valley of Eshcol, and spied it out.note
Y ellos se volvieron y subieron a la montaña y llegaron hasta el valle de Escol, y lo espiaron.
25
וַיִּקְחוּ בְיָדָם מִפְּרִי הָאָרֶץ, וַיּוֹרִדוּ אֵלֵינוּ; וַיָּשִׁבוּ אֹתָנוּ דָבָר, וַיֹּאמְרוּ, טוֹבָה הָאָרֶץ, אֲשֶׁר-יְהוָה אֱלֹהֵינוּ נֹתֵן לָנוּ.
And they took some of the fruit of the land in their hand and brought it down to us, and brought us back word and said, "The land that Jehovah our God is giving us is good."note
Y tomaron en su mano del fruto de la tierra y nos lo bajaron; y nos trajeron noticia y dijeron: «Buena es la tierra que Jehová nuestro Dios nos está dando».
26
וְלֹא אֲבִיתֶם, לַעֲלֹת; וַתַּמְרוּ, אֶת-פִּי יְהוָה אֱלֹהֵיכֶם.
But you were not willing to go up, and you rebelled against the mouth of Jehovah your God.note
Pero ustedes no quisieron subir, y se rebelaron contra la boca de Jehová su Dios.
27
וַתֵּרָגְנוּ בְאָהֳלֵיכֶם, וַתֹּאמְרוּ, בְּשִׂנְאַת יְהוָה אֹתָנוּ, הוֹצִיאָנוּ מֵאֶרֶץ מִצְרָיִם--לָתֵת אֹתָנוּ בְּיַד הָאֱמֹרִי, לְהַשְׁמִידֵנוּ.
And you grumbled in your tents and said, "It is because Jehovah hated us that he brought us out of the land of Egypt — to give us into the hand of the Amorite, to destroy us.note
Y murmuraron en sus tiendas y dijeron: «Porque Jehová nos odiaba nos sacó de la tierra de Egipto — para entregarnos en mano del amorreo, para destruirnos.
cross-refs⤷ Exod 1:11
28
אָנָה אֲנַחְנוּ עֹלִים, אַחֵינוּ הֵמַסּוּ אֶת-לְבָבֵנוּ לֵאמֹר עַם גָּדוֹל וָרָם מִמֶּנּוּ, עָרִים גְּדֹלֹת וּבְצוּרֹת, בַּשָּׁמָיִם; וְגַם-בְּנֵי עֲנָקִים, רָאִינוּ שָׁם.
Where are we going up? Our brothers have melted our heart, saying: A people greater and taller than we, cities great and fortified to the heavens — and we saw the sons of the Anakim there too."note
¿A dónde subimos? Nuestros hermanos han derretido nuestro corazón, diciendo: Un pueblo más grande y más alto que nosotros, ciudades grandes y fortificadas hasta los cielos — y también vimos allí a los hijos de los anaquitas».
29
וָאֹמַר, אֲלֵכֶם: לֹא-תַעַרְצוּן וְלֹא-תִירְאוּן, מֵהֶם.
And I said to you: Do not be terrified and do not be afraid of them.note
Y les dije: No se aterren ni les teman.
30
יְהוָה אֱלֹהֵיכֶם הַהֹלֵךְ לִפְנֵיכֶם, הוּא יִלָּחֵם לָכֶם: כְּכֹל אֲשֶׁר עָשָׂה אִתְּכֶם, בְּמִצְרַיִם--לְעֵינֵיכֶם.
Jehovah your God, who goes before you — he himself will fight for you, according to all that he did with you in Egypt before your eyes,note
Jehová su Dios, que va delante de ustedes — él mismo peleará por ustedes, conforme a todo lo que hizo con ustedes en Egipto ante sus ojos,
31
וּבַמִּדְבָּר, אֲשֶׁר רָאִיתָ, אֲשֶׁר נְשָׂאֲךָ יְהוָה אֱלֹהֶיךָ, כַּאֲשֶׁר יִשָּׂא-אִישׁ אֶת-בְּנוֹ--בְּכָל-הַדֶּרֶךְ אֲשֶׁר הֲלַכְתֶּם, עַד-בֹּאֲכֶם עַד-הַמָּקוֹם הַזֶּה.
and in the wilderness, where you saw how Jehovah your God carried you as a man carries his son, all the way that you went until you came to this place.note
y en el desierto, donde viste cómo Jehová tu Dios te llevó como lleva un hombre a su hijo, por todo el camino que anduvieron hasta llegar a este lugar.

Translator's Notes

Verses 1–5 · eleven days, and forty years

"These are the words." Elleh ha-devarim — and that is the book's Hebrew name: Devarim, "Words." Every book of the Torah is named in Hebrew from its own opening line, and this one could not be better named: Deuteronomy is a book of speech. Moses does almost nothing in it but talk. ⚠️ The English title comes from a different route entirely — the Greek Deuteronomion, "second law," from the Septuagint's rendering of a phrase at 17:18 that in Hebrew means "a copy of this instruction." The Greek title has stuck for two thousand years, and it is not quite what the Hebrew says: this is not a second law but the first one preached.

⚠️ Verse 2 is one of the most devastating sentences in the Bible, and it looks like a travel note. "Eleven days from Horeb by the way of Mount Seir to Kadesh-barnea." Then verse 3: "in the fortieth year." The narrator sets the length of the journey beside the length of the trip and says nothing else. Eleven days of walking took a generation forty years, and the whole of chapter 1 is the explanation of the gap. Nothing in the verse editorialises; the arithmetic does it.

"Across the Jordan." Be-ever ha-Yarden — literally "in the crossing-place / the other side of the Jordan." ⚠️ It is worth being honest that the phrase describes the eastern side from the standpoint of someone standing in Canaan, on the west — which is where Israel is not yet. It may simply be the settled name for the region (as "the Transjordan" is for us, whichever side we stand on), or it may be the voice of a narrator writing later, from inside the land. This translation renders it plainly and leaves the observation in the note; it is one of the details that features in the authorship discussion on the book's introduction page.

"Undertook to make this instruction plain." Ho'il… be'er et ha-torah. Be'er is a rare and concrete verb — to dig out, to engrave, to make a thing unmistakably legible (Habakkuk 2:2 uses it of writing large on tablets so a runner can read it). And torah is not "law" in the narrow legislative sense but instruction, teaching, direction. So the verse is not "Moses restated the legislation" but something closer to: he set about making the teaching plain. KJV "began Moses to declare this law"; ASV the same; the ESV "undertook to explain this law." That is the project of the whole book in one verb.

The place-names of verse 1 are a puzzle. Suph, Tophel, Laban, Di-zahab — several appear nowhere else, and no one can now locate them with confidence. ⚠️ An old Jewish reading takes them not as a map but as an indictment, hearing in the names the wilderness failures (Di-zahab, "enough gold," heard as the golden calf). That is homiletic rather than geographic, and this translation prints them as the place-names they present themselves to be.

Verses 6–8 · "you have stayed long enough at this mountain"

The first words God speaks in the book are an eviction notice. Rav lakhem shevet ba-har ha-zeh — you have much of sitting at this mountain; enough. And the mountain in question is Horeb — Sinai, the place of the fire and the covenant and the tablets, the most important address in Israel's short history. They had been camped there roughly a year. The command is to leave it. ⚠️ This is a strange and easily-missed theological move: the point of the mountain was never the mountain. Israel is not a people who live at a holy site; they are a people who are going somewhere, and the sign that the encounter was real is that they pack up.

The land is described as already given. "See — I have set the land before you. Go in and take possession." The perfect tense is not a promise about the future but a statement about a completed act, with the taking left to them. And the boundaries in verse 7 are the largest in the Torah — hill country, lowland, Negev, coast, Lebanon, all the way to the Euphrates. ⚠️ Israel never in its history held that whole extent; the closest approach is the reign of Solomon. Readers have taken this as an ideal horizon, as a conditional grant never met, or as a description of a sphere of influence rather than settlement. The text states the extent and does not qualify it.

"Which Jehovah swore to your fathers, to Abraham, to Isaac, and to Jacob." The oath is the same one Joseph quoted from his deathbed in the last verses of Genesis (50:24), naming the same three men in the same order. Deuteronomy 1 is standing at the border that sentence was pointing at.

Verses 9–18 · a man admits he cannot carry it

"I am not able to carry you by myself." The verb is nasa, to lift or bear — the same word used of carrying a load, and (in verse 31) of a father carrying a child. Moses uses it three times in four verses, and the admission is striking for a man in his position: the leader states plainly that the job is beyond one person. ⚠️ Note what he does not do. He does not treat the size of the people as a burden to complain about — verses 10-11 stop the argument to bless them, praying that God would multiply them a thousand times more. The problem is not that there are too many; it is that one man is too few.

⚠️ This is the third telling of the same event, and the tellings differ. In Exodus 18 the idea comes from Jethro, Moses' father-in-law, who watches him judging alone and proposes the system; in Numbers 11 God tells Moses to gather seventy elders and puts his spirit on them. Here Moses tells it with no Jethro and no seventy — he says it himself, the people approve, and the appointments follow. The three accounts are not easily merged, and the honest options are that they are different moments in one long process, or that a preacher retelling a story forty years on emphasises what serves his sermon. Deuteronomy is a retelling throughout, and this is the first place a reader can see it working.

"You shall not recognise faces in judgement." Lo takkiru panim — a Hebrew idiom for showing partiality (the New Testament's "respecter of persons" comes from the same picture). Then the two extraordinary clauses: hear the small as the great, and do not shrink before any man — the second addressing a judge's fear rather than a judge's greed, which is the rarer and harder corruption to legislate against. And the ground given is not fairness as a principle but ownership: "for the judgement is God's." A judge is handling something that is not his. KJV "ye shall not respect persons in judgment… for the judgment is God's"; NIV "Do not show partiality"; the NWT "you must not be partial in judgment."

Verses 19–25 · whose idea the spies were

⚠️ Here is the sharpest difference between this retelling and Numbers, and it should not be smoothed. In Numbers 13, God says to Moses, "Send men to spy out the land of Canaan" — the mission is divinely commanded. In Deuteronomy 1:22, Moses says: "all of you came near to me and said, Let us send men ahead of us" — the mission is the people's idea, and Moses only agrees to it ("the thing was good in my eyes"). These are genuinely different framings of the same event, and the difference does work in the sermon: Moses is building a case about a generation that would not simply go when told. The usual harmonisations are that the people proposed it and God then permitted and commanded it, or that Moses is assigning responsibility rhetorically rather than correcting the record. This translation reports both and adopts neither. It is exactly the kind of seam this project prefers to leave visible.

"Let them dig out the land." Ve-yachperu — literally to dig or search out, a different verb from the ordinary "spy" (ragal) used in verse 24. The people ask for a survey; what happens is reconnaissance.

And the report is good. Verse 25 is easy to skip past: they bring back fruit and say, "The land that Jehovah our God is giving us is good." In Moses' telling the spies' report contains no bad news at all. The fear in verse 28 is attributed not to the report but to the brothers who "melted our heart" — the discouragement is a second act, not the first. What went wrong was not information.

Verses 26–33 · "because Jehovah hated us"

The accusation is the low point of the chapter. "It is because Jehovah hated us that he brought us out of the land of Egypt." ⚠️ It is worth registering how complete an inversion this is. The exodus is the founding act of love in Israel's story, and here it is read as an act of hatred — a rescue reinterpreted as a trap, with a plan to hand them over to the Amorites. And they say it, the text notes precisely, in their tents: not to Moses' face, not in the assembly, but grumbling in private. Deuteronomy will later build its whole demand on love (6:5), and it opens by quoting the generation that accused God of the opposite.

"Cities fortified to the heavens." Deliberate hyperbole, and the book knows it — Moses will repeat the phrase at 9:1 and the reader is meant to hear the panic in it. The Anakim are the tall people of the hill country, later associated with Hebron and with Goliath's line.

"As a man carries his son." ⚠️ Verse 31 is the tenderest image in the book, and it is placed with real care: directly against the accusation of hatred. They said he brought them out to destroy them; Moses answers that he carried them, the way a father carries a boy too tired to walk. The verb is nasa again — the same word Moses used of the burden he could not carry alone (v. 9). What was too heavy for Moses was not too heavy for God. KJV "as a man doth bear his son"; ESV the same. And then the sentence lands hard in verse 32: "and in this thing you were not trusting Jehovah your God" — after the carrying, after the fire and the cloud.

Verses 34–40 · the sentence, and Moses' own exclusion

An "evil generation." The oath is that none of them will see the land — the forty years of verse 3, explained. Only Caleb is named as an exception here, "because he has fully followed Jehovah" (mille acharei, literally "he filled up after" — an idiom of wholehearted, unreserved following); Joshua is added in verse 38 in his own capacity, as the one who will lead. ⚠️ Both men are exceptions to a national judgement, which is itself worth noticing: the generation is condemned as a generation, and individuals within it are not.

⚠️ "With me also Jehovah was angry on your account." This is a real difficulty and the honest thing is to name it. In Numbers 20, Moses is barred from the land for what he himself did at the waters of Meribah — striking the rock and failing to treat God as holy before the people. Here, three times in Deuteronomy (1:37, 3:26, 4:21), Moses says he was excluded because of them. The readings offered: that both are true, since the people's provocation is what drove him to it; that Moses is speaking pastorally, folding his fate into theirs to make the lesson land; or that the sermon simply does not aim at the precision the narrative gives elsewhere. What should not be done is to pretend the two accounts say the same thing. They do not, and Deuteronomy is a sermon by a man who is about to die outside the land he spent forty years walking toward.

"Your children, who today do not know good from evil." The phrase for moral immaturity — the same pairing as the tree in Genesis 2 — and it is the hinge of the whole book. The people being addressed are those children, now grown. Moses is telling a second generation what happened to the first, and the entire sermon is aimed at making sure they do not repeat it.

Verses 41–46 · the repentance that came too late

They say the right words. "We have sinned against Jehovah. We ourselves will go up and fight." ⚠️ And it is refused — which makes this one of the hardest passages in the Torah, because a confession is answered with "do not go up, for I am not among you." The point is not that repentance is impossible but that obedience has a time. The command was to go up; they would not. Now the command is to turn back; they will not. Both refusals are the same refusal — doing what they have decided rather than what they have been told — and the second is dressed as repentance. The verb in verse 41 says it: va-tahinu, you thought it a light thing to go up.

"They chased you as bees do." A precise and humiliating image: not a rout by a superior army but a swarm — the smaller, angrier, more numerous thing driving them down the hill. KJV "chased you, as bees do."

"And you returned and wept before Jehovah, but Jehovah did not listen." The last line of the chapter is quieter and worse: they stayed in Kadesh "many days — as many days as you stayed." The Hebrew simply repeats itself, and translations differ over whether to smooth it into "a long time." Kept flat here, because the flatness is the point: nothing happened for a very long time. That is what the forty years were.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the words exact / distinct: "make this instruction plain" for be'er et ha-torah — instruction, not "law," and be'er's sense of engraving a thing legibly; "carry" for nasa kept the same in verse 9 and verse 31, so the burden Moses cannot lift and the son God carries share one verb; "you shall not recognise faces" for the partiality idiom, with "the judgement is God's" left as ownership rather than principle; "rebelled against the mouth of Jehovah" kept literal; "thought it a light thing" for va-tahinu; and verse 46's flat repetition left unsmoothed.

Echoes paid. Joseph's dying oath — "the land he swore to Abraham, to Isaac, and to Jacob" — quoted at the border it pointed to; the mountain of the covenant left behind on command; and knowing good and evil turned into a description of children too young to be guilty.

Echoes planted. Devarim — "words" — the name of the book and its method; the forty years whose cause this chapter supplies; Joshua, introduced here as the one to be strengthened; Caleb, who "fully followed"; and the generation "who do not know good from evil," who are the people Moses is actually talking to.
