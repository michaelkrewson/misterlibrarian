Translator's Notes

Verses 1–7 · the summons, and the three who lose everything

What kind of document this is. The longest poem in Genesis, and the last thing Jacob says. It is a tribal oracle as much as a family one — most of the sayings describe what the tribe will be like, not what the man was, and several are transparently about geography and history (Zebulun by the sea, Issachar under forced labour, Dan ambushing from the roadside). Be-acharit ha-yamim, "in the latter days," means the far end of things — the horizon of what is coming, without in itself specifying how far. ⚠️ Later readers took it as a technical term for the end of history and read this chapter accordingly; the phrase does not carry that weight on its own here, and this translation renders it plainly and leaves the reach of it open.

Reuben: three lines of grandeur, then the sentence. Verse 3 is genuinely magnificent — firstborn, might, first of my strength, excess of dignity and excess of power — and it exists to make the fall further. Then pachaz ka-mayim: reckless, unbridled, boiling over like water. ⚠️ Watch the grammar break at the end of verse 4. He has been speaking to Reuben — "you went up to your father's bed" — and then, mid-sentence, cannot: "he went up to my couch." The second person collapses into the third. Whether it is a stylistic flourish or an old man unable to keep looking at his son while saying it, the seam is in the Hebrew, and this translation keeps it rather than tidying the pronoun. The event is 35:22, reported there in one flat verse with the note that "Israel heard." He heard, and said nothing, for something like forty years.

Simeon and Levi: he curses the anger, not the men. ⚠️ This is precise and it matters. "Cursed be their anger" — not cursed be they. A father on his deathbed condemns a disposition and its consequences while stopping short of cursing his own sons; the distinction is deliberate and it is the only mercy in the verse. The crime is chapter 34, the massacre at Shechem, which he condemned at the time on prudential grounds ("you have brought trouble on me") and now condemns morally, thirty years too late to matter. "They hamstrung an ox" is troubling and not fully explained by chapter 34; it may refer to wanton destruction of livestock in the raid, and some read shor, "ox," as a poetic figure for a man.

"I will divide them and scatter them" — and both landed. Simeon's allotment was swallowed inside Judah's (Joshua 19:1-9) and the tribe effectively disappears. Levi received no territory at all, but was distributed through the country in forty-eight cities as the priestly tribe. ⚠️ The same sentence, on the same two brothers, becomes extinction for one and ordination for the other. Genesis says nothing about that asymmetry; it is worth noticing that a curse of scattering is exactly what a priesthood requires.

Verses 8–12 · Judah, and "until Shiloh comes"

The name is the blessing. Yehudah… yodukha — "Judah… they shall praise you." Leah named him from the verb yadah, "to give thanks / praise," at 29:35, and the poem simply hands the name back to him as a prediction. Then the hand on the enemy's neck, and the brothers bowing — which is what Joseph dreamed, awarded here to somebody else.

Why Judah gets this at all. He is the fourth son, and the three ahead of him have just been disqualified in seven verses. But the case for him was built over chapters, not conferred: he proposed the sale (37:26), was told he was more righteous than he was by a woman he had wronged (38:26), stood surety for Benjamin, and offered to be enslaved in his brother's place (44:33). The sceptre goes to the one who volunteered to be a slave.

⚠️ Verse 10 is the most disputed line in Genesis, and this translation will not pretend otherwise. The Hebrew is ad ki yavo shiloh, and there are four serious readings of that one word. (1) A name — "until Shiloh comes." This is what the Masoretic pointing gives and what this translation prints; it is also the least explanatory, since it leaves the referent undefined. (2) "Until he comes to Shiloh" — the town in Ephraim where the tabernacle stood, which would make it a historical marker rather than a person. (3) Reading the consonants as shello — "until that which is his comes," or "until he comes to whom it belongs." This is the ancient understanding behind the Septuagint and the Syriac, and Ezekiel 21:27 appears to allude to it ("until he comes whose right it is"). (4) Shai lo — "until tribute comes to him." ⚠️ It should be said plainly that this verse has been read messianically in both traditions for a very long time — the Jewish Targums render it "until Messiah comes," and Christian readers have applied it to Jesus — and equally plainly that the messianic reading rests on option (1) or (3) rather than on anything the consonants settle by themselves. This project's rule is to give readings with their pedigrees and not to vote. KJV "until Shiloh come"; ASV the same with a margin note offering "till he come to Shiloh"; the ESV "until tribute comes to him," footnoting the alternatives; the NWT "until Shiloh comes." The versions genuinely disagree, and that is the honest state of the question.

"From between his feet." A mechoqeq is a commander's staff, held or planted between the feet of a seated ruler; the phrase is about a dynasty seated in place. It has also been read as a euphemism for descendants, which would make the line about an unbroken line rather than an unbroken reign — the two readings largely converge.

The vineyard lines. Verses 11-12 are extravagance, not agriculture: a man so rich in vines that he tethers a donkey to one — which would strip it — and does his laundry in wine. "The blood of grapes" and the darkened eyes have drawn heavy later interpretation; the plain sense is abundance so great it is wasteful. KJV's "his eyes shall be red with wine" reads chakhlili as redness; "dark, dull, glowing" is the likelier range, and this keeps it comparative as the Hebrew does — darker than wine.

Verses 13–21 · six short sayings, and a prayer that interrupts

The puns are the point. Three of these sayings are built on the sound of the name and the translation keeps the play where English can carry it: Dan yadin — Dan will judge; Gad, gedud yegudennu… yagud — a raiding band will raid him, he will raid at their heel, three forms of the same root in six words. These are not decorations; in a tribal oracle the name is treated as containing the destiny.

Issachar is not a compliment. "A bony donkey crouching between the saddlebags" — strong, and settling down under a load. He saw that rest was good and that the land was pleasant, and traded his freedom for both: mas oved, forced labour, is the phrase used of state corvée. It is a portrait of a tribe that took the comfortable valley and paid tribute to keep it. ⚠️ Several of these sayings are like this — Reuben, Simeon, Levi, Issachar, and arguably Benjamin are not flattering — which is what makes verse 28's summary line ("he blessed them") worth stopping at.

Verse 18 is the strangest line in the chapter. "For your salvation I have waited, Jehovah." A dying man, mid-oracle, halfway through a list of his sons, breaks off and prays. It is addressed to God, not to the sons; it is in the first person; and then he simply resumes with Gad. ⚠️ Nothing in the text explains it. Readings offered: that the vision of Dan's serpent-ambush frightened him; that the poem's litany of struggle drew the prayer out; that it is an old liturgical line embedded in the poem. It also contains the noun yeshu'ah, "salvation" — the word behind the name Joshua and later Jesus — and this translation leaves the interruption exactly where it is, unsmoothed, because it is one of the most human moments in the book: a man reciting the future of a nation stops for a second and asks for help.

Verses 22–27 · Joseph, and the God with four names

⚠️ Verse 22 is genuinely obscure. Ben porat can be read as "a fruitful son" (from parah, to bear fruit — hence the traditional "fruitful bough by a well, whose branches run over the wall") or as "a wild colt," taking porat as a young animal and banot tsa'adah alei shur as wild asses or a colt striding — which fits the surrounding animal imagery (lion, donkey, serpent, doe, wolf) rather better. The Hebrew of the second half is broken enough that no translation is confident: KJV "a fruitful bough… whose branches run over the wall"; the ESV keeps the bough with a footnote; the NIV has "a fruitful vine" and footnotes "a wild colt." This keeps the fruitful reading, flags the other, and does not paper over the fact that "daughters — each strode over a wall" is close to untranslatable.

The archers. Verses 23-24 are the only place in the poem where Jacob describes what was done to a son. He never says who the archers were. Given that he is speaking to the ten men who threw this son into a pit, and that he apparently never learned the truth (nothing in Genesis says he was told), the line sits in the room with a weight the speaker may not know it has.

Four divine titles in two verses, three of them new. "The Mighty One of Jacob" (avir Ya'akov), "the Shepherd," "the Stone of Israel," and "Shaddai." Avir and even Yisrael appear here for the first time; "the Stone of Israel" occurs nowhere else in the Bible in that form. ⚠️ The Hebrew of verse 24's second half is compressed to the point of ambiguity — "from there, the Shepherd, the Stone of Israel" — and translations insert connectives freely; this keeps it bare.

The wordplay in verse 25 is the one Genesis planted long ago. "Shaddai" beside "blessings of shadayim — breasts — and womb." Whether the divine name is genuinely related to that noun is uncertain (see the dictionary entry), but the poet clearly hears them together, and the blessing is stacked in three tiers: heaven above, the deep below, and the human body between them.

"The one set apart from his brothers." Nezir echav — from the root behind nazir, one consecrated and separated by vow. It can mean "prince" by extension, and most versions render it that way (KJV "him that was separate from his brethren"; NIV "the prince among his brothers"). Kept as separation here, because that is what actually happened to him.

Benjamin the wolf. Two lines, and they are not affectionate. ⚠️ The saying is hard to reconcile with the boy the story has just spent five chapters protecting — but it is a tribal oracle, and the tribe of Benjamin went on to produce Ehud, the archers of Judges 20, and Saul. The poem is not describing the child in the room.

Verses 28–33 · "he blessed them" — and Leah

⚠️ Read verse 28 against what just happened. Reuben was demoted, Simeon and Levi's anger was cursed, Issachar was called a pack animal who sold himself for a comfortable valley, and Benjamin was called a wolf — and the narrator's summary is "and he blessed them, each man according to his blessing." The word covers the lot. It is worth taking seriously rather than explaining away: in this book a blessing is a true word about a future, spoken with authority, not a compliment. Telling a man the truth about what his descendants will be is inside the category. The qualifier does the work — "each according to his blessing," which is to say, his own, and not another's.

The last instruction is a property deed. He does not close with a prophecy but with a title search: the cave, the field, Ephron the Hittite, Machpelah, facing Mamre, bought, from the sons of Heth. He recites the transaction because the transaction is the point — it is the only ground the family owns in the land it has been promised, and he is being carried four hundred miles to lie in it. Compare what he told Pharaoh two chapters ago: the days of my sojournings.

⚠️ "And there I buried Leah." Four words, at the end of the list, and Genesis has never mentioned her death. We are told here, in passing, in a burial instruction. And the man who worked fourteen years for Rachel, who buried Rachel on a roadside because there was still some distance to go, and who was still measuring that distance one chapter ago (48:7), is asking to be laid beside Leah — in the family grave, with Abraham and Sarah and Isaac and Rebekah. The text offers no reflection whatsoever on this. It is simply the last arrangement he makes, and the wife he did not choose is the one he chooses to lie next to for good.

"He gathered his feet into the bed." He had gathered his strength and sat up when Joseph came in (48:2); now, having finished, he draws his feet back up and lies down. Then three verbs and it is over: he expired, and was gathered to his people — the same verb, asaf, as "gather" in the poem's opening line (v. 1) and as his feet in this one. The chapter begins with a man calling his sons to gather and ends with the man himself gathered.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the words exact / distinct: the pronoun break in verse 4 kept as the Hebrew has it ("you went up… he went up"), not tidied; "cursed be their anger," not cursed be they; the name-puns kept audible — Judah/praise, Dan/judge, Gad/raid; "until Shiloh comes" following the Masoretic pointing with all four readings in the note and no vote; "the one set apart from his brothers" for nezir rather than "prince"; "darker of eyes than wine" kept comparative; and Shaddai left beside shadayim so the wordplay survives.

Echoes paid. Reuben and Bilhah, forty years of silence ended in one line; Shechem condemned at last on the right grounds; Leah's naming of Judah — "this time I will praise" — handed back as prophecy; Joseph's dream of brothers bowing, awarded in this poem to Judah; Judah's offer to be enslaved answered with the sceptre; and Abraham's purchase at Machpelah recited as a deed by the last man to be buried in it.

Echoes planted. The sceptre of Judah, and everything the later books build on it; the Lion of Judah; Levi scattered — a curse that becomes a priesthood; Simeon absorbed into Judah and effectively gone; Shiloh, where the tabernacle will stand; and the bones of Jacob starting a journey his descendants will repeat.

Next installment in this book — and the last of Genesis: chapter 50. Joseph weeps on his father's face, and Egypt mourns him seventy days. Then the brothers, with the old man dead, invent a message from him and beg for their lives — and get the sentence the whole book has been building toward: "You meant evil against me, but God meant it for good."
