By the Chebar canal in Babylonia, five years into the exile of King Jehoiachin — the chariot vision, and the call of a priest who will never serve at an altar.
593 BC, and the book says so to the day: 'the fifth of the fourth month, the fifth year of the exile of King Jehoiachin' (597 BC + 5). ⚠ Verse 1's OWN date — 'the thirtieth year' — is counted from something the book never names; the readings (Ezekiel's age at 30, Josiah's reform in 622, a Babylonian era) are set out in the notes.

Translator’s Notes

Verses 1–3 · a date nobody can fix, a canal you can find on a map, and a priest with no temple

⚠ «In the thirtieth year» — the thirtieth year of WHAT? Ezekiel is the most obsessively dated book in the Old Testament: fourteen oracles carry a day, a month, and a year, all counted from one fixed point — the exile of King Jehoiachin in 597 BC. And the book’s very first date is the one it never explains. Verse 2 helpfully supplies the standard reckoning (“the fifth year” = 593 BC); verse 1 does not say what its thirty years are counted from. The old proposals, none provable:

• Ezekiel’s own age. Thirty is the year a priest entered service (Numbers 4:3) — so the vision falls in the year he should have begun at an altar he is a thousand miles from. The most attractive reading, and the oldest.
• From Josiah’s reform, when the book of the law was found in the temple — 622 BC, which lands almost exactly on 593.
• A Babylonian regnal era (Nabopolassar’s accession, 626/625 BC) — near, but not exact.
• A jubilee count. Suggested by later Jewish tradition; the arithmetic is not recoverable.

The river Chebar is a real place, and we know its name from outside the Bible. It is not a river at all but the nāru kabaru, the “Great Canal” — a major irrigation channel off the Euphrates running past Nippur, named in Babylonian cuneiform business documents from exactly this period. Judean deportees were settled in villages along it (one is called āl-Yāhūdu, “Judah-town,” in tablets recovered in the last few decades). Ezekiel’s vision has an address.

⚠ The seam in the person. Verse 1 is “I”; verse 3 is “him — Ezekiel son of Buzi”; from verse 4 it is “I” again. The book does not smooth this and neither do we. Some read verse 3 as an editor’s title inserted into a first-person memoir; others as the prophet formally introducing himself once, in the third person, as ancient superscriptions do. It is the sort of thing that is easy to tidy and expensive to lose.

«The word of Jehovah came, indeed came.» The Hebrew doubles the verb (hayoh hayah) — an infinitive absolute, the ordinary way Hebrew leans on a word. KJV keeps the weight with “came expressly”; ASV “came expressly” too; NIV flattens it to “came.” It matters here because the whole point of the sentence is that the word of Jehovah reached a man in the land of the Chaldeans — unclean ground, outside the land, where by every assumption of Ezekiel’s training it had no business arriving.

«And the hand of Jehovah was upon him there.» Yad YHWH is Ezekiel’s own phrase for what happens to him — it recurs at 3:14, 3:22, 8:1, 33:22, 37:1 and 40:1, always at the onset of a vision, and it is heavier than inspiration: it seizes, lifts, and carries him. And the last word of the verse is there. A priest who can never serve, in a country that should be God-forsaken, is where the hand falls.

Verses 4–14 · the storm out of the north, the living creatures — and a word nobody can translate

Out of the NORTH. Not a neutral compass point. North is the road Babylon’s armies came down (Jeremiah’s boiling pot tilts “from the north,” Jeremiah 1:13–14), and in the mythology of Israel’s neighbours the north is Zaphon, the mountain where the gods hold council. God arrives from the direction the disaster came from, in the vehicle of a storm.

⚠ Chashmal — left untranslated, because nobody knows. The word occurs three times in the Bible, all in Ezekiel (1:4, 1:27, 8:2), and its meaning is genuinely lost. The Septuagint guessed ēlektron — electrum, the pale gold-silver alloy, or amber — and the Vulgate followed with electrum, which is where KJV “the colour of amber” comes from; ASV and NIV both retreat to “glowing metal”; NWT prints “electrum.” We keep the Hebrew, on the same policy this translation uses for Sheol and El Shaddai: a word this uncertain should look uncertain. (A modern footnote: when Hebrew was revived as a spoken language, chashmal was drafted to mean electricity — taken straight from the Greek guess at this verse. It is now the word on Israeli power bills.)

«Like the gleam of» is literally «like the EYE of.» Ke-ein runs through the chapter (vv 4, 7, 16, 22, 27) — Hebrew uses the eye for the look of a thing, as English once said “to the eye.” We render it the same way each time so the reader can watch it, because it sets up one of the strangest images in the vision: the rims of the wheels are full of eyes (v18). The chapter is described with the word for sight, and then the machinery starts looking back.

The four living creatures (chayyot) are never named here. Ezekiel identifies them in chapter 10: they are the cherubim — not the plump infants of later painting but the composite guardian-beasts of the ancient Near East, the winged human-headed bulls and lions that stood at Assyrian and Babylonian palace gates, and the figures whose outstretched wings covered the ark. An exile in Babylon walked past that iconography daily; the vision takes the empire’s throne-guardians and puts them under the feet of somebody else’s throne.

The four faces — human, lion, ox, eagle — have had an enormous afterlife. Revelation 4:7 has four living creatures around the throne but gives each ONE face and reorders them; from Irenaeus onward the four became the emblems of the four Gospels (the assignments vary by author, which is why Matthew’s man and John’s eagle are not fixed until later). Jewish tradition read them as the four noblest of their kinds: humankind, the wild beasts, the domestic beasts, the birds — the whole animate creation carrying the throne.

⚠ A ketiv/qere in verse 8. The consonants as written say “and his hand”; the vowels the Masoretes supplied say “and the hands of.” We follow the read form (“human hands”) with everyone else, and note the written one, as this translation does wherever the two tracks diverge.

And the grammar is unstable, on purpose or otherwise. The creatures are grammatically feminine, but the chapter keeps slipping into masculine forms and back (“the four of them” alternates gender within single verses), and the singular and plural change places — “the living creature” in verses 20–22 for what verse 5 called four. Translations quietly regularize it. It is worth knowing that in Hebrew this passage reads like a man whose sentences keep coming apart in his hands.

«Ran and returned» (ratso vashov) — two infinitives, no main verb, the syntax as fast as the thing described. KJV “ran and returned” is exactly right and has never been improved on; NIV “darted back and forth” explains it at the cost of the strangeness. The comparison, bazaq, occurs nowhere else in the Bible and is usually taken as a lightning-flash.

Verses 15–21 · a wheel within a wheel, and rims full of eyes

The wheels arrive last and change what the thing is. Up to verse 14 the vision could be a cloud full of angels. From verse 15 it is a vehicle — and one built to move in any direction without turning, which is the whole engineering point of “a wheel within a wheel” (two rims set at right angles, so the machine can go straight ahead on any of its four sides). Ancient Near Eastern gods rode wheeled thrones; Israel’s God, whose throne everybody assumed was bolted to a room in Jerusalem, turns out to have one that goes anywhere. Written to a man in Babylon, that is not decoration. It is the argument.

Tarshish, the stone the wheels gleam like, is named for the distant port of Tarshish — the far end of the known trade routes. Nobody is sure which gem: KJV “beryl,” ASV “beryl,” NIV “topaz,” others chrysolite. We print “beryl” and flag the uncertainty; what the Hebrew is really saying is the most expensive-looking thing you have ever heard of, from the ends of the earth.

«They had height, and they had fearfulness.» The Hebrew is that blunt — the rims were high, and they were frightening. KJV “they were so high that they were dreadful” supplies a logic the Hebrew leaves out; ASV keeps them side by side, and so do we. Then: full of eyes all around. The commentaries offer omniscience, or the many-eyed guardians of Persian and Greek imagination, or simply the glitter of a jewelled rim. Ezekiel offers nothing. He is a witness, not an interpreter, and this chapter is one long refusal to explain.

«For the spirit of the living creature was in the wheels.» Ruach is doing at least two jobs at once here — it is the wind of the storm in verse 4 and the spirit that steers in verses 12 and 20, and Hebrew does not make the reader choose. Whatever it is, it is not in the wheels’ hubs: the machine is animate, and it goes where the will goes. The word for the wheels, ofannim, was later promoted by Jewish angelology into an order of angels in its own right — the Ophanim, ranked with the seraphim and the cherubim, all of them originally furniture from this chapter.

Verses 22–25 · the vault — and the sound

⚠ «The likeness of a VAULT.» The word is raqia, and this translation has rendered it “vault” since Genesis 1:6 — the hammered-out dome that divides the waters, which English inherited as “firmament” by way of the Greek stereōma and the Latin firmamentum. Every version that prints “firmament” in Genesis prints it here too, and it is worth stopping over what that means: Ezekiel is looking at the sky of Genesis 1 from underneath, and there is a throne on top of it. The creation account puts God above the vault by implication; Ezekiel says he saw it. This is the single loudest echo in the chapter, and it is invisible to a reader whose Bible uses two different words in the two places.

«Like the gleam of awesome ice.» Qerach is ice or frost; nora is the participle of fear — the same root that ended Malachi 1 (“my name is feared among the nations”). KJV “the colour of the terrible crystal”; ASV “the terrible crystal”; NIV “sparkling like ice, and awesome.” The older versions are better: the ice is not beautiful-and-also-frightening, it is frightening, and that is what it looks like.

«Like the sound of many waters, like the voice of Shaddai.» Three comparisons stacked for one sound: a flood, the voice of God under his oldest patriarchal name, and — the last and most ordinary — the noise of an army camp. A deported man reaches for the loudest thing he has actually heard, and it is the Babylonian military. KJV “the noise of an host”; the TLB paraphrase (“like the roar of a great army”) tidies away the word camp. And the image goes forward as well as back: “his voice like the sound of many waters” is how Revelation 1:15 describes the risen Christ.

And then the wings stop. Twice (vv 24, 25) the same clause: when they stood, they let their wings drop. The noise of the whole apparatus falls away, and into the silence comes a voice from above the vault. The chapter has been building a machine for twenty-one verses in order to switch it off.

Verses 26–28 · a throne, a human, and the war-bow in the cloud

⚠ The most careful sentence in the Old Testament. Read what verse 26 actually says: above the vault was the appearance of a sapphire stone, the likeness of a throne; and on the likeness of the throne was a likeness like the appearance of a human. Then verse 28 caps it: the appearance of the likeness of the glory of Jehovah — three nouns of distance stacked in front of the noun that matters. The chapter’s two workhorse words are demut (“likeness”) and mar’eh (“appearance”), and between them they occur about twenty times in twenty-eight verses, propped up by a wall of “like” and “as.” Ezekiel never once says he saw God. He says he saw something that looked like the look of the likeness of the weight of him. Every version preserves this; almost every summary of the chapter destroys it.

«A likeness like the appearance of a human.» The word is adam, and it is doing something enormous. Genesis 1:26 says God made adam in his image and after his demut — the same word — and here is the sentence from the other end: what is on the throne has the demut of an adam. The library will not press this further than Ezekiel does. But note that the figure on the throne is described only as far as a waist, in fire above and fire below; and that the very next thing this figure does (2:1) is address the prophet as “son of man” — ben adam, the title it will use for him ninety-three more times, insisting on the distance between the human on the throne and the human on the ground.

⚠ And the light around the throne is a WEAPON. “Like the appearance of the bow that is in the cloud on a day of rain” — the word is qeshet, and Hebrew has no separate word for a rainbow: it is the war-bow, which is exactly why Genesis 9:13 is so striking (“my bow I have set in the cloud” — a weapon hung up, pointing away). This translation has printed “bow” there since Genesis, and here is the payoff: the glory around the throne of the God who is about to destroy Jerusalem looks like the sign that he swore not to destroy the world. Ezekiel does not comment. He puts the two things in one sentence and falls over.

«The GLORY of Jehovah» is kavod — from the root that means heavy, the same one Malachi used two chapters ago to ask “where is my honor?” (to honor is to treat as weighty). God’s glory is his weight, and in Ezekiel it is a character in the plot: this glory will be watched leaving the temple in chapters 10 and 11, and watched coming back in chapter 43. Everything the book does turns on the fact that it was first seen abroad.

«And I fell on my face.» The right response, and the last thing he does under his own power. The voice that follows will spend the next two chapters getting him back on his feet, feeding him a scroll, and sending him to people who will not listen.

⚠ A note on how dangerous this chapter was thought to be. The vision became the seed of Ma’aseh Merkavah, “the Work of the Chariot” — the oldest strand of Jewish mysticism — and the Mishnah restricts it as it restricts nothing else: the chariot may not be expounded even to one student, unless he is wise and understands of his own knowledge (Hagigah 2:1). The Talmud further reports that Ezekiel was nearly withdrawn from public use because its laws contradict the Torah, until Hananiah ben Hezekiah burned three hundred jars of midnight oil reconciling them (Shabbat 13b). The word merkavah (chariot) never appears in this chapter. It is what readers called it because they could not stop reading it.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the exact / distinct words: “vault” for raqia, the same rendering as Genesis 1, so the throne is visibly sitting on the sky of the creation account; “bow” for qeshet, the weapon, so Genesis 9 lands; “like the gleam of” for ke-ein, literally “like the eye of,” held identical through the chapter so the eye-filled rims of v18 arrive as they should; chashmal left untranslated because it is genuinely unknown; “likeness” and “appearance” kept rigidly distinct (demut / mar’eh) rather than varied for style, since the hedging IS the theology; “ran and returned” for the verbless ratso vashov; and “the hand of Jehovah” for yad YHWH, the book’s own word for what happens to him.

Echoes paid. The vault of Genesis 1:6, seen from below with a throne on it; the war-bow in the cloud of Genesis 9:13, now the light around that throne; demut, the likeness humankind was made in (Genesis 1:26), turned around and used of the figure on the throne; kavod, the weight-word Malachi 1:6 asked after (“where is my honor?”), arriving in person; the storm out of the north, Jeremiah’s direction of disaster (Jeremiah 1:13–14); and a second ketiv/qere reported (v8), after Daniel 11.

Echoes planted. “Son of man” (ben adam), the title the voice is about to start using and will use ninety-three times. The glory itself, which becomes a character: it leaves the temple in chapters 10–11 and returns in chapter 43, and the whole book is the distance between those two journeys. The cherubim, unnamed here and identified at 10:20 (“I knew that they were cherubim”). The hand of Jehovah, which will lift him by a lock of his hair (8:3) and set him down in a valley of bones (37:1). And the four living creatures, which reach the New Testament as the four around the throne of Revelation 4 — and, through Irenaeus, become the emblems of the four Gospels.
