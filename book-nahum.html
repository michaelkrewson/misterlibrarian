📖 Nahum

נַחוּם Nachum — 'Comfort, consolation' — from nacham, 'to comfort' (the root behind Noah, Nehemiah, and Menachem). ⚠ The name is exact, not ironic: a book of three chapters of doom on an empire is titled 'Comfort' because Nineveh's fall IS the comfort — the good news — for every nation Assyria had crushed. Consolation written from the underside of an empire.
Ναούμ (Naoum) — The Septuagint keeps Nahum seventh among the Twelve. ⚠ Its most quoted line reaches the New Testament through its TWIN in Isaiah: Nahum 2:1 (English 1:15), 'the feet of him who brings good news (mevasser), who publishes peace,' is almost word for word Isaiah 52:7, and the herald's word basar underlies the Greek euangelion, 'gospel' — Paul quotes the line at Romans 10:15. The name Nahum itself does not appear in the New Testament except (probably by coincidence of spelling) in Luke's genealogy, and in the town-name 'Capernaum' (kefar Nahum).

The dark twin of Jonah: the same city, Nineveh, a century and a half later — and this time no mercy. A single sustained oracle celebrating the fall of the ancient world's most brutal empire, opening with a wrath-hymn built as a half-finished alphabet, insisting that the God who is 'slow to anger' is also 'great in power and will by no means clear the guilty.' A book of comfort for the conquered, written in the shape of a conqueror's obituary.

Chapter by chapter

Nahum 1The dark twin of Jonah: the same city, Nineveh, a century and a half later — and this time no mercy. Where Jonah's Nineveh repented and was spared, Nahum's has gone back to being the ancient world's most efficient killing-machine, and God announces its end. The book opens not with the city but with a wrath-HYMN built as an alphabetic acrostic that breaks off halfway — an ordered alphabet of judgment left unfinished — and it quotes the famous Exodus-34 formula tilted toward JUSTICE: “slow to anger and great in power, and he will by no means leave the guilty unpunished.” Yet in the exact middle sits the one verse of mercy (“Jehovah is good, a stronghold in the day of distress”) that makes the whole book of doom deserve its name — Nahum means COMFORT, because the fall of the destroyer is the good news for everyone it crushed. ⚠ And the famous “feet of him who brings good news” verse is NOT here — it is Hebrew 2:1 (English renumbers it 1:15); this site follows the Masoretic count.

About the book

Where it sits
SEVENTH of THE TWELVE in the Hebrew order (after Micah, before Habakkuk). Three chapters. ⚠ A versification offset at the chapter 1/2 seam: the famous 'good news / him who publishes peace' verse is Hebrew 2:1, which English Bibles renumber 1:15 (so English chapter 1 has 15 verses, Hebrew has 14, and the herald opens chapter 2). This site follows the Masoretic numbering.
Genre
PROPHETIC ORACLE (a massa, 'burden') in high, vivid poetry — some of the most brilliant battle-description in the Bible (the charging chariots, the flashing swords, the heaps of corpses of chapters 2-3). ⚠ Chapter 1 opens with an ALPHABETIC ACROSTIC hymn (vv2-8), each line beginning with the next Hebrew letter — but the acrostic is INCOMPLETE, running only about halfway (to kaph) before it breaks off, one of the most-debated features of the book: a damaged text, or a deliberately unfinished alphabet of judgment. Nahum calls his own work a 'book' (sefer), unusually, which may mark it as a written composition from the start.
Author
NAHUM THE ELKOSHITE, of whom nothing is known but his home town — and ELKOSH cannot be located (Galilee, southern Judah, or a village beside Nineveh's own ruins near Mosul are all proposed, with no real evidence). Unlike the social prophets Amos and Micah, Nahum shows no interest in Judah's internal sins; his entire subject is the judgment of a foreign empire, which has made some readers uneasy (is it prophecy, or nationalism?) and others grateful (it is the Bible taking the side of the crushed against the crusher).
Date written
c. 663–612 BC, and probably late in that range. Nahum names the fall of THEBES (No-Amon) as a past event (3:8) — Assyria sacked it in 663 — and the whole book foresees the fall of NINEVEH, which the Babylonians and Medes carried out in 612. So he wrote in the half-century between, most likely when Assyria's collapse was already visibly under way (the 620s-610s). He stands a century and a half after Jonah and a couple of generations after Micah and Isaiah.
Place
NINEVEH — the Assyrian capital on the Tigris (opposite modern Mosul), and the sole subject of the book — seen from JUDAH, which had been an Assyrian vassal since Hezekiah and is promised, in 1:13, that its yoke will be broken. The horizon also reaches to THEBES on the distant Nile (3:8), held up as the mirror of Nineveh's coming fate.
Audience
Judah under the Assyrian boot — a small nation that had watched Assyria destroy the northern kingdom, deport its people, and nearly take Jerusalem, and had paid crushing tribute for a century. To them the book is not abstract theology but news they had prayed for: the empire is finished. The one mercy-verse (1:7, 'Jehovah is good, a stronghold in the day of distress') is addressed to them; the rest is addressed, with terrible relish, to their oppressor.

How it's laid out

1THE WRATH-HYMN AND THE VERDICT — an acrostic hymn on the God who comes in storm, 'slow to anger and great in power' yet leaving no guilty party unpunished; the one verse of refuge for those who trust him (1:7); and the sentence pronounced — a full end, the yoke broken off Judah, the king's grave dug.
2THE SIEGE OF NINEVEH — a dazzling, staccato battle-scene: the herald with good news on the mountains (Hebrew 2:1), the scarlet shields and flashing chariots, the river-gates opened, the city plundered, and the taunt: 'where now is the lions' den?' — Assyria, who had made the nations its prey.
3WOE TO THE CITY OF BLOODS — the final oracle: 'woe to the bloody city, all full of lies and plunder'; the mountains of corpses; the comparison to fallen Thebes ('are you better than No-Amon?'); and the last line, an obituary read over the whole empire: 'all who hear the news of you clap their hands, for on whom has your endless cruelty not passed?'

What it's about

  • GOD'S JUSTICE ON EMPIRE — Nahum is the Bible's clearest statement that the great predator-nations are themselves answerable. Assyria had been God's rod (Isaiah 10) but is not therefore exempt; the God who is patient for a hundred and fifty years is not asleep, and 'will by no means clear the guilty' (1:3).
  • COMFORT AS THE FLIP SIDE OF DOOM — the book's name is its thesis. To the victim, the destruction of the destroyer is grace; the same event is Nineveh's obituary and Judah's liberation ('now I will break his yoke from off you,' 1:13). Nahum refuses to let the reader pretend the two can be separated.
  • THE TWO HALVES OF THE ONE FORMULA — Jonah and Nahum together hold the whole of Exodus 34:6-7. Jonah quotes its mercy ('gracious and compassionate, slow to anger') as a grievance; Nahum quotes its justice ('slow to anger… yet by no means clearing the guilty'). The God of both books is the same God, and the two truths are not in competition.
  • THE ACROSTIC THAT BREAKS — the half-finished alphabet of chapter 1 is, on the deliberate reading, itself a message: an ordered world coming apart, a completeness cut short — the fate of Nineveh enacted in the shape of the poem.
  • THE HERALD ON THE MOUNTAINS — the good-news verse (2:1) that Nahum shares with Isaiah 52 turns the whole book toward the New Testament's 'gospel': the runner bringing word that the tyrant is dead, which becomes, in Isaiah and Paul, the runner bringing word of God's own coming.

The source text

Translated from the pointed Hebrew Masoretic Text (as printed by Mechon-Mamre). ⚠ Nahum's Hebrew is superb and, in places, hard: chapter 1's acrostic is loose and locally damaged (verse 10, three images crushed together, is one of the thorniest half-verses in the prophets), and the rapid battle-poetry of chapters 2-3 is dense with rare words. This translation keeps the acrostic's Masoretic wording rather than 'repairing' it to a smoother alphabet, and follows the Hebrew chapter division (so the herald-verse is 2:1). Where a verse is genuinely uncertain it says so.

Looking forward

Nahum reaches the New Testament chiefly through the herald-verse it shares with Isaiah. ⚠ 'The feet of him who brings GOOD NEWS, who publishes PEACE' (Nahum 2:1 = Isaiah 52:7) supplies, through the Greek euangelion, the very word GOSPEL, and Paul quotes it of the preachers of Christ: 'how beautiful are the feet of those who bring good news' (Romans 10:15). More broadly, Nahum holds a truth the New Testament keeps: that God's patience is not indifference, and that final judgment on entrenched evil is itself good news to its victims — the note struck in Revelation's fall of 'Babylon the great' (Revelation 18), which borrows Nahum's imagery of the ruined predator-city and the same double edge, terror to the tyrant and 'rejoice, O heaven' to the oppressed. The book's insistence that the meek will be avenged and the crushing empire brought low runs straight into the Magnificat's 'he has brought down the mighty from their thrones.'

Where the debates are

THE ACROSTIC — genuine but damaged, deliberately incomplete, or (a minority) not really an acrostic at all; the single largest technical question in the book. THE MORAL PROBLEM — whether Nahum's undisguised joy at Nineveh's destruction is prophetic justice or mere nationalism (the counter-reading: it is the Bible siding with the victims of genocide against their killers, and 1:7 keeps it God-centred). THE UNITY — whether the hymn of chapter 1 is Nahum's own or an older liturgy he prefaced to his oracle. And the perennial mystery of ELKOSH, the one biographical fact the book gives, which no one can place.
