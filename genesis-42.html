Genesis 42 — The Mister Translation

1
וַיַּרְא יַעֲקֹב, כִּי יֶשׁ-שֶׁבֶר בְּמִצְרָיִם; וַיֹּאמֶר יַעֲקֹב לְבָנָיו, לָמָּה תִּתְרָאוּ.
And Jacob saw that there was grain in Egypt, and Jacob said to his sons, "Why do you keep looking at one another?"note
Y Jacob vio que había grano en Egipto, y Jacob dijo a sus hijos: «¿Por qué se están mirando unos a otros?»
2
וַיֹּאמֶר--הִנֵּה שָׁמַעְתִּי, כִּי יֶשׁ-שֶׁבֶר בְּמִצְרָיִם; רְדוּ-שָׁמָּה וְשִׁבְרוּ-לָנוּ מִשָּׁם, וְנִחְיֶה וְלֹא נָמוּת.
And he said, "Look — I have heard that there is grain in Egypt. Go down there and buy grain for us from there, that we may live and not die."note
Y dijo: «Mira — he oído que hay grano en Egipto. Bajen allá y cómprennos grano de allí, para que vivamos y no muramos».
3
וַיֵּרְדוּ אֲחֵי-יוֹסֵף, עֲשָׂרָה, לִשְׁבֹּר בָּר, מִמִּצְרָיִם.
And Joseph's brothers went down, ten of them, to buy grain from Egypt.note
Y los hermanos de José bajaron, diez de ellos, a comprar grano de Egipto.
4
וְאֶת-בִּנְיָמִין אֲחִי יוֹסֵף, לֹא-שָׁלַח יַעֲקֹב אֶת-אֶחָיו: כִּי אָמַר, פֶּן-יִקְרָאֶנּוּ אָסוֹן.
But Benjamin, Joseph's brother, Jacob did not send with his brothers, for he said, "Otherwise harm may befall him."note
Pero a Benjamín, hermano de José, Jacob no lo envió con sus hermanos, porque dijo: «No sea que le sobrevenga alguna desgracia».
5
וַיָּבֹאוּ בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל, לִשְׁבֹּר בְּתוֹךְ הַבָּאִים: כִּי-הָיָה הָרָעָב, בְּאֶרֶץ כְּנָעַן.
And the sons of Israel came to buy grain among those who came, for the famine was in the land of Canaan.note
Y los hijos de Israel vinieron a comprar grano entre los que venían, porque el hambre estaba en la tierra de Canaán.
6
וְיוֹסֵף, הוּא הַשַּׁלִּיט עַל-הָאָרֶץ--הוּא הַמַּשְׁבִּיר, לְכָל-עַם הָאָרֶץ; וַיָּבֹאוּ אֲחֵי יוֹסֵף, וַיִּשְׁתַּחֲווּ-לוֹ אַפַּיִם אָרְצָה.
And Joseph — he was the ruler over the land; he was the one who sold grain to all the people of the land. And Joseph's brothers came and bowed down to him, faces to the ground.note
Y José — él era el gobernante sobre la tierra; él era quien vendía grano a todo el pueblo de la tierra. Y los hermanos de José vinieron y se inclinaron ante él, rostro a tierra.

7
וַיַּרְא יוֹסֵף אֶת-אֶחָיו, וַיַּכִּרֵם; וַיִּתְנַכֵּר אֲלֵיהֶם וַיְדַבֵּר אִתָּם קָשׁוֹת, וַיֹּאמֶר אֲלֵהֶם מֵאַיִן בָּאתֶם, וַיֹּאמְרוּ, מֵאֶרֶץ כְּנַעַן לִשְׁבָּר-אֹכֶל.
And Joseph saw his brothers and recognized them — and made himself unrecognizable to them, and spoke harshly with them. And he said to them, "Where have you come from?" And they said, "From the land of Canaan, to buy food."note
Y José vio a sus hermanos y los reconoció — y se hizo irreconocible para ellos, y les habló con dureza. Y les dijo: «¿De dónde han venido?» Y ellos dijeron: «De la tierra de Canaán, a comprar alimento».

8
וַיַּכֵּר יוֹסֵף, אֶת-אֶחָיו; וְהֵם, לֹא הִכִּרֻהוּ.
And Joseph recognized his brothers, but they did not recognize him.note
Y José reconoció a sus hermanos, pero ellos no lo reconocieron a él.
9
וַיִּזְכֹּר יוֹסֵף--אֵת הַחֲלֹמוֹת, אֲשֶׁר חָלַם לָהֶם; וַיֹּאמֶר אֲלֵהֶם מְרַגְּלִים אַתֶּם, לִרְאוֹת אֶת-עֶרְוַת הָאָרֶץ בָּאתֶם.
And Joseph remembered the dreams that he had dreamed about them, and he said to them, "You are spies! You have come to see the nakedness of the land."note
Y José se acordó de los sueños que había soñado acerca de ellos, y les dijo: «¡Son espías! Han venido a ver la desnudez de la tierra».

10
וַיֹּאמְרוּ אֵלָיו, לֹא אֲדֹנִי; וַעֲבָדֶיךָ בָּאוּ, לִשְׁבָּר-אֹכֶל.
And they said to him, "No, my lord — your servants have come to buy food.note
Y ellos le dijeron: «No, señor mío — tus siervos han venido a comprar alimento.
11
כֻּלָּנוּ, בְּנֵי אִישׁ-אֶחָד נָחְנוּ; כֵּנִים אֲנַחְנוּ, לֹא-הָיוּ עֲבָדֶיךָ מְרַגְּלִים.
All of us are the sons of one man; we are honest men. Your servants have never been spies."note
Todos nosotros somos hijos de un mismo hombre; somos hombres honrados. Tus siervos nunca han sido espías».
12
וַיֹּאמֶר, אֲלֵהֶם: לֹא, כִּי-עֶרְוַת הָאָרֶץ בָּאתֶם לִרְאוֹת.
And he said to them, "No — you have come to see the nakedness of the land."note
Y él les dijo: «No — han venido a ver la desnudez de la tierra».
13
וַיֹּאמְרוּ, שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר עֲבָדֶיךָ אַחִים אֲנַחְנוּ בְּנֵי אִישׁ-אֶחָד--בְּאֶרֶץ כְּנָעַן; וְהִנֵּה הַקָּטֹן אֶת-אָבִינוּ הַיּוֹם, וְהָאֶחָד אֵינֶנּוּ.
And they said, "Your servants are twelve brothers, the sons of one man in the land of Canaan; and look — the youngest is with our father today, and the one is not."note
Y ellos dijeron: «Tus siervos somos doce hermanos, hijos de un mismo hombre en la tierra de Canaán; y mira — el menor está hoy con nuestro padre, y el uno no está».
cross-refs⤷ 37:30
14
וַיֹּאמֶר אֲלֵהֶם, יוֹסֵף: הוּא, אֲשֶׁר דִּבַּרְתִּי אֲלֵכֶם לֵאמֹר--מְרַגְּלִים אַתֶּם.
And Joseph said to them, "That is what I said to you: you are spies.note
Y José les dijo: «Eso es lo que les dije: son espías.
15
בְּזֹאת, תִּבָּחֵנוּ: חֵי פַרְעֹה אִם-תֵּצְאוּ מִזֶּה, כִּי אִם-בְּבוֹא אֲחִיכֶם הַקָּטֹן הֵנָּה.
By this you will be tested: as Pharaoh lives, you will not go out from here unless your youngest brother comes here.note
En esto serán probados: por la vida del faraón, no saldrán de aquí a menos que venga acá su hermano menor.
16
שִׁלְחוּ מִכֶּם אֶחָד, וְיִקַּח אֶת-אֲחִיכֶם, וְאַתֶּם הֵאָסְרוּ, וְיִבָּחֲנוּ דִּבְרֵיכֶם הַאֱמֶת אִתְּכֶם; וְאִם-לֹא--חֵי פַרְעֹה, כִּי מְרַגְּלִים אַתֶּם.
Send one of you, and let him fetch your brother, and you will be bound, and your words will be tested — whether the truth is with you. And if not, as Pharaoh lives, you are spies."note
Envíen a uno de ustedes, y que traiga a su hermano, y ustedes quedarán presos, y sus palabras serán probadas — si la verdad está con ustedes. Y si no, por la vida del faraón, son espías».
17
וַיֶּאֱסֹף אֹתָם אֶל-מִשְׁמָר, שְׁלֹשֶׁת יָמִים.
And he gathered them into custody for three days.note
Y los reunió bajo custodia por tres días.
18
וַיֹּאמֶר אֲלֵהֶם יוֹסֵף בַּיּוֹם הַשְּׁלִישִׁי, זֹאת עֲשׂוּ וִחְיוּ; אֶת-הָאֱלֹהִים, אֲנִי יָרֵא.
And on the third day Joseph said to them, "Do this and live — I fear God.note
Y al tercer día José les dijo: «Hagan esto y vivan — yo temo a Dios.
19
אִם-כֵּנִים אַתֶּם--אֲחִיכֶם אֶחָד, יֵאָסֵר בְּבֵית מִשְׁמַרְכֶם; וְאַתֶּם לְכוּ הָבִיאוּ, שֶׁבֶר רַעֲבוֹן בָּתֵּיכֶם.
If you are honest men, let one of your brothers be bound in your place of custody, and you go and carry grain for the hunger of your households.note
Si son hombres honrados, quede uno de sus hermanos preso en el lugar de su custodia; y ustedes vayan y lleven grano para el hambre de sus casas.
20
וְאֶת-אֲחִיכֶם הַקָּטֹן תָּבִיאוּ אֵלַי, וְיֵאָמְנוּ דִבְרֵיכֶם וְלֹא תָמוּתוּ; וַיַּעֲשׂוּ-כֵן.
And bring your youngest brother to me, and your words will be confirmed and you will not die." And they did so.note
Y traigan a su hermano menor, y sus palabras serán confirmadas y no morirán». Y así lo hicieron.
21
וַיֹּאמְרוּ אִישׁ אֶל-אָחִיו, אֲבָל אֲשֵׁמִים אֲנַחְנוּ עַל-אָחִינוּ, אֲשֶׁר רָאִינוּ צָרַת נַפְשׁוֹ בְּהִתְחַנְנוֹ אֵלֵינוּ, וְלֹא שָׁמָעְנוּ; עַל-כֵּן בָּאָה אֵלֵינוּ, הַצָּרָה הַזֹּאת.
And they said, each to his brother, "Truly we are guilty concerning our brother — that we saw the distress of his soul when he pleaded with us, and we did not listen. That is why this distress has come upon us."note
Y se dijeron el uno al otro: «Verdaderamente somos culpables respecto a nuestro hermano — pues vimos la angustia de su alma cuando nos suplicaba, y no escuchamos. Por eso ha venido sobre nosotros esta angustia».

22
וַיַּעַן רְאוּבֵן אֹתָם לֵאמֹר, הֲלוֹא אָמַרְתִּי אֲלֵיכֶם לֵאמֹר אַל-תֶּחֶטְאוּ בַיֶּלֶד--וְלֹא שְׁמַעְתֶּם; וְגַם-דָּמוֹ, הִנֵּה נִדְרָשׁ.
And Reuben answered them, saying, "Did I not say to you, 'Do not sin against the boy'? And you did not listen. And now — look — his blood is being required."note
Y Rubén les respondió, diciendo: «¿No les dije: "No pequen contra el muchacho"? Y no escucharon. Y ahora — miren — su sangre nos es demandada».
23
וְהֵם לֹא יָדְעוּ, כִּי שֹׁמֵעַ יוֹסֵף: כִּי הַמֵּלִיץ, בֵּינֹתָם.
And they did not know that Joseph was listening, for the interpreter was between them.note
Y ellos no sabían que José escuchaba, porque el intérprete estaba entre ellos.

And he turned away from them and wept; and he returned to them and spoke to them, and took Simeon from among them and bound him before their eyes.note
Y se apartó de ellos y lloró; y volvió a ellos y les habló, y tomó de entre ellos a Simeón y lo ató ante sus ojos.

25
וַיְצַו יוֹסֵף, וַיְמַלְאוּ אֶת-כְּלֵיהֶם בָּר, וּלְהָשִׁיב כַּסְפֵּיהֶם אִישׁ אֶל-שַׂקּוֹ, וְלָתֵת לָהֶם צֵדָה לַדָּרֶךְ; וַיַּעַשׂ לָהֶם, כֵּן.
And Joseph gave orders, and they filled their vessels with grain, and returned each man's silver to his sack, and gave them provisions for the road; and so it was done for them.note
Y José dio orden, y llenaron sus recipientes de grano, y devolvieron la plata de cada uno a su costal, y les dieron provisiones para el camino; y así se hizo con ellos.
26
וַיִּשְׂאוּ אֶת-שִׁבְרָם, עַל-חֲמֹרֵיהֶם; וַיֵּלְכוּ, מִשָּׁם.
And they loaded their grain on their donkeys and went from there.note
Y cargaron su grano sobre sus asnos y se fueron de allí.
27
וַיִּפְתַּח הָאֶחָד אֶת-שַׂקּוֹ, לָתֵת מִסְפּוֹא לַחֲמֹרוֹ--בַּמָּלוֹן; וַיַּרְא, אֶת-כַּסְפּוֹ, וְהִנֵּה-הוּא, בְּפִי אַמְתַּחְתּוֹ.
And one of them opened his sack to give fodder to his donkey at the lodging place, and he saw his silver — and look, it was in the mouth of his bag.note
Y uno de ellos abrió su costal para dar forraje a su asno en el mesón, y vio su plata — y mira, estaba en la boca de su saco.
28
וַיֹּאמֶר אֶל-אֶחָיו הוּשַׁב כַּסְפִּי, וְגַם הִנֵּה בְאַמְתַּחְתִּי; וַיֵּצֵא לִבָּם, וַיֶּחֶרְדוּ אִישׁ אֶל-אָחִיו לֵאמֹר, מַה-זֹּאת עָשָׂה אֱלֹהִים, לָנוּ.
And he said to his brothers, "My silver has been returned — and look, it is even in my bag!" And their heart went out of them, and they turned trembling each to his brother, saying, "What is this God has done to us?"note
Y dijo a sus hermanos: «¡Me han devuelto la plata — y mira, está en mi saco!» Y se les salió el corazón, y se volvieron temblando el uno al otro, diciendo: «¿Qué es esto que Dios nos ha hecho?»
29
וַיָּבֹאוּ אֶל-יַעֲקֹב אֲבִיהֶם, אַרְצָה כְּנָעַן; וַיַּגִּידוּ לוֹ, אֵת כָּל-הַקֹּרֹת אֹתָם לֵאמֹר.
And they came to Jacob their father in the land of Canaan and told him all that had befallen them, saying,note
Y vinieron a Jacob su padre, a la tierra de Canaán, y le contaron todo lo que les había sucedido, diciendo:
30
דִּבֶּר הָאִישׁ אֲדֹנֵי הָאָרֶץ, אִתָּנוּ--קָשׁוֹת; וַיִּתֵּן אֹתָנוּ, כִּמְרַגְּלִים אֶת-הָאָרֶץ.
"The man, the lord of the land, spoke harshly with us and treated us as spies of the land.note
«El hombre, señor de la tierra, nos habló con dureza y nos trató como a espías de la tierra.
31
וַנֹּאמֶר אֵלָיו, כֵּנִים אֲנָחְנוּ: לֹא הָיִינוּ, מְרַגְּלִים.
And we said to him, 'We are honest men; we have never been spies.note
Y le dijimos: "Somos hombres honrados; nunca hemos sido espías.
32
שְׁנֵים-עָשָׂר אֲנַחְנוּ אַחִים, בְּנֵי אָבִינוּ; הָאֶחָד אֵינֶנּוּ, וְהַקָּטֹן הַיּוֹם אֶת-אָבִינוּ בְּאֶרֶץ כְּנָעַן.
We are twelve brothers, sons of our father; the one is not, and the youngest is with our father today in the land of Canaan.'note
Somos doce hermanos, hijos de nuestro padre; el uno no está, y el menor está hoy con nuestro padre en la tierra de Canaán".
33
וַיֹּאמֶר אֵלֵינוּ, הָאִישׁ אֲדֹנֵי הָאָרֶץ, בְּזֹאת אֵדַע, כִּי כֵנִים אַתֶּם: אֲחִיכֶם הָאֶחָד הַנִּיחוּ אִתִּי, וְאֶת-רַעֲבוֹן בָּתֵּיכֶם קְחוּ וָלֵכוּ.
And the man, the lord of the land, said to us, 'By this I will know that you are honest men: leave one of your brothers with me, and take grain for the hunger of your households and go.note
Y el hombre, señor de la tierra, nos dijo: "En esto sabré que son hombres honrados: dejen conmigo a uno de sus hermanos, y tomen grano para el hambre de sus casas y váyanse.
34
וְהָבִיאוּ אֶת-אֲחִיכֶם הַקָּטֹן, אֵלַי, וְאֵדְעָה כִּי לֹא מְרַגְּלִים אַתֶּם, כִּי כֵנִים אַתֶּם; אֶת-אֲחִיכֶם אֶתֵּן לָכֶם, וְאֶת-הָאָרֶץ תִּסְחָרוּ.
And bring your youngest brother to me, and I will know that you are not spies but honest men. I will give you your brother, and you may trade in the land.'"note
Y tráiganme a su hermano menor, y sabré que no son espías sino hombres honrados. Les devolveré a su hermano, y podrán comerciar en la tierra"».
35

Translator's Notes

Verses 1–5 · "why do you keep looking at one another?"

A famine, and a family paralysed. Jacob's first line is impatience: lammah titra'u — literally "why are you looking at each other?" It is the sound of ten grown men standing in a yard avoiding a decision, and their father snapping at them to move. The KJV's "why do ye look one upon another?" keeps it exactly; the NIV softens to "why do you just keep looking at each other?" — the same picture. But there may be more in it. The one place these men are being told to go is Egypt, and the last thing that went to Egypt from this family was their brother. They know something their father does not, and the narrator has just put them in a room where nobody will meet anyone's eye.

"Go down." Redu — down to Egypt, the same verb that carried Joseph down (39:1) and that Judah used when he "went down from his brothers" (38:1). Egypt is always down in this book, geographically and otherwise.

Benjamin held back. "Joseph's brother" — the narrator's phrase, not Jacob's, and it tells you why the boy stays home: he is the other son of Rachel, and Jacob has already lost one on a journey. The reason given is ason, "harm, calamity" — a rare and heavy word that Jacob will use again at the chapter's end and once more in ch. 44. Note what it implies about the ten: their father does not trust them with this one. He does not say so. He just does not send him.

Verses 6–13 · the dream comes true, and a verb turns inside out

"And they bowed down to him, faces to the ground." Twenty-two years and five chapters after a seventeen-year-old told his brothers that their sheaves came around and bowed down to his sheaf — and they hated him for it and sold him to stop it — they do it. The narrator does not comment. He does not need to: he has just written the sentence the whole book has been aimed at, and put it in a single clause in the middle of a verse about grain administration. The dream did not come true despite the pit; it came true through it.

The chapter's masterstroke, in one word. Verse 7: Joseph saw them and recognized them — vayyakkirem — and made himself unrecognizable to them — vayyitnakker. Same root, nakar, twice in four words, in opposite directions: to know a face, and to make your own face unknown. English cannot do it in one word, and most versions do not try — the KJV reads "he knew them, but made himself strange unto them," which is actually the best of them and preserves the reversal; the NIV's "he pretended to be a stranger" reads smoothly and loses the pun entirely. This translation uses "recognized / made himself unrecognizable" to keep the seam visible. And note where this verb has been: Isaac failed to recognize the son in front of him; Jacob recognized a coat that was lying to him; Judah recognized his own seal and confessed. Now the fourth turn: a man recognizes his family perfectly and chooses to be unrecognized.

"And Joseph remembered the dreams." Vayyizkor — the book's rescue-verb, and here it is doing something more complicated. He remembers, and immediately accuses them of espionage. Readers have split on this for two thousand years: is this vengeance, a man with absolute power toying with the brothers who ruined him? Or is it the beginning of a deliberate test — a controlled reconstruction of the original crime (a favoured younger son, a chance to abandon him) to find out whether they are the same men? The text withholds a motive here and gives us one only in ch. 45. This translation lets the ambiguity stand, because it is genuinely there, and a reader who feels uneasy in this chapter is reading it correctly.

"The nakedness of the land." Ervah — the word for shameful exposure, used of a body. Applied to a country it means the undefended places, the weak points, and it is a plausible charge: Canaanites arriving in a group during a famine, at Egypt's vulnerable eastern approach, is exactly what a border official would look at twice. The accusation is not absurd. It is simply not true.

"And the one is not." Ve-ha-echad einennu. They tell the truth without telling it — three words, spoken to the man they are talking about, and it is the same phrase Reuben used at the empty pit, "the boy is not." They have been saying it for twenty-two years and it has never once been true.

Verses 14–20 · three days, and "I fear God"

Three days in custody. He puts them where he was — mishmar, the same word for the ward he was held in (40:3) — and for three days. Whatever else is happening, the man is giving his brothers a very short taste of a thing he had thirteen years of.

He changes the terms. His first demand (v. 16) was that nine stay bound and one go; on the third day he inverts it — one stays, nine go home with food. That is a softening, and it is the first evidence in the chapter that this is not simple cruelty: a man taking revenge does not reduce the sentence after sleeping on it.

"I fear God." Said to ten frightened foreigners by an Egyptian official with total power over them, and it is the most reassuring sentence available in the ancient world — it means there is a limit on what I will do to you. It is also, for the reader, the tell: this is the man who told Potiphar's wife he would not "sin against God," speaking now in an Egyptian accent through an interpreter, saying the one thing that would mean nothing to an Egyptian and everything to a son of Jacob — if only they could hear it.

Verses 21–24 · what they heard him do, and never told

The detail Genesis kept back for five chapters. "We saw the distress of his soul when he pleaded with us, and we did not listen." Go back to 37:24 and read it again: the brothers strip Joseph, throw him in the pit, and sit down to eat bread. The narrator never said Joseph begged. He never said a word about the boy in the hole at all. It is only now, twenty-two years later, in a foreign prison, that his brothers supply the sound-track — and the withholding was deliberate. Genesis let us watch the scene in silence so that we would hear the pleading for the first time exactly when they finally admit it. It is one of the great strokes of narrative in the Hebrew Bible, and it costs the reader something.

"We are guilty." Ashemim anachnu — the language of real culpability, not regret. And notice the theology in the second half: "that is why this distress has come upon us." They read their trouble as a moral ledger settling. They happen to be wrong about the mechanism — this is not heaven's justice, it is their brother running a test — but they are not wrong about the debt. The KJV's "We are verily guilty concerning our brother" is exactly right.

Reuben, being Reuben. The eldest's contribution is "I told you so" — accurate (he did try, 37:22) and useless, and delivered while his brother sits in a cell. He is the man who always says the right thing slightly too late to matter, and the narrative has been consistent about him since the pit.

"For the interpreter was between them." The single most quietly devastating line in the chapter. They speak freely in Hebrew in front of the Egyptian official because a melits stands between them — and he understands every syllable. Note also what this tells you about the disguise: Joseph has been speaking Egyptian this whole time, through a translator, to his own family. Then he turns away and weeps — the first of the times he will do this, each one closer to breaking — comes back, and binds Simeon in front of them. ⚠️ Why Simeon? The text does not say. He is the second-oldest, so taking him spares Reuben (who tried to save Joseph); he is also, with Levi, one of the two who massacred Shechem, so the reader has grounds to think of him as the family's dangerous man. Jewish tradition adds that Simeon was the one who threw Joseph into the pit. The text is silent; the silence is the kind this book keeps on purpose.

Verses 25–28 · the money in the sack

He will not take their money. Joseph fills the sacks, returns every man's silver, and adds provisions for the road. It is generosity delivered as a threat: ten men who have just been accused of espionage discover they are carrying, unexplained, the payment they thought they had made. Nobody in Egypt will believe they did not steal it. Whatever he intended, the effect is that they cannot come back and cannot stay away.

"Their heart went out of them." A physical idiom for panic — vayyetse libbam, the heart leaving the body. And their question is the chapter's second theological reading of events: "What is this God has done to us?" They are frightened by a kindness. Guilty men read gifts as traps, which is its own kind of sentence. And they are half right again: God is in this — just not in the way they think, and not without their brother's hand on the sack.

⚠️ A seam worth noting rather than smoothing: in verse 27 one brother finds his silver at the lodging place, but in verse 35 they all find it when they get home and are surprised again. Source critics read the two discoveries as two versions of the scene braided together; harmonizers note that only one sack was opened on the road (for fodder) and the rest were not unpacked until Canaan. Both readings work; the doubling is in the text and this translation leaves it there.

Verses 29–35 · the report, edited

What they leave out. Compare their account to their father with what actually happened. They tell him the man spoke harshly, called them spies, demanded Benjamin, and kept a brother — all true. They omit the three days in custody, they omit that Simeon was bound before their eyes, and they add a sweetener the man never said: "and you may trade in the land" (v. 34). They also do not mention the sentence they said to each other in that cell. This family manages its father with edited information, and has since a bloodied coat was carried home with the question "recognize, please."

Verses 36–38 · an old man counting his losses

"Joseph is not, and Simeon is not." Jacob uses the brothers' own word — einennu, the euphemism they have hidden behind for two decades (vv. 13, 32) — and applies it to two sons, one of whom is alive and governing Egypt and the other of whom is alive in Egyptian custody. Every man he names is fine. He is not wrong to grieve; he is simply, and completely, wrong.

"All of it has come upon me." Alay hayu khullanah — the KJV's famous "all these things are against me." Note the pronoun: me. There is real pain in the verse and there is also a man who has made a career of grief, and Genesis does not tidy either away. He is also, on the plain facts, accusing his sons of killing Joseph and Simeon — which for nine of them is nearly true, and they say nothing.

Reuben's offer, which is monstrous. "You may put my two sons to death if I do not bring him back." He is proposing that his father kill his own grandsons as security. It is meant as an extravagant pledge and it lands as evidence that Reuben still does not understand what a lost son costs — the same failure of imagination that made him suggest a pit instead of an escape. ⚠️ Contrast this, when you get there, with Judah in 43:9: "I will be surety for him; from my hand you shall require him." Judah offers himself. Jacob says yes to that one. It is the chapter's quietest measurement of how far Judah has come since he said "she is more righteous than I."

"Down to Sheol." The old man ends where he ended in 37:35 — she'olah, the same word, the same grey head, the same refusal of comfort, twenty-two years on. He has not moved. And the versions repeat their old inconsistency exactly: the KJV reads "bring down my gray hairs with sorrow to the grave" here, as it did there, while the ASV and NWT both read "Sheol." Keeping the Hebrew word lets the reader hear that this is not a fresh lament but the same sentence, unchanged, from the day the coat came back.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the words exact / distinct: "recognized… and made himself unrecognizable" for the nakar pun of v. 7, which the smooth versions dissolve; "the one is not" (einennu) left as the euphemism the brothers actually use, so it can land again in Jacob's mouth at v. 36; "Sheol" for the second time (v. 38), matching 37:35 where the KJV reads "grave" both times; "the nakedness of the land" for ervah; "we are guilty" for ashemim, not softened to regret; and "the interpreter was between them" left as flatly as the Hebrew says it.

Echoes paid. The sheaves that bowed — the dream of 37:7 fulfilled in a single clause, and fulfilled through the pit that was meant to stop it; nakar's fourth turn after Isaac, Jacob and Judah; zakhar — Joseph "remembered the dreams," the verb that has been failing and firing all through this story; einennu, Reuben's word at the empty pit; Jacob's grey head and Sheol, word for word, twenty-two years later; and the pleading in the pit that 37:24 deliberately did not report.

Echoes planted. Judah's surety — "I will be surety for him" (43:9) and "let your servant remain instead of the boy" (44:33) — the answer to Reuben's grotesque offer here, and the moment Joseph's test is finally passed; the silver in the sacks, which will be repeated on purpose with a divining cup (44:2); Benjamin, who must come; and Joseph's own verdict on all of it, still eight chapters off — "you meant evil against me, but God meant it for good" (50:20).

Next installment in this book: Genesis 43 — the grain runs out, and Jacob has to let Benjamin go. Judah puts himself up as surety in the words Reuben could not find; the brothers go down with double silver and a gift of balm and honey; and the man who accused them of spying seats them in his own house in birth order, stares at his mother's other son, and has to leave the room.
