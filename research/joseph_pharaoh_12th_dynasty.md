## The arithmetic (all from the site's already-adopted numbers)
- **Exodus = 1446 BC** (1 Kings 6:1 via Thiele — the date this translation follows, see the Exodus file).
- **+430 years in Egypt** (Exodus 12:40) → **Jacob's family arrives 1876 BC**.
- Joseph is **thirty** when he stands before Pharaoh (41:46) and **thirty-nine** when Jacob comes down
  (7 years plenty + 2 years famine, 45:6) → **Joseph elevated c. 1885 BC**, born c. 1915 BC.
- 1885 BC falls inside the **12th Dynasty (c. 1991–1802 BC)**, Middle Kingdom — in the reign of
  **Senusret II** (c. 1897–1878), with his career running on into **Senusret III** (c. 1878–1839).

## Why this matters: it rules the Hyksos out
The most popular popular-level theory puts Joseph under the **Hyksos** (c. 1650–1550 BC), the
Semitic-speaking rulers of Lower Egypt, reasoning that a foreign dynasty would more readily elevate a
Semite to vizier. It is an appealing argument **and it is incompatible with this site's chronology** —
roughly 235 years too late. The Hyksos view belongs to a *different* Exodus reckoning (generally the
late/Ramesses date). **Do not mix the two.** State the 12th-Dynasty placement, note the Hyksos theory
exists, and say plainly why it doesn't fit here.

Bonus: a strong centralized Middle Kingdom actually fits Genesis 41 *better* than a fragmented
Second Intermediate Period — the chapter describes a nationwide grain levy, provincial storage cities
and a vizier with authority over "all the land of Egypt," which presupposes a functioning central state.

## Corroborating evidence — GRADED (the site's honesty rule)
- **STRONG — Avaris / Tell el-Dab'a.** Manfred Bietak's excavations in the eastern Delta (the Goshen
  region) found a **Semitic/Asiatic settlement from the late 12th Dynasty**, with Levantine house forms
  and burial customs. Real archaeology, right region, right period. It does not name Joseph, and should
  never be presented as though it does — but Asiatics settling in the Delta at about this date is
  exactly the background Genesis 46-47 assumes.
- **MEDIUM — the Egyptian setting of ch. 41 itself.** Shaving before a royal audience, the signet ring,
  fine linen (*shesh*, an Egyptian loanword), the gold *shebyu*-collar of honour, the second chariot, a
  state grain administration, the Nile/`ye'or` and `achu` reed-grass loanwords, the *qadim*/khamsin east
  wind. None of this dates the chapter, but all of it is authentically Egyptian.
- **WEAK — the Bahr Yussef ("Joseph's Canal").** The channel feeding the Faiyum, whose hydraulic works
  Amenemhat III expanded. Frequently cited; the name is **medieval Arabic tradition, not an ancient
  inscription**. Use only with that caveat, or not at all.
- **SPECULATION — vizier identifications.** Attempts to equate Joseph with a specific named official
  (e.g. the vizier Ankhu, 13th Dynasty) are enthusiast reconstructions. Don't foreground.
- **NOT EVIDENCE — the Famine Stele.** The Sehel Island inscription telling of a seven-year famine under
  Djoser is **Ptolemaic**, ~2,000 years after the reign it describes. It shows the seven-year famine was
  an established Egyptian *tradition*; it is not independent confirmation. (Already flagged this way in
  the gen41 note — keep it that way.)

## How to deploy across the remaining Joseph chapters
- **Gen 42-45**: nothing chronological needed; the Egyptian court detail (interpreters, divining cup,
  Egyptians not eating with Hebrews 43:32) is setting, not dating.
- **Gen 46-47**: Goshen / the eastern Delta — the Avaris material is the relevant background. Also the
  one-fifth policy's endgame (47:13-26), which the gen41 note already flags as double-edged.
- **Gen 50 → Exodus 1**: the hinge. Joseph dies c. 1805 BC on these numbers; the "new king who did not
  know Joseph" is **Thutmose III**, centuries later — which is precisely why he doesn't. Keep the two
  research files consistent at that seam.
