Primary video (attached to the `exodus-pharaoh` encyclopedia entry):
"Tomb of the Exodus Pharaoh: What Was Found & Why You Don't Know About It!" — https://youtu.be/mJP4pVjnWpk

## The early date: 1446 BC
- **1 Kings 6:1** — Solomon began the temple "in the 480th year after the Israelites came out of Egypt,"
  in his 4th regnal year. Thiele's chronology puts Solomon's 4th year at **966 BC** → Exodus = **spring
  1446 BC**.
- **Judges 11:26** — Jephthah (~1100 BC) says Israel had held the land "300 years," corroborating an
  earlier (not 13th-c.) conquest.
- The **late date** (~1250 BC, Ramesses II) is the mainstream academic view, resting mainly on the
  store-city name "Raamses" (Ex 1:11). Kramer/ABR reject it (see the Raamses point below).

## The two pharaohs (Kramer's clean thesis)
- **Pharaoh of the OPPRESSION: Thutmose III** (reigned ~1479–1425 BC) — Egypt's greatest warrior-
  builder, "the Napoleon of Egypt," ~17 campaigns into Canaan; his empire-building drove the demand for
  the store-cities Israel built. He is the "new king who did not know Joseph" (Ex 1:8) and the pharaoh
  Moses fled from (Ex 2:15); "the king of Egypt died" (Ex 2:23) = his death ~1425.
- **Pharaoh of the EXODUS: Amenhotep II** (reigned ~1425–1400 BC) — the Exodus falls early in his reign
  (~1446, i.e. shortly after his accession on the high chronology). Evidence Kramer cites:
  - His aggressive Canaan campaigns **abruptly cease after ~1446** — consistent with losing his army.
  - His late-reign texts show **unusual hostility to "Asiatics"/Semites** and warnings about magicians.
  - **He did NOT drown.** A common misreading: Scripture says pharaoh's *army/chariots* drowned (Ex 14:28;
    15:4; Ps 136:15), never that pharaoh himself did. So Amenhotep II reigned on and was buried in
    **tomb KV35** in the Valley of the Kings — his mummy is known. That is the "tomb of the Exodus
    pharaoh you can actually visit" — the video's hook.

## The firstborn & the Dream Stele (the 10th plague evidence)
- The **Dream Stele of Thutmose IV** (Amenhotep II's son and successor), set between the paws of the
  Great Sphinx, records that the sun-god promised him the throne if he cleared the sand from the Sphinx.
- Read as royal propaganda covering the fact that **Thutmose IV was NOT the crown prince / firstborn** —
  the expected heir (the firstborn) had died. This matches **Exodus 12:29** (the firstborn of Pharaoh
  struck at Passover). A pharaoh needing a divine dream to legitimize his accession is the tell.

## The "Raamses" objection (Ex 1:11) — the early-date answer
- Ex 1:11 says Israel built the store-cities **Pithom and Raamses** — the mainstay of the LATE date
  (Pi-Ramesses, built by Ramesses II, 13th c.).
- Early-date answer: it's an **editorial modernization of the place-name** — the later name for a region
  that already existed. Direct biblical precedent: **"Dan"** in Genesis 14:14 (the city wasn't named Dan
  until Judges 18) and **"Ur of the Chaldeans"** (Chaldeans arrived centuries after Abraham). The site
  already flags exactly these anachronisms (see the gen14/gen11 notes). So "Raamses" is the reader's name
  for the place, not proof of the composition/event date.
- ⚠ This is the honest seam: present it as the early-date's answer, not a slam-dunk; the late-date remains
  the academic majority. The site's neutrality rule = give both with pedigrees, but Michael wants Kramer's
  foregrounded.

## Corroborating the early date (for later chapters / the conquest)
- **Merneptah Stele** (~1209 BC): "Israel is laid waste" — Israel already a *people in Canaan* by then,
  which fits an earlier exodus + conquest, not a 1250 exodus.
- **Amarna Letters** (~1350 BC): Canaanite kings begging Egypt for help against the **Habiru/Apiru**
  overrunning the hill country — read by early-date proponents as the Israelite settlement in progress.
- **Jericho**: Garstang's excavation dated a destruction ~1400 BC (early-date friendly); Kenyon redated
  it earlier/late — disputed. Bryant Wood (ABR) re-argues the ~1400 destruction (pottery). Use for Joshua.

## How to deploy across Exodus chapters
- **Ex 1** (this chapter): the oppression under Thutmose III; the store-cities; "Raamses" flagged as the
  editorial-update anachronism (same footing as Dan/Ur). Encyclopedia entry `exodus-pharaoh` leads with
  Thutmose III (oppression) / Amenhotep II (Exodus) + the video.
- **Ex 2–4**: Moses flees Thutmose III (2:15); "the king died" (2:23) = Thutmose III's death; Moses
  returns to **Amenhotep II**.
- **Ex 5–11 (plagues)**: frame the plagues as targeting specific Egyptian gods (Hapi/Nile→blood,
  Heqet/frog, Ra/darkness, etc.); the 10th plague ↔ the Dream Stele firstborn argument.
- **Ex 12 (Passover / firstborn)**: cite the Dream Stele (Thutmose IV not the heir).
- **Ex 14–15 (Red Sea / Yam Suph)**: pharaoh's ARMY drowned, not pharaoh — hence Amenhotep II's KV35
  tomb; his campaigns cease after 1446.
- Keep the 1446 date + Thutmose III/Amenhotep II pairing consistent throughout; always note the late-date
  (Ramesses II) exists but is not the view this translation foregrounds.

## The SON in the tomb — Webensenu (added 2026-07-21, Michael's ask)
The firstborn argument has a second leg besides the Dream Stele, and it is the stronger visual one —
it is the substance of the video already attached to the `exodus-pharaoh` entry
("Tomb of the Exodus Pharaoh", https://youtu.be/mJP4pVjnWpk — oEmbed 200, embeddable).
- Buried in **KV35 with Amenhotep II** is a son, **WEBENSENU**, who **predeceased his father** — that
  much is certain, because he is buried in his father's tomb.
- Attested by **canopic jars inscribed with his name** (holding his organs) and a **funerary statuette**;
  a **boy's mummy** from the tomb is generally identified as his, head shaved except for the **sidelock**
  that marked a royal child.
- Kramer's line: "We know for certain that Webensenu died before his father because he's buried in his
  tomb" — set against **Exodus 12:29**, the firstborn of Pharaoh "who sits on his throne."
- ⚠ **HONEST LIMITS — keep these attached whenever this is cited.** It does NOT establish that Webensenu
  was the crown prince/firstborn, nor how he died; Egyptology reads him as a prince who died young.
  Kramer himself: "I don't want to make too much of this… but I also don't want to make too little of it
  either." **Suggestive, not probative** — file it exactly that way (the site's neutrality rule).
- A further claim circulates that Amenhotep II's mummy shows unusual skin nodules read as the plague of
  boils. **Not used on the site** — I could not verify how Egyptologists read those lesions, and it is a
  weaker claim than the Webensenu one. Don't cite it without sourcing it properly.
