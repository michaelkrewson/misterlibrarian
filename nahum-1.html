A wrath-hymn (an alphabetic acrostic that breaks off halfway) announces the fall of Nineveh — the God who is 'slow to anger and great in power' will make a full end of Assyria, break its yoke off Judah, and dig the king's grave.
c. 663–612 BC, late in the window — Nahum names the fall of Thebes (663) as past (3:8) and foresees the fall of Nineveh, which came in 612 when the Babylonians and Medes destroyed it. He is a century and a half after Jonah preached to the same city; between them Assyria had crushed the northern kingdom (722) and besieged Jerusalem (701), and by Nahum's day its collapse was in the air. The book is comfort for a Judah still under the Assyrian yoke.

Translator’s Notes

Verse 1 · the sequel to Jonah — the same city, the opposite verdict

⚠ Nahum is the dark twin of Jonah, and the two books are meant to be read together. They are the only books in the Bible aimed entirely at Nineveh, and they say opposite things. In Jonah (about 760 BC) the great city hears one sentence of warning and repents, and God spares it — to the prophet’s fury. In Nahum, a century and a half later, the same city has gone back to being the most efficient killing-machine the ancient world had yet produced, and God announces its end. The mercy of Jonah and the judgment of Nahum are the two halves of one truth: the God who relents is also the God who does not relent forever.

⚠ And the prophet’s name is the joke — or the point. Nachum means “comfort, consolation” (from nacham, the same root behind Noah and Nehemiah). A book that is three chapters of unrelenting doom on an empire is titled Comfort — because Nineveh’s fall IS the comfort, the good news, for every small nation it had crushed. Nahum is consolation written from the underside of an empire.

The heading stacks three technical words. A massa is a “burden” — a heavy oracle of doom, the word carrying both “a load” and “a lifting up of the voice.” KJV, ASV and DRB all keep “the burden of Nineveh.” Then, unusually, Nahum calls his work a “book” (sefer) — the only prophet to label his own vision a written book from the outset, which some read as a sign it was composed for reading rather than street-preaching. And he is “the Elkoshite,” from a town called Elkosh whose location no one knows — guesses run from Galilee (a tradition since Jerome; “Capernaum” even means “village of Nahum”) to a village near Mosul, next door to Nineveh’s own ruins. DRB spells it “the Elcesite.”

⚠ The date is fixed by two cities Nahum names. He speaks of the fall of No-Amon — Thebes — as a past event (3:8): Assyria sacked Thebes in 663 BC. And the whole book anticipates the fall of Nineveh, which the Babylonians and Medes actually destroyed in 612 BC. So Nahum wrote somewhere in that half-century window, most likely near its end, when Assyria’s collapse was already in the air.

Verses 2–8 · a wrath-hymn built as a half-finished alphabet, and the one verse of mercy inside it

⚠ The book opens not with Nineveh but with a HYMN — and the hymn is built as an alphabetic acrostic that stops halfway. Verses 2–8 run down the Hebrew alphabet, each new line beginning with the next letter — aleph, bet, gimel … — but the pattern is loose, and it breaks off around the letter kaph, roughly the middle of the alphabet, and never finishes. Scholars have argued over this for a century: is the acrostic DAMAGED (a copyist scrambled it), or is it DELIBERATELY half-built — an alphabet of judgment left unfinished, the way the world it describes is about to be cut short? The library records the debate and keeps the Masoretic text as it stands; the effect, either way, is of a great ordered thing coming apart.

⚠ And the hymn’s content is the famous formula — but tilted toward JUSTICE. Every Israelite knew Exodus 34:6–7, God’s self-description to Moses: “Jehovah, Jehovah, a God compassionate and gracious, slow to anger… forgiving iniquity — yet by no means clearing the guilty.” Jonah had quoted the mercy half of it as a complaint (Jonah 4:2). Nahum quotes the SAME formula and leans on the other half: “slow to anger and great in power, and he will by no means leave the guilty unpunished” (v3). The patience is real — Nineveh had a hundred and fifty extra years — but patience is not amnesia. ASV “slow to anger, and great in power, and will by no means clear the guilty”; NWT “slow to anger and great in power, and Jehovah will by no means leave a person unpunished.”

Then a THEOPHANY out of the same storm-tradition as Micah 1: Jehovah comes in the whirlwind, the clouds are the dust of his feet; he rebukes the sea and it dries (the Red Sea and the creation-victory over chaos, in one image); the great green places — Bashan’s pastures, Carmel’s headland, Lebanon’s cedars — wither; the mountains quake and the hills melt. It is the standard vocabulary of God arriving in power (Judges 5, Psalm 18, Habakkuk 3), turned here against a specific empire.

⚠ And in the exact middle of the wrath sits verse 7, the hinge of the whole book. “Jehovah is good, a stronghold in the day of distress, and he knows those who take refuge in him.” The same God whose fury melts mountains is a ma’oz, a fortress, for the people being crushed — and “he KNOWS” them, in the intimate sense. The book is not sadism about Nineveh; it is protection FOR its victims, and this single verse is the reason the doom counts as “comfort.” ASV keeps it tight to the Hebrew — “Jehovah is good, a stronghold in the day of trouble; and he knoweth them that take refuge in him”; NWT “Jehovah is good, a stronghold in the day of distress. He is aware of those seeking refuge in him”; DRB softens the fortress to “the Lord is good and giveth strength in the day of trouble.” Verse 8 then names the manner of the fall: an “overflowing flood” — and the ancient accounts of Nineveh’s destruction do say a river-flood breached its walls, letting the attackers in.

Verses 9–11 · the folly of plotting against God, and a “counselor of worthlessness”

The address turns to Nineveh directly. “What are you plotting against Jehovah? He is making a full end” — kalah, a complete finish, the word repeated from verse 8. “Distress will not rise up a second time”: the blow will be final, no rematch. Verse 10’s Hebrew is thorny (three images crammed together — tangled thorns, drunken stupor, dry stubble burned up), and every version wrestles with it; the sense is that Nineveh, however tangled and sodden its defenses, will burn like dead straw.

⚠ Beliyaal (v11) is a word with a long afterlife. “From you came out one who plots evil against Jehovah, a counselor of beliyaal” — beliyaal means “worthlessness, good-for-nothingness” (possibly “without-profit,” beli-ya’al). The “one who came out” is the Assyrian king — most likely Sennacherib, who had besieged Jerusalem and mocked its God. But the word itself hardened over the centuries into a proper NAME: by the New Testament, “Belial” is a title for Satan — “what agreement has Christ with Belial?” (2 Corinthians 6:15). DRB, reading it as the name already, has “Belial shall no more pass through thee” two verses on; we keep the earlier sense, “a counselor of worthlessness,” and flag the name it became.

Verses 12–14 · the yoke broken — and where the “good news” verse actually is

⚠ Now the book does the thing its title promised: it turns to Judah and speaks COMFORT. “Thus says Jehovah: though they are at full strength and so many, even so they will be sheared off and pass away. Though I have afflicted you, I will not afflict you again” (v12). The message flickers between two addressees — the Assyrian oppressor (masculine) and daughter Judah (feminine) — and to Judah it is pure relief: “now I will break his YOKE from off you, and snap your bonds apart” (v13). Judah had been an Assyrian vassal since Hezekiah, paying crushing tribute; the empire’s fall is the lifting of a literal yoke.

Verse 14 pronounces the sentence on the king himself: “no more of your name will be sown” — no descendants, the dynasty ended — “I will cut off idol and image from the house of your gods; I will make your grave, for you are worthless” (qalota, “you are made light / of no weight,” the opposite of the “glory” an Assyrian king claimed). The gods of Assyria — Ashur, Ishtar, Nabu — will be dug out of their own temples. ASV “I will make thy grave; for thou art vile”; NWT “I will make a burial place for you, because you are of no account” — both catching the play on qalota, “you have made yourself LIGHT,” against the “heavy” glory a king craved. When Nineveh fell in 612, it was never rebuilt; within two centuries Greek soldiers marched past its mound without knowing a city had been there.

⚠ And here a reader should notice a seam: the famous “GOOD NEWS” verse is NOT in this chapter. “Behold, on the mountains the feet of him who brings good news, who publishes peace!” — the verse most readers know as Nahum 1:15 — is Hebrew 2:1, the first verse of the next chapter, which English Bibles pull back into chapter 1. This site follows the Masoretic numbering, so it opens chapter 2 here. ⚠ When it comes, it is worth knowing it has a twin: it is almost word for word Isaiah 52:7, “how beautiful on the mountains are the feet of the herald of good news” — and the herald’s word, mevasser, “one who brings good news,” is the Hebrew behind the Greek that becomes euangelion, “gospel.” Paul quotes the line at Romans 10:15. In Nahum the good news is that the destroyer is destroyed; in Isaiah and Paul it opens outward into the good news itself.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the exact / distinct words: “the burden” for massa (v1), the heavy doom-oracle; “slow to anger and great in power, and he will by no means leave the guilty unpunished” (v3), keeping BOTH halves of the Exodus 34 formula that Jonah had quoted only halfway; “a stronghold in the day of distress” for ma’oz (v7), the one mercy at the centre of the wrath; “a full end” for kalah (vv8–9), the final finish, repeated; “a counselor of worthlessness” for beliyaal (v11), flagged as the word that became the name “Belial”; and “you are worthless” for qalota (v14), the un-glorying of a king who lived for glory.

Echoes paid. Jonah’s Nineveh, spared in one book and doomed in this one — the two halves of the God who relents and the God who does not relent forever. The Exodus 34 formula, quoted for its MERCY by Jonah (4:2) and for its JUSTICE by Nahum (1:3). The storm-theophany of Micah 1 — mountains quaking, hills melting — turned here on Assyria. And the beliyaal that runs from “worthlessness” here to the demonic name in the New Testament.

Echoes planted. The fall of Nineveh, which the next two chapters describe blow by blow — the chariots raging in the streets, the lions’ den emptied, “woe to the city of blood.” Thebes / No-Amon (3:8), the great Egyptian city whose own fall Nahum holds up as Nineveh’s mirror. “The feet of him who brings good news” (Hebrew 2:1), twin of Isaiah 52:7 and the seed of the word gospel, quoted by Paul in Romans 10:15. And “comfort,” the meaning of Nahum’s own name — the whole book a consolation for the conquered, written in the shape of a conqueror’s obituary.
