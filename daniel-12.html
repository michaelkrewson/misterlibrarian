Translator's Notes — verse by verse

Each note explains this translation's choice and compares the seven versions on the shelf. Quotations from copyrighted versions (NIV, TLB, NWT) are kept to short phrases for comparison; KJV, Geneva, Douay-Rheims and ASV are public domain.

Verse 1 · מִיכָאֵל הַשַּׂר הַגָּדוֹל Mikha'el hassar haggadol

Michael stands up. The verb is amad — the word chapter 11 marched on, king after king "arising" and "standing" — handed at last to someone worth it: Michael, "the great prince who stands over the sons of your people" — the guardian-advocate introduced back in the vision's opening frame (10:13, 21) as "one of the chief princes," fighting the unseen "princes" of Persia and Greece. His name is itself a rhetorical question — mi-kha-El, "Who is like God?" — an angel named after the answer no one. The "time of distress such as has never been" (KJV "a time of trouble, such as never was"; NIV "a time of distress") is the phrase Jesus reaches for on the Mount of Olives (Matthew 24:21) — one breath after quoting this book's "abomination that desolates."

The escape-verb, paid. "Your people shall escape" — yimmalet, the very verb of 11:41, where Edom, Moab, and Ammon slipped the last king's hand. The old neighbors escaped by geography; here the measure is different — "everyone found written in the book." A ledger with names in it runs through scripture: Moses offers to be blotted out of "your book" (Exodus 32:32), Malachi's "book of remembrance," and Revelation's book of life. Deliverance, this verse says, is by enrollment, not by borders.

Verse 2 · אַדְמַת-עָפָר admat-afar

The Hebrew Bible's clearest resurrection sentence. Hints came earlier — but this verse is the first to say it flat out, both directions: sleepers waking, "these to everlasting life, and these to reproaches, to everlasting abhorrence" (the Hebrew really does point twice — elleh… ve'elleh, "these… and these"; KJV "some… and some"). Where they sleep is chosen with a jeweler's care: admat-afar, "the ground of dust" — adamah and afar, the two words of Eden's making and Eden's sentence ("dust from the ground," Genesis 2:7; "dust you are, and to dust you shall return," 3:19). The oldest sentence in the book is answered with an alarm clock.

Two phrases with long futures. "Everlasting life" — chayyei olam — occurs exactly once in the entire Hebrew Bible: here. John's Gospel will make the Greek of it (zōē aiōnios) its heartbeat, and John 5:28-29 is very nearly this verse translated — "those in the tombs… shall come out, to a resurrection of life… of judgment." At the other pole, dir'on, "abhorrence," appears only twice in scripture: here, and in Isaiah 66:24's closing image of corpses "whose worm shall not die" — the picture Jesus borrows for Gehenna. (NWT 1984 renders both poles with its signature clock: "indefinitely lasting life… indefinitely lasting abhorrence.") And the verse says "many… shall wake," not "all" — readers ancient and modern have argued whether that is selection or simply Hebrew's way of saying "the multitudes"; the honest report is that the text says many, and its notes should too.

Verses 3–4 · כְּזֹהַר הָרָקִיעַ ke-zohar haraqia

The teachers' reward. "The wise" — the maskilim of 11:33-35, who gave understanding to many and fell by sword and flame — "shall shine like the shining of the vault": the cognate kept (yazhiru ke-zohar), and raqia rendered as this project has rendered it since day two of creation — the hammered vault of Genesis 1:6 (KJV "the brightness of the firmament"). The martyrs' brightness is measured against the sky God beat out over Eden; "those who bring the many to righteousness, like the stars" — the very currency of the promise to Abram, out under the night sky of Genesis 15:5. Eight centuries later, the masterwork of Jewish mysticism took its title from this verse's word: the Zohar, "Radiance." And quietly, a thread: many wake (v2), the many made righteous (v3), many rove (v4), many refined (v10) — rabbim is this chapter's drumbeat.

Seal the book — and the famous half-verse. "Shut up the words and seal the book" — setom… chatom, a rhyme in the Hebrew — is apocalyptic notary practice: a witnessed document closed until its date (and the exact order Revelation will countermand for its own scroll — "do not seal… for the time is near," Revelation 1:3 announcing the unsealing, 22:10 giving the order). Then the half-verse every generation reads its own age into: "many shall rove about, and knowledge shall multiply" (KJV "many shall run to and fro, and knowledge shall be increased"; Douay, from the Vulgate, goes its own way — "Many shall pass over, and knowledge shall be manifold"). Moderns have heard jets and the internet in it. The roving-verb (shut) elsewhere describes searching — eyes ranging the earth, seekers roving for the word of God and not finding it (Amos 8:12, its mirror image) — so the likelier sense is readers roving through the sealed book itself, understanding multiplying as the end approaches (v10 agrees: "the wise shall understand"). Both readings are on the table; the sentence has earned its fame either way.

Verses 5–7 · לְמוֹעֵד מוֹעֲדִים וָחֵצִי le-mo'ed mo'adim va-chetsi

A courtroom on a riverbank. Two more figures appear, "one here on the bank of the river, and one here on the bank of the river" — the sentence repeated whole, as the scroll repeats it: a witness stationed on each bank, the Torah's arithmetic for establishing a matter. The river-word is a small treasure: ye'or — Egypt's own word for the Nile, the river of Pharaoh's dreams — borrowed here, uniquely, for the great river of this vision (10:4 names it: the Tigris). "And he said" — the Hebrew never says who (KJV supplies "And one said"): one of the two witnesses, presumably, asking the question the whole book has been holding — "How long?"

The oath with both hands. The Bible's oath posture is one raised hand — Abram: "I have raised my hand to Jehovah" (Genesis 14:22). The man in linen above the waters raises "his right hand and his left hand to the heavens" — the oath doubled, sworn "by the One who lives forever," to answer a question about time by the only party outside it. The answer: "an appointed time, appointed times, and a half" — mo'ed, the word of Genesis 1:14, where the lights were hung "for appointed times"; time itself is measured out in the calendar God installed on day four. (KJV "a time, times, and an half"; NWT, for once beside this translation, "an appointed time, appointed times and a half.") The shape is 1 + 2 + ½: three and a half — the broken seven, persecution's signature number, the same span the book's Aramaic half calls "a time, times and half a time" (7:25) and Revelation will count out as 1,260 days and 42 months. And the hinge: "when the shattering of the hand of the holy people is finished" — hand meaning power (KJV "to scatter the power") — "all these things shall be finished": the finishing-verb doubled, kalah answering kalah. Rock bottom, the oath swears, is on the schedule — and so is the end of it.

Verses 8–10 · וְלֹא אָבִין velo avin

The canon's most comforting admission. "I heard, but I did not understand." The seer himself — shown the entire scroll, angel at his side — doesn't get it, says so, asks a follow-up ("what shall come after these things?" — acharit, the after-part, the outcome), and is not rebuked. He is dismissed with his question unanswered: "Go, Daniel — for the words are shut up and sealed" (v4's rhyming pair, paid). Every honest reader of this book has been in good company since its first reader.

The furnace, finished. "Many shall be purified, made white, refined" — the same three-verb metallurgy the martyrs' falling was said to work in 11:35, now promised its completion. Meanwhile "the wicked shall act wickedly" — the cognate kept (hirshi'u resha'im) — "and none of the wicked shall understand": in this book understanding is a moral capacity, not an intellectual one; the sealed words stay sealed from a certain kind of reader. "But the wise shall understand" — the maskilim's final appearance, holding the book's last classroom.

Verses 11–13 · אַשְׁרֵי הַמְחַכֶּה ashrei hamechakkeh

1,290 and 1,335 — the numbers nobody has decoded. From the halted offering and the abomination that desolates (11:31's date: 15 Kislev, 167 BC), 1,290 days; then a beatitude on reaching 1,335 — 45 days more. The arithmetic teases: three and a half years of 360-day months is 1,260; 1,290 adds a month; 1,335 another 45 — and no event we can name lands on either (the temple's rededication — Hanukkah, 25 Kislev 164 — comes at about three years, short of both). Some readers hear successive recalculations as a first figure passed — the book's own honesty about waiting; traditional readers assign the numbers to the end of days; this page reports that after twenty-two centuries the count is still open. The text's practical counsel is the beatitude itself: "Happy the one who waits" — ashrei, the word the book of Psalms opens with (KJV "Blessed is he that waiteth").

A pension and a resurrection. The book's last sentence is addressed to one tired old man: "And you — go on to the end. And you shall rest" — nuach, the verb hiding inside Noah's name — "and you shall stand up to your lot at the end of the days" (Geneva "stand up in thy lot"; Douay "stand in thy lot unto the end of the days"). Goral is the allotted portion — the pebble-drawn share of land with your name on it — and amad sounds one last time: Michael stands (v1), the sleepers of verse 2 wake, and Daniel, dead now these many centuries, is promised he will stand in his allotment — the resurrection of verse 2 made personal, to a named man. The final word of the book is "the days" spelled the Aramaic way (ha-yamin) — a book that switched languages midstream, signing off with an accent. After it the text carries the closing mark Mechon-Mamre's edition prints at the end of a book: {ש} — the same sign that stands at the end of Genesis 50.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation sides with the literal shelf: "everlasting life" with the whole shelf (the phrase is exact, and unique in the Hebrew Bible); "shut up the words and seal the book" with the KJV family's doubled verbs; "Happy" for ashrei, with the Psalms tradition.

Where it goes its own way: "the ground of dust" for admat-afar (KJV "the dust of the earth") — keeping both Eden words audible; "shine like the shining of the vault" — the cognate kept, and raqia rendered as Genesis 1 rendered it (KJV "the brightness of the firmament"); "rove about" for the searching-verb (KJV "run to and fro"); "an appointed time, appointed times, and a half" — mo'ed kept as Genesis 1:14's word; and "escape" for yimmalet (KJV "delivered"), to keep 11:41's echo audible.

Echoes paid and planted. Paid: the escape-verb (11:41 → 12:1); the furnace triple (11:35 → 12:10); the maskilim's reward (11:33 → 12:3); the vault of day two (Genesis 1:6); the appointed times of day four (1:14); the dust of Eden (2:7, 3:19) given back its sleepers; Abram's stars (15:5) and Abram's raised hand (14:22), doubled by the man in linen; and the sealed book answered across the canon by Revelation's unsealed one (Revelation 1:3). Planted: Michael's war in heaven (Revelation 12:7, when this site gets there); the abomination-phrase still waiting on the Mount of Olives (Matthew 24:15); and "everlasting life," waiting for John 3:16.
