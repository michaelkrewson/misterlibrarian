Translator’s Notes

Verses 1–11 · “Tomorrow”

⚠ A versification note first. This chapter follows the Hebrew Masoretic numbering, in which the frog announcement stood at the end of chapter 7 (7:26-29). So Hebrew Exodus 8:1-28 corresponds to English 8:5-32 — the chapter opens where most English Bibles have 8:5. Nothing is missing; the offset simply continues from the previous chapter. KJV and virtually every English Bible number these verses 8:5-32.

The frogs come up — and the magicians bring up more. For the third time Egypt’s experts can duplicate the plague, which is precisely the problem: their art can add frogs, it cannot remove one. The professionals keep proving they can make things worse.

«Have the honor over me» (v5). A genuinely odd idiom (hitpa’er alai) — Moses hands Pharaoh the advantage, letting him name the hour of his own relief. KJV “Glory over me”; the ESV “Be pleased to command me.”

⚠ And Pharaoh answers: “TOMORROW.” Offered the end of the plague, he chooses one more night with the frogs — one of the strangest replies in the Bible. Two readings, both old: that he hoped the frogs would pass on their own by morning (so the timing would prove nothing), or simply that he could not bring himself to beg for now. Moses’ answer names the point of letting him choose: an hour fixed in advance is what proves “there is none like Jehovah our God.” The relief, when it comes, arrives as rot — frogs piled “in heaps upon heaps, and the land stank.”

⚠ Verse 11 is the whole cycle in miniature: “when Pharaoh saw that there was RELIEF, he made his heart HEAVY.” Relief produces hardening, not gratitude — the mercy that should soften him is the very thing he uses to stiffen. Note that here the verb is kaved, “made heavy,” and it is Pharaoh’s own act; four verses later it is chazaq, “strong.” Exodus keeps both hands on this — the man hardens himself, and God hardens him — and never resolves it.

Verses 12–15 · the finger of God

The third plague comes with no warning and no negotiation — no morning appointment at the river, no demand, just the dust of the earth turned to gnats. The word kinnim is uncertain: gnats, mosquitoes, or lice. KJV “lice”; the ESV and NIV “gnats.”

⚠ And the magicians COULD NOT. This is the turning point of the whole contest. They matched the staff, the blood, and the frogs; the dust defeats them. And it is they who say what it means, not Moses: “This is the FINGER OF GOD” (etzba Elohim). Egypt’s own professionals concede what the king refuses to — and Pharaoh, told plainly by his own experts, still will not listen. From here the sorcerers drop out of the story except to be struck by the boils they cannot stand up under (9:11).

The phrase travels. The tablets of the covenant will be written with “the finger of God” (31:18), and Jesus will say he casts out demons “by the finger of God” (Luke 11:20) — the same idiom for God acting directly, with no instrument and no effort.

Verses 16–28 · a division between my people and your people

The fourth plague introduces the DISTINCTION. Until now the blows fell on everyone in Egypt, Israel included. Now Jehovah “sets apart” the land of Goshen where his people live, and the sparing itself becomes the sign: “so that you may know that I am Jehovah IN THE MIDST OF THE LAND” (v18). The point is no longer only that Jehovah is powerful, but that he is present and discriminating — and the frontier runs between his people and Pharaoh’s.

The plague itself is unnamed. Arov means simply “swarm” — the Hebrew never says of what. KJV supplies “swarms of flies”; the Greek Old Testament read the dog-fly; some Jewish tradition took it as mixed wild animals. This translation keeps the bare “swarms” and lets the ambiguity stand.

⚠ And here the NEGOTIATIONS begin — a pattern worth watching for the rest of the cycle, because Pharaoh never simply refuses again; he bargains, and every offer keeps a hostage. First offer: “sacrifice — within the land” (worship, but do not leave). Moses refuses on grounds that are practical and religious at once: what Israel sacrifices is “an abomination to the Egyptians” — Egypt held certain animals sacred, and slaughtering them in public view would end in a stoning. (The same word marks Egyptian revulsion at Hebrew tables and shepherds, Genesis 43:32; 46:34.) Second offer: the wilderness is conceded, but with a leash — “only you shall not go very far.” Later he will offer the men without the children, and the people without the flocks. Each round gives ground and keeps a pledge.

Moses names the pattern before it repeats: “let Pharaoh not deal DECEITFULLY again.” And the chapter closes exactly as the last one did — the swarms removed, not one left, and a heart made heavy “this time also.”

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the exact / distinct words: “swarms” for arov, left unspecified as the Hebrew leaves it (no “flies” supplied); “gnats” for the uncertain kinnim, with the alternatives noted; “the finger of God” kept literal; “made his heart heavy” (kaved) held distinct from “his heart was strong” (chazaq), the two verbs most versions flatten into one; “abomination” for to’evah, the same word as Egypt’s revulsion in Genesis; and the Masoretic verse numbering (= English 8:5-32).

Echoes paid. The frogs announced at the end of the previous chapter; the magicians who could match the staff, the blood, and the frogs, and who fail at the dust; to’evah, the Egyptian revulsion that kept Joseph’s brothers at a separate table and their shepherding at arm’s length (Genesis 43:32; 46:34); and Goshen, the land Joseph secured for his family (Genesis 45:10), now marked out for protection.

Echoes planted. The distinction between Israel and Egypt, which will hold through the cattle, the hail, the darkness, and finally the blood on the doorposts; the bargaining ladder — in the land, not far, the men only, without the flocks — each offer keeping a hostage; the magicians, finished as rivals and soon unable even to stand (9:11); and “the finger of God,” which will write the covenant on stone (31:18).
