Exodus 4 — The Mister Translation

1
וַיַּעַן מֹשֶׁה, וַיֹּאמֶר, וְהֵן לֹא-יַאֲמִינוּ לִי, וְלֹא יִשְׁמְעוּ בְּקֹלִי: כִּי יֹאמְרוּ, לֹא-נִרְאָה אֵלֶיךָ יְהוָה.
And Moses answered and said, "But look, they will not believe me or listen to my voice, for they will say, 'Jehovah did not appear to you.'"note
Y Moisés respondió y dijo: «Pero mira, no me creerán ni escucharán mi voz, porque dirán: "No se te ha aparecido Jehová".»
2
וַיֹּאמֶר אֵלָיו יְהוָה, מזה מַה-זֶּה בְיָדֶךָ; וַיֹּאמֶר, מַטֶּה.
And Jehovah said to him, "What is that in your hand?" And he said, "A staff."note
Y Jehová le dijo: «¿Qué es eso que tienes en tu mano?» Y él dijo: «Una vara».
3
וַיֹּאמֶר הַשְׁלִיכֵהוּ אַרְצָה, וַיַּשְׁלִכֵהוּ אַרְצָה וַיְהִי לְנָחָשׁ; וַיָּנָס מֹשֶׁה, מִפָּנָיו.
And he said, "Throw it on the ground." And he threw it on the ground, and it became a serpent; and Moses fled from it.note
Y dijo: «Échala a tierra». Y la echó a tierra, y se convirtió en una serpiente; y Moisés huyó de ella.

וַיֹּאמֶר יְהוָה, אֶל-מֹשֶׁה, שְׁלַח יָדְךָ, וֶאֱחֹז בִּזְנָבוֹ; וַיִּשְׁלַח יָדוֹ וַיַּחֲזֶק בּוֹ, וַיְהִי לְמַטֶּה בְּכַפּוֹ.
And Jehovah said to Moses, "Reach out your hand and take it by the tail" — and he reached out his hand and seized it, and it became a staff in his palm —note
Y Jehová dijo a Moisés: «Extiende tu mano y tómala por la cola» —y extendió su mano y la agarró, y se volvió una vara en su palma—
5
לְמַעַן יַאֲמִינוּ, כִּי-נִרְאָה אֵלֶיךָ יְהוָה אֱלֹהֵי אֲבֹתָם: אֱלֹהֵי אַבְרָהָם אֱלֹהֵי יִצְחָק, וֵאלֹהֵי יַעֲקֹב.
"so that they may believe that Jehovah, the God of their fathers, the God of Abraham, the God of Isaac, and the God of Jacob, has appeared to you."note
«para que crean que se te ha aparecido Jehová, el Dios de sus padres, el Dios de Abraham, el Dios de Isaac y el Dios de Jacob».
6

«y si no te creen ni escuchan la voz de la primera señal, creerán a la voz de la señal siguiente.
9

Y si no creen ni siquiera a estas dos señales, ni escuchan tu voz, tomarás de las aguas del Nilo y las derramarás en la tierra seca; y el agua que tomes del Nilo se convertirá en sangre sobre la tierra seca».

Translator’s Notes

Verses 1–9 · three signs for an unbelieving people

Moses’ third objection. After “who am I?” (3:11) and “what is your name?” (3:13), Moses now says no one will believe him. God answers not with argument but with three signs (otot) — proofs to authenticate the messenger. The staff that becomes a serpent and back; the hand made leprous, white as snow, and healed; and water from the Nile turned to blood on the ground. Each will return: the staff-serpent will swallow the magicians’ snakes (ch 7), and the Nile-to-blood is the first plague in miniature. God gives Moses the plagues’ opening act to carry in his own hand.

Note the mercy in the order (v8): if they will not believe the first sign, then the second; if not the second, then the third. God stacks the evidence, patient with unbelief before it has even happened.

Verses 10–17 · heavy of mouth

Moses’ fourth objection is his speech: “I am not a man of words… heavy of mouth and heavy of tongue” (kevad peh) — a stammer, a slowness, or simply the excuse of a reluctant man. God’s answer is one of the great sentences on providence and human limitation: “Who made man’s mouth? Or who makes him mute, or deaf, or seeing, or blind? Is it not I, Jehovah?” The God who made the mouth can use even a heavy one. KJV “I am slow of speech, and of a slow tongue.”

The fifth and last objection is no longer an excuse but a plain refusal: “send by the hand of whomever you will send” — send someone else. And “Jehovah’s anger burned against Moses.” Yet even the anger is a concession: Aaron, already on his way, will be the mouth, and Moses will be “as God” to Aaron — the arrangement that becomes, at 7:1, “I have made you as God to Pharaoh, and Aaron your prophet.” The reluctant man is not replaced; he is given help. (Moses joins the line of the called who protest their inadequacy — and, like Jeremiah after him, “I am only a youth,” he is overruled by presence, not by argument.)

Verses 18–23 · return to Egypt — and “my firstborn son”

«All the men who sought your life are dead» (v19). The pharaoh Moses fled (2:15) has died. ⚠ On the early-date chronology this project foregrounds (the Exodus in 1446 BC, following Associates for Biblical Research / Joel Kramer), the pharaoh of the oppression was Thutmose III — Egypt’s great warrior-king — whose death (~1425 BC) is “the king of Egypt died” of 2:23; Moses now returns to his successor, Amenhotep II, the pharaoh of the Exodus. (The mainstream academic view dates the Exodus later, ~1250 BC under Ramesses II; the early date rests on 1 Kings 6:1’s “480 years” and is given here as the reading the project leads with, not a settled fact.)

Two things are announced before Moses reaches Egypt. First, “I will harden his heart” (v21) — the first of many statements that God will harden Pharaoh; the book will also say Pharaoh hardened his own heart, and the interplay of the two is one of Scripture’s hardest knots, left standing. Second, and staggering: “Israel is my firstborn son… let my son go, that he may serve me; if you refuse, I will kill your firstborn son” (vv22-23). Here, at the very outset, the tenth plague is already spoken. Israel is God’s bekhor — a claim of covenant sonship that reframes the whole conflict as a father reclaiming his son. And on the early date, the firstborn struck at Passover (12:29) is read alongside the Dream Stele of Thutmose IV, Amenhotep II’s successor, which reads as propaganda covering that he was NOT the expected heir — the crown prince, the firstborn, having died. KJV “Israel is my son, even my firstborn”; the ASV “Israel is my son, my first-born.”

Verses 24–26 · a bridegroom of blood

⚠ One of the strangest and most obscure passages in the Torah — and honesty requires saying so plainly. On the way to Egypt, “Jehovah met him and sought to put him to death” — whom is not certain (Moses, most likely; possibly his son). Zipporah acts at once: she takes a flint, circumcises their son, touches the foreskin to “his feet,” and says, “Surely you are a bridegroom of blood to me.” And the danger passes.

Nearly every word is disputed. The most common reading: Moses, about to demand that Pharaoh release God’s “son” and to warn of the firstborn, had left his OWN son uncircumcised — outside the covenant sign (Genesis 17) — and God will not send a covenant-breaker on a covenant errand; Zipporah’s emergency circumcision, and the blood she smears, sets it right, and foreshadows the blood on the doorposts that will save Israel’s firstborn (ch 12). ⚠ But the pronouns are slippery, “his feet” may be a euphemism, and “bridegroom of blood” (chatan damim) has no certain sense; the passage resists a confident solution, and this translation renders it closely and lets it stand strange. KJV “a bloody husband art thou to me.”

Verses 27–31 · the brothers, and a people who believe

Aaron, sent by God, meets Moses “at the mountain of God” — the same Horeb where the bush burned — and kisses him; the two brothers, reunited after Moses’ forty years in Midian, go together to the elders. Aaron speaks the words and Moses does the signs, and the response is the very one the third objection feared would never come: “the people believed.”

And what they believe is not the wonders but what the wonders mean — “that Jehovah had VISITED the sons of Israel and had seen their affliction.” Paqad, the visitation verb, is the word Joseph swore by at the very end of Genesis: “God will surely visit you… and carry up my bones” (50:24). The promise made over Joseph’s coffin, four hundred years earlier, is now being kept. The people bow their heads and worship. The exodus has its messenger, its spokesman, and, at last, its people.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the exact / distinct words: “signs” for otot, the three authenticating wonders; “heavy of mouth and heavy of tongue” for kevad peh, kept literal rather than smoothed to “slow of speech”; “as God” for the arrangement that makes Moses Elohim to Aaron; “harden” for chazak, the hardening-of-the-heart verb left in its full difficulty; “firstborn son” for bekhor; and “bridegroom of blood” for chatan damim, rendered closely and left strange.

Echoes paid. The reluctant-prophet line — Moses’ “heavy of mouth” answered, as Jeremiah’s “I am only a youth” will be, by presence not argument (Jeremiah 1:6); “I will be with your mouth” (ehyeh), the same verb as the Name at the bush (Exodus 3:14); and paqad — Jehovah “visited” Israel, the very word Joseph swore by over his coffin (Genesis 50:24).

Echoes planted. The staff that becomes a serpent and the Nile-to-blood, the plagues’ opening act carried in Moses’ hand (ch 7-8); the hardening of Pharaoh’s heart, a thread through ten plagues; “Israel is my firstborn son” and the threat to Pharaoh’s own, the tenth plague announced at the outset (ch 12); and the blood Zipporah smears, foreshadowing the blood on the doorposts that will save Israel’s firstborn.

Next in this book: Exodus 5 — Moses and Aaron before Pharaoh at last: “Who is Jehovah, that I should obey his voice?” — and Pharaoh answers by ordering the Israelites to make bricks without straw, so that the first result of the deliverance is that it gets worse.
