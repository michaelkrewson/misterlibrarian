Genesis 47 — The Mister Translation

1
וַיָּבֹא יוֹסֵף, וַיַּגֵּד לְפַרְעֹה, וַיֹּאמֶר אָבִי וְאַחַי וְצֹאנָם וּבְקָרָם וְכָל-אֲשֶׁר לָהֶם, בָּאוּ מֵאֶרֶץ כְּנָעַן; וְהִנָּם, בְּאֶרֶץ גֹּשֶׁן.
And Joseph came and told Pharaoh, and said, "My father and my brothers, and their flocks and their herds and all that is theirs, have come from the land of Canaan; and look — they are in the land of Goshen."note
Y José vino y se lo contó al faraón, y dijo: «Mi padre y mis hermanos, y sus ovejas y sus vacas y todo lo que tienen, han venido de la tierra de Canaán; y miren — están en la tierra de Gosén».
2
וּמִקְצֵה אֶחָיו, לָקַח חֲמִשָּׁה אֲנָשִׁים; וַיַּצִּגֵם, לִפְנֵי פַרְעֹה.
And from among his brothers he took five men and presented them before Pharaoh.note
Y de entre sus hermanos tomó cinco hombres y los presentó delante del faraón.
3
וַיֹּאמֶר פַּרְעֹה אֶל-אֶחָיו, מַה-מַּעֲשֵׂיכֶם; וַיֹּאמְרוּ אֶל-פַּרְעֹה, רֹעֵה צֹאן עֲבָדֶיךָ--גַּם-אֲנַחְנוּ, גַּם-אֲבוֹתֵינוּ.
And Pharaoh said to his brothers, "What is your occupation?" And they said to Pharaoh, "Your servants are shepherds of flocks, both we and our fathers."note
Y el faraón dijo a sus hermanos: «¿Cuál es su oficio?» Y ellos dijeron al faraón: «Tus siervos son pastores de ovejas, tanto nosotros como nuestros padres».
cross-refs⤷ 46:34
4
וַיֹּאמְרוּ אֶל-פַּרְעֹה, לָגוּר בָּאָרֶץ בָּאנוּ, כִּי-אֵין מִרְעֶה לַצֹּאן אֲשֶׁר לַעֲבָדֶיךָ, כִּי-כָבֵד הָרָעָב בְּאֶרֶץ כְּנָעַן; וְעַתָּה יֵשְׁבוּ-נָא עֲבָדֶיךָ, בְּאֶרֶץ גֹּשֶׁן.
And they said to Pharaoh, "We have come to sojourn in the land, for there is no pasture for the flocks that belong to your servants, for the famine is heavy in the land of Canaan; and now, please let your servants dwell in the land of Goshen."note
Y dijeron al faraón: «Hemos venido a residir como forasteros en la tierra, porque no hay pasto para las ovejas que tienen tus siervos, porque el hambre es pesada en la tierra de Canaán; y ahora, por favor, que tus siervos habiten en la tierra de Gosén».
5
וַיֹּאמֶר פַּרְעֹה, אֶל-יוֹסֵף לֵאמֹר: אָבִיךָ וְאַחֶיךָ, בָּאוּ אֵלֶיךָ.
And Pharaoh said to Joseph, saying, "Your father and your brothers have come to you.note
Y el faraón habló a José, diciendo: «Tu padre y tus hermanos han venido a ti.
6
אֶרֶץ מִצְרַיִם, לְפָנֶיךָ הִוא--בְּמֵיטַב הָאָרֶץ, הוֹשֵׁב אֶת-אָבִיךָ וְאֶת-אַחֶיךָ: יֵשְׁבוּ, בְּאֶרֶץ גֹּשֶׁן--וְאִם-יָדַעְתָּ וְיֶשׁ-בָּם אַנְשֵׁי-חַיִל, וְשַׂמְתָּם שָׂרֵי מִקְנֶה עַל-אֲשֶׁר-לִי.
The land of Egypt is before you; in the best of the land settle your father and your brothers — let them dwell in the land of Goshen. And if you know that there are capable men among them, then set them as chiefs of livestock over what is mine."note
La tierra de Egipto está delante de ti; en lo mejor de la tierra asienta a tu padre y a tus hermanos — que habiten en la tierra de Gosén. Y si sabes que hay entre ellos hombres capaces, ponlos por jefes de ganado sobre lo que es mío».
7
וַיָּבֵא יוֹסֵף אֶת-יַעֲקֹב אָבִיו, וַיַּעֲמִדֵהוּ לִפְנֵי פַרְעֹה; וַיְבָרֶךְ יַעֲקֹב, אֶת-פַּרְעֹה.
And Joseph brought in Jacob his father and stood him before Pharaoh; and Jacob blessed Pharaoh.note
Y José trajo a Jacob su padre y lo puso de pie delante del faraón; y Jacob bendijo al faraón.
8
וַיֹּאמֶר פַּרְעֹה, אֶל-יַעֲקֹב: כַּמָּה, יְמֵי שְׁנֵי חַיֶּיךָ.
And Pharaoh said to Jacob, "How many are the days of the years of your life?"note
Y el faraón dijo a Jacob: «¿Cuántos son los días de los años de tu vida?»
9
וַיֹּאמֶר יַעֲקֹב, אֶל-פַּרְעֹה, יְמֵי שְׁנֵי מְגוּרַי, שְׁלֹשִׁים וּמְאַת שָׁנָה: מְעַט וְרָעִים, הָיוּ יְמֵי שְׁנֵי חַיַּי, וְלֹא הִשִּׂיגוּ אֶת-יְמֵי שְׁנֵי חַיֵּי אֲבֹתַי, בִּימֵי מְגוּרֵיהֶם.
And Jacob said to Pharaoh, "The days of the years of my sojournings are a hundred and thirty years. Few and evil have been the days of the years of my life, and they have not reached the days of the years of the lives of my fathers in the days of their sojournings."note
Y Jacob dijo al faraón: «Los días de los años de mis peregrinaciones son ciento treinta años. Pocos y malos han sido los días de los años de mi vida, y no han alcanzado los días de los años de la vida de mis padres en los días de sus peregrinaciones».
cross-refs⤷ 23:9
10
וַיְבָרֶךְ יַעֲקֹב, אֶת-פַּרְעֹה; וַיֵּצֵא, מִלִּפְנֵי פַרְעֹה.
And Jacob blessed Pharaoh, and went out from before Pharaoh.note
Y Jacob bendijo al faraón, y salió de delante del faraón.
11
וַיּוֹשֵׁב יוֹסֵף, אֶת-אָבִיו וְאֶת-אֶחָיו, וַיִּתֵּן לָהֶם אֲחֻזָּה בְּאֶרֶץ מִצְרַיִם, בְּמֵיטַב הָאָרֶץ בְּאֶרֶץ רַעְמְסֵס--כַּאֲשֶׁר, צִוָּה פַרְעֹה.
And Joseph settled his father and his brothers, and gave them a holding in the land of Egypt, in the best of the land, in the land of Rameses, as Pharaoh had commanded.note
Y José asentó a su padre y a sus hermanos, y les dio una posesión en la tierra de Egipto, en lo mejor de la tierra, en la tierra de Ramesés, como el faraón había mandado.
12
וַיְכַלְכֵּל יוֹסֵף אֶת-אָבִיו וְאֶת-אֶחָיו, וְאֵת כָּל-בֵּית אָבִיו--לֶחֶם, לְפִי הַטָּף.
And Joseph provided his father and his brothers and all his father's house with bread, according to the little ones.note
Y José sustentó a su padre y a sus hermanos y a toda la casa de su padre con pan, conforme a los pequeños.
13
וְלֶחֶם אֵין בְּכָל-הָאָרֶץ, כִּי-כָבֵד הָרָעָב מְאֹד; וַתֵּלַהּ אֶרֶץ מִצְרַיִם, וְאֶרֶץ כְּנַעַן, מִפְּנֵי, הָרָעָב.
And there was no bread in all the land, for the famine was very heavy; and the land of Egypt and the land of Canaan languished because of the famine.note
Y no había pan en toda la tierra, porque el hambre era muy pesada; y la tierra de Egipto y la tierra de Canaán desfallecían a causa del hambre.
14
וַיְלַקֵּט יוֹסֵף, אֶת-כָּל-הַכֶּסֶף הַנִּמְצָא בְאֶרֶץ-מִצְרַיִם וּבְאֶרֶץ כְּנַעַן, בַּשֶּׁבֶר, אֲשֶׁר-הֵם שֹׁבְרִים; וַיָּבֵא יוֹסֵף אֶת-הַכֶּסֶף, בֵּיתָה פַרְעֹה.
And Joseph gathered all the silver that was found in the land of Egypt and in the land of Canaan, for the grain they were buying; and Joseph brought the silver into Pharaoh's house.note
Y José recogió toda la plata que se hallaba en la tierra de Egipto y en la tierra de Canaán, por el grano que compraban; y José llevó la plata a la casa del faraón.
cross-refs⤷ 41:34
15
וַיִּתֹּם הַכֶּסֶף, מֵאֶרֶץ מִצְרַיִם וּמֵאֶרֶץ כְּנַעַן, וַיָּבֹאוּ כָל-מִצְרַיִם אֶל-יוֹסֵף לֵאמֹר הָבָה-לָּנוּ לֶחֶם, וְלָמָּה נָמוּת נֶגְדֶּךָ: כִּי אָפֵס, כָּסֶף.
And the silver from the land of Egypt and from the land of Canaan came to an end, and all Egypt came to Joseph, saying, "Give us bread! And why should we die in front of you? — for the silver is gone."note
Y se acabó la plata de la tierra de Egipto y de la tierra de Canaán, y todo Egipto vino a José, diciendo: «¡Danos pan! ¿Y por qué hemos de morir delante de ti? — porque la plata se ha agotado».
16
וַיֹּאמֶר יוֹסֵף הָבוּ מִקְנֵיכֶם, וְאֶתְּנָה לָכֶם בְּמִקְנֵיכֶם--אִם-אָפֵס, כָּסֶף.
And Joseph said, "Give your livestock, and I will give you for your livestock, if the silver is gone."note
Y José dijo: «Den su ganado, y yo les daré a cambio de su ganado, si la plata se ha agotado».
17
וַיָּבִיאוּ אֶת-מִקְנֵיהֶם, אֶל-יוֹסֵף, וַיִּתֵּן לָהֶם יוֹסֵף לֶחֶם בַּסּוּסִים וּבְמִקְנֵה הַצֹּאן וּבְמִקְנֵה הַבָּקָר, וּבַחֲמֹרִים; וַיְנַהֲלֵם בַּלֶּחֶם בְּכָל-מִקְנֵהֶם, בַּשָּׁנָה הַהִוא.
And they brought their livestock to Joseph, and Joseph gave them bread for the horses and for the livestock of the flocks and for the livestock of the herds and for the donkeys; and he led them along with bread that year, for all their livestock.note
Y trajeron su ganado a José, y José les dio pan por los caballos y por el ganado de las ovejas y por el ganado de las vacas y por los asnos; y aquel año los condujo con pan, a cambio de todo su ganado.
18
וַתִּתֹּם, הַשָּׁנָה הַהִוא, וַיָּבֹאוּ אֵלָיו בַּשָּׁנָה הַשֵּׁנִית וַיֹּאמְרוּ לוֹ לֹא-נְכַחֵד מֵאֲדֹנִי, כִּי אִם-תַּם הַכֶּסֶף וּמִקְנֵה הַבְּהֵמָה אֶל-אֲדֹנִי: לֹא נִשְׁאַר לִפְנֵי אֲדֹנִי, בִּלְתִּי אִם-גְּוִיָּתֵנוּ וְאַדְמָתֵנוּ.
And that year came to an end, and they came to him in the second year and said to him, "We will not hide from my lord that the silver is spent, and the herds of cattle are my lord's; there is nothing left before my lord but our bodies and our ground.note
Y se acabó aquel año, y vinieron a él el segundo año y le dijeron: «No le ocultaremos a mi señor que la plata se ha gastado, y los rebaños de ganado son de mi señor; no queda delante de mi señor sino nuestros cuerpos y nuestra tierra.
19
לָמָּה נָמוּת לְעֵינֶיךָ, גַּם-אֲנַחְנוּ גַּם אַדְמָתֵנוּ--קְנֵה-אֹתָנוּ וְאֶת-אַדְמָתֵנוּ, בַּלָּחֶם; וְנִהְיֶה אֲנַחְנוּ וְאַדְמָתֵנוּ, עֲבָדִים לְפַרְעֹה, וְתֶן-זֶרַע וְנִחְיֶה וְלֹא נָמוּת, וְהָאֲדָמָה לֹא תֵשָׁם.
Why should we die before your eyes, both we and our ground? Buy us and our ground for bread, and we and our ground will be servants to Pharaoh; and give seed, that we may live and not die, and that the ground not become desolate."note
¿Por qué hemos de morir ante tus ojos, tanto nosotros como nuestra tierra? Cómpranos a nosotros y a nuestra tierra por pan, y nosotros y nuestra tierra seremos siervos del faraón; y danos semilla, para que vivamos y no muramos, y la tierra no quede desolada».
20
וַיִּקֶן יוֹסֵף אֶת-כָּל-אַדְמַת מִצְרַיִם, לְפַרְעֹה, כִּי-מָכְרוּ מִצְרַיִם אִישׁ שָׂדֵהוּ, כִּי-חָזַק עֲלֵהֶם הָרָעָב; וַתְּהִי הָאָרֶץ, לְפַרְעֹה.
And Joseph bought all the ground of Egypt for Pharaoh, for every Egyptian sold his field, because the famine was strong upon them; and the land became Pharaoh's.note
Y José compró toda la tierra de Egipto para el faraón, porque cada egipcio vendió su campo, porque el hambre se hizo fuerte sobre ellos; y la tierra vino a ser del faraón.
21
וְאֶת-הָעָם--הֶעֱבִיר אֹתוֹ, לֶעָרִים: מִקְצֵה גְבוּל-מִצְרַיִם, וְעַד-קָצֵהוּ.
And the people — he moved them into the cities, from one end of the border of Egypt to its other end.note
Y al pueblo — lo trasladó a las ciudades, desde un extremo del territorio de Egipto hasta el otro extremo.
22
רַק אַדְמַת הַכֹּהֲנִים, לֹא קָנָה: כִּי חֹק לַכֹּהֲנִים מֵאֵת פַּרְעֹה, וְאָכְלוּ אֶת-חֻקָּם אֲשֶׁר נָתַן לָהֶם פַּרְעֹה--עַל-כֵּן, לֹא מָכְרוּ אֶת-אַדְמָתָם.
Only the ground of the priests he did not buy, for the priests had a fixed allowance from Pharaoh, and they ate their allowance that Pharaoh gave them; therefore they did not sell their ground.note
Solamente la tierra de los sacerdotes no compró, porque los sacerdotes tenían una ración fija de parte del faraón, y comían la ración que el faraón les daba; por eso no vendieron su tierra.
cross-refs⤷ 41:45
23
וַיֹּאמֶר יוֹסֵף אֶל-הָעָם, הֵן קָנִיתִי אֶתְכֶם הַיּוֹם וְאֶת-אַדְמַתְכֶם לְפַרְעֹה; הֵא-לָכֶם זֶרַע, וּזְרַעְתֶּם אֶת-הָאֲדָמָה.
And Joseph said to the people, "Look — I have bought you today, and your ground, for Pharaoh. Here is seed for you, and you shall sow the ground.note
Y José dijo al pueblo: «Miren — hoy los he comprado a ustedes y a su tierra para el faraón. Aquí tienen semilla, y sembrarán la tierra.
24
וְהָיָה, בַּתְּבוּאֹת, וּנְתַתֶּם חֲמִישִׁית, לְפַרְעֹה; וְאַרְבַּע הַיָּדֹת יִהְיֶה לָכֶם לְזֶרַע הַשָּׂדֶה וּלְאָכְלְכֶם, וְלַאֲשֶׁר בְּבָתֵּיכֶם--וְלֶאֱכֹל לְטַפְּכֶם.
And it shall be at the harvests that you shall give a fifth to Pharaoh, and four of the parts shall be yours, for seed of the field and for your food, and for those in your houses, and for your little ones to eat."note
Y será que en las cosechas darán una quinta parte al faraón, y las cuatro partes serán de ustedes, para semilla del campo y para su comida, y para los que están en sus casas, y para que coman sus pequeños».
25
וַיֹּאמְרוּ, הֶחֱיִתָנוּ; נִמְצָא-חֵן בְּעֵינֵי אֲדֹנִי, וְהָיִינוּ עֲבָדִים לְפַרְעֹה.
And they said, "You have kept us alive! Let us find favour in the eyes of my lord, and we will be servants to Pharaoh."note
Y ellos dijeron: «¡Nos has conservado la vida! Hallemos gracia a los ojos de mi señor, y seremos siervos del faraón».
cross-refs⤷ 45:5
26
וַיָּשֶׂם אֹתָהּ יוֹסֵף לְחֹק עַד-הַיּוֹם הַזֶּה עַל-אַדְמַת מִצְרַיִם, לְפַרְעֹה--לַחֹמֶשׁ: רַק אַדְמַת הַכֹּהֲנִים, לְבַדָּם--לֹא הָיְתָה, לְפַרְעֹה.
And Joseph made it a statute to this day over the ground of Egypt — the fifth for Pharaoh. Only the ground of the priests alone did not become Pharaoh's.note
Y José lo puso por estatuto hasta el día de hoy sobre la tierra de Egipto — la quinta parte para el faraón. Solamente la tierra de los sacerdotes, ella sola, no vino a ser del faraón.
27
וַיֵּשֶׁב יִשְׂרָאֵל בְּאֶרֶץ מִצְרַיִם, בְּאֶרֶץ גֹּשֶׁן; וַיֵּאָחֲזוּ בָהּ, וַיִּפְרוּ וַיִּרְבּוּ מְאֹד.
And Israel dwelt in the land of Egypt, in the land of Goshen; and they took holdings in it, and were fruitful and multiplied greatly.note
E Israel habitó en la tierra de Egipto, en la tierra de Gosén; y tomaron posesiones en ella, y fueron fecundos y se multiplicaron muchísimo.
cross-refs⤷ 1:28⤷ Exod 1:7
28
וַיְחִי יַעֲקֹב בְּאֶרֶץ מִצְרַיִם, שְׁבַע עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה; וַיְהִי יְמֵי-יַעֲקֹב, שְׁנֵי חַיָּיו--שֶׁבַע שָׁנִים, וְאַרְבָּעִים וּמְאַת שָׁנָה.
And Jacob lived in the land of Egypt seventeen years; and the days of Jacob, the years of his life, were a hundred and forty-seven years.note

Translator's Notes

Verses 1–12 · an old shepherd blesses the king of Egypt

The script works, and Pharaoh improves on it. They say exactly what Joseph told them to say (46:34) — shepherds, both we and our fathers — and it lands: Goshen is granted. Then Pharaoh goes further than asked and offers to put the capable ones over the royal herds, which is the opposite of the arm's-length settlement Joseph engineered. Genesis does not say whether any of them took the job.

Jacob blesses Pharaoh. Twice. This is the strangest thing in the chapter and it is easy to skate over. A landless herdsman, brought in off the frontier, walks into the presence of a man his own culture regarded as a living god — and blesses him, on the way in and on the way out, framing the entire audience. He does not petition. He does not flatter. The only thing he is recorded as giving Pharaoh is a blessing, which is the one currency the throne cannot issue for itself. The letter to the Hebrews later builds an argument on exactly this asymmetry ("the lesser is blessed by the greater," 7:7); Genesis simply reports it twice and lets the shape speak.

"Few and evil." ⚠️ He is a hundred and thirty years old and he calls it few — measured against Abraham's hundred and seventy-five and Isaac's hundred and eighty, which he says outright he has not reached. And ra'im, "evil, hard, bad," is a startling word to hand a king who politely asked your age. It is also, on the evidence, accurate: a stolen blessing, twenty years under Laban, a night of wrestling, Dinah, Rachel dead on the road to Bethlehem, and twenty-two years believing Joseph was torn to pieces. KJV "few and evil have the days of the years of my life been"; ASV the same; the NIV softens to "few and difficult," which is kinder than the Hebrew is.

"Sojournings," not "life." Pharaoh asks about the days of his life; Jacob answers about the days of his megurim — his residing-as-an-alien, the word for a resident foreigner with no title to the ground under him. He applies it to his fathers too. Standing in the richest country on earth, having just been handed the best of its land, he describes three generations as people who never owned anywhere. It is the theological centre of the patriarchal story said in one word to a man who owns everything he can see.

⚠️ "The land of Rameses." Here is a genuine difficulty and it should not be smoothed over. The Egyptian city Pi-Ramesses takes its name from Ramesses II, who reigned in the thirteenth century BC — centuries after any date proposed for Joseph. Two honest options. (1) It is an editorial updating of a place name, so readers could find it on their own map: the same book calls a town "Dan" (14:14) long before the tribe of Dan existed to give it that name, so the practice is demonstrably at work inside Genesis itself. (2) The name is original and our Egyptology is incomplete — much the weaker case, since the royal name is well attested and late. ⚠️ It should also be said plainly that this verse, together with the store-city "Raamses" of Exodus 1:11, is one of the strongest arguments for dating the exodus to the thirteenth century — which is not the chronology this project follows elsewhere (see the note on Joseph's pharaoh). The updated-place-name reading is what reconciles them, and it is a reading, not a proof.

Verses 13–26 · how Joseph bought Egypt

Read the sequence for what it is. In four stages, a famine transfers an entire country to the crown. First the silver — all of it, from two countries, into Pharaoh's house. Then the livestock, when the silver runs out. Then, in the second year, the people themselves propose the last stage, because there is nothing else left: "buy us and our ground." Joseph does. He acquires the land, relocates the population, and imposes a permanent twenty-percent levy on every harvest. ⚠️ It is worth being clear-eyed about the mechanism: the grain he is selling back to them is the grain his own administration took from them in the seven good years (41:34). They are buying back their own surplus, and paying for it with everything they own.

Genesis does not tell you what to think about this. There is no word of praise and no word of blame — the same flat reportorial voice used for the sale of Joseph himself. Readers have gone both ways for two thousand years. It is a competent famine administration that keeps a nation alive when the alternative is mass starvation; it is also the invention of a serf state, carried out by a foreigner, in a book whose next act is Egypt enslaving his descendants. Both readings survive contact with the text. Neither is imposed by it.

"You have kept us alive." Hecheyitanu — the same root as the michyah, "to preserve life," that Joseph used to explain his whole career to his brothers (45:5). Now the sentence is in the mouths of people who have just sold themselves into servitude, and they say it gratefully. Preservation and subjugation come out of the same policy and are named by the same word. Genesis places the two uses two chapters apart and offers no comment.

⚠️ Verse 21 has a real textual variant, and it changes the verse. The Masoretic Hebrew reads he'evir otam le-arim, "he moved them to the cities" — a population transfer, perhaps to put people near the granaries. The Septuagint and the Samaritan Pentateuch read he'evid otam la-avadim, "he made them servants" — which differs by a single letter (resh ר and dalet ד are all but identical in Hebrew script) and turns the line into an explicit statement of enslavement. This translation follows the Masoretic Text, as throughout, and flags the alternative rather than choosing the more dramatic reading. Modern versions divide: KJV and ASV keep "removed them to cities"; the ESV and NIV follow the Greek and Samaritan with "made servants of them" / "reduced the people to servitude." Note that verses 19, 23 and 25 already say plainly that they became Pharaoh's servants, so the variant sharpens the point rather than creating it.

The priests keep their land. Verse 22 is not incidental. The one class exempted from the whole operation is the Egyptian priesthood, who hold their fields because the crown pays them a fixed allowance. ⚠️ Joseph is married to the daughter of a priest of On (41:45). Genesis records the exemption and the marriage and never connects them. Whether the reader should is left entirely open.

The twenty percent is real economic history, or close to it. A crown that owns the land and takes a fixed share of the harvest, with a tax-exempt temple estate alongside it, is a recognisable description of later Egyptian agriculture. "To this day" tells you the narrator is explaining to his readers why the country they know is organised the way it is — the same kind of aside that explains a place name or a custom elsewhere in Genesis.

Verses 27–28 · one sentence that changes key

Watch the sentence immediately after Egypt is enslaved. "And Israel dwelt in the land of Egypt… and they took holdings in it, and were fruitful and multiplied greatly." Vayifru vayirbu — the creation blessing (1:28), and word for word the opening of Exodus 1:7, where the same verbs describe a people so numerous the land is filled with them and a new king becomes afraid. In a single verse the seventy who came down begin to become a nation, on the very ground where everyone else has just lost theirs. ⚠️ And vayye'achazu, "they took holdings," is from the root behind achuzzah — a permanent possession, the word used of the family's one purchased grave in Canaan (23:9). They are putting down roots in the wrong country. The next two verses are an old man making very sure his own body does not stay there.

Seventeen years. Jacob raised Joseph for seventeen years before losing him (37:2), and now Joseph keeps his father for seventeen years. The book does not point at the symmetry. It just gives the same number twice.

Verses 29–31 · the oath, and a famous ambiguity

"Put your hand under my thigh." The same oath Abraham required of his servant before sending him for a wife (24:2), and both times it concerns the future of the line rather than an ordinary promise. The gesture is generally understood as an oath sworn by the organs of generation — by the covenant sign and the descendants who carry it — which is why it appears exactly twice in the Bible and both times for the survival of the family's identity. This translation keeps it literal and lets the reader meet it as ancient readers did.

"Kindness and truth." Chesed ve-emet — steadfast loyalty joined to reliability, the pair used of covenant faithfulness itself. Jacob is not asking a favour; he is invoking the standard by which the family is supposed to treat its own. And the thing he wants is startlingly specific: not to be buried in the richest country on earth, in the province he has just been given, but carried out and laid in a cave in a land his family owns almost none of.

Why it matters that he insists. He has spent seventeen comfortable years in Goshen, and his last recorded act is to make his most powerful son swear an oath that the family's future is not here. It is the practical form of the promise he was given at Beersheba — "I will surely bring you up again." Joseph will make the same request of his own brothers at the end of the book, and the bones will wait four hundred years.

⚠️ "At the head of the bed" — or "on the top of his staff." One of the best-known variants in Genesis, and it comes down to vowels. Written without them, המטה can be read ha-mittah, "the bed," or ha-matteh, "the staff." The Masoretes pointed it as bed, which this translation follows; the Septuagint read staff, and that is the reading quoted in Hebrews 11:21 — "leaning on the top of his staff." Neither is a mistake; they are the same consonants read two ways, and the New Testament is quoting the Greek Bible its readers used. KJV "bowed himself upon the bed's head"; NWT "bowed down over the head of his bed." ⚠️ A further open question: to whom does he bow? The text does not say. Toward God in thanks is the traditional reading; toward Joseph is grammatically available and would quietly complete the dream of 37:10, the one Jacob rebuked his son for — "shall I and your mother indeed come to bow down to you?" Genesis leaves the object of the verb out, and this translation leaves it out too.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the words exact / distinct: "sojournings" for megurim, so Jacob's answer visibly does not answer Pharaoh's question; "few and evil" for me'at ve-ra'im, unsoftened; "the fifth" for chomesh; "bodies" for geviyah in verse 18, which is what the people call themselves once the livestock is gone; "You have kept us alive" sharing the root of michyah at 45:5; "kindness and truth" for chesed ve-emet; and "he moved them into the cities" following the Masoretic consonants, with the Greek and Samaritan "made them servants" in the note rather than in the line.

Echoes paid. The coached script delivered and accepted; the fifth taken in the good years now sold back and made permanent; "God sent me… to preserve life" answered by a nation saying "you have kept us alive" as it sells itself; Abraham's oath under the thigh; and "I will surely bring you up again" turned into a sworn instruction.

Echoes planted. "Fruitful and multiplied greatly," the exact verbs Exodus 1:7 opens with, and the fear that follows them; the bones that must leave Egypt, which Joseph will ask for himself at the end of the book and which wait four centuries; a country where the crown owns the land and everyone works it for a fifth, which is the state the Israelites will eventually be building store cities for; and the slow inversion Genesis never comments on — the Hebrew who made Egypt Pharaoh's servants, and the Egypt that makes his descendants slaves.
