A prophet in the northern kingdom is told to marry a promiscuous woman and to name their three children after the sentence on his own country: a massacre, an unpitied daughter, and a people God will not call his.
Begun under JEROBOAM II of Israel (d. c. 747 BC), at the height of northern prosperity, and running through the collapse that followed — four of the next six kings assassinated — toward Samaria's fall in 722. ⚠ The heading dates the book by four kings of JUDAH (Uzziah, Jotham, Ahaz, Hezekiah), the same span as Isaiah 1:1, which is almost certainly the work of the Judean editors who preserved a northern prophet's book after the north was destroyed. ⚠ This chapter has NINE verses in the Hebrew; English Bibles print 1:10-11, which the Masoretic text numbers 2:1-2.

Translator’s Notes

Verse 1 · four kings from the wrong country, and a chapter that ends early

⚠ Look at whose reigns date this book. Uzziah, Jotham, Ahaz and Hezekiah — the identical four-king span that heads Isaiah 1:1 — are kings of JUDAH. But Hosea is a prophet of the NORTH: he preaches to Israel, about Israel, and every place he names is northern. And for Israel the heading gives exactly one king, Jeroboam son of Joash (Jeroboam II, d. c. 747 BC), even though the decades that follow contained six more — Zechariah, Shallum, Menahem, Pekahiah, Pekah and Hoshea, four of whom were assassinated. A prophet who watched that happen does not date his book by a Judean list. The heading is almost certainly the work of the Judean editors who preserved the book after the north was destroyed, which is also why it survives at all.

⚠ This chapter has NINE verses, not eleven. English Bibles print 1:10–11 (“yet the number of the children of Israel shall be as the sand of the sea…”); in the Hebrew those are 2:1–2, and the offset runs through the whole of chapter 2 (Hebrew 2:1–25 = English 1:10–2:23). We follow the Masoretic chapter division, so the reversal of the three names arrives at the top of the next chapter rather than at the foot of this one. It is the same class of numbering difference this site has already met in 2 Chronicles.

Verses 2–3 · the command, and the oldest argument about it

⚠ «Take yourself a wife of promiscuity» is the most disputed sentence in the prophets, and it has been disputed since antiquity. The Hebrew is eshet zenunim — not the ordinary word for a prostitute (zonah) but an abstract plural, a woman of promiscuities. The readings, all old and all still held:

(1) Literal, and she was already promiscuous when he married her — the plain sense of the words, and the reading that gives the book its force. (2) Literal, but the description is retrospective: Hosea married an ordinary woman who later betrayed him, and the command is written from the far side of the marriage. (3) A vision or a parable, never physically enacted — the position of a long rabbinic tradition (see the discussion in the Talmud, Pesachim 87a-b, which takes the marriage as real but frames the whole episode around God’s answer to the prophet) and of Jerome and many later readers who found the literal command morally intolerable. (4) A cultic reference — a woman involved in Canaanite fertility worship — which is now generally regarded as resting on evidence that has not held up.

The versions do not hedge: KJV “Go, take unto thee a wife of whoredoms”; ASV the same; NIV “Go, marry a promiscuous woman”; NWT “Go, take for yourself a wife of prostitution.” We translate it as it stands and do not decide. What is worth saying plainly is that the text shows no discomfort with the command and offers no explanation of it — the discomfort is entirely ours, and it is the point.

«Through Hosea» — the Hebrew preposition in verse 2 is be-, “in/by/through,” not the “to” of verse 1. God speaks by means of this man, and the sentence is exact: the marriage is not the background to the message, it is the message. Hosea’s household becomes the sermon, and his three children are its three sentences — the fullest example in the Bible of a prophet’s acted sign, the category Isaiah names when he says “I and the children whom Jehovah has given me are for signs and portents in Israel.”

Gomer daughter of Diblaim is named and then never speaks. Diblayim looks like a dual of develah, a pressed fig-cake, and readers since antiquity have tried to hear something in it — a prostitute’s fee, or a link to the raisin-cakes of 3:1. It may equally be an ordinary patronymic. This translation notes the guess and marks it as a guess.

Verses 4–5 · a name that is a war crime, and a tension the Bible does not resolve

Jezreel means “God sows” — and here it is a sentence of judgment, because of what happened in the valley of that name. Jezreel is where JEHU carried out the coup of 2 Kings 9–10: Joram shot in the back and thrown on Naboth’s field, Jezebel dropped from a window, the seventy sons of Ahab beheaded and their heads stacked in two heaps at the city gate.

⚠ And here is the problem this verse creates, which no amount of harmonising dissolves. In 2 Kings 10:30 God commends Jehu for exactly that — “because you have done well in carrying out what is right in my eyes … your sons to the fourth generation shall sit on the throne of Israel.” Here, a century later, God says he will call the house of Jehu to account for the blood of Jezreel. Both texts are in the canon. The usual explanations — that Jehu exceeded his commission, that the promise had a term and this is its expiry, that Kings and Hosea are simply two verdicts — are all defensible and none is stated by either text. The library prints both and does not choose.

Verse 7’s «I will save them» is a quiet pun — the prophet’s own name, Hoshea, is built on that verb: the man called Salvation announces who will and will not do the saving. «I will break the bow of Israel» — the bow is the army, and the valley of Jezreel is the great flat plain where northern armies fought and where Israel’s kingdom would in fact end. Jeroboam II died around 747; within twenty-five years Samaria had fallen and the northern kingdom was gone. KJV “I will break the bow of Israel in the valley of Jezreel”; NWT “I will break the bow of Israel in the Valley of Jezreel.”

⚠ But the name is double-edged, and the book will turn it over. Yizre’el is built on zara, to SOW seed. In Hebrew 2:25 the same name becomes a promise: I will sow her for myself in the land. Hosea does not replace the name of judgment; he plants it and lets it germinate, which is a piece of craft worth watching for.

Verses 6–7 · Not-Pitied, and a verse about Judah that may not belong

⚠ «Lo-Ruchamah» is built on the word for a WOMB. Rachamim, the ordinary Hebrew for compassion, comes from rechem — so Hebrew’s word for mercy is literally mother-feeling, the physical ache of a parent for a child. To name a newborn daughter Not-Mother-Loved is therefore worse in Hebrew than it is in English, and it is deliberate: the child is a girl, and the word is the one for what a mother has. KJV “Lo-ruhamah… for I will no more have mercy”; ASV the same with a marginal “that is, That hath not obtained mercy”; NIV “Lo-Ruhamah (which means ‘not loved’)”; NWT “Lo-ruhamah, for I will no longer show mercy.”

The second half of verse 6 is genuinely hard. Ki naso essa lahem — an emphatic doubling of nasa, the verb that means to lift, carry and therefore also to forgive (to lift a burden of guilt away). So it can be read “that I should ever forgive them” (KJV “but I will utterly take them away”; ASV “that I should in any wise pardon them”), or “that I should keep carrying them” (NIV “that I should at all forgive them”; NWT “for I will certainly take them away”). The shelf is genuinely split. We keep carry, which holds both senses in one English word.

⚠ Verse 7 is the one verse in this chapter that many readers think was added later. The reasons are worth stating: it interrupts a tight sequence of three northern children with a sentence about Judah; it is warm about Judah where the rest of Hosea is not; and “not by bow or sword or war or horses or horsemen” reads very like the deliverance of Jerusalem from Sennacherib in 701 BC, after Hosea’s time. Against that: the verse is in every manuscript and version we have, including the Greek, and Hosea elsewhere does distinguish Judah from Israel. The library prints it and records the argument.

Verses 8–9 · the third name, and the sentence that unsays the divine name

«She weaned Lo-Ruchamah» is a small, terrible piece of realism. Weaning in the ancient Near East came at two or three years, so the three children span the better part of a decade. This is not a vision that happens in an afternoon; it is a man’s family, growing up under names their father was told to give them.

⚠ «Lo-Ammi» reverses the covenant formula exactly. “I will take you to me for a people, and I will be your God” (Exodus 6:7) is the sentence the whole relationship runs on, and it appears in that form throughout the Law and the Prophets. Here it is taken apart: you are not my people.

⚠ And then the second half, which most English Bibles quietly complete for the reader. The Hebrew is ve-anokhi lo-ehyeh lakhem — literally and I will not be EHYEH to you. There is no word for “God” in the clause. KJV supplies it (“and I will not be your God”), and so do ASV, NIV and NWT — a reasonable reading of the covenant formula, and a sentence the Hebrew does not actually say.

What the Hebrew says is stranger. Ehyeh is the name God gave himself at the bush — ehyeh asher ehyeh, Exodus 3:14, “I WILL BE what I will be” — and it is the same word in the promise two verses earlier, ehyeh immakh, “I WILL BE with you.” A number of commentators take verse 9 as a deliberate un-saying of that: not merely I am not your God but I am not, for you, the one who said I WILL BE. We translate it that way and flag it, because the allusion is the strongest thing in the chapter and every English version has covered it over. It is also, on that reading, the most extreme sentence in the prophets — God withdrawing not a benefit but the name he introduced himself by.

And it does not stand. Two verses later in the Hebrew (2:1, English 1:10) the same book says: in the place where it was said to them, ‘you are not my people,’ it will be said to them, ‘sons of the living God.’ All three names are reversed — Not-Pitied pitied, Not-My-People called my people, and Jezreel turned from a massacre back into a sowing. Hosea builds the worst sentence he can and then, within a few lines, takes it apart. Paul quotes the reversal at Romans 9:25–26, and 1 Peter 2:10 draws on it as well — both applying to gentiles a promise made here to the northern kingdom.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the exact / distinct words: “a wife of promiscuity” for eshet zenunim — the abstract plural, not the ordinary word for a prostitute — translated as it stands, with all four readings of the command set out and none chosen; “through Hosea” in verse 2, because the preposition changes from verse 1 and the marriage is the message rather than its background; the three names transliterated AND translated (Jezreel, Lo-Ruchamah — Not-Pitied, Lo-Ammi — Not-My-People), since each is a sentence; “bring to account” for paqad, the verb of God turning his attention toward someone; “carry” in verse 6, which holds both halves of a genuinely split verb; and, in verse 9, “I will not be I-WILL-BE to you” — with no word for “God” supplied, because the Hebrew supplies none.

Echoes paid. The four Judean kings of Isaiah 1:1, heading a northern prophet’s book. Rachamim, mercy built on the word for a womb, used to name a daughter Not-Mother-Loved. Ehyeh, the name from the burning bush at Exodus 3:14, withdrawn in verse 9. Jehu’s coup at Jezreel (2 Kings 9–10) and God’s commendation of it (2 Kings 10:30), set against this chapter’s sentence on the same act. And the covenant formula of Exodus 6:7, taken apart clause by clause.

Echoes planted. The marriage, which becomes the argument of chapters 2 and 3 and then the book’s central metaphor for the whole relationship. Jezreel, a name of judgment built on the verb to sow, which the next chapter turns into a promise. The three reversed names, arriving at Hebrew 2:1–3 — and quoted, centuries later, at Romans 9:25–26 about people who were never in the northern kingdom at all. And chesed, loyal love, which Hosea will make the word the whole book turns on.
