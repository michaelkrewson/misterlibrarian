📖 Psalms

תְּהִלִּים Tehillim — 'PRAISES' — from halal, to praise, boast, shine (the root of hallelu-yah, 'praise Yah'). ⚠ A surprising title for a book that is, by count, more LAMENT than praise — as if the whole collection is claimed for praise by where it is GOING, not where most of it stands. A single poem in it is a mizmor, 'a song sung to a plucked string,' the word behind the Greek title.
Ψαλμοί (Psalmoi) — 'Songs sung to a plucked instrument' (from psallo, to pluck a string) — the Septuagint's title, kept in English as 'Psalms' and the whole book as the 'Psalter' (from psalterion, the harp). ⚠ The Greek and Latin NUMBERING runs one BEHIND the Hebrew for most of the book: the Septuagint joins Hebrew Psalms 9 and 10 into one, so from there the Greek number is one lower (the 'shepherd' psalm is Hebrew 23 but Greek/Vulgate 22) until Psalm 147, which the Greek splits back into two. Psalms 1-8 carry the same number in every tradition.

The hymnbook and prayerbook of Israel — 150 poems for every weather of the soul: praise and rage, trust and terror, thanksgiving and the ache of God's silence. It is the most-quoted book in the New Testament, the songbook Jesus sang, and the words the church has prayed daily for two thousand years. Psalm 1 is its front door — not a prayer at all, but a wisdom poem on the two ways, teaching you to READ the rest as 'the law of Jehovah,' delighted in and murmured day and night.

Chapter by chapter

Psalms 1The front door of the Bible’s hymnbook. The 150 psalms are Israel’s prayers and songs for every weather of the soul — but the book does not open with a prayer. It opens with a WISDOM POEM, Psalm 1, that stands at the gate and tells you how to read everything behind it: there are two ways, the way of the righteous and the way of the wicked, and the whole of a life turns on which one you walk. The happy man — and the Psalter’s very first word is “Happy” (ashrei), the same word that opens the Beatitudes — does not drift down the staircase of the wicked (walk, then stand, then sit) but delights in the law of Jehovah and murmurs it day and night; and so he is a tree planted by water, fruitful and green, while the wicked are chaff the wind simply blows away. “Jehovah knows the way of the righteous, but the way of the wicked will perish.” A quiet poem that is the doorway to the songbook Jesus sang.

About the book

Where it sits
The first book of the KETUVIM (the Writings), the third division of the Hebrew Bible, and its longest book. It is arranged into FIVE BOOKS (1-41, 42-72, 73-89, 90-106, 107-150), each sealed by a doxology — a five-fold shape traditionally read as the Psalter's answer to the five books of Moses. At Qumran the Psalms were the most-copied biblical book (some scrolls in a different order, with extra psalms), and they are the Old Testament book the New Testament quotes most.
Genre
HEBREW POETRY — 150 individual poems, not a narrative. The engine of the verse is not rhyme or meter but PARALLELISM: the second line answers the first, echoing it (synonymous), opposing it (antithetic, as in all of Psalm 1), or completing it (synthetic). The poems fall into recognizable types — hymns of praise, individual and communal LAMENTS (the largest group), thanksgivings, royal psalms, wisdom psalms (Psalm 1 among them), songs of Zion, and enthronement psalms — and many carry superscriptions naming an author, an occasion, or a tune.
Author
Traditionally DAVID, and 73 psalms carry his name in the superscription — but the book is an anthology gathered over centuries. Other named authors include the sons of KORAH and ASAPH (the temple singers), SOLOMON, MOSES (Psalm 90), and Heman and Ethan; many psalms are anonymous. ⚠ The Hebrew 'le-David' can mean 'by,' 'for,' 'about,' or 'in the style of' David, so even the Davidic headings are debated. Psalm 1 itself is untitled and anonymous — most likely a late editorial preface written to frame the whole collection.
Date written
The individual psalms span the whole sweep of Israel's history — from ones plausibly Davidic (10th century BC) through the monarchy, the fall of Jerusalem and the EXILE (Psalm 137, 'by the rivers of Babylon'), to the post-exilic RETURN. The collection reached its final five-book shape in the post-exilic period, probably by the third century BC; Psalm 1, as the editor's doorway, belongs to that final shaping.
Place
Israel and its worship — above all the TEMPLE in Jerusalem and Mount Zion, where most of these songs were sung, with the choirs, instruments and festivals of the liturgical year. But the Psalter's truest 'place' is the human heart before God, in every circumstance a life can meet.
Audience
Everyone, in every condition. The genius of the Psalter is that it gives WORDS to the whole range of experience before God, so the synagogue and church have used it as the very script of prayer — a psalm for joy and a psalm for despair, for confession and for fury, for the deathbed and the festival.

How it's laid out

Book I · 1-41Mostly Davidic laments and songs of trust, opened by the wisdom preface of Psalm 1 and the royal-messianic Psalm 2 (the twin gateway). Holds Psalm 8 ('what is man?'), 19 ('the heavens declare'), 22 ('my God, why have you forsaken me?'), 23 ('Jehovah is my shepherd').
Book II · 42-72The Korah collection and more of David; ends 'the prayers of David son of Jesse are ended.' Holds Psalm 46 ('God is our refuge') and 51 (the great confession).
Book III · 73-89The Asaph psalms and the dark centre of the Psalter — Psalm 73 ('my feet had almost slipped') and the bleakest of all, Psalm 88.
Book IV · 90-106Opened by Moses' Psalm 90 ('teach us to number our days') and the enthronement psalms, 'Jehovah reigns.'
Book V · 107-150The great Hallel, the Songs of Ascents (120-134, the pilgrim songs), the huge Torah-psalm 119, and the closing crescendo — five 'Hallelujah' psalms (146-150) that end the whole book on pure praise: 'Let everything that has breath praise Jah.'

What it's about

  • THE TWO WAYS — Psalm 1's opening frame: the road of the righteous that Jehovah knows, and the road of the wicked that perishes; the wisdom lens through which the whole book asks to be read.
  • THE FULL RANGE OF PRAYER — the Psalter's great gift is honesty. It prays not only praise but rage, doubt, vengeance, and the terror of God's silence; nothing human is unsayable to God here.
  • THE ANOINTED KING — from Psalm 2's 'you are my son' onward, the royal psalms hold out the hope of God's Messiah, the thread the New Testament hears fulfilled in Jesus.
  • GOD AS REFUGE AND SHEPHERD — the trust that runs under even the darkest laments: the rock, the fortress, the shepherd, the shelter of his wings.
  • PRAISE AS THE END — the collection is named 'Praises' and closes in an unbroken shout of it, so that whatever any single psalm passes through, the book as a whole is bent toward doxology.

The source text

Translated from the pointed Hebrew Masoretic Text (as printed by Mechon-Mamre). The Psalter's Hebrew is ancient poetry — dense, elliptical, and in places genuinely uncertain — and its superscriptions carry musical and liturgical terms (selah, miktam, maskil, 'to the choirmaster') whose meanings are partly lost; this translation will flag them rather than guess. Psalm 1 itself is straightforward, and its craft is kept: the antithetic parallelism (righteous against wicked, line by line), the three-step descent of verse 1 (walk / stand / sit), and the tree-against-chaff image. ⚠ Note the Hebrew/Greek numbering divergence from Psalm 9 onward (see above); Psalm 1 is the same number in every tradition.

Looking forward

The Psalms are the New Testament's deepest well. Jesus quotes them constantly, prays Psalm 22 ('my God, my God, why have you forsaken me?') and Psalm 31 ('into your hands I commit my spirit') from the cross, and sings the Hallel at the Last Supper; the apostles read Psalms 2, 16, 22 and 110 as prophecies of his death, resurrection and reign (Psalm 110 is the single most-quoted Old Testament verse in the New). Psalm 1's own contribution is the FRAME: its 'Happy is the man' (ashrei) is the Septuagint's makarios, the very word that opens the Beatitudes (Matthew 5:3); its 'two ways' become the Sermon on the Mount's narrow and broad roads; and the flawlessly fruitful man it pictures is read by Christian tradition as finding its one true fulfilment in Christ.

Where the debates are

AUTHORSHIP and the meaning of 'le-David' in the superscriptions. THE DATE and setting of individual psalms, and of the final five-book editing. WHETHER Psalms 1 and 2 were composed, or at least placed, as a single deliberate introduction (their shared 'Happy' frame and the absence of a title on both are the main evidence). THE 'IMPRECATORY' psalms — the prayers for the enemies' destruction (Psalm 137's 'happy is he who dashes your little ones on the rock') — and how they can be prayed. And the lost meanings of the musical terms.
