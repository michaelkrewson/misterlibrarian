Translator's Notes

Verses 1–4 · the restraint fails, and the room is cleared

"Could no longer hold himself in." Lo yakhol lehit'appeq — and this is a debt being paid, not a fresh phrase. The same verb was counted twice already: at 43:31 Joseph washed his face and held himself in to get through a meal, and the whole of chapter 44 is a man holding himself in while his brother speaks. Here the verb goes negative. Everything he has built for thirteen years — the Egyptian name, the interpreter, the persona — comes apart in one clause. The KJV has "could not refrain himself," the ASV the same; the NWT "was no longer able to control himself," which is the sense exactly.

He clears the room first. This is the last administrative act of the vizier, and it is done to protect them. What he is about to say — you sold me — is a confession of a capital crime, spoken in the palace of a foreign king, in front of witnesses who work for that king. So there are no witnesses. The Egyptians go out. What follows is heard by no one who could use it, which is precisely why verse 2 matters: he cannot keep the sound in even when he has kept the room clear. They hear him through the walls; they do not hear what he says.

"I am Joseph. Is my father still alive?" Three words in Hebrew, then four. And note that the question is not a request for information — they have just told him, at length, that their father is alive and grieving (44:30-31). He knows. It is the question that has been sitting underneath every interview, every accusation, every planted cup: not is he alive but is he still my father. ⚠️ Some read it as a genuine gasp — the man has been maintaining a fiction so long that the truth arrives as a shock even to him. Others read it as the first sentence of the real conversation, the one he has been unable to have. The Hebrew supports either and prefers neither.

"They were terrified before him." Nivhalu — struck dumb, thrown into panic. Not joy. Not relief. The most powerful man they have ever met has just told them he is the boy they threw in a pit, and the arithmetic is instant and terrible. Genesis does not give them a line. It gives them silence, and it is the correct silence.

"Come near to me, please." Geshu-na — and then he says it again, adding the two things that make it survivable and the one thing that makes it true: your brother, and whom you sold into Egypt. He does not euphemise. When he told the cupbearer his story he said only "I was stolen away," leaving the criminals out of the sentence. Now, to their faces, with the door shut, he names the act and the actors in four words — and then spends the next four verses making it bearable. Both halves are the same speech. Nobody in this chapter pretends it did not happen.

Verses 5–13 · "it was not you who sent me here, but God"

The two sentences that have to be held together. In verse 4 he says you sold me. In verse 8 he says it was not you who sent me here. These are not a contradiction and the second is not a retraction — if it were, verse 4 would not be in the chapter. Genesis puts them nine words apart on purpose. The brothers did it, freely, and it was evil; and God was working through it toward something they did not intend. The book never resolves the tension by deleting one side. Five chapters on it will state both in a single sentence — "you meant evil against me, but God meant it for good" (50:20) — and this is the first half of that verdict, spoken while it is still a decision rather than a summary.

What he is actually doing pastorally. "Do not be pained, and let it not burn in your own eyes." He is not telling them their guilt was imaginary. He is telling them not to be destroyed by it — because a group of men paralysed by shame in a famine will not go home and fetch their father. He needs them functional. The theology in verse 8 is doing work in the room, not just in the commentary.

The salvation vocabulary arrives here. Three words in three verses, and all three will carry enormous freight later in the Hebrew Bible: michyah, "to preserve life" (v. 5); she'erit, "a remnant" (v. 7) — the word the prophets will use for the surviving fragment of Israel; and peletah, "a great deliverance" or escape (v. 7). This translation keeps all three distinct rather than flattening them into "to save your lives." The KJV has "to preserve you a posterity in the earth, and to save your lives by a great deliverance"; ASV "to preserve you a remnant"; the ESV "to keep alive for you many survivors." Joseph is describing a family of seventy people, and he is using the language of national survival.

"A father to Pharaoh." Av le-Phar'oh — not a metaphor invented by the narrator but a genuine Egyptian court title (it-netjer, "father of the god," borne by senior officials and royal tutors). ⚠️ The correspondence is real but should not be pushed too hard: the title is attested across long stretches of Egyptian history and cannot by itself date the story. It is one of a cluster of details in these chapters that read as at home in Egypt rather than invented from outside it. See the encyclopedia entry on Joseph's pharaoh for how the dating question actually stands.

"It is my mouth speaking to you." Verse 12, and it is the quietest payoff in the chapter. At 42:23 the narrator paused to tell us there was an interpreter between them, because Joseph was pretending not to understand Hebrew — and then let us watch him overhear their confession and turn away to weep. The interpreter is now gone. He is speaking his own language, in his own mouth, and he tells them to notice. Benjamin's eyes are named separately because Benjamin is the only one who never knew him.

Goshen, named for the first time. The eastern Nile Delta, good pasture, close to Canaan and slightly outside Egypt proper — which is exactly why a family of shepherds can live there without being absorbed. It is also, four hundred years later, where Israel will be enslaved and from which they will leave. The address of the redemption is given here, in an invitation.

Verses 14–24 · weeping, wagons, and five changes of garments

Benjamin first, and no words. Joseph weeps four times in this story and the trajectory is worth tracking: he turned away to weep (42:24), he went into a room to weep (43:30), the Egyptians heard him through a wall (45:2), and now he simply falls on his brother's neck in front of everyone. Benjamin gets no line in the entire Joseph story — not one — but he weeps back, and that is his whole part.

"And after that his brothers spoke with him." Verse 15, and it is the reunion's actual hinge. The kissing and weeping come first; speech takes longer. They had not been able to answer him in verse 3. It takes an embrace and a kiss and a lot of crying before ten men can say anything at all to the brother they sold — and Genesis notes the delay rather than skipping it.

Pharaoh is glad, and that is not a small thing. A famine, a foreign vizier, and the arrival of a large foreign family with livestock: a king could reasonably be cool about this. Instead he doubles Joseph's offer unprompted — the good of the land, the fat of the land, wagons at state expense — and adds the line in verse 20 that is almost a joke: don't waste a thought on your furniture. The KJV's "regard not your stuff" is famous and, for once, exactly right in tone.

The wagons. Agalot — Egyptian wheeled carts, not something a Canaanite herdsman owned or could easily fake. Remember them for verse 27: they are what finally convinces Jacob, when words could not.

⚠️ Five changes of garments. Read verse 22 slowly. Every brother gets a change of clothes; Benjamin gets five, plus three hundred pieces of silver, in front of the other ten. This is the man who was nearly murdered over a coat his father gave him (37:3-4), handing Rachel's other son a conspicuous multiple of what everyone else receives. Readings differ and the text does not adjudicate: some take it as a final quiet test — will they resent it now? — though the testing is plainly over; some as generosity toward the brother he has just got back; and some, most uncomfortably, as evidence that the family's oldest pattern survives the reconciliation intact, reasserting itself in the very hour of it. Genesis reports the number and moves on. It is not in the habit of protecting its heroes.

"Do not be agitated on the way." Al tirgezu ba-derekh, and the verb ragaz covers a range: to tremble, to be stirred up, to quarrel. So the versions split. The KJV's "see that ye fall not out by the way" takes it as don't argue — and the old Jewish reading behind it is pointed: don't spend the journey assigning blame for who suggested the pit. The NWT's "do not get upset" and the NIV's "don't quarrel" split the same difference; the ESV has "do not quarrel." This translation keeps agitated, which is where the Hebrew actually sits and leaves both readings open — though given what he has just handed Benjamin in front of them, the quarrelling sense is hard to dismiss.

Verses 25–28 · an old man's heart goes numb

"His heart went numb." Vayyafag libbo — the root pug means to grow faint, sluggish, cold; it is used of a limb that has lost feeling. Not joy, and not quite shock: a system refusing to process an input. The KJV and ASV have "Jacob's heart fainted," which is close; the NWT "his heart grew numb," which is closer to the physical metaphor. And the reason is given without decoration: he did not believe them. Twenty-two years ago these same men handed him a bloodied coat and let him conclude the rest. He has no reason to believe anything they bring him.

It is the wagons that do it, not the words. Verse 27 is careful about the order: they told him everything Joseph had said — and he saw the wagons. Egyptian carts, sent to carry him. Testimony did not move him; evidence did. Genesis lets the detail stand without a word of comment, and it is one of the most human sentences in the book.

"The spirit of Jacob their father revived." Vatechi ruach — literally lived, from the same root as the "keep you alive" of verse 7. The man who said he would go down to Sheol mourning (37:35, 42:38, 44:31 — four times in all) comes back to life in a clause.

And the name changes in the last verse. Watch it: verse 25 Jacob their father, verse 27 the spirit of Jacob — and then verse 28, "and Israel said." The narrator has been switching between the two names all through this story, and the switch here lands on the decision. The grieving father is Jacob; the man who says "Enough — let me go and see him" is Israel. Rav, "enough," is blunt to the point of rudeness: stop talking, it is sufficient, I have heard what I needed. He does not ask about the ruling of Egypt or the wagons or the honour. One thing is still alive and he wants to see it before he dies.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the words exact / distinct: "could no longer hold himself in," the same verb as 43:31, so the failure of the restraint is visible as a failure; "to preserve life" / "a remnant" / "a great deliverance" kept as three distinct words (michyah, she'erit, peletah) rather than merged into one idea; "a father to Pharaoh" left as the court title it is; "agitated" for ragaz, holding the quarrel/tremble ambiguity open; "his heart went numb" for the physical sense of pug; and "Enough!" for rav, kept as blunt as the Hebrew.

Echoes paid. The restraint counted at 43:31, failing in the first clause; "there was an interpreter between them" answered by "it is my mouth speaking to you"; "I was stolen away" — the sentence with the criminals left out — replaced by "whom you sold"; turning away to weep and weeping in a locked room, now audible through the palace wall; and the coat that started it, reappearing as five changes of garments handed to Rachel's other son in front of the same men.

Echoes planted. Goshen, the address of the descent, which becomes the address of the slavery and then of the Exodus; the wagons, which persuade Jacob where testimony cannot; "it was not you who sent me here, but God," half of a verdict completed at 50:20; and the name Israel arriving on the decision to go down to Egypt — a journey God will shortly have to promise him is safe (46:3-4).

Next installment in this book: Genesis 46 — Israel packs up everything he owns and goes as far as Beersheba, where he stops and offers sacrifices, and God speaks to him in the visions of the night: "Do not be afraid to go down to Egypt." Then the seventy names, listed one by one.
