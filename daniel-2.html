Nebuchadnezzar's dream of the four-metal statue and the stone cut without hands — the four kingdoms and the everlasting fifth; Daniel reveals both the dream and its meaning.
'The second year of Nebuchadnezzar' (v1), c. 603 BC — early in the Babylonian exile. The Aramaic section of the book (2:4b-7:28) begins here.

Daniel 2 — The Mister Translation

1
וּבִשְׁנַת שְׁתַּיִם, לְמַלְכוּת נְבֻכַדְנֶצַּר, חָלַם נְבֻכַדְנֶצַּר, חֲלֹמוֹת; וַתִּתְפָּעֶם רוּחוֹ, וּשְׁנָתוֹ נִהְיְתָה עָלָיו.
And in the second year of the reign of Nebuchadnezzar, Nebuchadnezzar dreamed dreams, and his spirit was troubled, and his sleep left him.note
Y en el año segundo del reinado de Nabucodonosor, Nabucodonosor tuvo sueños, y su espíritu se turbó, y se le fue el sueño.
2
וַיֹּאמֶר הַמֶּלֶךְ לִקְרֹא לַחַרְטֻמִּים וְלָאַשָּׁפִים, וְלַמְכַשְּׁפִים וְלַכַּשְׂדִּים, לְהַגִּיד לַמֶּלֶךְ, חֲלֹמֹתָיו; וַיָּבֹאוּ, וַיַּעַמְדוּ לִפְנֵי הַמֶּלֶךְ.
And the king commanded to call the magicians, the enchanters, the sorcerers, and the Chaldeans, to tell the king his dreams. And they came and stood before the king.note
Y el rey mandó llamar a los magos, los encantadores, los hechiceros y los caldeos, para que le contaran al rey sus sueños. Y vinieron y se presentaron delante del rey.
3
וַיֹּאמֶר לָהֶם הַמֶּלֶךְ, חֲלוֹם חָלָמְתִּי; וַתִּפָּעֶם רוּחִי, לָדַעַת אֶת-הַחֲלוֹם.
And the king said to them, "I have dreamed a dream, and my spirit is troubled to know the dream."note
Y el rey les dijo: «He tenido un sueño, y mi espíritu se turba por conocer el sueño».
4
וַיְדַבְּרוּ הַכַּשְׂדִּים לַמֶּלֶךְ, אֲרָמִית: מַלְכָּא, לְעָלְמִין חֱיִי--אֱמַר חֶלְמָא לעבדיך לְעַבְדָךְ, וּפִשְׁרָא נְחַוֵּא.
And the Chaldeans spoke to the king in Aramaic: "O king, live forever! Tell your servants the dream, and we will show the interpretation."note
Y los caldeos hablaron al rey en arameo: «¡Oh rey, vive para siempre! Cuenta el sueño a tus siervos, y mostraremos la interpretación».
5
עָנֵה מַלְכָּא וְאָמַר לכשדיא לְכַשְׂדָּאֵי, מִלְּתָה מִנִּי אַזְדָּא: הֵן לָא תְהוֹדְעוּנַּנִי, חֶלְמָא וּפִשְׁרֵהּ, הַדָּמִין תִּתְעַבְדוּן, וּבָתֵּיכוֹן נְוָלִי יִתְּשָׂמוּן.
The king answered and said to the Chaldeans, "The word from me is sure: if you do not make known to me the dream and its interpretation, you shall be cut in pieces, and your houses shall be laid in ruins.note
Respondió el rey y dijo a los caldeos: «La palabra de mí es firme: si no me dan a conocer el sueño y su interpretación, serán descuartizados, y sus casas serán convertidas en ruinas.
6
וְהֵן חֶלְמָא וּפִשְׁרֵהּ, תְּהַחֲוֺן, מַתְּנָן וּנְבִזְבָּה וִיקָר שַׂגִּיא, תְּקַבְּלוּן מִן-קֳדָמָי; לָהֵן, חֶלְמָא וּפִשְׁרֵהּ הַחֲוֺנִי.
But if you show the dream and its interpretation, you shall receive from me gifts and rewards and great honor. Therefore show me the dream and its interpretation."note
Pero si muestran el sueño y su interpretación, recibirán de mí dones y recompensas y gran honra. Por tanto, muéstrenme el sueño y su interpretación».
7
עֲנוֹ תִנְיָנוּת, וְאָמְרִין: מַלְכָּא, חֶלְמָא יֵאמַר לְעַבְדוֹהִי וּפִשְׁרָה נְהַחֲוֵה.
They answered a second time and said, "Let the king tell his servants the dream, and we will show its interpretation."note
Respondieron por segunda vez y dijeron: «Diga el rey el sueño a sus siervos, y mostraremos su interpretación».
8
עָנֵה מַלְכָּא, וְאָמַר--מִן-יַצִּיב יָדַע אֲנָה, דִּי עִדָּנָא אַנְתּוּן זָבְנִין; כָּל-קֳבֵל דִּי חֲזֵיתוֹן, דִּי אַזְדָּא מִנִּי מִלְּתָא.
The king answered and said, "I know for certain that you are buying time, because you see that the word from me is sure —note
Respondió el rey y dijo: «Con certeza sé que ustedes ganan tiempo, porque ven que la palabra de mí es firme —
9
דִּי הֵן-חֶלְמָא לָא תְהוֹדְעֻנַּנִי חֲדָה-הִיא דָתְכוֹן, וּמִלָּה כִדְבָה וּשְׁחִיתָה הזמנתון הִזְדְּמִנְתּוּן לְמֵאמַר קָדָמַי, עַד דִּי עִדָּנָא, יִשְׁתַּנֵּא; לָהֵן, חֶלְמָא אֱמַרוּ לִי, וְאִנְדַּע, דִּי פִשְׁרֵהּ תְּהַחֲוֻנַּנִי.
that if you do not make known to me the dream, there is one sentence for you. You have agreed to speak lying and corrupt words before me until the time changes. Therefore tell me the dream, and I shall know that you can show me its interpretation."note
que si no me dan a conocer el sueño, una sola sentencia hay para ustedes. Se han puesto de acuerdo para hablar delante de mí palabras mentirosas y corruptas, hasta que cambie el tiempo. Por tanto, díganme el sueño, y sabré que pueden mostrarme su interpretación».
10
עֲנוֹ כשדיא כַשְׂדָּאֵי קֳדָם-מַלְכָּא, וְאָמְרִין--לָא-אִיתַי אֱנָשׁ עַל-יַבֶּשְׁתָּא, דִּי מִלַּת מַלְכָּא יוּכַל לְהַחֲוָיָה: כָּל-קֳבֵל, דִּי כָּל-מֶלֶךְ רַב וְשַׁלִּיט, מִלָּה כִדְנָה לָא שְׁאֵל, לְכָל-חַרְטֹם וְאָשַׁף וְכַשְׂדָּי.
The Chaldeans answered before the king and said, "There is no one on earth who can show the king’s matter — for no great and mighty king has ever asked such a thing of any magician or enchanter or Chaldean.note
Respondieron los caldeos delante del rey y dijeron: «No hay hombre sobre la tierra que pueda mostrar el asunto del rey — pues ningún rey, por grande y poderoso que fuera, ha pedido jamás cosa semejante a mago, encantador o caldeo alguno.
11
וּמִלְּתָא דִי-מַלְכָּה שָׁאֵל, יַקִּירָה, וְאָחֳרָן לָא אִיתַי, דִּי יְחַוִּנַּהּ קֳדָם מַלְכָּא; לָהֵן אֱלָהִין--דִּי מְדָרְהוֹן, עִם-בִּשְׂרָא לָא אִיתוֹהִי.
The thing the king asks is difficult, and there is no other who can show it before the king except the gods, whose dwelling is not with flesh."note
La cosa que el rey pide es difícil, y no hay otro que pueda mostrarla delante del rey sino los dioses, cuya morada no está con la carne».
12
כָּל-קֳבֵל דְּנָה--מַלְכָּא, בְּנַס וּקְצַף שַׂגִּיא; וַאֲמַר, לְהוֹבָדָה, לְכֹל, חַכִּימֵי בָבֶל.
Because of this the king was angry and greatly enraged, and commanded to destroy all the wise men of Babylon.note
Por esto el rey se airó y se enfureció en gran manera, y mandó destruir a todos los sabios de Babilonia.
13
וְדָתָא נֶפְקַת, וְחַכִּימַיָּא מִתְקַטְּלִין; וּבְעוֹ דָּנִיֵּאל וְחַבְרוֹהִי, לְהִתְקְטָלָה. {פ}
So the decree went out, and the wise men were to be killed; and they sought Daniel and his companions, to kill them.note
Y salió el decreto, y los sabios habían de ser muertos; y buscaron a Daniel y a sus compañeros para matarlos.
14
בֵּאדַיִן דָּנִיֵּאל, הֲתִיב עֵטָא וּטְעֵם, לְאַרְיוֹךְ, רַב-טַבָּחַיָּא דִּי מַלְכָּא--דִּי נְפַק לְקַטָּלָה, לְחַכִּימֵי בָּבֶל.
Then Daniel answered with prudence and discretion to Arioch, the chief of the king’s guard, who had gone out to kill the wise men of Babylon.note
Entonces Daniel respondió con prudencia y discreción a Arioc, jefe de la guardia del rey, que había salido para matar a los sabios de Babilonia.
15
עָנֵה וְאָמַר, לְאַרְיוֹךְ שַׁלִּיטָא דִי-מַלְכָּא, עַל-מָה דָתָא מְהַחְצְפָה, מִן-קֳדָם מַלְכָּא; אֱדַיִן מִלְּתָא, הוֹדַע אַרְיוֹךְ לְדָנִיֵּאל.
He answered and said to Arioch the king’s captain, "Why is the decree so harsh from the king?" Then Arioch made the matter known to Daniel.note
Habló y dijo a Arioc, capitán del rey: «¿Por qué es tan riguroso el decreto de parte del rey?» Entonces Arioc dio a conocer el asunto a Daniel.
16
וְדָנִיֵּאל, עַל וּבְעָה מִן-מַלְכָּא: דִּי זְמָן יִנְתִּן-לֵהּ, וּפִשְׁרָא לְהַחֲוָיָה לְמַלְכָּא. {פ}
And Daniel went in and asked the king to give him time, that he might show the king the interpretation.note
Y Daniel entró y pidió al rey que le diera tiempo, para mostrar al rey la interpretación.
17
אֱדַיִן דָּנִיֵּאל, לְבַיְתֵהּ אֲזַל; וְלַחֲנַנְיָה מִישָׁאֵל וַעֲזַרְיָה חַבְרוֹהִי, מִלְּתָא הוֹדַע.
Then Daniel went to his house and made the matter known to Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah, his companions,note
Entonces Daniel fue a su casa y dio a conocer el asunto a Ananías, Misael y Azarías, sus compañeros,
18
וְרַחֲמִין, לְמִבְעֵא מִן-קֳדָם אֱלָהּ שְׁמַיָּא, עַל-רָזָא, דְּנָה--דִּי לָא יְהוֹבְדוּן דָּנִיֵּאל וְחַבְרוֹהִי, עִם-שְׁאָר חַכִּימֵי בָבֶל.
that they might seek mercy from the God of heaven concerning this mystery, so that Daniel and his companions might not perish with the rest of the wise men of Babylon.note
para que pidieran misericordia del Dios del cielo acerca de este misterio, a fin de que Daniel y sus compañeros no perecieran con el resto de los sabios de Babilonia.
19
אֱדַיִן, לְדָנִיֵּאל בְּחֶזְוָא דִי-לֵילְיָא--רָזָא גְלִי; אֱדַיִן, דָּנִיֵּאל, בָּרִךְ, לֶאֱלָהּ שְׁמַיָּא.
Then the mystery was revealed to Daniel in a vision of the night; then Daniel blessed the God of heaven.note
Entonces el misterio fue revelado a Daniel en una visión de la noche; entonces Daniel bendijo al Dios del cielo.
20
עָנֵה דָנִיֵּאל, וְאָמַר--לֶהֱוֵא שְׁמֵהּ דִּי-אֱלָהָא מְבָרַךְ, מִן-עָלְמָא וְעַד עָלְמָא: דִּי חָכְמְתָא וּגְבוּרְתָא, דִּי לֵהּ-הִיא.
Daniel answered and said: "Blessed be the name of God from age to age, for wisdom and might are his.note
Daniel respondió y dijo: «Bendito sea el nombre de Dios de siglo en siglo, porque suyas son la sabiduría y el poder.
21
וְהוּא מְהַשְׁנֵא עִדָּנַיָּא, וְזִמְנַיָּא, מְהַעְדֵּה מַלְכִין, וּמְהָקֵים מַלְכִין; יָהֵב חָכְמְתָא לְחַכִּימִין, וּמַנְדְּעָא לְיָדְעֵי בִינָה.
And he changes the times and the seasons; he removes kings and sets up kings; he gives wisdom to the wise and knowledge to those who know understanding.note
Y él cambia los tiempos y las sazones; quita reyes y pone reyes; da sabiduría a los sabios y conocimiento a los que saben entender.

22
הוּא גָּלֵא עַמִּיקָתָא, וּמְסַתְּרָתָא; יָדַע מָה בַחֲשׁוֹכָא, ונהירא וּנְהוֹרָא עִמֵּהּ שְׁרֵא.
He reveals the deep and the hidden things; he knows what is in the darkness, and the light dwells with him.note
Él revela lo profundo y lo oculto; conoce lo que está en las tinieblas, y con él mora la luz.
23
לָךְ אֱלָהּ אֲבָהָתִי, מְהוֹדֵא וּמְשַׁבַּח אֲנָה, דִּי חָכְמְתָא וּגְבוּרְתָא, יְהַבְתְּ לִי; וּכְעַן הוֹדַעְתַּנִי דִּי-בְעֵינָא מִנָּךְ, דִּי-מִלַּת מַלְכָּא הוֹדַעְתֶּנָא.
To you, God of my fathers, I give thanks and praise, for you have given me wisdom and might, and have now made known to me what we asked of you — for you have made known to us the king’s matter."note
A ti, Dios de mis padres, te doy gracias y te alabo, porque me has dado sabiduría y poder, y ahora me has dado a conocer lo que te pedimos — porque nos has dado a conocer el asunto del rey».
24
כָּל-קֳבֵל דְּנָה, דָּנִיֵּאל עַל עַל-אַרְיוֹךְ, דִּי מַנִּי מַלְכָּא, לְהוֹבָדָה לְחַכִּימֵי בָבֶל; אֲזַל וְכֵן אֲמַר-לֵהּ, לְחַכִּימֵי בָבֶל אַל-תְּהוֹבֵד--הַעֵלְנִי קֳדָם מַלְכָּא, וּפִשְׁרָא לְמַלְכָּא אֲחַוֵּא. {ס}
Therefore Daniel went in to Arioch, whom the king had appointed to destroy the wise men of Babylon; he went and said to him, "Do not destroy the wise men of Babylon. Bring me in before the king, and I will show the king the interpretation."note
Por tanto, Daniel entró a Arioc, a quien el rey había puesto para destruir a los sabios de Babilonia; fue y le dijo así: «No destruyas a los sabios de Babilonia. Llévame delante del rey, y mostraré al rey la interpretación».
25
אֱדַיִן אַרְיוֹךְ בְּהִתְבְּהָלָה, הַנְעֵל לְדָנִיֵּאל קֳדָם מַלְכָּא; וְכֵן אֲמַר-לֵהּ, דִּי-הַשְׁכַּחַת גְּבַר מִן-בְּנֵי גָלוּתָא דִּי יְהוּד, דִּי פִשְׁרָא, לְמַלְכָּא יְהוֹדַע.
Then Arioch quickly brought Daniel in before the king, and said to him, "I have found a man among the exiles of Judah who will make known to the king the interpretation."note
Entonces Arioc llevó de prisa a Daniel delante del rey, y le dijo así: «He hallado un hombre de los cautivos de Judá que dará a conocer al rey la interpretación».
26
עָנֵה מַלְכָּא וְאָמַר לְדָנִיֵּאל, דִּי שְׁמֵהּ בֵּלְטְשַׁאצַּר: האיתיך הַאִיתָךְ כָּהֵל, לְהוֹדָעֻתַנִי חֶלְמָא דִי-חֲזֵית--וּפִשְׁרֵהּ.
The king answered and said to Daniel, whose name was Belteshazzar, "Are you able to make known to me the dream that I have seen, and its interpretation?"note
Respondió el rey y dijo a Daniel, cuyo nombre era Beltsasar: «¿Puedes darme a conocer el sueño que he visto, y su interpretación?»
27
עָנֵה דָנִיֵּאל קֳדָם מַלְכָּא, וְאָמַר: רָזָא, דִּי-מַלְכָּא שָׁאֵל--לָא חַכִּימִין אָשְׁפִין חַרְטֻמִּין גָּזְרִין, יָכְלִין לְהַחֲוָיָה לְמַלְכָּא.
Daniel answered before the king and said, "The mystery that the king asks, no wise men, enchanters, magicians, or diviners can show to the king.note

Translator’s Notes

Verses 1–13 · an impossible test — and a change of language

⚠ The language changes here. From the middle of verse 4 — “the Chaldeans spoke to the king in Aramaic” — through the end of chapter 7, Daniel is written not in Hebrew but in ARAMAIC, the international language of the Babylonian and Persian empires. The switch is deliberate and structural: the Aramaic chapters (2–7) concern the Gentile world-empires and the kings who rule them, told in the tongue those empires spoke; the Hebrew chapters (1, 8–12) turn more to Israel’s own hope. The seam falls, famously, mid-sentence in verse 4.

The impossible test. Nebuchadnezzar does not merely ask his wise men to interpret a dream — he demands they first tell him the dream itself, which he will not disclose. It is a test built to expose fraud: any charlatan can spin a meaning for a dream told to him, but only true revelation can recover a dream unheard. “The word from me is sure” (v5) — the decree is fixed; fail, and be torn limb from limb. KJV reads the difficult phrase “the thing is gone from me,” i.e. the king has forgotten the dream; most modern versions read it as “my decision is firm.”

«Except the gods, whose dwelling is not with flesh.» The wise men’s honest protest (v11) is the hinge of the chapter: no one can do this except the gods — and the gods are remote, unreachable, they do not dwell with mortals. The chapter is about to answer them: there is a God in heaven who reveals mysteries, and who speaks to a man. (“The Chaldeans” here names not an ethnic group but a professional guild — the elite Babylonian astrologer-diviners; the demand for the dream’s interpretation is pishra, the interpreting word that runs through the whole book.)

Verses 14–18 · prudence, and a prayer for mercy

Daniel answers the executioner “with prudence and discretion” — not with panic. Arioch, captain of the guard, is already going house to house; Daniel asks the king for time (the very thing the king refused the others, and grants him), and goes home to his three friends — Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah, whom the reader knows better by their Babylonian names, Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego (1:7). The deliverance of the whole chapter turns on four young exiles praying in a house.

«This mystery.» Raz — an Aramaic word, borrowed from Persian, for a secret or mystery; it rings through the chapter eight times, and God is its “revealer” (v28). The word travels: the Greek Bible, and then the New Testament, render it mysterion — Paul’s “mystery” of the gospel, once hidden and now revealed (Romans 16:25; Ephesians 3), is Daniel’s word.

Verses 19–23 · the God who reveals — Daniel’s song

The mystery is revealed “in a vision of the night,” and Daniel’s first act is not to run to the king but to bless God. His doxology (vv20–23) is one of the great hymns of the Old Testament and the theological heart of the whole book: “he changes the times and the seasons; he removes kings and sets up kings.” This is Daniel’s thesis — that the God of a conquered, exiled people is the one who raises and topples the very empires that conquered them. The kings think they rule history; God is quietly turning it. “He reveals the deep and the hidden things… and the light dwells with him.” KJV “he revealeth the deep and secret things… and the light dwelleth with him.”

Verses 24–30 · not because of any wisdom in me

Brought before the king, Daniel refuses the credit before it is offered. “No wise men… can show this to the king, but there is a God in heaven who reveals mysteries.” The contrast with verse 11 is exact: the wise men said only the gods could do this, and the gods do not dwell with flesh; Daniel says the God of heaven has done it — through a man. He is careful that the wonder point past himself: “not because of any wisdom that I have more than any living.”

«What will be in the latter days.» Acharit yomayya — the days to come, the end of days. The dream is not merely about Nebuchadnezzar’s reign but about the shape of history toward its close. And note the king calls Daniel “Belteshazzar” (1:7) — the name Babylon gave him, carrying the name of a Babylonian god; the reader keeps the name his parents gave, which carries the true God’s: Daniel, “God is my judge.”

Verses 31–35 · the statue, and the stone

The dream. A colossal statue of four metals, descending in value from a head of fine gold to feet of iron mixed with clay — and then a stone, “cut out, not by hands,” that strikes the feet and shatters the whole image into chaff, while the stone itself grows into “a great mountain that filled the whole earth.” Two things are said before a word of interpretation: the empires of the earth are a single top-heavy image, magnificent and brittle; and what ends them is not another empire but a stone no human hand quarried — and it does not merely replace them, it grows to fill the world.

Verses 36–45 · the four kingdoms, and the everlasting one

«You are the head of gold.» Nebuchadnezzar, and Babylon, is named outright (v38); the other three kingdoms (malku) are left unnamed, and there the great interpretive divide begins. ⚠ Two main readings, each with a long pedigree, and the note takes no vote:

• The TRADITIONAL reading (held since antiquity and by most conservative interpreters): gold = Babylon, silver = Medo-Persia, bronze = Greece, iron = ROME — the fourth kingdom that broke all others, its iron-and-clay feet Rome’s later divided weakness, and the stone the kingdom of God breaking in “in the days of those kings,” i.e. in the days of Rome.

• The CRITICAL reading (the majority of modern scholarship): gold = Babylon, silver = MEDIA, bronze = PERSIA, iron = GREECE (Alexander and his successors), the iron-and-clay feet the Seleucid and Ptolemaic kingdoms that “mixed by marriage but did not hold together” (v43), and the vision written down in the 2nd century BC, in the crisis under Antiochus IV. This reading turns on the dating of the book (see the intro).

⚠ The two readings largely track the DATING of Daniel — a sixth-century Daniel writing genuine long-range prophecy, or a second-century author reviewing history as prophecy down to his own day. What both agree on is the climax: a fifth kingdom, “not made by hands,” set up by “the God of heaven,” that “shall never be destroyed” and “shall stand forever.” Whatever the four metals are, the stone is the point. The New Testament hears it fulfilled in Christ: the “stone the builders rejected” that falls and grinds to powder, set right after a parable of a kingdom “given to another” (Matthew 21:42–44), and the kingdom of God that starts small and grows like a mountain. KJV “a stone was cut out without hands”; the ESV “by no human hand.”

Verses 46–49 · a pagan king on his face

Nebuchadnezzar falls on his face before Daniel and confesses, “Truly your God is the God of gods and the Lord of kings, and a revealer of mysteries.” ⚠ It is a real acknowledgment, and it is not conversion: he says “your God is God of gods” — the highest among gods, not the only one — and by the very next chapter he has built a golden image ninety feet high and demanded it be worshiped. The Gentile kings of Daniel keep confessing the true God in the moment of wonder and forgetting him at the next test.

And Daniel does not forget his friends. Exalted to rule the province, his first request is that Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego be given office too — which sets the stage for chapter 3, where they, and not Daniel, will stand before the furnace.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the exact / distinct words: the Aramaic of 2:4b onward left as what it is (the empire chapters in the empire’s tongue); “mystery” for raz, the theme-word that becomes the New Testament’s mysterion; “interpretation” for pishra; “kingdom” for malku, kept the same across the four earthly ones and the everlasting fifth; “cut out, not by hands” for the stone, kept literal; and “the latter days” for acharit yomayya.

Echoes paid. The gods “whose dwelling is not with flesh” (v11) answered by “a God in heaven who reveals mysteries” (v28); Daniel’s Babylonian name Belteshazzar and his three friends’ renaming (ch 1); and the doxology’s “he removes kings and sets up kings,” the book’s thesis, played out in detail through the kings of Persia and Greece (ch 11).

Echoes planted. The four kingdoms and the stone-become-mountain, which return as the four beasts and the “one like a son of man” given everlasting dominion (ch 7); the everlasting kingdom that “shall stand forever,” the hope the whole book leans toward; and the golden image, which the king who just bowed to God will raise ninety feet high in chapter 3, sending Daniel’s three friends into the furnace.

This library already carries Daniel 1 (the court education), 11 (the kings of north and south), and 12 (the resurrection and the end). Next in sequence: Daniel 3 — the golden image, the fiery furnace, and the fourth figure in the flames, “like a son of the gods.”
