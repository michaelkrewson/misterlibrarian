Genesis 19 — The MiSTeR Translation

1
וַיָּבֹאוּ שְׁנֵי הַמַּלְאָכִים סְדֹמָה, בָּעֶרֶב, וְלוֹט, יֹשֵׁב בְּשַׁעַר-סְדֹם; וַיַּרְא-לוֹט וַיָּקָם לִקְרָאתָם, וַיִּשְׁתַּחוּ אַפַּיִם אָרְצָה.
And the two angels came to Sodom in the evening, as Lot was sitting in the gate of Sodom. When Lot saw them, he rose to meet them and bowed with his face to the ground,note
2
וַיֹּאמֶר הִנֶּה נָּא-אֲדֹנַי, סוּרוּ נָא אֶל-בֵּית עַבְדְּכֶם וְלִינוּ וְרַחֲצוּ רַגְלֵיכֶם, וְהִשְׁכַּמְתֶּם, וַהֲלַכְתֶּם לְדַרְכְּכֶם; וַיֹּאמְרוּ לֹּא, כִּי בָרְחוֹב נָלִין.
and said, "Look now, my lords — turn aside, please, to your servant's house; spend the night, wash your feet, and rise early and go on your way." And they said, "No — we will spend the night in the square."note
3
וַיִּפְצַר-בָּם מְאֹד--וַיָּסֻרוּ אֵלָיו, וַיָּבֹאוּ אֶל-בֵּיתוֹ; וַיַּעַשׂ לָהֶם מִשְׁתֶּה, וּמַצּוֹת אָפָה וַיֹּאכֵלוּ.
But he pressed them hard, and they turned aside to him and came into his house; and he made them a feast, and baked unleavened bread, and they ate.note

טֶרֶם, יִשְׁכָּבוּ, וְאַנְשֵׁי הָעִיר אַנְשֵׁי סְדֹם נָסַבּוּ עַל-הַבַּיִת, מִנַּעַר וְעַד-זָקֵן: כָּל-הָעָם, מִקָּצֶה.
Before they lay down, the men of the city — the men of Sodom — surrounded the house, young and old, all the people, from every quarter,note
5
וַיִּקְרְאוּ אֶל-לוֹט וַיֹּאמְרוּ לוֹ, אַיֵּה הָאֲנָשִׁים אֲשֶׁר-בָּאוּ אֵלֶיךָ הַלָּיְלָה; הוֹצִיאֵם אֵלֵינוּ, וְנֵדְעָה אֹתָם.
And they called to Lot: "Where are the men who came to you tonight? Bring them out to us, that we may know them."note

וַיֵּצֵא אֲלֵהֶם לוֹט, הַפֶּתְחָה; וְהַדֶּלֶת, סָגַר אַחֲרָיו.
And Lot went out to them at the doorway, and shut the door behind him,note
7
וַיֹּאמַר: אַל-נָא אַחַי, תָּרֵעוּ.
and said, "Please, my brothers — do not do this evil.note
8
הִנֵּה-נָא לִי שְׁתֵּי בָנוֹת, אֲשֶׁר לֹא-יָדְעוּ אִישׁ--אוֹצִיאָה-נָּא אֶתְהֶן אֲלֵיכֶם, וַעֲשׂוּ לָהֶן כַּטּוֹב בְּעֵינֵיכֶם; רַק לָאֲנָשִׁים הָאֵל, אַל-תַּעֲשׂוּ דָבָר, כִּי-עַל-כֵּן בָּאוּ, בְּצֵל קֹרָתִי.
Look now — I have two daughters who have not known a man. Let me bring them out to you, and do to them what is good in your eyes; only do nothing to these men, for they have come under the shadow of my roof."note
9
וַיֹּאמְרוּ גֶּשׁ-הָלְאָה, וַיֹּאמְרוּ הָאֶחָד בָּא-לָגוּר וַיִּשְׁפֹּט שָׁפוֹט--עַתָּה, נָרַע לְךָ מֵהֶם; וַיִּפְצְרוּ בָאִישׁ בְּלוֹט מְאֹד, וַיִּגְּשׁוּ לִשְׁבֹּר הַדָּלֶת.
But they said, "Stand back!" And they said, "This one came here to sojourn, and now he keeps playing the judge! Now we will deal worse with you than with them." And they pressed hard against the man, against Lot, and moved in to break down the door.note
cross-refs⤷ 18:25
10
וַיִּשְׁלְחוּ הָאֲנָשִׁים אֶת-יָדָם, וַיָּבִיאוּ אֶת-לוֹט אֲלֵיהֶם הַבָּיְתָה; וְאֶת-הַדֶּלֶת, סָגָרוּ.
But the men reached out their hands and pulled Lot in to them, into the house, and shut the door;note
11
וְאֶת-הָאֲנָשִׁים אֲשֶׁר-פֶּתַח הַבַּיִת, הִכּוּ בַּסַּנְוֵרִים, מִקָּטֹן, וְעַד-גָּדוֹל; וַיִּלְאוּ, לִמְצֹא הַפָּתַח.
and the men at the doorway of the house they struck with blindness, small and great alike, so that they wore themselves out groping for the doorway.note
12
וַיֹּאמְרוּ הָאֲנָשִׁים אֶל-לוֹט, עֹד מִי-לְךָ פֹה--חָתָן וּבָנֶיךָ וּבְנֹתֶיךָ, וְכֹל אֲשֶׁר-לְךָ בָּעִיר: הוֹצֵא, מִן-הַמָּקוֹם.
And the men said to Lot, "Who else do you have here? A son-in-law, your sons, your daughters — whomever you have in the city — bring them out of this place.note
13
כִּי-מַשְׁחִתִים אֲנַחְנוּ, אֶת-הַמָּקוֹם הַזֶּה: כִּי-גָדְלָה צַעֲקָתָם אֶת-פְּנֵי יְהוָה, וַיְשַׁלְּחֵנוּ יְהוָה לְשַׁחֲתָהּ.
For we are about to ruin this place, for the outcry against them has grown great before Jehovah, and Jehovah has sent us to ruin it."note
14
וַיֵּצֵא לוֹט וַיְדַבֵּר אֶל-חֲתָנָיו לֹקְחֵי בְנֹתָיו, וַיֹּאמֶר קוּמוּ צְּאוּ מִן-הַמָּקוֹם הַזֶּה, כִּי-מַשְׁחִית יְהוָה, אֶת-הָעִיר; וַיְהִי כִמְצַחֵק, בְּעֵינֵי חֲתָנָיו.
So Lot went out and spoke to his sons-in-law, who were to marry his daughters: "Up — get out of this place, for Jehovah is about to ruin the city!" But in his sons-in-law's eyes, he seemed like one who was joking.note
cross-refs⤷ 17:17
15
וּכְמוֹ הַשַּׁחַר עָלָה, וַיָּאִיצוּ הַמַּלְאָכִים בְּלוֹט לֵאמֹר: קוּם קַח אֶת-אִשְׁתְּךָ וְאֶת-שְׁתֵּי בְנֹתֶיךָ, הַנִּמְצָאֹת--פֶּן-תִּסָּפֶה, בַּעֲוֺן הָעִיר.
As dawn was breaking, the angels urged Lot on: "Up — take your wife and your two daughters who are here, or you will be swept away in the punishment of the city."note
16
וַיִּתְמַהְמָהּ--וַיַּחֲזִיקוּ הָאֲנָשִׁים בְּיָדוֹ וּבְיַד-אִשְׁתּוֹ וּבְיַד שְׁתֵּי בְנֹתָיו, בְּחֶמְלַת יְהוָה עָלָיו; וַיֹּצִאֻהוּ וַיַּנִּחֻהוּ, מִחוּץ לָעִיר.
But he lingered — and the men seized his hand, and his wife's hand, and the hands of his two daughters, in the compassion of Jehovah on him; and they brought him out and set him down outside the city.note
17
וַיְהִי כְהוֹצִיאָם אֹתָם הַחוּצָה, וַיֹּאמֶר הִמָּלֵט עַל-נַפְשֶׁךָ--אַל-תַּבִּיט אַחֲרֶיךָ, וְאַל-תַּעֲמֹד בְּכָל-הַכִּכָּר: הָהָרָה הִמָּלֵט, פֶּן-תִּסָּפֶה.
And when they had brought them outside, he said, "Escape for your life! Do not look behind you, and do not stop anywhere in the plain. Escape to the hills, or you will be swept away."note
18
וַיֹּאמֶר לוֹט, אֲלֵהֶם: אַל-נָא, אֲדֹנָי.
And Lot said to them, "Oh no, my Lord —note
19
הִנֵּה-נָא מָצָא עַבְדְּךָ חֵן, בְּעֵינֶיךָ, וַתַּגְדֵּל חַסְדְּךָ אֲשֶׁר עָשִׂיתָ עִמָּדִי, לְהַחֲיוֹת אֶת-נַפְשִׁי; וְאָנֹכִי, לֹא אוּכַל לְהִמָּלֵט הָהָרָה--פֶּן-תִּדְבָּקַנִי הָרָעָה, וָמַתִּי.
Look now — your servant has found favor in your eyes, and great is the kindness you have shown me in keeping my life alive. But I — I cannot escape to the hills; the disaster will overtake me, and I will die.note
20
הִנֵּה-נָא הָעִיר הַזֹּאת קְרֹבָה, לָנוּס שָׁמָּה--וְהִוא מִצְעָר; אִמָּלְטָה נָּא שָׁמָּה, הֲלֹא מִצְעָר הִוא--וּתְחִי נַפְשִׁי.
Look — this town is near enough to flee to, and it is a little one. Let me escape there — is it not a little one? — and my life will be saved."note
21
וַיֹּאמֶר אֵלָיו--הִנֵּה נָשָׂאתִי פָנֶיךָ, גַּם לַדָּבָר הַזֶּה: לְבִלְתִּי הָפְכִּי אֶת-הָעִיר, אֲשֶׁר דִּבַּרְתָּ.
And he said to him, "Look — I have granted you this favor too: I will not overthrow the town of which you have spoken.note
22
מַהֵר, הִמָּלֵט שָׁמָּה, כִּי לֹא אוּכַל לַעֲשׂוֹת דָּבָר, עַד-בֹּאֲךָ שָׁמָּה; עַל-כֵּן קָרָא שֵׁם-הָעִיר, צוֹעַר.
Hurry — escape there, for I can do nothing until you arrive there." Therefore the name of the town was called Zoar.note
23
הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ, יָצָא עַל-הָאָרֶץ; וְלוֹט, בָּא צֹעֲרָה.
The sun had risen over the earth when Lot came to Zoar.note
24
וַיהוָה, הִמְטִיר עַל-סְדֹם וְעַל-עֲמֹרָה--גָּפְרִית וָאֵשׁ: מֵאֵת יְהוָה, מִן-הַשָּׁמָיִם.
And Jehovah rained on Sodom and on Gomorrah sulfur and fire — from Jehovah, out of the heavens —note
25
וַיַּהֲפֹךְ אֶת-הֶעָרִים הָאֵל, וְאֵת כָּל-הַכִּכָּר, וְאֵת כָּל-יֹשְׁבֵי הֶעָרִים, וְצֶמַח הָאֲדָמָה.
and he overthrew those cities, and all the plain, and all the inhabitants of the cities, and what grew from the ground.note

26
וַתַּבֵּט אִשְׁתּוֹ, מֵאַחֲרָיו; וַתְּהִי, נְצִיב מֶלַח.
But his wife looked back from behind him — and she became a pillar of salt.note
27
וַיַּשְׁכֵּם אַבְרָהָם, בַּבֹּקֶר: אֶל-הַמָּקוֹם--אֲשֶׁר-עָמַד שָׁם, אֶת-פְּנֵי יְהוָה.
And Abraham rose early in the morning and went to the place where he had stood before Jehovah,note

28
וַיַּשְׁקֵף, עַל-פְּנֵי סְדֹם וַעֲמֹרָה, וְעַל-כָּל-פְּנֵי, אֶרֶץ הַכִּכָּר; וַיַּרְא, וְהִנֵּה עָלָה קִיטֹר הָאָרֶץ, כְּקִיטֹר, הַכִּבְשָׁן.
and he looked down over the face of Sodom and Gomorrah, and over the whole face of the land of the plain — and he saw, and look: the smoke of the land went up like the smoke of a kiln.note
29
וַיְהִי, בְּשַׁחֵת אֱלֹהִים אֶת-עָרֵי הַכִּכָּר, וַיִּזְכֹּר אֱלֹהִים, אֶת-אַבְרָהָם; וַיְשַׁלַּח אֶת-לוֹט, מִתּוֹךְ הַהֲפֵכָה, בַּהֲפֹךְ אֶת-הֶעָרִים, אֲשֶׁר-יָשַׁב בָּהֵן לוֹט.
And so it was, when God ruined the cities of the plain, that God remembered Abraham — and sent Lot out of the midst of the overthrow, when he overthrew the cities where Lot had lived.note

30
וַיַּעַל לוֹט מִצּוֹעַר וַיֵּשֶׁב בָּהָר, וּשְׁתֵּי בְנֹתָיו עִמּוֹ, כִּי יָרֵא, לָשֶׁבֶת בְּצוֹעַר; וַיֵּשֶׁב, בַּמְּעָרָה--הוּא, וּשְׁתֵּי בְנֹתָיו.
And Lot went up from Zoar and lived in the hills, his two daughters with him, for he was afraid to stay in Zoar; and he lived in a cave, he and his two daughters.note
31
וַתֹּאמֶר הַבְּכִירָה אֶל-הַצְּעִירָה, אָבִינוּ זָקֵן; וְאִישׁ אֵין בָּאָרֶץ לָבוֹא עָלֵינוּ, כְּדֶרֶךְ כָּל-הָאָרֶץ.
And the firstborn said to the younger, "Our father is old, and there is no man on earth to come in to us, after the way of all the earth.note
32
לְכָה נַשְׁקֶה אֶת-אָבִינוּ יַיִן, וְנִשְׁכְּבָה עִמּוֹ; וּנְחַיֶּה מֵאָבִינוּ, זָרַע.
Come — let us give our father wine to drink, and lie with him, and keep offspring alive from our father."note
33
וַתַּשְׁקֶיןָ אֶת-אֲבִיהֶן יַיִן, בַּלַּיְלָה הוּא; וַתָּבֹא הַבְּכִירָה וַתִּשְׁכַּב אֶת-אָבִיהָ, וְלֹא-יָדַע בְּשִׁכְבָהּ וּבְקוּמָהּ.
So they gave their father wine to drink that night, and the firstborn went in and lay with her father; and he knew nothing of her lying down or of her rising.note
34
וַיְהִי, מִמָּחֳרָת, וַתֹּאמֶר הַבְּכִירָה אֶל-הַצְּעִירָה, הֵן-שָׁכַבְתִּי אֶמֶשׁ אֶת-אָבִי; נַשְׁקֶנּוּ יַיִן גַּם-הַלַּיְלָה, וּבֹאִי שִׁכְבִי עִמּוֹ, וּנְחַיֶּה מֵאָבִינוּ, זָרַע.
And the next day the firstborn said to the younger, "Look — last night I lay with my father. Let us give him wine to drink tonight also; then go in and lie with him, and we will keep offspring alive from our father."note
35
וַתַּשְׁקֶיןָ גַּם בַּלַּיְלָה הַהוּא, אֶת-אֲבִיהֶן--יָיִן; וַתָּקָם הַצְּעִירָה וַתִּשְׁכַּב עִמּוֹ, וְלֹא-יָדַע בְּשִׁכְבָהּ וּבְקֻמָהּ.
So they gave their father wine to drink that night also, and the younger rose and lay with him; and he knew nothing of her lying down or of her rising.note
36
וַתַּהֲרֶיןָ שְׁתֵּי בְנוֹת-לוֹט, מֵאֲבִיהֶן.
And both of Lot's daughters became pregnant by their father.note
37
וַתֵּלֶד הַבְּכִירָה בֵּן, וַתִּקְרָא שְׁמוֹ מוֹאָב: הוּא אֲבִי-מוֹאָב, עַד-הַיּוֹם.
And the firstborn bore a son and called his name Moab; he is the father of Moab to this day.note
38
וְהַצְּעִירָה גַם-הִוא יָלְדָה בֵּן, וַתִּקְרָא שְׁמוֹ בֶּן-עַמִּי: הוּא אֲבִי בְנֵי-עַמּוֹן, עַד-הַיּוֹם. {ס}

Each note explains this translation's choice and compares the seven versions on the shelf. Quotations from copyrighted versions (NIV, TLB, NWT) are kept to short phrases for comparison; KJV, Geneva, Douay-Rheims and ASV are public domain.

Verses 1–3 · שְׁנֵי הַמַּלְאָכִים shenei hamal'akhim

"The two angels." Chapter 18's arithmetic quietly resolves: three visitors came to Mamre; two arrive at Sodom — the third, on the oldest reading, was Jehovah, who stayed behind in the road to hear Abraham's argument (18:22) and "went his way" when it ended. Lot is "sitting in the gate of Sodom" — the gate is the ancient city's forum and courtroom, where elders sit and cases are heard: the man who pitched his tent toward Sodom (13:12) and was living in it by chapter 14 now has a seat in its civic life. The drift is complete.

The mirror. Lot's welcome is written deliberately against his uncle's, one chapter back: he sees, rises, bows, presses, feasts — a good host, genuinely. But every detail is a shadow-version: Mamre's blazing noon becomes Sodom's evening; the tent becomes a city house; and where Sarah baked rich cakes, Lot bakes unleavened bread (matzot) — the bread of haste, and its first appearance in the Bible: the same flat loaves will mark the other great rescue-by-night, out of Egypt (Exodus 12). "We will spend the night in the square," the angels first answer — in Sodom, an open threat the reader doesn't yet understand; Lot does, and presses them hard.

Verses 4–11 · וְנֵדְעָה אֹתָם venede'ah otam

"That we may know them." The verb is yada — Hebrew's own word for intimacy ("the man knew his wife," 4:1), and the very word of Abraham's election two verses back across the chapter seam: "I have known him" (18:19). One verb, covenant and violation. The demand is the sexual assault of guests, and the text makes the city's guilt total on purpose: "young and old, all the people, from every quarter" — the inspection of 18:21 has arrived, and there are not ten. The shelf splits over the verb by translation philosophy: KJV, Geneva and ASV keep it — "that we may know them" — while NIV spells it out ("so that we can have sex with them") and the NWT likewise ("have intercourse with them"). This translation keeps "know," not from delicacy but for the echo: spell the verb away, and the terrible rhyme with "I have known him" goes with it.

Lot's terrible bargain. "I have two daughters… do to them what is good in your eyes." The text reports it and does not approve it: hospitality's sacred duty ("they have come under the shadow of my roof") has been twisted into a father's unforgivable arithmetic, and the chapter will not let it go unanswered — what Lot offered of his daughters, his daughters will take of him, in the cave at the chapter's end. The mob's sneer, meanwhile, is loaded with chapter 18: "This one came here to sojourn" — a ger, a resident alien, 15:13's word — "and now he keeps playing the judge!" (vayishpot shaphot, the doubled verb of 18:25 — KJV renders the doubling "he will needs be a judge") — they mock the amateur judge at the door while the Judge of all the earth stands inside it. The angels end the scene: Lot pulled in, the door shut, and the mob struck with blindness (sanverim, a rare word — perhaps a dazzling — found again only at 2 Kings 6:18), and still they "wore themselves out groping for the doorway": blind, and trying to the last.

Verses 12–14 · וַיְהִי כִמְצַחֵק vayehi khimtsacheq

"Like one who was joking." The angels state their commission in the chapter's now-familiar vocabulary — "we are about to ruin this place" (shachat, the flood's verb, twice in one verse), "for the outcry has grown great before Jehovah" (18:20's word, quoted back). But the verse to hear is the last one: to his sons-in-law Lot "seemed like one who was joking" — metsacheq, the laugh-root, tsachaq. Abraham laughed face-down (17:17); Sarah laughed behind the tent flap (18:12); and now the same sound turns fatal — doom announced in Sodom lands as a punch line. Three laughs in three chapters: incredulous joy, frightened wonder, and unseriousness unto death. (The root has one more dark turn coming, at 21:9.) The sons-in-law stay, and are never mentioned again.

Verses 15–22 · וַיִּתְמַהְמָהּ vayitmahmah

"But he lingered" — and over this one word the Masoretes hung the shalshelet, the rarest cantillation mark in the Torah (it occurs four times in the whole Pentateuch): a zigzag sign sung as a long, wavering trill. The melody itself dawdles — the reader's voice hesitates with Lot, at dawn, in a city with hours to live. What saves him is not his resolve: "the men seized his hand, and his wife's hand, and the hands of his two daughters, in the compassion of Jehovah (chemlah) on him" — four people dragged bodily out of the story's way. (A shelf note: the ASV prints the name natively here — "Jehovah being merciful unto him" — exactly this project's convention, a century and a quarter early; KJV reads the substitute title.)

Bargaining with angels, mid-apocalypse. Told to run for the hills, Lot negotiates — nephew of his uncle to the last, but for a smaller prize: not fifty righteous, one small town. "Great is the kindness you have shown me" — chesed, its first appearance in the Bible: the steadfast, covenant-keeping love that becomes one of Scripture's great untranslatables (behind the "mercy" of the Psalms, and — paired with truth — behind John's "grace and truth," see the charis entry in the dictionary). "This town is near… and it is a little one (mits'ar)… is it not a little one?" — and the town is named from his own plea: Zoar, "Little." The answer grants it with an idiom worth keeping in the notes: "I have lifted your face" (nasati fanekha, here "granted you this favor") — nasa again, the carrying verb that "spared" Sodom's hypothetical fifty (18:26): grace pictured as a face lifted up off the ground. "I can do nothing until you arrive there" — the destruction waits, literally, on one slow man's walking pace.

Verses 23–29 · גָּפְרִית וָאֵשׁ gophrit va'esh

"Sulfur and fire — from Jehovah, out of the heavens." The sentence doubles strangely: "Jehovah rained on Sodom… from Jehovah, out of the heavens." Readings, each with a pedigree: Hebrew grammarians hear a solemn idiom of self-reference (kings speak of themselves this way in decrees, and the Bible does it elsewhere); some ancient Christian readers, as early as Justin Martyr, heard two divine persons — Jehovah on earth (the visitor of ch. 18) and Jehovah in heaven — and the verse has served in that argument ever since (the whole case, both ways, is on the Is Jesus God? page). This translation prints the doubled text and reports the argument. As for what fell: KJV, Geneva, Douay and ASV all rain "brimstone and fire" — the phrase that gave English its "fire and brimstone" — while NIV modernizes to "burning sulfur"; this translation reads "sulfur and fire," the plain mineral, in the Hebrew's own order. The verb of the event is haphakh, "overthrew" — and in verse 29 the event becomes a noun, "the overthrow" (hahafekhah): from here on, "like the overthrow of Sodom" is the Bible's standing measure of total ruin (Deuteronomy 29:23; Isaiah 13:19; Jeremiah 49:18).

Lot's wife, and Abraham's window. "His wife looked back from behind him — and she became a pillar of salt." One line, no psychology; the command had been flight without a backward tether, and she is caught mid-tether — fixed forever in the direction of what she could not leave. (The southern Dead Sea shore is salt-pillar country to this day — Mount Sodom's spires; the old tradition of Wisdom 10:7 already pointed at "a standing pillar of salt" as the story's monument. "Remember Lot's wife," Luke 17:32, needs only three words.) Then the camera climbs: Abraham, early in the morning, at "the place where he had stood before Jehovah" — yesterday's courtroom (18:22) — looks down over the plain and sees only smoke "like the smoke of a kiln" (kivshan — the word that will describe Sinai's smoke, Exodus 19:18). No word is recorded. And the narrator hands us the hinge quietly: "God remembered Abraham" — zakhar, the remembering-that-acts of "God remembered Noah" (8:1) — "and sent Lot out of the midst of the overthrow." Ten righteous were not found; but the argument of chapter 18 was not wasted — its other mercy, carrying the few out, is exactly what happened.

Verses 30–38 · מוֹאָב … בֶּן-עַמִּי Moav … Ben-Ammi

The cave. Lot — who begged for a town because he feared the hills (v19) — now fears the town too, and ends in the hills after all, in a cave, with nothing: the man who chose the well-watered plain "like the garden of Jehovah" (13:10) has run out of Sodoms. His daughters, watching the smoke of the only world they knew, draw a conclusion the text lets us hear in full: "there is no man on earth to come in to us" — they appear to believe the overthrow was the end of the world, a second flood in fire, and themselves its Noah's household. Their phrase is ark-vocabulary: "to keep offspring alive" (lehachayot zera) is what the animals were carried aboard to do (7:3). It is desperation, not appetite — and it is also the dark answer to verse 8: the father who once offered his daughters' bodies to a mob has his own body taken by his daughters, wine-blind, "and he knew nothing" — yada again, negated twice. (Over "and of her rising" in verse 33 the scribes set a row of dots — one of ten such dotted spots in the whole Torah, an ancient scribal flag; the rabbis read it as a wink that his ignorance was not quite total. Reported as received.)

Moab and Ben-Ammi. The sons are named with the text's own sound-glosses — Moav, heard as me'av, "from father"; Ben-Ammi, "son of my kin" — and they father Moab and Ammon, Israel's two kin-nations across the Jordan, whose long, prickly cousinhood with Israel the rest of the Bible remembers ("do not harass Moab… I have given Ar to the sons of Lot," Deuteronomy 2:9). Genesis tells its neighbors' origin without flattery — and then history hands the story its grace note: from Moab, from this cave's line, comes Ruth — "your people shall be my people" — great-grandmother of David (Ruth 4:17) and named in the genealogy of Jesus (Matthew 1:5). Lot leaves the story here, silent since Zoar, unmourned by the text; the last thing his family line is given is redemption. The closed break {ס} ends the chapter.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation sides with the literal shelf: "that we may know them" (the verb kept, not paraphrased away), "pillar of salt" plain, the doubled "Jehovah… from Jehovah" printed as it stands, and the daughters' ark-phrase "keep offspring alive" kept exact.

Where it goes its own way: "ruin" for shachat again (the flood's verb, audible through the angels' commission), "he seemed like one who was joking" (keeping the tsachaq laugh-root in view), and the scribal apparatus reported — the shalshelet trill on "but he lingered," and the ten-dots tradition at verse 33.

Echoes paid and planted. Paid: "that we may know them" against "I have known him" (18:19); the mob's "playing the judge" against the Judge of all the earth (18:25); the joking-laugh pays the laugh-thread (17:17, 18:12); the kikkar "like the garden of Jehovah" burns as chapter 13 foretold (13:10); Abraham returns to where he "stood before Jehovah" (18:22); and "God remembered Abraham" is Noah's hinge (8:1). Planted: matzot, the bread of the night-rescue (Exodus 12); the laugh-root's last dark turn (21:9); "like the overthrow of Sodom" as the prophets' byword; and Ruth the Moabite, the cave's unlikely grace (Ruth 4; Matthew 1:5).
