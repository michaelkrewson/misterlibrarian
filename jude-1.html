A single-chapter warning against false teachers who 'crept in unnoticed' — with the letter's two famous citations of works outside the canon (the Assumption of Moses, and 1 Enoch).
Hard to date; commonly placed c. AD 65-80. Its close kinship with 2 Peter 2, and its appeal to 'remember the words of the apostles' as an established past, put it a generation into the church.

Translator’s Notes

Verses 1–2 · the brother of James

«Jude, a slave of Jesus Christ and brother of James.» The name is Ioudas — the same Greek name as Judah and Judas; English keeps “Jude” to set this one apart. He introduces himself with striking humility: not “brother of Jesus” but “slave (doulos) of Jesus Christ,” and then “brother of James” — which is exactly how the first readers would place him. James the Just, the leader of the Jerusalem church, was a brother of Jesus (Mark 6:3 lists “James and Joses and Judas and Simon” among the Lord’s brothers). So Jude is, on the plainest reading, another brother of the Lord — who calls himself only his slave, and points not to his blood but to his more famous brother. ⚠ Because both names are common, a few take him for a different Jude; but the brother-of-James signature points to the family of Jesus.

«Mercy and peace and love.» Even a letter this fierce opens on love (agape), and will close on it (v21). The salutation asks not that these be given but that they be multiplied — the same verb the Greek Old Testament uses of a people made to increase.

Verses 3–4 · the faith once for all delivered

«I found it necessary.» Jude meant to write a warm letter “about our common salvation” and had to write a battle-dispatch instead. The thesis is verse 3: contend (epagonizesthai — the wrestling-and-athletics word, our “agonize”) for “the faith once for all (hapax) delivered to the holy ones.” Hapax is the hinge of the letter: the faith was handed over ONCE, complete — not a thing to be added to, updated, or traded away.

«Crept in unnoticed.» Pareisedysan — a rare word for slipping in by a side door. The danger is not outside the church but already inside the love-feast (v12). They “turn the grace of our God into licentiousness” (aselgeia, grace twisted into an excuse for unrestraint) and “deny our only Master (despotes) and Lord, Jesus Christ.” Despotes is the owner of a slave (our “despot”); Jude, the slave of Christ in verse 1, names the intruders as slaves who deny the one who owns them.

Verses 5–7 · three ruins remembered

⚠ «Jesus… saved a people out of Egypt.» A famous variant, and this translation prints the harder reading the SBL text adopts. The oldest and best witnesses — the early papyrus P72 and the great codices among them — read that JESUS saved the people out of Egypt and then destroyed the unbelievers: a startling statement that the pre-incarnate Christ led the Exodus. Other manuscripts read “the Lord,” and a few “God.” It is one of the most theologically loaded textual decisions in the New Testament, and the note keeps the reading the manuscripts best support. KJV “the Lord”; the ESV prints “Jesus” with a footnote.

Three warnings, in order. The wilderness generation, saved out of Egypt and then destroyed for unbelief (Numbers 14) — rescue is no guarantee. The angels who “did not keep their own domain (arche)” but left their dwelling, now held in chains for the judgment of the great day — even angels fell (the reading of Genesis 6 in Jewish tradition, and of the Enoch material Jude will quote in v14). And Sodom and Gomorrah, “set forth as an example (deigma) of eternal fire.” Three ruins from the old story, each a picture of a mercy received and thrown away.

Verses 8–10 · Michael would not — but these do

⚠ Michael and the body of Moses. Verse 9 refers to a story found nowhere in the Old Testament: the archangel Michael disputing with the devil over the corpse of Moses. It comes from a Jewish work outside the canon — by ancient testimony, the lost ending of the Assumption (or Testament) of Moses. Jude uses it as an illustration: even Michael, contending with the devil himself, “did not dare bring a blasphemous judgment” but left the rebuke to God — “May the Lord rebuke you,” the very words the angel of Jehovah speaks to Satan in Zechariah 3:2. The intruders, by contrast, “blaspheme glories” they do not understand. ⚠ That Jude draws an illustration from a non-canonical book does not make that book Scripture; ancient and modern readers take it as Jude reaching for a story his audience knew — as a preacher might cite a familiar legend to land a true point. But it is worth naming plainly, and it sets up the larger case at verse 14.

«By instinct, like the unreasoning animals.» Their knowledge is animal, not spiritual; and the one thing they think they grasp is the very thing that destroys them.

Verses 11–13 · the woe, and the images

The woe triad. Three archetypes of rebellion, each with a name: the way of Cain (Genesis 4 — the first murderer, envy turned to blood); the error of Balaam “for pay” (Numbers 22–24; 31:16 — the prophet who sold his gift for a fee); and the rebellion (antilogia, the “speaking-against”) of Korah, swallowed by the earth for defying God’s appointed leaders (Numbers 16). Greed, false teaching, and revolt against authority — the three charges, laid out as three men.

The images. Jude piles up pictures of promise without substance: hidden reefs (spilades) in the love-feasts, wrecking the ship from below; waterless clouds that promise rain and give none; autumn trees “twice dead” and uprooted, barren when the fruit is due; wild waves foaming out their own shame; and “wandering stars” (asteres planetai — the planets, which drift off the fixed courses sailors steer by), “for whom the gloom of darkness has been kept forever.”

Verses 14–16 · Enoch prophesied

⚠ Jude quotes ENOCH — and the book is not in the Bible. This is the single most-discussed feature of the letter. Verses 14–15 are a direct quotation of 1 Enoch (1:9), a Jewish apocalypse composed in the centuries before Christ and NOT part of the Hebrew or of most Christian canons. Jude names the source — “Enoch, the seventh from Adam” — and calls it prophecy. This is a canonical book quoting a non-canonical one as a true word, and readers have taken it several ways: that Jude endorses only this saying, not the whole book; that he cites 1 Enoch the way Paul quotes pagan poets (Acts 17:28), for a truth it happens to carry; or — the view of the Ethiopian Orthodox Church, which alone keeps 1 Enoch in its canon — that the book is genuinely Scripture. The one thing the text will not let us do is pretend the quotation is not there. KJV “ten thousands of his saints”; the NWT “holy myriads.”

«Grumblers, malcontents.» The portrait sharpens to a tone of voice: complainers who follow their appetites, whose mouths speak swollen words (hyperonka, bombast), flattering the powerful for whatever can be got from them.

Verses 17–19 · remember what you were told

«Remember the words of the apostles.» Against the intruders’ claim to novelty Jude sets memory: the false teachers were predicted — “in the last time there will be mockers.” ⚠ Note that Jude speaks of the apostles as an established authority whose warnings are already handed down — a hint that the letter is written to a church that already looks back to the apostolic word. “These cause divisions (apodiorizontes) — worldly (psychikoi, ‘soulish’), not having the Spirit”: the sharpest line in the letter, that the very men splitting the church are the ones without the Spirit they boast of.

Verses 20–23 · but you — build, pray, keep, wait; and rescue

«But you, beloved.» The turn to the faithful comes in a near-trinitarian rhythm: build yourselves up in the faith, pray in the Holy Spirit, keep yourselves in the love of God, wait for the mercy of the Lord Jesus Christ. Contending for the faith (v3) turns out to be less an attack than a community holding itself in love and prayer.

⚠ «Have mercy… snatch from the fire… mercy with fear.» Verses 22–23 are among the most textually tangled in the New Testament — the manuscripts vary between two and three groups and cannot all be reconciled; this translation follows the SBL three-clause reading. The mercy is fierce and careful at once: steady the wavering, snatch some straight out of the fire, and show even the compromised a mercy mixed with fear — “hating even the garment (chiton) stained by the flesh,” an image from Zechariah 3, where filthy garments are stripped from the high priest. Love the person; fear the contagion. ESV and NIV divide the groups differently.

Verses 24–25 · the doxology

One of the great doxologies of Scripture — and a deliberate answer to the whole letter. Jude has spent twenty-three verses on those who fall; he ends on the One “able to keep you from stumbling” (aptaistous, ‘from not-tripping’) and to make you stand before his glory “blameless” (amomous — the sacrificial word, without blemish), “with great joy.” The intruders defiled and denied; the doxology is all glory and majesty and dominion and authority to “the only God our Savior, through Jesus Christ our Lord” — before all time, now, and forever. A book about danger ends in safe hands.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the exact / distinct words: “slave” for doulos and “Master” for despotes, kept in the same family so the intruders’ denial answers Jude’s own title; “once for all” for hapax, the letter’s hinge; “contend” for epagonizesthai, the wrestling word; “licentiousness” for aselgeia; “crept in unnoticed” for pareisedysan; “hidden reefs” for spilades and “wandering stars” for asteres planetai, the images left vivid; and the reading “Jesus” at verse 5, the harder text the manuscripts best support.

Echoes paid. The wilderness generation destroyed after the Exodus (Numbers 14); the fallen angels of Genesis 6 as Jewish tradition read them; Sodom and Gomorrah (Genesis 19); “May the Lord rebuke you” from Zechariah 3:2; the way of Cain (Genesis 4), the error of Balaam (Numbers 22–24), the rebellion of Korah (Numbers 16); the stained garment of Zechariah 3; and the great doxology’s “blameless” (amomos), the sacrificial word for a spotless offering.

The two openly-named extra-canonical sources. Jude is the clearest case in the Bible of a canonical book drawing on works outside the canon: the dispute over the body of Moses (v9), from the lost Assumption of Moses; and the prophecy of Enoch (vv14–15), a direct quotation of 1 Enoch 1:9. The notes lay the facts out with their pedigrees and take no vote — the citations are there, and pretending otherwise is not translation.

Jude is a single-chapter book: with this chapter it is complete. Its nearest kin is 2 Peter 2, which shares much of this material — the fallen angels, Sodom, Balaam, the waterless clouds — and the relationship between the two (which drew on which) is one of the New Testament’s standing literary questions.
