Keturah's sons and the peoples of the east; Abraham dies at 175, buried by Isaac and Ishmael; and the twins Esau and Jacob are born and the birthright sold.
Not one date but a span, in the toldot (generations) style: the twins are born when Isaac is 60 (AM 2108 · c. 1836 BC), and Abraham dies at 175 fifteen years later (AM 2123 · c. 1821 BC) — so the grandfather lived to see the boys grow. The chapter closes the first generation of the promise and opens the third.

Translator's Notes — verse by verse

Genesis 25 is a hinge — it closes the first generation of the promise and opens the third. Quotations from copyrighted versions (NIV, TLB, NWT) are kept to short phrases for comparison; KJV, Geneva, Douay-Rheims and ASV are public domain.

Verses 1–11 · וַיֵּאָסֶף אֶל-עַמָּיו vayye'asef el-ammav — "and he was gathered to his people"

The last acts of a long life. Abraham marries again — Keturah — and fathers six more sons who fan out east as the peoples of Arabia and the caravan routes; chief among them is Midian, so the Midianites Moses will one day live among descend from Abraham himself. But the inheritance is not divided: "Abraham gave all he had to Isaac," and sent the others away with gifts, "eastward, to the land of the east." Then, at 175, "he breathed his last and died … and was gathered to his people." The phrase is worth stopping on — it is not the same as burial (he is "gathered" in v8, then buried in v9). Ne'esaf el-ammav reaches past the grave to a joining of the ancestors: a quiet, undogmatic hint of a life continued with one's own, long before the Hebrew Bible speaks plainly of resurrection. And the burial is its own quiet wonder: Isaac and Ishmael — the heir and the son sent into the desert — stand together to lay their father in the cave he had bought for Sarah (23:19). Then Isaac settles at Beer-lahai-roi, Hagar's well: the son of the promise makes his home where the seeing-God once met the cast-out.

Verses 12–18 · שְׁנֵים-עָשָׂר נְשִׂיאִם shneim-asar nesi'im — "twelve chieftains"

Ishmael's promise, kept. Before the story narrows to Isaac's line, it pauses to honor the other son with a toldot ("generations") of his own — and to record that God kept his word to him exactly. Ishmael fathers twelve chieftains (nesi'im, the same "prince" word the Hittites used of Abraham, 23:6) — precisely the "twelve chieftains" promised at 17:20. He too dies at a full age and is "gathered to his people" — the same dignity given him as his father. And his people settle "over against all his brothers" — the very words the angel spoke to Hagar in the desert, "he will dwell in the face of all his brothers" (16:12), now written down as history. The sent-away son is not forgotten by the God who first named him "God hears."

Verses 19–26 · וְרַב יַעֲבֹד צָעִיר ve-rav ya'avod tsa'ir — "the elder will serve the younger"

Two nations at war in a womb. Rebekah is barren twenty years, until Isaac entreats Jehovah — and the Hebrew turns the same verb around for the answer: "Isaac entreated … and Jehovah was entreated of him" (v21), one word for the asking and the yes. The pregnancy is violent — the twins "struggle" inside her — and Rebekah does the remarkable thing of going straight to God: she is the first woman in the Bible to "inquire of Jehovah," and it is she, not Isaac, who receives the oracle. And what an oracle: "the elder will serve the younger." (The Hebrew rav ya'avod tsa'ir is, strictly, ambiguous — with no object-marker it could even read "the elder, the younger will serve" — but the whole of Genesis resolves it: the promise runs through the chosen son, not the firstborn, the pattern Isaac himself embodied over Ishmael, 21:12.) The names are minted at birth: the first out is ruddy and hairy — Esau — and the second grips his twin's heel (aqev), so he is Jacob, "heel-holder" — the very word of the first promise, "you will strike his heel" (3:15). The whole struggle of the seed is folded into a newborn's fist.

Verses 27–34 · אִישׁ תָּם ish tam — "a blameless man"

The word that surprises. Esau grows into a hunter, "a man of the field"; Jacob is an ish tam — and here this translation makes its most surprising choice, following the NWT toward the literal: "a blameless man." The word tam is the one used of Noah and of Job — "blameless, complete, whole" — and the shelf scatters, uneasy, exactly along that tension: the KJV reaches for "a plain man," the ASV for "a quiet man," softening the moral weight to fit a man who is about to drive a hard bargain. Kept literal, it carries the root sense — whole, sound, all-of-a-piece, settled — the domestic man of the tents set against the roving hunter; and it lets the narrator's irony stand undimmed, that the "blameless" one is the one who buys his brother's hunger.

Sold for a bowl of red. Esau comes in famished and cannot even name the food: "let me gulp down some of that red — that red stuff" — and the crude cry earns him his second name, Edom ("Red"). Jacob's price is his brother's whole future: the birthright, the firstborn's double portion and the covenant line itself. Esau takes the trade with a shrug — "I am about to die; what good is a birthright to me?" — and the narrator nails him to it with five flat verbs: "he ate and drank and rose and went his way. So Esau despised his birthright." No lightning, no rebuke — just the terrible casualness of a man who sells the eternal for one meal, which is why the New Testament makes him the very type of the "profane" person (Hebrews 12:16).

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the words exact / distinct: "gathered to his people" for ne'esaf el-ammav (kept distinct from burial, so its reach past the grave stays visible); "a blameless man" for the surprising tam (v27), with the NWT and against the KJV's "plain" and the ASV's "quiet," the note carrying the irony; "he despised his birthright" for bazah (the same verb the fool aims at wisdom, Proverbs 1); and the entreated / was entreated echo of atar kept audible (v21).

Echoes paid. Ishmael's twelve chieftains (v16) — the promise of 17:20 kept; "over against all his brothers" (v18) — the angel's word to Hagar; "the elder will serve the younger" (v23) — the reversal of 21:12; Jacob's grip on the heel (v26) — the aqev of the first promise; and Isaac and Ishmael together at Machpelah (v9), the grave Abraham bought for Sarah.
