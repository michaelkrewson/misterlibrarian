Jacob flees toward Haran; at Bethel he dreams of the stairway between earth and heaven, the covenant is confirmed to the fugitive, and he vows his first vow.
AM ≈ 2185 · c. 1760 BC — the first night of the flight begun in ch. 27, Jacob about 77. His twenty years with Laban start here; the theophany at Bethel is his first personal meeting with the God of his fathers, alone on the road with a stone for a pillow.

Translator's Notes — verse by verse

The fugitive meets God — a stone for a pillow, a stairway to heaven, and the covenant handed on. Quotations from copyrighted versions (NIV, TLB, NWT) are kept to short phrases for comparison; KJV, Geneva, Douay-Rheims and ASV are public domain.

Verses 1–9 · בִּרְכַּת אַבְרָהָם birkat Avraham — "the blessing of Abraham"

The blessing, this time with open eyes. Isaac blesses Jacob again — but now knowing exactly whom he is blessing, and for the first time naming what he passes on: not just dew and dominion (that was the stolen blessing of ch. 27) but "the blessing of Abraham," the covenant itself, "to you and to your seed … to take possession of the land." He blesses in the name Abraham knew God by, El Shaddai (17:1; KJV "God Almighty," kept here untranslated as the covenant-name it is). And the charge that carries the plot is the same one that has run the family since Abraham sent his servant east: no wife from the daughters of Canaan.

Esau, still just missing it. Watching all this, Esau finally grasps that his Canaanite marriages had grieved his parents (26:34-35) — and tries to fix it. But he fixes it the way he does everything, half-right: he goes to Ishmael's line and marries Mahalath, a granddaughter of Abraham — inside the family, yes, but the rejected branch, the son who was sent away. Even his repentance lands just outside the promise.

Verses 10–15 · סֻלָּם sullam — the stairway

Jacob's "ladder" — or stairway. The word sullam occurs only once in the whole Bible, so its exact shape is uncertain. The KJV's "ladder" gave English "Jacob's ladder," but the likely root is salal, "to cast up (a ramp or highway)," which points to a stairway — the monumental temple-stair of a Mesopotamian ziggurat, built precisely as a stepped bridge between earth and heaven for the gods to come down. NWT and most modern scholars read "stairway," and the broad steps fit the traffic the dream shows — angels "ascending and descending" both at once. (Two small ambiguities ride along: the angels move "on it," or possibly "on him," Jacob; and Jehovah stands "over it," the stairway, or "beside him" — the Hebrew allows either.)

Grace to a man on the run. What God says is the whole Abrahamic covenant, spoken now to the grandson — the land he is fleeing (13:15), seed like the dust, "in you and your seed all the families of the earth will be blessed" (12:3) — and then the personal word Jacob most needs on his first night as a fugitive: "I am with you, and I will keep you wherever you go, and I will bring you back." There is not a syllable of condition or rebuke. The deceiver has just cheated his brother and lied to his blind father, and God meets him on the road with pure promise. It is one of the clearest pictures of unearned grace in the book.

Verses 16–19 · בֵּית אֱלֹהִים … שַׁעַר הַשָּׁמָיִם Beit Elohim … sha'ar ha-shamayim

"And I did not know it." Jacob wakes stunned: "Surely Jehovah is in this place, and I did not know it." He had thought he was leaving God behind with his father, in the land he was fleeing — and finds him waiting at a nameless roadside stop. "How awesome (nora; KJV "dreadful," in its old sense of awe-filled) is this place! This is none other than the house of God, and this is the gate of heaven." So he takes his stone pillow, stands it up as a pillar (matsevah), and pours oil on it — the Bible's first anointing of a sacred stone — and names the place Bethel, "house of God" (the town had been called Luz). The stone that cushioned a fugitive's head becomes the marker of the gate of heaven. Expedition Bible has walked the ruins of the site; their film is on the Bethel entry.

Verses 20–22 · וַיִּדַּר יַעֲקֹב נֶדֶר vayyiddar Ya'aqov neder — "Jacob vowed a vow"

Even here, he bargains. God has just given Jacob everything, free and unconditional — and Jacob answers with the Bible's first recorded vow, and it is pure Jacob: an if / then. "If God will be with me and keep me and feed and clothe me and bring me home safe — then Jehovah will be my God." The very things God has already promised outright, Jacob turns into the terms of a deal; the man who bought a birthright and stole a blessing negotiates even with heaven. And yet it is a real beginning: he pledges the stone as God's house and vows a tenth of all he receives — the first vowed tithe, echoing the tenth Abraham gave Melchizedek (14:20). The faith is transactional and immature, but it is faith, and God will spend the next twenty years growing it up.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the words exact / distinct: "stairway" for the once-only sullam (with the NWT and the ziggurat image, keeping the KJV's "ladder" in view as the traditional name); El Shaddai kept untranslated as a covenant-name (vs the shelf's "God Almighty"); "pillar" for matsevah, the sacred stone the Law will later forbid; and the alternate readings of v12–13 ("on it / on him," "over it / beside him") flagged rather than hidden.

Echoes paid — and one still ahead. The land-grant of 13:15; "all the families of the earth blessed" (12:3); El Shaddai from 17:1; "I am with you," the word to Isaac now to Jacob; and the tithe of Melchizedek. And the great one ahead: Jesus tells Nathanael he will see "the angels of God ascending and descending on the Son of Man" (John 1:51) — he is the stairway, the true Bethel where heaven and earth meet.
