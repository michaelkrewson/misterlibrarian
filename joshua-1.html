Translator's Notes

Verses 1–5 · "Moses my servant is dead"

The book of Joshua begins where the Torah stopped, and it does not soften the fact. The first words after "and it happened" are the last thing anyone wanted to hear: Moses is dead. ⚠️ This is the seam between the Five Books and everything that follows — in the Hebrew Bible, Joshua opens the section called the Former Prophets — and it is handled with a single flat clause and an immediate order to move. There is no eulogy here (Deuteronomy 34 gave that). The point of verse 2 is that the death of the greatest figure in Israel's history is not permitted to become a reason to stop: "Moses my servant is dead. And now, rise, cross."

The two titles are doing work. Moses is eved YHWH, "the servant of Jehovah" — the highest title in the Hebrew Bible, given to Moses again and again. Joshua, at the start, is only mesharet Moshe, "Moses' attendant," the word for a personal aide who waits on someone (the young Samuel is a mesharet in the temple). ⚠️ Watch the word across the book: the man who begins as Moses' assistant will himself be called "the servant of Jehovah" in the very last chapter, at his own death (24:29). Joshua 1 is the story of that promotion beginning, and it opens by naming the gap he has to cross.

"Every place the sole of your foot treads." The grant is enormous — wilderness to Lebanon to the Euphrates to the Great Sea (the Mediterranean, called here mevo ha-shemesh, "the going-in of the sun," i.e. the west). ⚠️ It is the same ideal extent promised in Deuteronomy 1:7, and the same one Israel never in fact held except briefly under Solomon. The land is given in the perfect tense — "to you I have given it" — while the treading is left to them; possession is declared as a settled fact and worked out as a task, which is the theological engine of the whole book.

"I will not let you go and I will not forsake you." Two verbs of holding-on, both negated. The promise is exactly what was made to the man who just died — as I was with Moses, I will be with you — and Hebrews 13:5 quotes this line as a promise to every believer. KJV "I will not fail thee, nor forsake thee"; the ASV the same.

Verses 6–9 · be strong and resolute — three times

The command is repeated, and it escalates. Chazak ve-ematz, "be strong and resolute," lands three times in four verses — verse 6, verse 7 (with "very"), and verse 9 — framing everything between. It is not a pep talk. KJV "Be strong and of a good courage"; the NIV "Be strong and courageous"; the ESV "Be strong and courageous." It is a military formula (David gives it to Solomon, and it recurs through the historical books), and here it brackets the one thing Joshua is actually told to be strong for: not the fighting, but the keeping of the instruction. ⚠️ Note where the "very" falls — verse 7, about obedience to the torah, not verse 6, about conquering. The book is quietly saying the harder courage is the second kind.

"You shall murmur over it day and night." The verb is hagah, and it is concrete and strange. It means to make a low sound — a lion growling over prey (Isaiah 31:4), a dove moaning, a person muttering. Applied to the scroll it is not silent study but reading aloud under the breath, the ancient practice of chewing a text over by voicing it continually. ⚠️ It is the very same word that opens the Psalter — the blessed man "murmurs" (hagah) in the torah of Jehovah day and night (Psalm 1:2) — and the overlap is not accidental: Joshua 1 and Psalm 1 are the two great "day and night in the torah" texts, one at the head of the Prophets and one at the head of the Writings. Most versions render it "meditate," which is not wrong but loses the sound. KJV "thou shalt meditate therein day and night"; NWT "you must read it in an undertone day and night," which keeps the voice.

"Then you will act wisely." Taskil — from sakal, the same root as the verb that gave the crossed hands their meaning in Genesis 48: to act with insight, to succeed because one acts with understanding. It brackets verse 8 with tatzliach, "prosper," so the two promises are success and skill — and both are attached, pointedly, to the scroll rather than the sword. ⚠️ This is the Bible's own definition of "success" for a general, and it is bookish: prosperity follows from doing what is written, not from strength of arms. The KJV's famous "then thou shalt have good success" (v. 8) reads taskil as prospering, which is close.

"Have I not commanded you?" The section closes by folding the courage-command and the presence-promise into one rhetorical question — the reason not to be terrified is not Joshua's competence but the sentence that has run through the whole passage: Jehovah your God is with you wherever you go.

Verses 10–11 · three days, and provisions

The first thing Joshua does with the command is delegate it. He has just been told, by God, to be strong; his first recorded act is to send the officers through the camp with an entirely practical order — pack food, we cross in three days. ⚠️ There is a small chronological puzzle here that the book does not stop to resolve: the "three days" of verse 11 and the spies of chapter 2 and the actual crossing of chapters 3-4 do not line up into a tidy single count, and readers have proposed various orderings. It is the ordinary looseness of a narrative that is telling a story rather than filing a report; noted, not smoothed. What matters here is the tone — the towering theophany of verses 1-9 comes down immediately to rations and a marching order.

Verses 12–15 · the two-and-a-half tribes, and a debt called in

Joshua's first move is to hold people to a promise. Reuben, Gad and half of Manasseh had asked Moses, back in Numbers 32, to settle on the east side of the Jordan — the good grazing land they had already conquered — rather than cross into Canaan proper. Moses agreed on one condition: that their fighting men cross over armed, ahead of their brothers, and not go home until the land was won. ⚠️ Joshua opens his command by cashing that promise. The families and herds stay east; the warriors go west. It is a deliberate picture of the nation as one body — the tribes who already have their inheritance do not sit it out while the others fight.

"Rest." Meniach lakhem, "giving you rest" — nuach, the word for settling, ceasing, coming to a stop. ⚠️ It is one of the book's key words and one of its promises: the point of the crossing is not conquest for its own sake but rest, a place to stop moving after forty years of not stopping. Hebrews 4 later reads this "rest" as unfinished — Joshua gave them a land, but "if Joshua had given them rest, God would not have spoken of another day" — which is why the Greek form of the name, Iēsous (the same name as Jesus), is doing quiet work in that argument.

Verses 16–18 · "as we listened to Moses, so we will listen to you"

The people accept the succession — with a condition attached. Their pledge is total ("all that you have commanded us we will do") and then, in the same breath, they pin it to the one thing that actually matters: only may Jehovah be with you, as he was with Moses. ⚠️ It is not a hedge; it is the right instinct. Their loyalty to Joshua is real but derivative — they will follow him precisely insofar as God is with him, which is exactly what the first nine verses promised. The people, without having heard God's private words to Joshua, name the very condition God had just guaranteed.

⚠️ "Shall be put to death." The sentence is harsh and worth being honest about: they undertake to execute anyone who rebels against Joshua's word. This is the language of a nation under military discipline crossing into a war, not a general ethic — the same generation is bound by a marching order, and desertion in a campaign was a capital matter across the ancient world. It is reported, not commended; the book states the terms the people set and moves on.

And they give the command back to him. The people's last word to Joshua is the very phrase God opened with: rak chazak ve-ematz, "only be strong and resolute." ⚠️ The charge God laid on him in verse 6 comes back out of the mouths of the people in verse 18 — the encouragement has gone all the way round the camp and returned to its source. A leader is told to be strong by God, and then told the same thing by the people he leads. It is the chapter's quiet last note, and a generous one.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation keeps the words exact / distinct: "Moses' attendant" for mesharet, so the gap between the aide and the "servant of Jehovah" he becomes is visible; "be strong and resolute" for chazak ve-ematz, the military formula, kept identical across all three occurrences; "murmur over it" for hagah, keeping the sound rather than flattening to "meditate"; "act wisely" for taskil, holding the insight-and-success sense; "rest" for nuach; and "the going-in of the sun" for the west, left as the picture the Hebrew draws.

Echoes paid. Deuteronomy 1's ideal borders, repeated as the land is finally entered; the insight-verb of the crossed hands, now the promise made to Joshua; and Moses' charge at the end of Deuteronomy — "be strong and resolute" — handed to his successor and then handed back by the people.

Echoes planted. Joshua the attendant, on his way to "the servant of Jehovah"; "day and night in the torah," which Psalm 1 will make the mark of the blessed man; the rest that Hebrews 4 will call unfinished; and the two-and-a-half tribes' pledge, which the book will test at the very end when they build an altar by the Jordan and are nearly attacked for it (Joshua 22).
