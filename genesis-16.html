Translator's Notes — verse by verse

Each note explains this translation's choice and compares the seven versions on the shelf. Quotations from copyrighted versions (NIV, TLB, NWT) are kept to short phrases for comparison; KJV, Geneva, Douay-Rheims and ASV are public domain.

Verses 1–3 · אוּלַי אִבָּנֶה מִמֶּנָּה ulai ibbaneh mimmennah

"An Egyptian slave-girl, and her name was Hagar." After the night of stars, the morning after. Sarai — introduced as barren back at 11:30, the sentence the whole promise strains against — has now waited ten years in Canaan (v3), and takes matters into her own hands. Hagar is a shiphchah, a female slave belonging to the mistress. The shelf upholsters the title — KJV, ASV and Douay "handmaid," Geneva "maid," NWT "maidservant" — while NIV (2011) says "slave"; MiSTeR keeps it plain: slave-girl. That she is Egyptian is not decoration: Pharaoh's parting gifts in the Egypt sojourn included "male and female servants" (12:16) — the likeliest door by which an Egyptian girl entered this household (rabbinic tradition went further and made her Pharaoh's own daughter; legend, but it shows how old the question is).

"Perhaps I shall be built up through her." The idiom is exact and this translation keeps it: ibbaneh, "I shall be built," puns on ben, "son" — a woman is "built up" by children (Rachel will use the identical formula with her own slave-girl, Genesis 30:3; the smoother shelf renderings — KJV "obtain children by her" — pave the pun). And this was law before it was desperation: a barren wife giving her slave as a surrogate, the child counting as the wife's, is written into the region's law codes (Hammurabi's among them) and marriage contracts. The Torah reports the custom; what it thinks of it, the next three verses show. Meanwhile the narrator reaches for the garden's exact grammar: Abram "listened to the voice of" his wife — the phrase of the verdict on Adam, "because you listened to the voice of your wife" (3:17, word for word) — and Sarai took and gave to her husband (v3) as the woman took and gave in 3:6. The scene is written to make you hear Eden: a plan that looks good in human eyes, and the wreckage after.

Verses 4–6 · וַתֵּקַל גְּבִרְתָּהּ בְּעֵינֶיהָ vateqal gevirtah be'eineha

"Her mistress became slight in her eyes." The verb is qalal, "to be light, slight" — the very root of the promise's warning, "the one who belittles you I will curse" (12:3). The moment Hagar conceives, the household's weights shift: the fruitful slave outweighs the barren mistress, and everyone feels it. Sarai's explosion runs on the same scales — "I became slight in her eyes" — and on a heavier word: "the wrong done to me is on you!" is chamasi, from chamas, the flood's word for violence (6:11), as if to say the household is filling with the old poison. "May Jehovah judge between me and you" — yishpot, from shaphat; hold that word, because the Judge of all the earth is two chapters away (18:25).

"And Sarai afflicted her." Abram, handed the quarrel, hands the girl back — "your slave-girl is in your hand; do to her what is good in your eyes" — and goes quiet. (Notice that neither he nor Sarai ever speaks Hagar's name; in the whole chapter only one voice will.) Then the verse the chapter turns on: vate'anneha, "she afflicted her" — the precise verb of the prophecy one chapter back, "they will be enslaved and afflicted" (15:13). Before Egypt afflicts Israel for four hundred years, Israel's own house afflicts an Egyptian. The Torah writes the reversal down and does not flinch — and Hagar flees (barach, the verb Exodus will use of Israel fleeing Egypt, Exodus 14:5): the Bible's first runaway slave runs from Abram's tents.

Verses 7–8 · מַלְאַךְ יְהוָה mal'akh YHWH

"The angel of Jehovah" — the first angel in the Bible, and he appears not to Abram but to a runaway Egyptian slave-girl. Mal'akh means simply messenger (the Greek angelos is the same word; wings and halos are later furniture — the word is used of human couriers too). Who is he? The text is deliberately double-edged: the messenger speaks in God's own first person ("I will greatly multiply," v10), and the narrator calls the speaker simply "Jehovah" (v13). Three readings, each with a pedigree: an envoy who bears his sender's very words and may be addressed as the sender, as ancient messengers were; a visible manifestation of Jehovah himself, the view of many commentators ancient and modern; and the later Christian reading that sees in "the angel of Jehovah" the pre-incarnate Son (the arguments both ways are catalogued on the Is Jesus God? page). The text slides between "the angel of Jehovah" and "Jehovah" without embarrassment, and this translation leaves the seam showing.

"On the way to Shur." Geography tells the story: Shur is the desert fronting Egypt's north-east frontier, and the road Hagar is on is the road home — an Egyptian, pregnant and alone, walking back toward Egypt. The angel's question is the Bible's third divine question to a human on the run — "Where are you?" (3:9), "Where is Abel your brother?" (4:9), and now "where have you come from, and where are you going?" She can answer only half of it: from the face of my mistress; about where to, silence. And notice — "Hagar, slave-girl of Sarai": the messenger is the first speaker in the chapter to call her by name.

Verses 9–12 · וְקָרָאת שְׁמוֹ יִשְׁמָעֵאל veqarat shemo Yishma'el

"Return… and submit." The hard verse first, and honestly: hit'anni is the reflexive of the very verb of verse 6 — "submit yourself," literally "let yourself be afflicted, under her hands." The angel does not pretend the house is safe; he sends her back into it, with a promise strapped to the order. Readers have wrestled with this ever since, and this translation leaves the difficulty standing (as it left "the iniquity of the Amorite" standing at 15:16). Note, too, the drumbeat: three verses in a row each restart "And the angel of Jehovah said to her" (vv 9, 10, 11) — three stacked oracles: an order, a promise, a name. Hebrew narrative repeats; this translation keeps all three.

The promise, and the name. "I will greatly multiply your offspring, so many they cannot be counted" — the counting verb of the stars ("count the stars, if you are able to count them," 15:5), the scale of the dust (13:16): the patriarchal promise itself, and Hagar is the only woman in Genesis to receive it from God directly. Then the Bible's first annunciation — "look — you are pregnant, and you shall bear a son, and you shall call his name…" — a formula that will return for Isaac (17:19), for Samson (Judges 13), in Isaiah's Immanuel sign (Isaiah 7:14), and at Nazareth (Luke 1:31). It begins with Hagar. The name is the sentence: Yishma-El, "God hears," given "for Jehovah has heard (shama) your affliction" — and "your affliction" (onyekh) is the anah root a third time, now in God's mouth: what Sarai dealt, God heard. Israel will one day be heard in the identical vocabulary: "I have seen the affliction of my people… and have heard their cry" (Exodus 3:7).

"A wild donkey of a man." Not a slur in the desert's own ears: the pere is the untamable free-ranger of the steppe — Job 39:5-8 sings its freedom (it "laughs at the city's commotion" and "hears no driver's shout") — and to a slave, whose child would otherwise be born property, "no one will ever hold his halter" is a promise. (KJV "he will be a wild man" loses the animal and reads like an insult; the Hebrew names the animal.) "His hand against all, and the hand of all against him" — the free life has its price — "and in the face of all his brothers he shall dwell": al-penei can mean "alongside, over against" his kin (as 25:18 will settle his descendants) or "in defiance of" them; the versions split, and the phrase likely winks both ways. One more thread: the woman fleeing "from the face of" her mistress (vv 6, 8) carries a son who will live "in the face of" everyone — nobody's runaway.

Verses 13–14 · אַתָּה אֵל רֳאִי attah El Ro'i

"You are El Ro'i." The astonishing thing, easy to read past: Hagar names God. Everywhere else in the Bible God announces his own names; here a foreign slave-girl coins one, and the text keeps it — she is the only person in the Bible who confers a name on God. El Ro'i — "God of seeing" — and the Hebrew holds two readings at once: "the God who sees me" and "the God whom I have seen." She means both, and the chapter has been building the word all along: slight in her eyes (vv 4–5), good in your eyes (v6), the spring — Hebrew ayin, the same word as eye (v7) — and now the God of seeing. The woman nobody in the household would name, names God: the God who saw her.

"Have I even here seen after the one who sees me?" The second half of verse 13 is famously rough, and this translation keeps the roughness rather than paving it. Word-for-word: "Have I even here (halom) seen after (acharei) the one who sees me?" The shelf scatters: KJV, Geneva and ASV "have I also here looked after him that seeth me"; Douay, translating the Vulgate, reads "seen the hinder parts of him that seeth me" — taking "after" as God's back, the way Moses will be shown "my back" because "no one may see my face and live" (Exodus 33:20-23); NIV (2011) smooths to "I have now seen the One who sees me"; and many scholars suspect a phrase like "God — and lived" has dropped out (compare "we shall surely die, for we have seen God," Judges 13:22). The astonishment is not in doubt, only its wording: I looked at the One looking at me — and I am still here.

"Beer-lahai-roi." The well keeps her sentence: Be'er-lachai-ro'i, "the well of the Living One who sees me" — a place-name minted from a slave-girl's theology, fixed "between Kadesh" (the En-mishpat of the war of the kings, 14:7) "and Bered" — a place named nowhere else, so the well is lost to the map (said plainly). And the story is not done with it: Isaac — the son who will displace Ishmael — will one day arrive from, and settle beside, this same well (24:62; 25:11). Hold that thought for eight chapters.

Verses 15–16 · וַיִּקְרָא אַבְרָם שֶׁם-בְּנוֹ … יִשְׁמָעֵאל vayiqra Avram shem-beno … Yishma'el

"And Abram called the name of his son… Ishmael." A quiet detail with a story inside it: the angel told Hagar to name the boy ("you shall call his name," v11 — the mother, unusually, as name-giver); verse 15 has Abram do it. Between the two verses sits an unrecorded conversation: she went back, and she told the household everything — and the name on Abram's lips means the desert revelation was received as a word from God. The ledger then closes in the census style of chapter 5: Abram was 86 — and the numbers square: 75 leaving Haran (12:4), ten years in Canaan (16:3), 86 at the birth. Thirteen silent years now pass between this verse and the next ("Abram was 99 years old," 17:1) — years in which Ishmael is Abram's only son, which is exactly the tension the next chapter walks into. Genesis will give Ishmael twelve princes (25:12-16) and a place beside Isaac at their father's grave (25:9); later tradition — Jewish, Christian, and Islamic — remembers him as ancestor of the Arab peoples. The closed scroll-break {ס} draws the line under the ledger.

Patterns worth carrying forward

Where this translation sides with the literal shelf: "built up through her" (the ben/"build" pun kept, not smoothed to "obtain children"), "a wild donkey of a man" (the animal kept, against KJV's "wild man"), and verse 13 left rough ("seen after the one who sees me") rather than paved.

Where it goes its own way: "slave-girl" for shiphchah (where the shelf's "handmaid" upholsters the reality), "became slight in her eyes" (keeping the qalal scales visible), and "afflicted" kept identical across 16:6, 16:9 and 16:11 so the wire back to 15:13 stays live.

Echoes paid and planted. Paid: anah, "afflict" — prophesied of Israel in Egypt (15:13), dealt first by Israel's house to an Egyptian; the star-count verb of 15:5 returns in "so many they cannot be counted"; chamas (6:11) resurfaces in Sarai's "the wrong done to me"; qalal — "the one who belittles you" (12:3) — trips on Hagar's contempt; "listened to the voice of" reaches back to the garden (3:17); and Kadesh was fixed on the map at 14:7. Planted: shaphat, "may Jehovah judge" (18:25 is coming); the annunciation formula ("you are pregnant, and you shall bear a son…") that returns at 17:19 and far beyond; Beer-lahai-roi, where Isaac will settle (24:62); and thirteen silent years until "Abram was 99."
